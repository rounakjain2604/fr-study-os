The Map

Hold this skeleton in your head. Every question in this chapter lands on exactly one of these six nodes.

Node 1

Core principle

Directly attributable to a qualifying asset → capitalise. Everything else → expense when incurred.

Node 2

Scope exclusions

Cost of equity is never in. QAs at fair value & mass-produced repetitive inventories are optionally exempt.

Node 3

What counts as BC & QA

EIR interest, lease interest (Ind AS 116), eligible forex differences. QA = substantial time to get ready.

Node 4

How much

Specific: actual cost less temporary investment income. General: capitalisation rate × expenditure. Ceiling: actual costs incurred.

Node 5

When

Commence on the latest of three conditions. Suspend on extended interruption. Cease when substantially complete.

Node 6

Disclose

Amount capitalised in the period + the capitalisation rate used. Just two items.

¶ 3.1

Core Principle

Capitalise
Directly attributable costs

Borrowing costs directly attributable to the acquisition, construction or production of a qualifying asset are included in the cost of that asset — capitalisation is mandatory, not a choice, once conditions are met: (i) probable future economic benefits, and (ii) costs can be measured reliably.

Expense
All other borrowing costs

Recognised as an expense in the period in which they are incurred. There is no third route — no deferral, no reserve, no equity adjustment.

Litmus test the standard uses: eligible borrowing costs are those that would have been avoided had the expenditure on the qualifying asset not been made. Every tricky question resolves against this test.
¶ 3.2

Scope & Exclusions

Two different kinds of "out of scope" — and the exam loves to blur them. One is an absolute exclusion; the other two are optional exemptions from the capitalisation requirement.

Absolute exclusion

Cost of equity

Actual or imputed cost of equity — including preference capital not classified as a liability — is never a borrowing cost. So: equity dividends, equity issue costs, dividend on equity-classified preference shares — all out. (Example 1)

Not required

QA measured at fair value

e.g. a biological asset under Ind AS 41. Capitalisation wouldn't change the balance sheet; it would merely reshuffle finance cost vs fair-value movement within P&L.

Not required

Repetitive mass inventories

Inventories manufactured in large quantities on a repetitive basis. Rationale: allocating and monitoring borrowing costs across such inventories is impractical.

Trap: "exemption" ≠ "prohibition". Cheese with a long production cycle is mass-produced, so capitalisation is not required — but it is permitted because the cycle takes a substantial period (Illustration 1). Read the question stem: "required to" vs "can".
¶ 3.3

Key Definitions

Borrowing costs interest + other costs on borrowing of funds

  • Interest expense calculated using the effective interest rate method per Ind AS 109
  • Interest on lease liabilities recognised per Ind AS 116
  • Exchange differences on foreign-currency borrowings, only to the extent regarded as an adjustment to interest costs
Not dividends on equity, not notional interest on the entity's own cash. Ind AS 23 caps capitalisation at actual borrowing costs incurred.

Qualifying asset necessarily takes substantial time to get ready for intended use or sale

  • Can be: inventories, manufacturing plants, power generation facilities, intangible assets, investment properties, bearer plants
  • Never: financial assets; inventories produced over a short period; assets ready for intended use or sale when acquired
  • "Substantial period": Ind AS 23 deliberately gives no bright line — facts and circumstances; 12 months or more is likely substantial

The five definition illustrations — judgement calls

ILL 2Computer software under development taking substantial time — qualifying asset? Does management intention matter?
Yes and yes. An intangible taking substantial time to get ready is a QA. Management assesses at acquisition whether the asset is "ready for intended use" — if an acquired asset can only be used with a larger group of assets, or was acquired for constructing one specific QA, assess on a combined basis.

ILL 4Real estate co. buys (a) a permit for one building, (b) equipment usable across many projects. Capitalise on each?

ILL 5Interest on a finance lease of a crane / dockyard used to build a ship — capitalise?
Yes. Lease interest is capitalised if the leased asset is a QA or is used solely to construct one. The ship is the QA; lease interest on the crane is capitalised — but only up to the point construction of the ship is complete.

¶ 3.4

Exchange Differences

The most-tested computation. The logic: you borrowed in a foreign currency to save interest. The exchange loss is treated as borrowing cost only up to the interest you "saved" versus borrowing in your functional currency.

Eligible exchange difference = min [ Exchange loss , (Functional-currency interest that would have been incurred − Actual FC interest) ]
Total capitalisable BC can never exceed the functional-currency benchmark.

Worked anchor — Illustration 6

Loan USD 20,000 @ 5%, taken 1-Apr-20X1 at ₹45/$; closing ₹48/$. INR alternative: 11%.
(a) Actual interest: 20,000 × 5% × ₹48₹ 48,000
(b) INR benchmark: 20,000 × ₹45 × 11%₹ 99,000
(c) Headroom (b − a)₹ 51,000
Exchange loss: 20,000 × (48 − 45)₹ 60,000
Eligible exchange diff = min(60,000, 51,000)₹ 51,000
Capitalise 48,000 + 51,000₹ 99,000
Expense balance loss₹ 9,000

The reversal rule — Example 2 & 3 logic subsequent gains claw back prior loss adjustments

Where an unrealised exchange loss was treated as an adjustment to interest, and a later realised or unrealised gain arises on the same borrowing, the gain is recognised as an adjustment to interest — but only to the extent of the loss previously recognised.

  • Example 2: $1,000 @ 4% at ₹40→₹50; INR rate 12%. Interest ₹2,000; benchmark ₹4,800; eligible loss capped at ₹2,800; total BC = ₹4,800. If closing were ₹41: total FC cost ₹2,640 < ₹4,800, so the entire ₹1,000 loss is eligible.
  • Example 3 (₹50→₹48): gain ₹2,000 adjusts interest, being < prior ₹2,800 loss. (₹50→₹44): gain ₹6,000, but adjustment limited to ₹2,800. On part repayment ($600 repaid): adjustment on the $400 balance limited to its share of prior loss, ₹1,120 (= 2,800 × 400/1,000).
¶ 3.5

Specific vs General Borrowings

Specific
Borrowed for that asset

Capitalise actual borrowing costs incurred during the period less investment income on the temporary investment of those borrowings. Direct identification, no rate needed.

General
Pool of everything else

Capitalise capitalisation rate × expenditure on the QA. The rate is the weighted average cost of all general borrowings outstanding in the period — excluding specific borrowings until their asset is substantially complete.

Capitalisation rate = Σ (weighted-avg borrowing costs on general borrowings) ÷ Σ (total general borrowings outstanding)
BC capitalised = Expenditure on QA × rate × time outstanding   |  Ceiling: actual BC incurred in the period

Illustration 8 — when specific turns general

An 8% loan funded Building A, completed 31-Mar-20X3, but stays outstanding till 20X7. From completion, it joins the general pool: had cash not been spent on other QAs, it could have repaid this loan — so its cost is now "avoidable", i.e. directly attributable to the new Building B.

The expenditure base ¶ 3.5.3 – 3.5.4

  • Counts only expenditures via cash paid, transfers of other assets, or assumption of interest-bearing liabilities
  • Reduced by progress payments received and government grants (Ind AS 20)
  • Average carrying amount during the period (incl. BC already capitalised) is a reasonable approximation of the base
  • If carrying amount / expected cost exceeds recoverable amount or NRV → write down per other standards (and write back where those standards permit)
¶ 3.6

Period of Capitalisation

Commence — ¶ 3.6.1

Latest of three

Begin on the date the entity first meets all three: (a) incurs expenditure for the asset, (b) incurs borrowing costs, (c) undertakes activities necessary to prepare the asset. Activities include technical & administrative work pre-construction (e.g. permits) — but not merely holding an asset with no development.

Suspend — ¶ 3.6.2

Extended interruption

Suspend during extended periods when active development is interrupted — those are costs of holding a part-finished asset. Do not suspend for temporary delays that are a necessary part of the process: seasonal high water on a bridge, inventory undergoing slow maturation.

Cease — ¶ 3.6.3

Substantially complete

Stop when substantially all activities needed for intended use or sale are done — normally physical completion, even if routine admin or minor modifications (e.g. buyer's decoration) remain. Completed-in-parts: cease per part if each is usable alone (business park); whole-asset rule if sequential (steel mill).

Commencement-date checker

Pre-loaded with Illustration 10. The answer is always the latest date.

Illustration 10: interest from 15-May, site planning from 2-Jun, expenditure from 19-Jun, construction 18-Jul → commencement 19-Jun-20X1. Physical construction start is irrelevant once planning counts as "activities".

Cessation judgement calls

  • Example 5 (H Ltd): commercial complex ready 31-May-20X1; only 10% rented by year-end. Capitalise only up to 31-May — occupancy is irrelevant; readiness governs.
  • Example 4(a): construction halted Oct–Jan, equipment shifted to another site → suspend.
  • Example 4(b): 20-day technical delay near completion → continue capitalising.
  • Land: BC capitalised while land is under development; not while it is merely held for building with no activity.
¶ 3.7

Disclosure

Only two disclosures

  • (a) the amount of borrowing costs capitalised during the period
  • (b) the capitalisation rate used to determine the amount eligible for capitalisation
Memory hook: "How much, and at what rate." Note — disclosure of the rate is an Ind AS 23 requirement that AS 16 does not have (difference no. 9).

Live example — Larsen & Toubro, FY 2021-22

Additions to buildings / CWIP included ₹8.83 crore of borrowing cost capitalised under Ind AS 23 (previous year ₹27.75 crore), and the capitalisation rate used was 6.23% (previous year 5.71%). L&T's policy: borrowing costs net of investment income on temporary investment of related borrowings are capitalised till the asset is ready; all other borrowing costs go to P&L.

¶ 3.8

Special Situations

3.8.1Dividends on redeemable preference shares, redeemable at the holder's option — borrowing cost?
Yes. Such shares are financial liabilities under Ind AS 32, so the dividend is in substance interest — it meets the borrowing-cost definition and is capitalised following the specific borrowing principles (Example 6). Contrast: dividend on equity-classified preference capital is excluded from scope.
tap to reveal
3.8.2Parent borrows, subsidiary builds the qualifying asset. Who capitalises what?
Group FS: capitalise if the amount fairly reflects the group's interest cost on third-party borrowings that could have been avoided. Subsidiary's own FS: nothing — it has no borrowings. But if it takes an intra-group loan, it capitalises the actual costs it incurs on that loan (Illustration 11).
tap to reveal
3.8.3Whisky matures at 3 years but keeps improving. Keep capitalising borrowing costs after year 3?
Yes, conditionally. If holding-to-mature is consistent with the business model and the item demonstrably keeps increasing in value solely because of age (not market movements or inflation), continue. If that can't be demonstrated, the stock is held-for-sale — stop capitalising (Example 7).
tap to reveal
¶ 3.5Hyperinflationary economy (Ind AS 29 applies) — full borrowing cost capitalised?
No. The part of borrowing costs that compensates for inflation during the period is recognised as an expense, not capitalised.
tap to reveal
TYK 7Bonds issued at a discount fund a QA ready at end of year 2 of 4. How much discount/interest is capitalised?
Borrowing cost = EIR interest on the carrying amount (transaction price ₹1,80,000 for the ₹2,00,000 bond at 10% discount). Capitalise years 1–2 only: ₹24,102 + ₹24,651 = ₹48,753; years 3–4 EIR interest is expensed.
tap to reveal
¶ 3.5.1.1Entity uses its own cash to build a plant. Capitalise the interest the cash "could have earned"?
Never. Notional borrowing costs cannot be capitalised; Ind AS 23 limits capitalisation to actual borrowing costs incurred, and does not address actual or imputed cost of equity.

Exam Trap Sheet

Read this the night before the paper. Each line is a place candidates routinely bleed marks.

  • Investment income is deducted only for specific borrowings. The general-borrowing computation is rate × expenditure — no income offset appears in it.
  • The ceiling: capitalised BC in a period can never exceed actual BC incurred in that period (see TYK 6 — eligible ₹1,68,000 vs incurred ₹2,10,000; capitalise only ₹1,68,000 as it is lower).
  • Exclude specific borrowings from the capitalisation-rate pool until their asset is substantially complete (Illustration 9's debentures) — and pull them into the pool once the asset is done but the loan is still outstanding (Illustration 8).
  • Commencement = latest of the three conditions, and "activities" start with technical/admin work (planning, permits) — not physical construction (Illustration 10: 19-Jun, not 18-Jul).
  • Mere holding ≠ activity. Land held for building with no development → no capitalisation; land under development → capitalise.
  • Suspension vs necessary delay: equipment shifted away for months → suspend; routine seasonal high water or maturing inventory → continue.
  • Cessation looks at readiness, not usage. H Ltd: complex ready 31-May, only 10% let out — capitalisation still stops 31-May.
  • Forex cap: eligible exchange loss = headroom up to functional-currency benchmark interest; the excess is an FX loss in P&L, not borrowing cost. Subsequent gains reverse only up to prior loss adjustments.
  • Forex benchmark under Ind AS 23 is the functional currency (AS 16 / AS 11 compared local vs foreign currency).
  • Substantial period: Ind AS 23 gives no definition (12 months is only a likely indicator); AS 16 explains it as twelve months. If a renovation completes within 12 months and management doesn't consider the period substantial, everything is expensed (TYK 8 alternate: full ₹68.20 lakh to P&L).
  • Working-capital portion of a "project" loan is not for a QA — apportion and expense it (TYK 8: 56/620 share = ₹6.16 lakh expensed).
  • Notional interest on own funds — never. Cost of equity (incl. equity-classified preference dividend) — never. Liability-classified preference dividend — yes, as specific borrowing cost.
  • Consolidation: eliminate intra-group profit margin from the QA expenditure base before applying the rate (TYK 6: 15,40,000 ÷ 1.1).
  • Discounted bonds: capitalise EIR interest, not coupon, and only for the construction period (TYK 7: ₹48,753 over years 1–2).
  • Repetitive inventories exemption is optional — capitalisation is permitted where the cycle is substantial (cheese, whisky), required nowhere, prohibited nowhere within that class.
