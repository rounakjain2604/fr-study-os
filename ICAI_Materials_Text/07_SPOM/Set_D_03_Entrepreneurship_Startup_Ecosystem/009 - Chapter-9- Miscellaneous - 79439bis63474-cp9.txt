     9.2 SOURCES OF FUNDS AND FUNDING AGENTS

 Source of Funds                            Description

 Bootstrapping      Using personal savings, credit cards, or funds from friends and family
                        to finance the startup's initial operations.

                   ownership equity or convertible debt. They often offer expertise and
                    mentorship in addition to funding.

                    ownership. Venture capitalists typically invest larger sums of money

                 management and decision-making.

 Crowdfunding      Raising small amounts of money from a large number of individuals
                    through online platforms. There are different types of crowdfunding,
                      including rewards-based (e.g., Kickstarter), equity-based, and debt-
                  based crowdfunding.

Funding agents play a pivotal role in the startup ecosystem, providing essential capital, resources,
and support to fuel the growth and success of innovative ventures. These agents encompass a
diverse array of entities, each offering unique avenues for startups to secure funding and propel
their ideas forward.
Angel investors are often the first port of call for early-stage startups, offering not just financial
backing but also valuable expertise, mentorship, and networking opportunities. Their willingness to
take calculated risks on promising ideas can be instrumental in kickstarting a startup's journey.
Venture capital firms, on the other hand, provide startups with larger sums of capital in exchange for
equity stakes. These firms typically invest in startups with high growth potential, offering not only
financial support but also strategic guidance and industry connections to help startups scale rapidly.
Crowdfunding platforms democratize the funding process by enabling startups to raise capital from
a  large  pool  of  individual  investors. Whether  through rewards-based  crowdfunding,  equity
crowdfunding, or debt crowdfunding, these platforms empower startups to engage directly with their
target audience and secure funding while building a community around their idea.

In the early 2000s, Netflix faced a significant challenge as it transitioned from a DVD rental service
to a streaming platform. At the time, the company's business model relied heavily on mailing DVDs
to customers, but the rise of digital streaming threatened its future viability.

To navigate this transition successfully, Netflix's leadership team, including CEO Reed Hastings and
Chief Content Officer Ted Sarandos, made a series of bold decisions. They invested heavily in
developing  original  content and  securing  licensing  agreements  with  major  studios,  while
simultaneously improving the user experience of their streaming platform.
Behind the scenes, Netflix's engineering and product teams played a crucial role in building and
optimizing the streaming infrastructure to deliver high-quality video content to millions of subscribers
worldwide. Their collaborative efforts resulted in innovations such as adaptive bitrate streaming,
personalized recommendations, and seamless playback across devices.

enabled the company to not only survive the transition but also thrive in the increasingly competitive

Investors seek to exit their startup investments to realize returns, rebalance portfolios, and meet
liquidity needs. Startup investments are typically long-term, and exiting allows investors to capture
gains and reinvest in new opportunities. As it is said, diversification is crucial for managing risk, and
thus, exiting a startup investment enables investors to allocate capital across different asset classes.
Liquidity considerations also play a role, as investors may require cash for personal or business
expenses.
Also, strategic factors such as changes in market conditions or industry dynamics may prompt
investors to exit investments to optimize returns or mitigate risks. Ultimately, the decision to exit a
startup investment is driven by a combination of financial objectives, portfolio considerations, and
strategic considerations, with the aim of maximizing returns and aligning investment strategies with
evolving market trends and opportunities.
Return on investment (ROI) expectations for investors can vary widely depending on factors such
as the stage of the startup, the industry, and the specific investment opportunity. However, investors
in startups generally seek high potential returns to compensate for the significant risks associated
with early-stage investing.
While there is no fixed benchmark for ROI in the startup world, investors often look for opportunities
that have the potential to generate returns several times higher than their initial investment. This can
range from a 5x  to 10x return  for early-stage investments, and even higher  for successful
investments in later-stage startups.
Investors may consider other factors such as the growth potential of the startup, the scalability of its
business model, the strength of  its management team, and the competitive landscape when
evaluating ROI expectations.
Startups have several exit strategies available to them to support Investor return expectations -
depending on their goals, growth stage, and market conditions;
♦    Acquisition: An acquisition occurs when a larger company purchases a startup, usually for
      a combination of cash, stock, or a combination of both. This can provide a lucrative exit for
      founders, investors, and employees, as well as access  to additional resources and
       opportunities for the startup to scale within the acquiring company.
♦      Initial Public Offering (IPO): An IPO involves offering shares of a startup's stock to the
       public for the first time on a stock exchange. This allows the startup to raise significant
       capital and provides  liquidity  for existing shareholders, including founders and early
       investors. However, IPOs are complex and expensive processes, typically pursued by
       larger, more established startups with strong financial performance.

♦    Merger: A merger occurs when two companies combine to form a single entity. In some
      cases, startups may pursue mergers with complementary companies to achieve economies
       of scale, expand their market reach, or strengthen their competitive position. Mergers can
      provide an exit opportunity for founders and investors, as well as potential synergies and
      growth opportunities for the combined entity.
♦    Management Buyout (MBO): In an MBO, the existing management team of a startup
      acquires a controlling stake in the company from  its current owners, such as venture
       capitalists or angel investors. This allows founders and key employees to retain ownership
     and control of the company while providing liquidity for existing investors.
♦     Liquidation: In some cases, startups may choose to liquidate their assets and shut down
       operations. While this is typically considered a last resort, it can provide an exit for founders
     and investors if the startup is unable to achieve profitability or secure additional funding.
♦    Secondary Sale: A secondary sale involves selling shares of a startup to another private
       investor or investment firm. This allows existing shareholders to cash out their holdings
       without the need for an IPO or acquisition. Secondary sales can provide liquidity for

9.6.1 IPO (Initial Public Offering)


                                     Initial Public Offering
                                      (IPO)


An Initial Public Offering (IPO) is the process by which a privately held company becomes a publicly-
traded entity by offering its shares to the general public for the first time on a stock exchange. The
IPO process involves several steps, beginning with the selection of investment banks, also known
as underwriters, to manage the offering. These underwriters assist the company in preparing the
necessary documentation, including a prospectus that provides information about the company's
business, financials, and the intended use of proceeds from the offering.

Once the documentation is prepared, the company files its IPO registration statement with the SEBI
(Securities & Exchange Board of India), which reviews the filing to ensure compliance with regulatory
requirements. During this period, known as the "quiet period," the company and its underwriters
engage in a roadshow to market the offering to potential investors, including institutional investors,
retail investors, and analysts.
Following SEBI’s approval of the registration statement, the company sets the IPO price and the
number of shares to be offered. On the day of the IPO, the company's shares begin trading on the
designated stock exchange, and investors have the opportunity to buy and sell shares in the open
market. The price at which the shares trade is determined by supply and demand dynamics, as well
as investor sentiment and market conditions.
After the IPO, the company becomes subject to ongoing reporting and disclosure requirements
imposed by securities laws and regulations. This includes regular financial reporting, disclosure of
material events, and compliance with corporate governance standards to ensure transparency and
accountability to shareholders.
An IPO is a significant milestone for a company, providing access to capital, liquidity for existing
shareholders, and increased visibility and credibility in the marketplace. However, the process can
be complex and time-consuming, requiring careful planning, coordination, and compliance with
regulatory requirements.
