     1.  INVENTORY VALUATION

Introduction:

Valuation is a complex process involving the assessment of various parameters, external factors,
and assumptions to estimate the worth of an asset. Even though supported by thorough research, it
inherently requires judgment, necessitating scrutiny of the reasonableness of assumptions. Mere
estimations without substantiation hinder the independent evaluation of fair value.

In the context of financial reporting under IND AS 2, accurate inventory valuation is essential for
reliable financial statements and consequent decision-making. The book value of inventory often
deviates significantly from its fair value, highlighting the importance of understanding the value chain
dynamics from procurement to sale when determining fair value.

1.1  Basics of Inventory Valuation
1.1.1  Definition of Inventory Valuation:

IND AS-2 on “Inventory” defines Inventory as: - “Inventories are assets:

(a)    held for sale in the ordinary course of business;

(b)     in the process of production for such sale; or

1.1.2 Methods under IND AS 2: Cost Formulas

IND AS 2 prescribes methods such as Specific  Identification, FIFO  (First-In, First-Out), and
Weighted Average for inventory valuation, emphasizing compliance with the standard.

FIFO (First-In, First-Out)- It assumes that the items of inventory that were purchased or produced
first are sold first, and consequently the items remaining in inventory at the end of the period are
those most recently purchased or produced. Hence under this method, the inventory is being valued
close to the current replacement cost. It is the most used inventory measurement method.

Weighted Average Cost (WAC)- The cost of each item is determined from the weighted average of
the cost of similar items at the beginning of a period and the cost of similar items purchased or
produced during the period. The average maybe calculated on a periodic basis or as each additional
shipment is received, depending upon the circumstances of the entity.

Specific Identification Method- It attaches the actual cost to an identifiable unit of product. Under
the specific identification method, the entity must identify each unit in inventory, unless it is unique,
with a unique identification tag or serial number. This method is particularly used by entities dealing
or manufacturing high valued large inventory items such as airplanes, railway coaches, etc.

LIFO (Last-In, First-Out) - It assumes that the items of inventory that were purchased or produced
most recently are sold first, and consequently the items remaining in inventory at the end of the

1.2  Significance in Financial Reporting and Strategic Decision-Making

Valuation of inventory has a direct impact on both the balance sheet and income statement, thereby
influencing key financial indicators. Beyond its necessity for financial reporting and accounting
purposes, the fair value of inventory assumes significance in various corporate activities such as
mergers, demergers, liquidation, insolvency, transfer pricing,  litigation, and fundraising efforts,
among others. Precise and accurate inventory valuation is imperative for conducting comprehensive
profitability analyses, facilitating informed resource allocation, and guiding investment decisions.

The role of inventory valuation extends to  its pivotal influence on determining optimal pricing
strategies, thereby shaping a company's competitiveness within the market.

Strategic planning benefits significantly from the precision afforded by inventory valuation which
ensures realistic budgeting and enhances the accuracy of forecasting processes, contributing to the
overall efficiency and effectiveness of organizational management.

1.2.1 Impact of Non-Financial Parameters on the value of Inventory

The value of inventory is intricately influenced by several non-financial parameters that extend
beyond mere monetary considerations.

i     Perishability plays a pivotal role, as items with a limited shelf life demand swift turnover to
      avoid losses.

i     Seasonality introduces  fluctuations, impacting the demand and subsequently  affecting
       inventory value.

i    Obsolescence is another crucial factor, rendering outdated items less valuable over time.

i    The strength of an inventory system, including its efficiency and accuracy, directly impacts
      value by minimizing errors and ensuring optimal management.

i     Regulatory  compliance  is  essential,  as  adherence  to  industry  standards and  legal
      requirements safeguards the integrity and value of the inventory.

By considering these non-financial parameters, businesses can enhance their overall inventory
management strategies and maintain a more accurate reflection of value.
1.2.2  Trial Balance and Closing Stock

The trial balance summarizes the balances of all ledger accounts at a specific point in time, typically
the end of an accounting period. However, it excludes the closing stock value as it represents the
cost of unsold inventory on hand and is not directly tied to any specific account balance.
1.2.3 Impact on Profitability

The closing stock value directly impacts the calculation of the cost of goods sold (COGS), which in
turn affects the final profit figure. An accurate closing stock valuation is crucial for presenting a true
and fair view of the company's financial performance.
1.2.4 Manipulation and Ethical Concerns

The importance of inventory valuation can unfortunately lead to unethical practices. Undervaluing
closing inventory inflates the cost of goods sold (COGS), leading to a lower reported profit. This can
be tempting for companies aiming to reduce their tax liabilities or mislead investors about their
profitability. Conversely, overvaluing inventory can  artificially boost the COGS, leading to an
artificially inflated profit figure. This can be tempting for companies aiming to smooth out earnings
or mislead investors about their profitability.

1.2.5 Valuers and Best Practices

Valuers play a  critical role in safeguarding against such manipulation and ensuring  fair value
representation.

They employ various techniques and best practices to achieve this:

i    Physical Inventory Count: This involves physically counting the inventory on hand at the
      end of the accounting period. While seemingly straightforward, proper counting procedures
      and documentation are crucial for accuracy.

i    Market Comparison: Benchmarking the closing stock value against prevailing market prices
        for similar goods can provide valuable insights and identify potential discrepancies.

i    Scrutiny  of  Inventory  Records: Reviewing   historical  inventory  records, purchase
       invoices, and sales records can help identify any unusual fluctuations or inconsistencies that
      might indicate manipulation attempts.

i    Professional Scepticism: Maintaining a healthy level of scepticism throughout the valuation
      process is crucial. Valuers should critically analyse the information provided by management
      and be alert to potential red flags that might suggest attempts to manipulate the closing stock
       value.

An "investment" refers to the commitment of resources, typically capital, with the expectation of

2.    Debt

3.     Hybrid

Debt: Debt investments involve lending money to an entity, typically in exchange for periodic interest
payments and the return of the principal amount at maturity. Bonds and fixed-income securities are
common examples.

Hybrid: Hybrid investments combine characteristics of both equity and debt. Convertible bonds,
preferred stocks, and certain types of preferred equity instruments are examples of hybrid securities.

2.1.2 Income Approach

The Income Approach is a valuation method that converts maintainable or future amounts (e.g., cash
flows or income and expenses) to a single current (i.e., discounted, or capitalised) amount. The fair
value measurement is determined based on the value indicated by current market expectations
about those future amounts. Valuers may opt for the Income Approach in situations where there are
no market comparables, fewer relevant market comparables, or when the asset generates income
with reasonably projectable future cash flows.

Common valuation methods under the Income Approach include the Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)
Method, Relief from Royalty (RFR) Method, Multi-Period Excess Earnings Method (MEEM), With
and Without Method (WWM), and option pricing models like Black-Scholes-Merton formula. The DCF
Method is extensively used, involving the discounting of projected cash flows over the asset's life,
and determining a terminal value for assets with indefinite life.

The DCF Method starts by analyzing projections, selecting appropriate cash flows, determining
discount and growth rates, and applying these to calculate present values. Adjustments may be
made for factors not covered by cash flow forecasts or discount rates. The method incorporates
considerations for control or non-controlling interests.

Some of the important inputs for the DCF method include cash flows, discount rates, and terminal
value. Cash flows are derived from projections, and the explicit forecast period's length depends on
the nature, life, and sufficient period for stability of the asset. Discount rates, representing expected
returns, can be determined through methods like Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) or Weighted

forecast period, can be estimated using various methods such as Gordon Growth Model or Exit

valuation approaches may be less applicable or reliable.

2.1.3 Cost Approach

The Cost Approach is a valuation method that reflects the amount needed currently to replace the
service capacity of an asset, commonly known as current replacement cost. The historical cost of
the asset may be considered in specific situations based on regulations, laws, or guidelines, or when
appropriate for the asset's nature. Valuers apply the Cost Approach when an asset can be quickly
recreated with similar utility, in cases of liquidation value determination, or when the income or
market approach cannot be utilized.

Examples of situations where Cost Approach is applied include scenarios where the asset has not
yet generated income, an asset of similar utility can be recreated with regulatory or legal restrictions,
or the asset was recently created. Two commonly used valuation methods under the Cost Approach
are the Replacement Cost Method and the Reproduction Cost Method.

(i)    Replacement Cost Method:  It involves valuing an asset based on the cost a market
       participant  would  incur  to  recreate  an  asset  with  comparable   utility,  adjusted  for
      obsolescence. Obsolescence includes physical deterioration, functional (technological), and
      economic factors. Steps include estimating costs for recreation, assessing obsolescence
       loss, and adjusting to derive the asset's value.


© The Institute of Chartered Accountants of India

(ii)   Reproduction Cost Method:  It involves valuing an asset based on the cost a market
       participant would incur to recreate a replica of the asset, adjusted for obsolescence. Similar
      steps to the Replacement Cost Method, including estimating costs, assessing obsolescence
       loss, and adjusting to determine the asset's value.

(iii)   Obsolescence: Under both methods, estimated costs are adjusted for depreciation due to
      obsolescence. Types  of obsolescence include physical, functional  (technological), and
      economic (external) factors. Physical obsolescence relates to decreased usefulness as the
       asset's  life expires, functional obsolescence involves  inefficiency due  to technological
      advancements, and economic obsolescence results from external economic factors.

The Cost Approach is typically used for the valuation of property, plant, equipment, and certain
intangible assets, providing specific guidance within the ICAI Valuation Standards.

2.2  Valuation of Preferred Stock

Valuing preferred stock requires navigating its unique characteristics, which lie somewhere between
traditional bonds and common stocks. Unlike bonds, preferred stock typically doesn't have a fixed
maturity date and carries dividend payments that can be suspended in certain circumstances. While


© The Institute of Chartered Accountants of India

1.166.16           VALUATION


offering preferential rights to dividends over common stockholders, preferred shares generally lack
voting rights and the potential for capital appreciation seen with common shares.

Specific  features  like cumulative  dividends,  convertibility,  callability, and  participating  rights
significantly impact the valuation of preferred stock. For instance, a cumulative preferred stock
accrues any unpaid dividends, making it more valuable than a non-cumulative one.

Similarly, a convertible preferred stock, offering the option to convert into common shares, may
command a premium due to its potential for capital gains. On the other hand, callability, allowing the
issuer to 'call back' the shares at a predetermined price, might reduce the stock's value due to the
potential for an earlier redemption of the investment.

Valuers translate these qualitative features into quantitative adjustments by employing various
models. The dividend discount model is often used, where the present value of expected future
dividends is calculated. Adjustments are made to the discount rate or dividend amount to reflect the
risks and potential benefits associated with specific features, ultimately arriving at a fair value
estimate for the preferred stock.

2.3  Valuation of Convertible Instruments

Convertible instruments, the financial chameleon, occupy a peculiar space in the capital structure,
blending debt and equity characteristics. As such, their valuation demands a nuanced approach that
captures their multifaceted nature. Unlike traditional bonds, convertibles offer the potential for equity-
like upside through their conversion option, allowing the holder to exchange them for common stock
at a predetermined price and ratio. This potential upside, however, comes at the cost of a lower
coupon rate compared to traditional debt instruments.

The valuation journey of a convertible instrument begins with acknowledging the inherent trade-off
between debt-like security and equity-like optionality. Valuers often employ a two-stage process. In
the first stage, the instrument's intrinsic value as debt is estimated using traditional bond valuation
techniques, such as the discounted cash flow (DCF) model. This stage accounts for the fixed interest
payments and the principal repayment at maturity. In the second stage, the optionality embedded in
the conversion feature is valued using option pricing models, like the Black-Scholes model. This
model incorporates factors such as the underlying stock's volatility, the conversion price, the time to
maturity, and the risk-free interest rate to arrive at a fair value for the conversion option.

Ultimately, the final valuation of a convertible instrument represents the sum of its intrinsic debt
value and the option value, aptly reflecting its hybrid nature. This delicate dance between debt-like
certainty and equity-like potential necessitates a comprehensive understanding of both traditional

2.4  Valuation of Hybrid Instruments

Hybrid instruments, the captivating creations of financial engineering, defy easy categorization. They
combine between debt and equity, borrowing features from both while forging their unique identity.
This intricate dance presents both challenges and opportunities for valuation professionals.

The valuation of hybrid instruments demands a multifaceted approach that acknowledges their two
faced nature.  Traditional methodologies, honed  for pure debt or equity instruments, require
adaptation to capture the nuances of these hybrids. Similar to valuing convertible instruments, a
two-stage approach often proves effective. The  first stage involves estimating the instrument's
intrinsic value, often using discounted cash flow (DCF) models or comparable company analysis,
depending on the instrument's specific characteristics. This stage accounts for the fixed or variable
cash flows embedded in the instrument.

The second stage focuses on the non-traditional features that distinguish the hybrid from its pure-
play counterparts. These features can include participation rights in the issuer's profits, contingent
payments linked to specific performance metrics, or call options granting the issuer the right to
repurchase the instrument at a predetermined price. Valuing these features requires expertise in
option pricing models and careful consideration of the instrument's unique structure. They may be
valued individually and then combined with the intrinsic value to arrive at a final valuation.

Therefore, the valuation of hybrid instruments necessitates a comprehensive understanding of both
traditional valuation techniques and specialized option pricing models. By diligently addressing the
instrument's unique blend of debt and equity characteristics, and by  skillfully employing the
appropriate valuation tools, professionals can arrive at a fair and informed valuation, ensuring a
sound decision-making process.

Instruments with both derivative and non-derivative elements present a unique challenge  to

features of traditional debt or equity with embedded derivative components, blurring the lines
between asset classes and demanding a nuanced valuation approach.

The first step in valuing such instruments involves a thorough understanding of their structure. This

models. Conversely, investors, focused on maximizing returns and mitigating risk, might adopt a
more conservative stance. They may incorporate higher discount rates to reflect perceived risk,

Offered by banks and credit unions, CDs are time deposits with a fixed interest rate and maturity
date, typically ranging up to five years. The interest rates are usually higher than those of savings

A bond is a type of fixed-income investment where an investor loans money to an entity, typically a
corporation or government, for a defined period at a predetermined interest rate. In return, the issuer
of the bond agrees to pay the investor back the original amount borrowed (the principal) at a
specified future date, known as the maturity date, along with periodic interest payments. The bond’s
features, including the issuer, maturity, par value, coupon rate and frequency, and currency
denomination are important elements to consider. These features determine the bond’s scheduled

3.3  Bonds

Bonds, regardless of who issues them, share fundamental characteristics like maturity, principal
amount, coupon size, payment frequency, and currency. Various entities issue bonds, such as
individuals, governments, and corporations. Major issuers include Governments (both national and
local), Corporates, Supranational Organizations (like The World Bank), and special legal entities.

Maturity refers to the date on which a bond's issuer must repay the bond's full principal amount.
The term "tenor" describes the time left until the bond reaches its maturity date. Tenor is crucial for
evaluating a bond's risk and potential return since  it shows how long the bondholder will receive
interest payments and when the principal will be fully repaid.

The par value of a bond, also known as the principal amount, is the sum that the issuer commits to
repay the bondholders upon maturity. This value is also commonly referred to as par, face value,
nominal value, redemption value, or maturity value. Bonds can have varying par values.

The coupon rate, also known as the nominal rate, of a bond is the yearly interest rate the issuer
promises to pay up until the bond matures. This rate determines the annual interest payments,
referred to as the coupon. To calculate a bond's coupon, you multiply its coupon rate by its par value.
These interest payments can be made on an annual, semi-annual, or quarterly basis.

All bonds, whether they offer a fixed or floating interest rate, provide periodic coupon payments,
except for zero-coupon bonds. Zero-coupon bonds, also known as pure discount bonds, are sold
at a price below their par value and redeemed at par upon maturity. The interest earned on a zero-
coupon bond is implicit and equals the difference between the par value and the purchase price.

A traditional bond offers a fixed interest rate, meaning  its coupon payment remains constant
throughout its lifespan. However, some bonds feature a floating interest rate, known as floating-
rate notes (FRNs) or floaters, where the interest payment adjusts according to market rates.

Market participants commonly use various yield measures to assess bonds. The current yield, or
running yield, is calculated by dividing the bond's annual coupon by its price and expressing it as a
percentage.

For instance, if a bond with a 5% coupon rate, $1,000 par value, and $1,020 price, the current yield
is 4.90% ($50 ÷ $1,020) (Remaining duration is assumed as one year). This yield is similar to the
dividend yield for stocks.

The most prevalent measure is the yield-to-maturity (YTM), also known as the redemption yield.
YTM is the internal rate of return on a bond's expected cash flows, equating the present value of its
expected cash flows with its price. It's an estimate of the bond's expected return, representing the
annual return for an investor who buys the bond now and holds  it until maturity, reinvesting all
coupons at the same yield. YTM and bond price have an inverse relationship: higher YTM leads to
lower prices, and vice versa. Thus, investors anticipating lower interest rates (and a lower YTM)
expect positive returns from price appreciation.

Bond pricing involves discounted cash flow analysis, the complexity of which depends on the bond's
features and the discounting rate(s) applied. For traditional fixed-rate bonds without options, future
cash flows comprise coupon interest payments and  full principal repayment at maturity. These
payments occur on scheduled dates, such as annually or semi-annually, with the final coupon often
paid along with the principal at maturity. The bond's issuance price represents the present value of
these cash flows, calculated using the market discount rate. This rate, also known as the required
yield or rate of return, reflects investors' required return considering the bond's risk.

1.     Relief from Royalty Method (RRM)-

      Concept: The RRM calculates value based on the hypothetical royalty payments that would
      be saved by owning the asset rather than licensing  it. The rationale behind the RRM is
        intuitive: Owning an intangible asset means the underlying entity doesn’t have to pay for the
       privilege of deploying that asset.

       the owner did not own the asset. This rate is then used to calculate the present value of the
      revenue generated from the asset spread through its useful life, which becomes the value of
       the asset. The RRM contains assumptions from both the market approach and income
      approach.

      Real Life Use Case: The RRM is often used to value domain names, trademarks, licensed
      computer software, and in-progress R&D that can be tied to a specific revenue stream and
      where data on royalty and license fees from other market transactions are available.

3.    With and Without Method-

      Concept: This method compares the financial performance of a business with the intangible
      asset ("with") and without the intangible asset ("without"). The difference in performance

       Calculation: The valuation involves assessing the financial metrics of the business with the

      Concept: OPM values an intangible asset by treating it as a call option. It reflects the flexibility

       intangible, determining the expected life of the option, and applying an option pricing model
      (such as the Black-Scholes model) to calculate the present value of the intangible.

     Use Case: OPM  is often applied to value real options embedded in intangible assets,

6.    Obsolescence Factor in Replacement Cost Method:

     The Replacement Cost Method (RCM) estimates the value of an asset by considering the
       cost to replace  it with a modern equivalent. However, complete replacement might not be
       ideal due to:

       1.    Functional Obsolescence: The original asset's functionality may be outdated or less
                efficient compared to modern alternatives.

       2.    Economic Obsolescence: External factors like changes in market conditions or
              technological advancements might render the original asset less valuable.

       Therefore, we need to factor in obsolescence to adjust the replacement cost and reflect the
       actual value of the asset.

     The Mathematical Formula:

        Obsolescence Factor (OF) =  1 - (Depreciated Replacement Cost - Current Market Value
                                              of Asset) / Replacement Cost (New)
      Where:

   ¾    Depreciated Replacement Cost  is  the replacement  cost  adjusted  for  physical
             depreciation using an appropriate method (e.g., straight-line depreciation).

   ¾    Current Market Value of Asset is the fair market value of the asset in its current
              condition, considering any functional or economic obsolescence.

   ¾    Replacement Cost (New) is the cost to acquire a new, identical asset at current market
              prices.

       This formula calculates the proportion of value lost due to obsolescence.

      Example:

     Suppose a company has created a software for ` 50 lacs. The current software was estimated
       to have a useful life of 5 years. We are at the end of year 2. With rapid technology advances,
      a newer, more efficient software can now be developed for ` 25 lacs. The current market
      value of a similar software is ` 20 lacs

      Depreciated Replacement Cost = 50,00,000 – (50,00,000 / 5 * 2) = 30,00,000

      Current Market Value of similar software = 20,00,000

      Replacement Cost (new) = 25,00,000

Understanding the Weighted Average Cost of Capital i.e. WACC

To understand WACC we first need to understand the cost of capital. The cost of capital indicates
how much a company needs to pay to finance its operations.  It can be viewed as the minimum
amount the company has to earn to avoid defaulting on loans and handle its current debt and equity
responsibilities. The cost of capital depends on the capital structure of a company.

When a company uses both debt and equity to fund operations, that funding mix is called the
company’s capital structure. The weighted average cost of capital looks at a company’s capital
structure and compares equity and debt to their respective proportions of the capital structure. The
debt portion typically involves the company’s interest rates and loan payments, while equity may
involve dividend payments to investors.

 WACC =              [(Equity/Total Capital) x Cost of Equity]
                   +
                       [(Debt/Total Capital) x (After-Tax  Cost of debt)]

OR

WACC = (E/V* Ke) + (D/V* Kd(1-t))

Where

i   E = Market value of the business’s equity

i   V = Total value of capital (equity + debt)

i    Ke = Cost of equity

i   D = Market value of the business’s debt

i    Kd = Cost of debt

i    T = Tax rate

Calculating Cost of Equity i.e. Ke

Cost of equity is the return that an investor requires for investing in a company, or the required rate
of return that a company must receive on an investment or project. There are two ways to calculate
cost of equity: using the dividend capitalization model or the capital asset pricing model (CAPM).
Neither method is completely accurate because the return on investment is a calculation based on
predictions about the stock market.

Understanding the Weighted Average Return on Assets i.e. WARA

The weighted average return on assets, or WARA, is the collective rates of return on the various
types of tangible and intangible assets of a company. The presumption of a WARA is that each class
of a company's asset base (such as property, plant and equipment, contracts, software, brand
names, etc.) carries its own rate of return, each unique to the asset's underlying operational risk as
well as its ability to attain debt and equity.

When performing the WARA calculation, it is important to remember that the asset values used to
calculate the WARA need to represent normal operating levels required to sustain the value of the
entity.  In other words,  all non-operating assets and  liabilities are excluded from the WARA
calculation (both from the individual assets included and from the purchase price used in the
calculation). For example, a non-operating asset such as excess cash is excluded, and the WARA
calculation reflects only normal levels of working capital for the entity (based on market participant
perspectives)

The WARA Formula = [(Asset A/Total Assets)  * Return on Asset A] + [(Asset B/Total
Assets)*Return on Asset B]+….. [(Asset n/Total Assets) * Return on Asset n]

Reconciling WACC and WARA

In essence, comparing the Weighted Average Return on Assets (WARA) to the Weighted Average
Cost of Capital (WACC) serves as a diagnostic tool for valuation specialists. This comparison helps
in reconciling the rates of return required by capital providers (WACC) with the returns earned by
various asset classes (WARA). The WARA calculation is crucial for assessing the reasonableness
of asset-specific returns for identified intangible assets and the implied return on goodwill.  It is
calculated by adding the required rates of return for normalized working capital, fixed assets, and
intangible assets, each weighted by its proportionate share of the entity's total value. A significant
deviation between WARA and WACC may necessitate a reassessment of asset values and assumed
returns to align with market participant assumptions. A higher WARA compared to WACC  is
generally viewed as a positive indicator of the company's financial health. It is to be noted that in
each intangible asset valuation, WACC WARA reconciliation must be provided.

The measurement of financial liabilities under the Indian Accounting Standards (IND AS), specifically
as per IND AS 109  - "Financial Instruments," involves several key principles. While the  initial
recognition is done at fair value, subsequent measurement of financial liabilities depends on their
classification, which includes - Amortized Cost, Fair Value through Profit or Loss or Fair Value
through Other Comprehensive Income.

Amortized Cost: Financial  liabilities that are measured at amortized cost are those held for
collection of contractual cash flows where the objective is to hold the asset to collect these cash
flows. Measurement is based on the effective interest rate method, where the carrying amount is
adjusted for the amortization of any discount or premium and the accrual of interest income.

Fair Value Through Other Comprehensive Income (FVOCI): Certain financial liabilities, primarily
debt instruments, may be designated as measured at  fair value through other comprehensive
income. Changes in fair value are recognized in other comprehensive income (OCI), and only
interest revenue, impairment losses, and foreign exchange gains or losses are recognized in profit
or loss.

Fair Value Through Profit or Loss (FVTPL): Financial liabilities that are not classified as amortized
cost or FVOCI are measured at fair value through profit or loss. Changes in fair value are recognized
in profit or loss, and the instrument is revalued at each reporting date.

Derecognition: A financial liability is derecognized when it is extinguished, i.e., when the obligation
specified in the contract is discharged, cancelled, or expired.

Non-financial liabilities typically refer to obligations that are not financial instruments and are not
measured at fair value through profit or loss. While IND AS does not explicitly define "non-financial
liabilities" as a separate category, liabilities that do not meet the criteria of financial liabilities under
IND AS 32 may fall into the broader category of non-financial liabilities.

Non-financial liabilities can be thought of as liabilities representing obligations that do not involve
the transfer of financial assets but rather require some form of non-monetary settlement or
performance. Provisions, Lease Liabilities, Employee Benefits, Warranties and Product Liabilities,
Deferred Revenue, Contingent Liabilities are some examples of Non- Financial Liabilities.

5.1  Let us elaborate further on Provision for Doubtful Debt and why it is
    a Non-Financial Liability.

Let us first understand the nature of such provisions. The provision for doubtful debts is created by
businesses to account for the expected loss due to the non-payment of trade receivables.  It
represents a liability that arises from a past event (sale of goods or services on credit) and gives
rise to a present obligation (the expectation that some customers may not pay the full amount). The
creation of a provision for doubtful debts  is often a result of contractual obligations or legal
requirements. For example, accounting standards (such as IND AS 109 and IND AS 37) require
entities to recognize expected credit losses and create provisions for doubtful debts.

Since the contractual obligation that is created does not  fit into the scope of the definition of a
Financial Liability, as there is no payment to be made from the entity’s side and no exchange of
financial assets or liabilities, it should be thought of as a Non-Financial Liability.

5.2  Similarly, let us look further in Warranties and their treatment as Non-
     Financial Liabilities

In accounting and financial reporting, the terms "warranty in the nature of guarantee" and "warranty
in the nature of assurance" refer to different types of warranty obligations, and their treatment is
guided by the principles outlined in the Indian Accounting Standards (IND AS).

Warranty Guarantee: A warranty in guarantee implies that the warranty provided by the entity is
essentially a form of insurance or guarantee against specified risks or events. In this case, the entity
may be obligated to compensate the customer or fulfil the warranty commitment in the event of
certain predefined circumstances, even if those circumstances are beyond the entity's control.

Warranty Assurance: A warranty in assurance, on the other hand, implies that the warranty is more
of a commitment to stand behind the quality and performance of the product or service. It does not
necessarily involve a financial guarantee against specific risks but rather assures the customer that
the product or service will meet certain specified standards or expectations.

If a warranty is a guarantee, the entity might recognize a provision for the expected costs
associated with  fulfilling the warranty  obligation. The recognition and measurement  of such
provisions are guided by IND AS 37 - "Provisions, Contingent Liabilities, and Contingent Assets."
The provision is recognized when there is a present obligation because of a past event (sale of
goods or services) and it is probable that an outflow of resources embodying economic benefits will
be required to settle the obligation. If the entity provides a guarantee that involves a financial liability,

Warranties meet the criteria of non-financial liabilities as they arise from the sale of goods or services

goods, services, or repairs, and they do not represent a direct contractual obligation to deliver cash
or financial instruments. The primary focus is on meeting the performance obligation associated with
the warranty terms, which is why warranties are generally considered non-financial liabilities.

Valuing warranty provisions can be approached through various methods, each with its own set of
assumptions and data requirements. Three common methods are the Historical Warranty Claim Rate
Method, Predictive Modelling Method, and Adjusted Historical Method.

1.     Historical Warranty Claim Rate Method

       This method relies on historical data to estimate future warranty costs. It's straightforward but
     assumes that past performance is a reliable indicator of future results. Firstly you gather
        historical data on past sales and the corresponding warranty claims, including costs
      associated with parts, labour, and overhead. Then Claim Rate  is to be calculated by
      determining the warranty claim rate by dividing the total number of warranty claims by the
        total number of units sold in the same period. Consequently, by dividing the total warranty
       costs by the total number of claims, the Average Cost per warranty claim is calculated. Finally,
       the Future Warranty Costs are estimated by multiplying the historical claim rate and the
      average cost per claim by the number of units sold in the current period. This provides an
      estimate of the total expected warranty costs for the current sales.

      Example:

     A company sells 10,000 units of a product in a year. From historical data, it knows that 1% of
         all units sold typically result in a warranty claim, with an average cost of $50 per claim.

2.     Predictive Modelling Method

       Predictive modelling involves using statistical techniques to forecast future warranty claims
      based on a broader set of variables, potentially offering a more accurate prediction than
      simple historical rates.

    We begin by choosing qualitative variables such as product features, manufacturing process
      changes, and external factors (e.g., usage conditions) that are likely to influence warranty
       claims. Then we collect historical data for both warranty claims and the selected variables.
     Then by using statistical software to develop a model that predicts warranty claims based on
       the variables. Common approaches include regression analysis, machine learning models,
      and time series analysis. After testing and fine tuning the model, we apply the model to current
      data on the variables to estimate future warranty claims and associated costs.

3.    Adjusted Historical Method

     The Adjusted Historical Method modifies historical data to account for known changes or
       trends that might affect future warranty claims, making  it more nuanced than the simple
        historical method.

       Similar to the Historical Warranty Claim Rate Method, we start by analysing past sales and
      warranty claim data. Further, we look for factors that could influence future warranty costs
        differently than in the past. This could include changes in product design, manufacturing
      processes, or quality control measures. Then we modify the historical claim rates or costs
      based on the identified changes. For example, if a new quality control process is expected to
      reduce warranty claims by 10%, adjust the historical claim rate accordingly. Finally, we apply
       the adjusted claim rates or costs to the current period's sales to estimate the warranty
       provision.

Selecting a Method

The choice among these methods depends on the availability and quality of data, the complexity of
the products, and the company's capacity to perform sophisticated analyses. The Historical Warranty
Claim Rate Method is the simplest and may be sufficient for products with stable quality and usage
patterns. Predictive Modelling is more complex but can provide more accurate estimates for products
with variable or changing factors. The Adjusted Historical Method offers a middle ground, allowing
for adjustments to historical data without the need for complex statistical models.

IND AS 37 does not prescribe a specific method to calculate warranty provisions, however, advises
to use sufficient judgement and assumptions for calculation.
