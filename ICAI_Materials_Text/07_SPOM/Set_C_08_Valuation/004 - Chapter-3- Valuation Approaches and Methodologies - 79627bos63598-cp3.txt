3.3      VALUATION APPROACHES AND METHODOLOGIES      1.3


     1.  INTRODUCTION

Valuation, in the realm of finance and accounting, serves as a cornerstone for decision-making
processes across various sectors and industries. It entails the estimation of the worth or fair value
of assets,  liabilities, businesses, or securities, providing stakeholders with  critical insights for
investment, financial reporting, taxation, and other strategic endeavors (e.g., Buyback, M&A etc.).
Valuation approaches and methodologies encompass a diverse array of techniques, each tailored
to the specific context and purpose of the valuation exercise.

These approaches often blend elements of financial theory, market analysis, and quantitative
modeling to arrive at a credible and defensible valuation outcome. From traditional methods such
as the discounted cash flow (DCF) and comparable company analysis (CCA) to more specialized
techniques like option pricing models (Black Scholes Model) and real options analysis, practitioners
must navigate a nuanced landscape to accurately capture the intrinsic value of assets and entities.
Moreover, the evolving dynamics of global markets, regulatory frameworks, and technological
advancements continuously shape and refine valuation practices, underscoring the importance of a
robust understanding of valuation principles and methodologies for accounting professionals.
In this chapter, we explore the key valuation methods, their theory, practical uses, and how they impact
financial decisions in today's business world.

     2.  APPROACHES OF VALUATION
The following are the 3 widely accepted approaches:

i    Market based approaches

i    Income / Earning based approaches

i    Asset / Cost based approaches

Market Approach measures value based on what other purchasers in the market have paid for
assets that can be considered reasonably similar to those being valued.

Income / Earning Approach is based on the present value of future earnings from the asset.

 Observable Inputs are the Inputs that are developed using market data, such as publicly
 available information about actual events or transactions, and that reflect the assumptions that
 Market Participants would use when pricing the Asset or Liability (Para 6.25 of ICAI VS 101).

 Unobservable Inputs are those Inputs for which market data are not available and that are
 developed using the best information available about the assumptions that Market Participants
 would use when pricing the Asset or Liability (Para 6.42 of ICAI VS 101).

 Market Participants (Para 6.24 of ICAI VS 101) are willing buyers and willing sellers in the
 principal (or most advantageous) market for the Asset or Liability that have all the following
 characteristics:

 (a)    they are independent of each other, i.e., they are not related parties as defined under
        applicable accounting framework and set of reporting  / accounting standards therein,
        although the price in a related party transaction may be used as an input to a fair value
       measurement  if the entity has evidence that the transaction was entered into at market
        terms;

 (b)    they are knowledgeable, having a reasonable understanding about the Asset or Liability
       and the transaction using all available information, including information that might be
        obtained through due care that is usual and customary;

  (c)    they can enter a transaction for the Asset or Liability; and

 (d)    they are willing to enter a transaction for the Asset or Liability, i.e., they are motivated but
        not forced or otherwise compelled to do so.

Situations where Market Approach is relevant:

1.   When the Asset to be Valued is TRADED in the Active Market; or

2.   When there are RECENT, Orderly Transactions in the Asset to be Valued which are identical
           / comparable in nature; or

3.    INFORMATION is AVAILABLE and RELIABLE on the recent orderly transactions in identical

       Active Market is a market in which transactions for the Asset or Liability take place with
         sufficient frequency and volume to provide pricing information on an ongoing basis (Para
        6.1 of ICAI VS 101).

       For listed securities, Active Market would refer to one where activity  / transaction is
       ongoing in the Stock Exchanges (BSE, NSE, etc.,) and as defined in the guidelines issued
       by SEBI.

       Orderly Transaction is a transaction that assumes exposure to the market for a period
        before the Valuation Date to allow for marketing activities that are usual and customary for

       Asset would refer to the Assets that need to be Valued during an engagement and will
        also include a group of Assets, a Liability or a group of Liabilities, business, or business
       ownership interests. Any reference to the term Asset also includes Liability (Para 6.2 of
        ICAI VS 101).

Certain benefits of using Market Approach:

1.        It is easy to use, simple to comprehend, less time consuming and easily understood by users.

2.        It incorporates information from other Valuations in a simple way and provides consistency in
       the Valuation process by ensuring that Valuation is in line with other comparative Valuations.

3.        It relies upon market information and implicitly embodies current market consensus about
      assumptions such as the discount rate and growth rate. Hence, it reflects the current mood
       of the market.

Certain limitations of Market Approach:

1.     Relative Valuation is as good as the Valuation of the comparable companies and suffers from
         volatility of the market.

      For Example,  in case the market  is overvaluing comparable companies, the Relative
       Valuation will also lead to overstatement of Value.

       riskiness of business is a difficult task and finding a perfect match is extremely challenging.

       to many debates.

3.     Differences in accounting policies related to revenue recognition, depreciation, etc., though
       adjustable to a certain extent, can result in a distorted valuation.
2.1.1 Market Price Method

A Valuer considers the traded price observed over a reasonable period while Valuing Assets which
are traded in the Active Market under the market price method. A Valuer also considers the market
where the trading volume of asset is the highest when such asset is traded in more than one active
market. Further the Valuer should consider using weighted average or volume weighted average to
reduce the impact of volatility or any one-time event in the asset.

Example-1: The Equity Shares of Reliance Industries Limited (RIL) are listed on both National Stock
Exchange (‘NSE’) and Bombay Stock Exchange (‘BSE’) and shares of RIL are widely traded with
high-volume transactions, further RIL is a going concern entity.

Accordingly, the Market Price can be considered for determining its Value. The Valuer will have to
also consider whether to adopt the price as on a specific date or an average  / volume weighted
average over a period. The Valuer must also consider the length of such period based on facts and
circumstances for valuing a company like RIL.

Example-2: The Equity Shares of Sika Interplant Systems (SIS) is listed only on Bombay Stock
Exchange (‘BSE’) and not on National Stock Exchange (‘NSE’) and shares of SIS is not traded
frequently, further SIS is a going concern entity.

Accordingly, either the Market Price may not be considered for determining its Value or if used, be
given a lower weightage in overall evaluation.

2.1.2 Comparable Companies Multiple (CCM) Method

This is also an internationally accepted method under IVS 105 – Valuation Approaches and Methods.

CCM Method involves Valuing an Asset based on Market Multiples derived from prices of market
comparable companies which are traded on Active Market and is also known as Guideline Public
Company (GPC) Method,  internationally  this method  is known as Guideline Publicly Traded
Comparable (GPTC) Method.

A Comparable Company  / Assets selection  is based on various factors including the
following:

1.     Operational Processes;

2.    Cash Flows;

3.    Growth Potential; and

4.     Risks which are similar to the Company being Valued.

The inherent drawback of CCM method is that, finding a direct comparable company becomes a
very difficult exercise. Such direct comparable company can be traced only in very few industries.

The CCM method uses a contextual approach to derive the Value of a Business or Asset, as opposed
to Income Approach that Value a Company internally based on Cash Flow and other factors, without
any comparisons.

Important Steps in deriving a Value using the CCM:

1.      Identification of Market Comparable Companies;

2.     Selection and Computation of Market Multiples;

3.    Comparison of Assets with Comparable Company’s Assets to trace for material differences;

4.      Identification of adjustments of material differences to the Market Multiple;

5.     Application of adjusted Market Multiple to the relevant parameter of the Asset to be Valued;

6.    Computation of Value of the Asset using Market Multiples based on different metrics  /
      parameters.

The above steps are further explained in the forthcoming paragraphs:

1.     Identification and Selection of Market Comparable Companies:

       Traditionally, when looking at the companies within a specific industry / sector, provides a
      reasonable number  of comparable companies (example, Infosys  vs. TCS  vs. Wipro).

2.1.3 Comparable Transaction Multiple (CTM) Method

This is also an internationally accepted method under IVS 105 – Valuation Approaches and Methods.

CTM Method also known as the Guideline Transaction (GT) Method involves Valuing a business
based on Transaction Multiples derived from prices paid in transaction of Asset to be Valued / Market
Comparables (Comparable Transactions).

It is a variant of the CCM Method and uses Transaction Multiples instead of Trading Multiples. The
Transaction Multiples, as the name suggests, are the multiples implied in the recent acquisition /
disposals of comparable companies / business.

A Comparable Transaction / Business selection is based on various factors including the following:

1.     Comparability of the entity involved in the transaction

2.    Recency of the Transaction;

3.    Background of the Transaction; and

4.    Deal Structure of the Transaction.

The inherent drawback of CT method is that, finding a direct comparable transaction becomes a
very difficult exercise. Further, many a time, the details relating to actual transactions are not
available in the public domain when the company is in unlisted space. Such direct comparable
transactions can be traced only in very few industries.

Important Steps in deriving a Value using the CTM:

1.      Identification of Transaction Comparable;

2.     Selection and Computation of Transaction Multiples;

2.2  Income / Earning Based Approach

The Income / Earning based Approach of Valuations are based on the premise that converts the
maintainable future amounts (cash flows) into a single current amount (discounted / capitalized).

The Current Value of any business is a function of the Future Value that an investor can expect to
receive from purchasing all or part of the business. It is generally used for valuing businesses that
are expected to continue operating for the foreseeable future.

Instances when Income Approach can be used:

1.    Where the Asset does not have any Market Comparable or Comparable Transaction;

2.    Where the Asset has fewer relevant Market Comparables; or

3.    Where the Asset is an Income producing Asset for which the future cash flows are available

2.2.2 Discounted Cash Flow Method

This is an internationally accepted method under IVS 105 – Valuation Approaches and Methods.

The Discounted Cash Flow (“DCF”) method, an application of the Income Approach is arguably one
of the most recognized methods to determine the Value of a Business.

The DCF method indicates the Fair Value of a Business based on the value of cash flows that the
business is expected to generate in future. This method involves the estimation of post-tax cash
flows for the projected period, after considering the Business’s requirement of reinvestment in
terms of capital expenditure and incremental working capital. These cash flows are then discounted
at a cost of capital that reflects the risks of the business and the capital structure of the entity.

While DCF method is a reliable and acceptable means to Valuation but appropriate care has to be
taken to ensure that key assumptions are vetted and common pitfalls/mistakes are avoided.

1.     Merits of DCF Method:

(a)    Theoretically, it is a very sound model because it is based upon expected future cash flows
       that will determine an investor’s actual return;

(b)       It is based on expectations of performance specific to the business, and is not influenced by
       short-term market conditions or non-economic indicators;


© The Institute of Chartered Accountants of India


====================== PAGE 22 ======================


1.223.22           VALUATION


(c)       It is not as vulnerable to accounting conventions like depreciation, inventory valuation in
      comparison with the other techniques  / approaches since  it is based on cash flows rather
      than accounting profits;

(d)       It is appropriate for valuing start-up projects, as these projects have little or no asset base or
      earnings which render the Cost Approach (net asset value) or the Market Approach
       (application of market multiples) inappropriate. However, it is important that the Valuer must
      recognize the additional risks in such a case (e.g. project execution risk, lack of past track
       record, etc.) by using an appropriate discount rate.

2.    Drawbacks of DCF Method:

(a)       It is only as good as its input assumptions. Following the “garbage in, garbage out” principle,
            if the inputs  - Cash Flow Projections, Discount Rate, and Terminal Value  - are not
       appropriately selected, then the value generated by using DCF Method will not reflect the
       Fair Value.

(b)   DCF Method does not consider several other factors, such as investment risk associated with
       opportunity cost, i.e., investments that could return greater cash flow yields would add an
       unrealized element of risk, unforeseen variations in future cash flow, and other non-financial
       factors.

3.     Variants of DCF Method:There are two widely used variants of the DCF Methodology, while
one determines the Equity Value the other helps in determining the Enterprise Value.

The key difference between the two is that Equity Value is the Total Value of all outstanding shares
of the company whereas Enterprise Value is the Total Worth of a company without factoring in the
financial structuring involved.

Equity Value  is calculated by multiplying price of a single share with the number of shares
outstanding whereas Enterprise Value is calculated inclusive of the Market Value of Equity and
Market Value of Net Debt.

4.    Free Cash Flow to Equity (“FCFE”): This method estimates the Equity Value which is the
Cash distributable among the Equity Shareholders after  all expenses, reinvestments and debt
obligations have been met by the company. The Equity Value is the sum of Present Value of all Free
Cash Flows, discounted using the Cost of Equity (“COE” / Ke).

The formula for estimating FCFE:

 FCFE = Net Income or Profit After Tax – Dividend on Preference Shares + Depreciation & Amortization
 – Capital Expenditure – Increase in Non-Cash Working Capital + Increase in Long-Term Debt or Short-
 Term Debt or Preference Shares – Decrease in Long-Term Debt or Short-Term Debt or Preference
 Shares.

5.    Free Cash Flow to the Firm (“FCFF”):This method estimates the Enterprise Value (“EV”)
which is the Net Amount of Cash available for distribution among Equity Shareholders, Preference
Shareholders and Debt Holders, after all Expenses and Re-investment requirements have been met.
EV is the sum of Present Value of all Free Cash Flows, discounted using the Weighted Average
Cost of Capital (“WACC”).

The Equity Value can be ascertained by adjusting the non-equity claims such as External Debt,
Preference Shares, Minority Interest and Cash & Cash Equivalents from the EV.

The formula for estimating FCFF:

 FCFF = Earnings Before Interest and Tax * (1–tax rate) + Depreciation – Capital Expenditure –
 Increase in Non-Cash Working Capital.

Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)  is used to Discount the Future Cash Flows. An
adjustment with respect to the Premium / Discount may have to be factored to both the COE and
WACC to arrive at the Discount Rate based on the factors such as liquidity, control, size, specific

The Cost of Equity (“COE”) reflects the return expected by the equity shareholders, to compensate
for the risk assumed through their investment in the business.

The Cost of Debt (“COD”) is based on the current or expected borrowing rate for the company, which
may be provided by the management of the company and is generally assumed to be the market
rate.

The Weighted Average Cost of Capital (“WACC”) is based on the proportionate weights of each
component of the source of capital, i.e., weighted average of COE and COD wherein the ratio of
Equity and Debt on total capital is proportion to their respective weights.

WACC constitutes all capital sources:

(a)    Equity Shares

(b)    Preference Shares

(c)    Long-term Debts

(d)    Short-term Debts.

While calculating WACC, the Market Value Weights of the above sources are preferred over Book
Value Weights.  If the proportion of these sources is expected to change during explicit forecast
period, then  it is appropriate to calculate WACC separately for each year in the explicit forecast
period. But, if there are no significant changes expected, then a single WACC may be used for the
entire explicit forecast period.

It may also be appropriate in certain cases to consider the Weights based on industry average  /
norms instead of using the Actual Weights as applicable to the business case.

Further, though Market Weights are preferred, it is not uncommon in India to use Book Weights for
WACC computation.

8      (c)    Terminal Value: The Value of a Business should account for the Cash Flows over the
entire life of a company / entity, which can be assumed to be infinite, but in explicit forecast period,
the Cash Flows are only estimated over a certain period (generally in the range of 3-5 years) hence
a Terminal Value is computed to reflect the Value of the Cash Flows arising after the explicit forecast
period till infinity.

Terminal Value represents the Present Value at the end of explicit forecast period of all subsequent
Cash Flows to the end of the life of the Asset or into perpetuity (if the asset has an indefinite life).

3 methods of calculating Terminal Value, as follows:

Method-1: Gordon (Constant) Growth Model

Method-2: Exit Multiple

Method-3: Salvage or Liquidation Value

Method-1: Gordon (Constant) Growth Model:

Gordon Growth Model or Perpetual Growth Model assumes that a Business has an infinite life and
a stable growth rate of cash flows. Terminal Value  is derived mathematically by dividing the
perpetuity cash flows (cash flows which are expected to grow at a stable pace) with the discount
rate as reduced by the stable growth rate.

 Terminal Value(n) = Expected FCF (n+1) / (Discount Rate – Expected Growth Rate)

Estimation of the stable growth rate is of great significance because even a minor change in the
stable growth rate assumption can have a compounding impact on the Terminal Value and hence
the overall Business Value. Various factors like the size of a company, existing growth rate,
competitive landscape, profit reinvestment ratio, etc. have to be kept in mind while estimating the
stable growth rate.


© The Institute of Chartered Accountants of India


====================== PAGE 36 ======================


1.363.36           VALUATION


If the expected growth rate tends towards the Discount Rate, Terminal Value will approach infinity
and then turn negative, if expected growth rate exceeds the discount rate. The expected growth
rate should be constrained (capped) to be less than or equal to the growth rate of the economy
in which the business operates. If the growth rate of the company is more than the growth rate of
the economy for infinite period (perpetuity), the business will eventually become larger than the
economy (which has not been seen in real life, practically speaking). This method is most widely
used among the 3 commonly used methods.

Illustration on computation of Terminal Value using Gordon Growth Model:

PQR Ltd, a private limited company, wanted to know the present value of its Terminal Value of their
business based on the Cash Flows given below. It has a Ke of 10.95% and perpetuity growth rate of
3%.

(iv)   What is the current market share and expected market share?

Method-2: Exit Multiple:

The estimation of Terminal Value under the Exit Multiple method involves application of Market
Multiples (EV/EBITDA, EV/Sales, etc.) to the perpetuity earnings / income. This method undercuts
the notion of Intrinsic Value, which is what DCF method is designed to measure. The multiple that
is used to estimate the Terminal Value comes from looking at what peer group companies are trading
in the market. Although this approach is comparatively simple but since the Terminal Value multiple
has a huge impact on Valuation it is extremely critical how it is being obtained.

Illustration on computation of Terminal Value using Exit Multiple:

MNO Ltd has a sales of INR 10 crores per annum and is expected to have a Terminal Value of INR
25 crores at the end of 7 years.

The Terminal Value to Sales Multiple is 2.5x (INR 25 crores ÷ INR 10 crores)

For instance, IJK Ltd is operating in the same industry having a revenue of INR 20 crores will be
estimated to have a Terminal Value of INR 50 crores (INR 20 crores x 2.5) at the end of 7 years.

Hence under the Exit Multiple of computing Terminal Value, we are ultimately using the “Market
Approach” to compute the single largest component of Cash Flow in the DCF Method, which is under
the “Income Approach”. Hence the end result is a mix of both approaches.

Method-3: Salvage or Liquidation Value:

The Salvage Value / Liquidation Value method is used in case we see the relevant Company / Entity
/ Firm will no longer operate as a going concern and might cease operations and go into liquidation.
The Terminal Value is calculated by determining the Salvage or Realizable Value of all the Assets
less costs to be incurred for disposing such an Asset.

Terminal Value is estimated to be the Liquidation Value, which is based on the Book Value of the
Assets typically adjusted for inflation, but this does not reflect the earning power of the Assets.
Alternatively, discounting the expected Cash Flows from sale of such Assets using an appropriate
Discount Rate would provide a better estimation of Liquidation Value.

However, Salvage Value / Liquidation Value is usually lower than the Book Value and Market Value.
Using this approach to estimate the Terminal Value could give a result lower than the real Value of
the Business after the explicit forecast period.

Certain Key Assumptions on Terminal Value:

(a)    Terminal Value often constitutes around 50% or more of the entire Business Value derived
      using the DCF Method and is usually computed based on the “Gordon Growth Method” which
      takes into consideration the Cash Flows of the last year of the explicit forecast period and
      grows them by a Long-term Growth Rate. As such an unrealistic assumption associated with
       the terminal value can profoundly skew the Resulting Present Value.

(b)    Increasing the Long-term Growth Rate creates a significant impact on Terminal Value. If there
        is an assumption of a Growth Rate that is higher than the prevalent Long-term inflationary
       estimates,  it implies that the business will continue to grow ad-infinitum  (till infinity), which
      has proven to be almost impossible for any company / entity / firm.

(c)    Other issues associated with the Terminal Value may include the following:

          (i)    Inadequate Long-term maintenance of Capital Expenditure;

           (ii)    Inadequate Working Capital Assumptions relative to the Terminal Growth Rate;

           (iii)   Large discrepancy between Depreciation and Capital Expenditure levels;

         (iv)   Lower effective tax rate (due to prior year tax losses, limited period for tax exemption,
                etc.,) which will typically be applicable for the initial few years post the discrete period.
2.2.3 Discounted future earnings method (earning measure non DCF based valuation)

The Discounted Future Earnings Method (DFE) is a valuation technique used to estimate the present
value of a company's future earnings. This method involves projecting the company's future earnings
over a specific period and then discounting those earnings back to their present value using a
discount rate.

To apply the Discounted Future Earnings Method, following aspects are required:

Earnings Projection: Future earnings of the company are forecasted based on historical financial
data, industry trends, and future expectations. These earnings projections typically cover a specific
period, such as five or ten years.

Discount Rate Selection: A discount rate is chosen based on factors like the company's risk profile,
prevailing interest rates, market conditions, and industry benchmarks. The discount rate represents
the investor's required rate of return or the opportunity cost of investing in the company.


© The Institute of Chartered Accountants of India


====================== PAGE 39 ======================


3.39      VALUATION APPROACHES AND METHODOLOGIES      1.39


Discounting Future Earnings: The projected future earnings are then discounted back to their
present value using the chosen discount rate. This involves dividing each future earnings figure by
the appropriate discount factor corresponding to the time period in which  it is expected to be
received.

Summation: Finally, the present values of all projected future earnings are summed to arrive at the
total present value of the company's future earnings.

The Discounted Future Earnings Method provides a comprehensive way to estimate the intrinsic
value of a company based on its expected future cash flows. However, it requires accurate earnings
forecasts and careful consideration of the discount rate to ensure the reliability of the valuation.
Additionally, like any valuation method, the Discounted Future Earnings Method has its limitations
and should be used in conjunction with other valuation techniques for a more robust analysis.

  Utility means the quality of being useful.

 Replica means a very good or exact copy of something.

Note the difference between the words “Comparable Utility” and “Replica”.

This implies that, under the Replacement Cost method, only the Quality of Asset (even if it is from a
second-hand asset) shall be ensured to be replaced, whereas under the Reproduction Cost method,
the Asset shall be of an Exact Copy (it must be an appropriate duplicate).


Major steps in deriving a value using the Replacement Cost method  / Reproduction Cost
Method:

1.     Estimating the costs that will be incurred by a Market Participant for creating an Asset with
      Comparable Utility / to Replicate an existing Asset.

2.    Assessing whether there  is any loss on account  of physical,  functional, or economic
      obsolescence in the Asset to be Valued; and

3.     Adjusting for the Obsolescence Value,  if any as determined above from the total costs
      estimated above, to arrive at the Value of the Asset.

 Meaning of Obsolescence

 The Physical Properties of the new Asset may or may not be similar to the one under Valuation,
 but the Asset should bear Comparable Utility.

 Obsolescence includes

 (a)    Physical Deterioration;

 (b)    Functional (technological) Obsolescence; and

  (c)   Economic (external) Obsolescence.

 The term Obsolescence connotes a wider meaning than the term depreciation adopted for
 financial reporting or tax purposes.

  I.     Physical Obsolescence represents the loss in Value on account of decreased usefulness
         of the asset as the useful life expires.

  II.    Functional (technological) Obsolescence represents the loss in Value on account of

2.3.3 Valuation of Assets

The Valuation of Assets can be carried out using the following approaches, i.e.,

1.    Book Value: When the Valuation of Assets is done based on their Book Value, certain
      adjustments shall be made.  It should be recognized that the Book Value reflects only the
        historical cost of assets, which in the case of old entities, may be much lower than their
       current Economic Value. Immoveable properties, Investments etc. are generally recognized
       at their Market Value even where other Assets are considered at their Book Value.

2.    Net Replacement Value:  If an entity is being Valued on a going concern basis, the Net
      Replacement Value of the Assets gives a fair measure of their Current Value. However, in
        this case too Intangible Assets may need to be Valued and added to recognize the fact that
       individual Net Replacement Value of the Assets does not represent the inherent strength of
       the entity.

3.    Net Realizable Value: Where an entity is being wound up, its Assets are best Valued based
      on their Net Realizable Value. In such cases, the total Net Realizable Value (after reducing
       notional tax applicable on realization) will often be less than that based on a going concern.

     3.  FAIR VALUE AND ITS COMPUTATION

Fair Value is the price that would be received to sell an Asset or paid to transfer a Liability in an
orderly transaction between market participants at the Valuation Date.

This definition as per ICAI Valuation Standards is akin to the one given under Ind AS 113 on Fair
Value Measurement too.

Under the Income Approach, the Fair Value measurement is determined based on the Value, as
indicated by the current market expectations about those future amounts.

In the context of ICAI Valuation Standards, Fair Value is the Price in an orderly transaction in the
Principal Market (Most Advantageous) at the Valuation Date under the Current Market Conditions
(i.e., Exit Price) regardless of whether that Price is directly observable or estimated using the other
Valuation  technique.  Fair Value  is  also synonymous  with Market  Value, except  in  certain
circumstances, where the characteristic of an Asset translates into a special Asset Value for the
parties involved.

The purpose of a potential acquirer of the controlling stake would be to purchase the stake at a “Fair
Price, no less, no more”.

A Chartered Accountant’s perspective to “Fair Value” would automatically envisage a transaction to
be measured at the arm’s length (this purely emanates from the definition given in the Indian
Accounting Standards)

From the perspective of a Financial Analyst, this would mean the Present Value of an Entity in Cash
Terms.

       build a forward-looking multiple, one can choose a forecast year for EBITDA / NOPAT that

       considered.
