     1.  VALUATION DOCUMENTATION

1.1  Introduction to Documentation

ICAI Valuation Standard 202- Reporting and Documentation deals with valuer’s responsibility for
documenting the work. As per the standard “Documentation includes the record of valuation
procedures performed, relevant evidence obtained and conclusions that the valuer has reached”.
The standard does not limit the documentation requirements mentioned in laws, regulations or as
mentioned in other standards as applicable.

1.4  Rights and Time Period for Preservation of Documentation by Valuer

As per the Companies (Registered Valuers and Valuation) Rules, 2017, it is provided that a Valuer
shall maintain proper working papers for a period of 3 years or such longer period as required in its
contract for a specific valuation. Valuation documentation is property of the valuer unless specified
by a law or regulation. The Valuer shall retain such documents for at least 8 years as per the ICAI
Valuation Standards from the date of valuation report. The valuer may retain such documents in
physical or electronic form.

1.5  Documentation Checklist for Valuer

i   A valuer is required to gather enough and relevant data when drawing judgments that serve
      as the foundation for his valuation report.

i    Recognize the ideas, nature, and goals of valuation documentation.

i   A valuer must promptly document the valuation evidence they have obtained.

i     Professional judgment is required to determine the extent of documentation, which must be
      prepared at the time of the valuation assignment.

i   A valuer is responsible for making sure that the documentation is kept up to date in a way
       that allows a reviewer appointed by a  relevant  professional  organization  or another
       professional with no affiliation to the engagement to examine the valuation process and
       findings.

i     Policies and processes that ensure confidentiality, safe custody, integrity, accessibility, and
        retrievability must be established by a valuer or their organization.

2.1  Management Representation Letters as Evidence

i    To complete the valuation assignment, a valuer may request written representations from the
        client or management. The valuer has the last say over whether to request a representation
        letter.

i   A written statement from the management or those in charge of governance is included in the
       valuer's evidence collection, which serves as the foundation for his valuation report.

i   A valuer must acknowledge the existence of any written representations from management
       or clients regarding the data that forms the basis of the valuation assignment, as well as the
       reliance that was put on them.

       the valuation even in the presence of a management representation letter. When doing his
       valuation assignment, the valuer must follow the necessary processes regarding the data
      contained in the management representation letter.

     3.  VALUATION REPORT

3.1  Introduction

A valuation report is a written document that provides the client with the subject interest's computed
value or conclusion of value. It ought to provide the valuer's in-depth evaluation of the business. A
valuation report is a document created by a registered valuer with the intention of determining the
fair market value of the asset being valued on the reporting date. The scope and predetermined
purpose of the report are mutually agreed upon by the registered valuer and the intended users.

3.2  Objective

Presenting the results of a thorough appraisal and identifying a user-specific value for one or more
things is the aim of a valuation report.

3.3  Characteristics of Valuation Report

i    Understandability – An essential quality of the underlying information provided in the valuation
       report is that it must be readily understandable by the intended users. However, just because
      a complicated topic can be too difficult for some users to understand does not mean that it
      should be deleted from the valuation report simply because it is relevant to the users' needs
        for economic decision-making.

i    Relevance – To be useful, the underlying information in a valuation report must be relevant
       to the decision-making needs of the intended users. Information provided in the valuation
       report has the quality of relevance when  it influences the economic and other decisions of
       users.

i     Materiality – Materiality depends on the size of the item or error judged in the circumstances
       of its omission or misstatement. Therefore, materiality is not a core qualitative property that
       information must possess to be helpful; rather, it serves as a threshold or cut-off point.

i     Reliability – A valuation report's underlying data must be trustworthy for  it to be helpful.
     When information is free of bias and significant inaccuracies and can be trusted by users to
       accurately represent what  it either claims to represent or could reasonably be expected to
       represent, it is said to have the quality of dependability.

i     Faithful  representation  –It has  three  characteristics, namely,  error  free,  neutrality and
       completeness. There is occasionally a chance that the data in the valuation report may not
       accurately reflect what it is meant to represent. This isn't biased; rather, it could be the result
       of intrinsic challenges in determining the best approach, method, or strategies to use in
       valuing.

i    Substance over form –If the underlying information of valuation report represents faithfully
       the value, that  it purports to represent,  it is necessary that they are evaluated in valuation
      assignment in accordance with their substance and economic reality and not merely by their
       legal form. The substance of transactions such as acquisition or disposal of assets is not
       necessarily consistent with that which is apparent from their legal or contrived form.

i     Neutrality –To be reliable, the information contained in the valuation report must be neutral,
       that is, free from bias. The valuation reports are not considered neutral if, by the selection or

3.5  Constraints on relevant and reliable information in valuation report

i    Cost-benefit balance - Rather than being a qualitative trait, the cost-benefit balance is a
       ubiquitous limitation. The information in a value report should be more beneficial than it costs
       to provide. Nonetheless, the process of weighing costs and rewards is a judgemental process.

i    Equilibrium between qualitative attributes - In real-world situations, it's frequently required
       to strike a balance, or make a trade-off, between qualitative attributes. Typically, the goal is
       to attain a suitable equilibrium between the attributes to fulfil the purpose of the valuation
       report. It takes expertise to determine which traits are more important in which situations.

3.7   Illustration of a Valuation Report

i    Terms of Reference - Details of Appointment of Registered Valuer along with appointment
        is done by whom is mentioned in this section. A brief description of the nature of the
      appointment is also mentioned.

i    Background  - The aim  is to provide the unfamiliar and familiar reader alike, with the
       particulars of the company, business, assets/liabilities that form the subject of the valuation
       with as much context as required to gain an accurate understanding of the assignment.
      Transaction  details, summary  of  historical  financials,  capital structure,  identification  of
       pertinent facts such as related party issues, changes in shareholding pattern, impact of
       restructuring or proposed transaction etc. typically form part of this section.

i    Purpose of Valuation and Appointing Authority - The clear statement of the valuation's
       context, purpose, and commissioning authority is necessary. For example, the disclosure of
       the Management's decision to seek an advisory opinion or the Audit Committee's appointment
       of an independent valuer should be made. Valuers must also include the "premise of value"
        in this portion of the report.

i     Identity of the valuer and any other experts involved - The identity of the valuer (together
       with his registration number), the company doing the valuation, and any other team members
       or outside experts consulted during the valuation process ought to be disclosed.

i    Disclosure of Valuer Interest/Conflict,  if any - Since this part of the report is crucial to
       maintaining transparency, valuers should be sure to disclose any pertinent information that
      could create a real or perceived conflict of interest when conducting the analysis. Therefore,
       the valuer in his or her report should reveal any potential conflicts of interest and material
       interests, such as  affiliation or proposed  affiliation with the business,  its partners, the
       counterparty to the transaction, or its partners, in the capacity of lead advisor, auditor, or in
      any other role, as well as the type of fee arrangements for the valuation services performed.

i    Date of appointment, valuation date and date of the report - Assignments for valuation
      are completed as of a specific date. It is crucial to specify the Valuation Date, or the date the
       valuation analysis was completed, in this way. Additionally, it is important to indicate the date
       of the valuer's appointment and the report's publication. Furthermore, the Report should also
      mention the timeline associated with the validity of the report and/or within which the user
      must accept or reject the report.

i    Sources of information - Principal sources of information that form the basis of the valuation
       report should be clearly listed. This typically includes information such as company financials

i    Conclusion The report must contain a clear statement of the value ascribed, including the
       Valuation Date, the Premise of Value, and the valuation methods relied upon, and appropriate
      weightage placed on each.

i    Caveats, Limitations and Disclaimers These should be clearly stated, often in a separate
       section of the report and serve to inform the reader of the caveats associated with the valuation
      and help them assess the impact of these clauses on the credibility and reliability of the report.
      For example, in the preparation of a valuation report, the valuer should not disclaim liability
        for their expertise or duty of care. However, an independent valuer will prepare the report
      based on information and records provided by management. The independent valuer can
       disclaim the  reliability of management provided projections and may disagree with the
       projections  if they are conjectural or fantastic or bordering on the unreal. An independent
       valuer has the right to demand relevant information and the basis of the projections before
      commenting thereon. The components highlighted in this article are not exhaustive and the valuer
         will continually need to apply care and discretion to ensure that a valuation report  is
      comprehensive and defensible if subjected to scrutiny.

     4.  PRACTICAL  CASE   STUDIES  ON  VALUATION
      DOCUMENTATION
Case-1 Technology Company Valuation- Incorporating Market Volatility

Background- Mango Limited, a dynamic technology firm, sought a valuation amid a period of
heightened market volatility. The company's leadership aimed to gain insights into its current worth,
considering the rapidly changing landscape of the technology sector.

Challenges- The primary challenge was capturing the impact of market  volatility  on Mango
Limited's valuation. The technology sector, known for  its swift changes, demanded a valuation
approach that could adapt to dynamic market conditions.

Approach-Valuation team, cognizant of the challenges, adopted a meticulous approach to Valuation
Documentation.  It began by thoroughly documenting  all assumptions, methodologies, and data
sources employed throughout the valuation process. Recognizing the importance of addressing market
volatility, they incorporated such analysis into their methodology. This allowed them to model
different market scenarios and assess the potential impact on Mango Limited's valuation.

Outcome- The resulting Valuation Report for Mango Limited not only presented a precise valuation
figure but also served as a comprehensive document that stakeholders could scrutinize. The
document outlined the intricacies of the valuation process, detailing the rationale behind chosen
assumptions and methodologies. The scenario analysis provided valuable insights into how the
company's valuation would fluctuate under varying market conditions. This transparent and well-
documented approach  instilled confidence  in stakeholders, showcasing the adaptability of the
valuation model to the dynamic technology market.

Case - 2 Real Estate Valuation: Economic Downturn and Documentation
Strategies

Background- ABC Properties, a real estate company, faced the challenge of valuing its diverse
portfolio amid an economic downturn. The company aimed to obtain an accurate estimate of its real
estate assets' worthwhile navigating the uncertainties of the economic climate.

Challenges- The primary challenge involved addressing economic uncertainties and accurately
reflecting them in the Valuation Report. With real estate values susceptible to economic downturns,
it was crucial to adopt a documentation strategy that transparently communicated the impact of
economic challenges on the valuation.

Approach- The valuation team prioritized robust Documentation Strategies. Each step of the
valuation process, from data collection to the application of valuation methods, was meticulously

assess how the valuation model would perform under various economic scenarios. The Valuation

process and considerations.

Outcome- The resulting Valuation Report for ABC Properties not only provided a fair estimate of the

could scrutinize the document to understand how economic challenges were factored into the

Case - 3 Pharmaceutical Company Valuation: Addressing Legal Risks in Documentation

Background- RD Pharmaceuticals, a leading pharmaceutical company, faced the challenge of
valuing its business while dealing with pending legal cases that could impact future cash flows. The
company sought a valuation that would transparently communicate the potential risks associated
with the legal issues.

Challenges- The key challenge was addressing legal risks and effectively incorporating them into
the Valuation Report. Quantifying  potential  legal costs and ensuring  that stakeholders  fully
understood the impact of legal uncertainties were critical aspects of the valuation process.

Approach- The valuation team adopted a meticulous approach  to Valuation Documentation,
collaborating closely with  legal experts. Every step  of the valuation process involving  legal
considerations was documented. The team quantified potential legal costs, and the Valuation Report
became a comprehensive document that transparently communicated the impact of legal risks on
RD Pharmaceuticals' valuation.

Outcome- The resulting Valuation Report for RD Pharmaceuticals not only delivered a robust
valuation but also became a key document for risk management. Stakeholders could access a
detailed account of the legal due diligence conducted, the quantification of potential legal costs, and
how these factors influenced the company's overall valuation. This approach not only satisfied the
immediate valuation requirements  of RD Pharmaceuticals but also provided a foundation for
strategic decision-making considering potential legal challenges.
