2.3              NEED AND PURPOSE OF VALUATION


Valuation is the process of determining the intrinsic value of a company, investment, or asset, as
opposed to its cost or current market value. Intrinsic worth pertains to the perceived value of a
security based on future earnings or other non-market-related attributes. This process serves as a
pivotal tool in determining the fair value of a security, defined by the price a buyer is willing to pay
and a seller is willing to accept in a transaction provided both parties engage voluntarily.

A valuation  is undertaken  for a spectrum  of purposes, including valuation  of any business,
acquisition, merger, and de-merger,  for liquidation, start-up valuation, distress firm valuation,
intangible asset valuation, among others. Conducting a valuation requires a comprehensive grasp
of diverse factors influencing value, informed professional judgment, and a methodical approach.
The need for valuation depends upon prevailing market conditions, the objectives of the involved
parties, and the circumstances underlying the valuation endeavour.

     1.  PURPOSE OF VALUATION

As mentioned above, the purpose of valuation  is multifaceted, serving various objectives and
stakeholders in financial decision-making processes. Primarily, valuation enables individuals and
entities to determine the  fair and accurate worth of a company, investment, or asset. This
determination is crucial for:

(i)    Investment Decisions: Valuation  assists investors  in assessing whether a  particular
      investment opportunity aligns with their financial objectives by evaluating its potential return
        relative to its perceived risk.

(ii)    Financial Reporting:   Valuation  is essential for financial reporting purposes, such as
      determining the value of assets and liabilities for balance sheets, assessing goodwill in
      mergers and  acquisitions, and  complying  with  accounting  standards and  regulatory
       requirements.

(iii)   Mergers and Acquisitions (M&A): In M&A transactions, valuation helps in establishing the
       appropriate price to be paid for acquiring a business or merging with another entity, ensuring
       that the transaction is beneficial to all parties involved.

(iv)   Corporate Finance: Valuation supports corporate finance activities such as raising capital,
       issuing stocks or bonds, and making strategic decisions regarding capital allocation and
      investment opportunities.

     2.  TRANSACTIONS BASED VALUATION

Transaction analysis utilizes publicly available data to derive a reasoned estimate of multiples or
premiums paid for publicly traded companies. It scrutinizes the investor profile of similar acquisitions
in the past and assesses the likelihood of future acquisitions by the acquiring companies.

One of the most important components of transaction analysis is identifying the transactions that are
the most  relevant. A  pivotal  aspect  of  transaction  analysis  involves  determining  relevant
transactions. Firstly, companies should be chosen based on having similar financial characteristics
and for being in the same industry. Then the size of the transactions should be similar in size to the
transaction that is being considered for the target company. Consequently, the type of transaction
and the characteristics of the buyer should be similar. Transactions that occurred more recently are
considered more valuable in terms of usefulness for analysis.

Example: Company ADP Limited, a conglomerate  is looking  to  fully acquire a Media and
Entertainment company, JPA Pvt. Limited, to increase its market capitalization and become M&E
segment leader. Upon conducting market research, various similar transactions were found.

To be able to curate a list of the most appropriate comparable transactions, the valuer reviewed
transactions and filtered them using various qualitative and quantitative criteria such as Acquisition
of Majority Stake, Media and Entertainment Industry, Date of Announcement to be able to evaluate
recent most transactions, Transaction Value etc.

scenario. For instance,  shifts  in competitor landscape or business cycle phases may pose
challenges.

Although each transaction is unique, transaction analysis aids in offering a broad evaluation of
market demand  for a  specific asset and an approximate valuation  thereof. However, such

     3.  REGULATORY & TAX BASED VALUATION

(a)   Companies Act, 2013: Companies Act, 2013 states where a valuation is required to be made
in respect of any property, stocks, shares, debentures, securities or goodwill or any other assets or
net worth of a company or its liabilities, it shall be valued by a registered valuer. Thus, all valuations
required under the Companies Act, 2013 is to be undertaken by a registered valuer.

The Act also states that the valuation shall be done in such manner, on such terms and conditions
as may be prescribed and appointed by the audit committee or in its absence by the Board of
Directors of that company.

The Act expects the valuer to assume the following responsibilities:

i    Must make an impartial, true, and fair valuation of assets that are being valued.

i    Must exercise due diligence and care.

i    Must make the valuation in accordance with the prescribed rules.

i      Is prohibited to undertake valuation of assets in which he has a direct or indirect interest or
     becomes interested at any time during or after the valuation of that asset.

The Act specifically requires valuation to be undertaken for issue of new shares, issue of sweat
equity, non-cash transactions involving directors, in respect of compromise arrangements, mergers
and amalgamations, acquisition of minority shareholding, winding up or liquidation etc.

It is also pertinent to note that the Companies Act has not prescribed mandatory valuation in respect
of buy back of shares, reduction of capital and consolidation of capital and consequential payment
for fractional entitlements.

For valuation required under the Ind AS (Accounting Standard), the more appropriate interpretation
is that as the valuation is required to be provided under the Accounting Standards, which are
mandated by the Companies Act, 2013, the requirements of Companies.

Act, 2013 applies and it should be a Registered Valuer. However, another interpretation could be
that the valuation could be from any expert acceptable from an “accounting perspective”. For
instance, fair valuation of plan assets is done by actuaries.

(b)   Income Tax Act, 1961: Valuation requirements under the Income Tax Act, 1961 (ITA)
encompass various sections and rules aimed at ensuring accurate assessment of taxable income
and tax liabilities. Sections 56(2)(viib), 56(2)(x), 50B, 50C, and 92C, along with associated rules,
impose specific valuation requirements for different types of transactions and assets, ranging from
equity shares to immovable properties and transactions between associated enterprises.

Section 56(2) (viib) of the ITA addresses taxation of income arising from issuance of shares at a
premium by certain companies. There is a valuation requirement to substantiate the premium on
such issue and one of the options is to get a fair market value determination undertaken using
discounted cash flow method.

Similarly, Section 56(2)(x) deals with taxation of income arising from receipt of shares at a
consideration exceeding the fair market value. When an individual or entity receives shares at a
consideration higher than their fair market value, the excess consideration is taxed as income under
the head "Income from Other Sources". Rule 11UA of the Income Tax Rules, 1962, provides
guidelines for determining the fair market value of unquoted equity shares, ensuring consistency
and fairness in valuation for tax purposes.

Section 50B of the ITA pertains to taxation of capital gains arising from slump sale transactions. In
a slump sale, the consideration received or accruing from the transfer of an undertaking or business
as a whole is deemed as the full value of consideration for computing capital gains tax. Valuation of
the undertaking or business transferred in a slump sale is crucial for accurate tax assessment and
is conducted as per the provisions of Rule 11U and Rule 11UA of the Income Tax Rules, 1962.

Furthermore, Section 50C of the ITA mandates adoption of stamp duty value as the deemed
consideration for transfer of immovable properties if the actual sale consideration is lower than the
stamp duty value. This provision prevents undervaluation of immovable properties for tax evasion
purposes and ensures fair taxation based on the property's deemed market value.

Additionally, Section 92C of the ITA deals with transfer pricing regulations for transactions between
associated enterprises. Under this provision, transactions between associated enterprises must be
conducted at arm's length prices, which are determined based on the fair market value of the goods,
services, or intangible property transferred. Compliance with transfer pricing regulations requires
adherence to prescribed valuation methodologies and documentation requirements to justify the
arm's length nature of the transactions.

In conclusion, valuation requirements under the Income Tax Act and associated rules are diverse
and encompass various transactions and assets. Compliance with these provisions ensures
accurate assessment of taxable income and tax liabilities, promotes fairness and transparency in

(c)    Foreign Exchange Management Act, 1999: The Foreign Exchange Management Act
(FEMA), 1999 and its associated regulations govern foreign exchange transactions and regulate
cross-border transactions in India. Under FEMA, certain valuation requirements are mandated to
ensure compliance with foreign exchange regulations and facilitate transparent and fair dealings in
international transactions.

One of the key valuation requirements under FEMA pertains to the valuation of assets and liabilities
for transactions involving non-residents. FEMA mandates that transactions between residents and
non-residents must be conducted at arm's length prices, which are determined based on fair market
value. This necessitates the use of valuation techniques to ascertain the fair value of assets, such
as shares, immovable properties, or intellectual property rights, involved in such transactions.

Furthermore, FEMA regulations require valuation of shares issued or transferred to non-residents
for compliance with pricing guidelines specified by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI). Valuation of
shares must adhere to prescribed methodologies, such as the discounted cash flow method or the
net asset value method, to ensure compliance with pricing norms and prevent undervaluation or
overvaluation of shares issued to non-residents.

In addition to asset valuation, FEMA also mandates valuation requirements for transactions involving
foreign exchange derivatives, such as options, forwards, and swaps. Valuation of derivatives is
essential for determining their fair value and ensuring compliance with FEMA regulations governing
foreign exchange derivative transactions. Valuation methodologies approved by RBI or recognized
international standards may be used for valuing derivatives, with a focus on transparency and
accuracy in valuation practices.

Moreover, FEMA regulations impose valuation requirements for transactions involving overseas
investments and disinvestments by Indian residents. Valuation of investments and divestments in
foreign securities, joint ventures, or subsidiaries must comply with FEMA guidelines to facilitate
reporting and repatriation of funds in accordance with foreign exchange regulations.

Overall, valuation requirements under FEMA are aimed at promoting transparency, fairness, and
compliance in cross-border transactions involving non-residents and foreign exchange derivatives.
Adherence to valuation norms prescribed by FEMA and RBI ensures that transactions are conducted
at arm's length prices, mitigating the risk of underreporting or mispricing of assets and liabilities in
international dealings. Additionally, proper valuation practices  facilitate regulatory compliance,

(d)   SEBI Regulations: Valuation requirements under Securities and Exchange Board of India
(SEBI) regulations are crucial elements governing various corporate actions in India's capital
markets.  Specifically,  SEBI  regulations  address  valuation  guidelines  concerning  takeover
transactions, preferential allotments, and employee stock option plans (ESOPs), ensuring fairness,
transparency, and investor protection.

Under the Takeover Code regulated by SEBI, valuation assumes significant importance during
acquisition attempts to ensure equitable treatment of shareholders. Acquirers are mandated to
disclose the offer price and provide a fair exit opportunity to minority shareholders, with valuation
assessments conducted by independent valuers to determine the offer price.

Preferential allotments, another area governed by SEBI regulations, require fair valuation to prevent
dilution of existing shareholders' interests. SEBI mandates that companies seeking preferential
allotments disclose detailed valuation reports to ensure transparency and safeguard investors'
rights.

Similarly, SEBI regulations governing ESOPs necessitate fair valuation methodologies to determine
the exercise price of employee stock options. Companies issuing ESOPs must adhere to prescribed
valuation norms to safeguard employees' interests and prevent unfair dilution of equity.

In summary, SEBI's valuation requirements under the Takeover Code, preferential allotments, and
ESOP regulations are essential  for maintaining market  integrity, ensuring transparency, and
protecting investors' interests. Adherence to these regulations fosters investor confidence and
contributes to the overall efficiency and credibility of India's capital markets.

One of the primary uses of business valuation in performance evaluation  is to measure the
company's financial performance relative to its intrinsic value. By comparing the actual market value
of the business with its estimated intrinsic value derived from valuation analysis, management can
gauge  the  effectiveness  of  value  creation  initiatives and  identify  areas  for  improvement.
Discrepancies between market value and intrinsic value may signal opportunities for strategic
realignment or operational enhancements to enhance shareholder wealth.

Moreover, business valuation assists in benchmarking the company's performance against industry
peers and competitors. Comparative valuation analysis enables management to assess relative
valuation  metrics,  such  as  price-to-earnings  ratios,  enterprise  value  multiples,  or  market
capitalization, against industry benchmarks and identify competitive positioning and performance
gaps. This comparative analysis informs strategic decision-making processes, such as pricing
strategies, market positioning, and competitive differentiation efforts.

Furthermore, business valuation supports performance evaluation by providing insights into the
drivers of value creation within the organization. Valuation methodologies, such as discounted cash
flow analysis or economic value-added models, help identify key value drivers, such as revenue
growth,  profitability, and  capital  efficiency. By  focusing on enhancing these value  drivers,
management can align operational priorities and resource allocation decisions with value creation
objectives, thereby optimizing overall performance and shareholder returns.

(h)   Stakeholder Communication: Effective stakeholder communication is essential for fostering
transparency, building  trust, and maintaining positive relationships with investors, employees,
customers, and other key stakeholders. Business valuation serves as a valuable tool in stakeholder
communication by providing transparent and credible insights into the company's value drivers,
financial performance, and growth prospects.

One of the primary uses of business valuation in stakeholder communication is in investor relations.
Transparent disclosure of valuation methodologies, assumptions, and key valuation metrics in
financial reports and investor presentations enhances investor understanding of the company's
financial performance and value creation potential. By articulating the factors influencing the
company's valuation and providing clear explanations of valuation outcomes, management can instill
investor confidence and attract capital to support growth initiatives.

Moreover, business valuation facilitates effective communication with employees by providing
insights into the company's financial health and performance. Transparent communication of
valuation  results and  their  implications  for employee  compensation,  incentive  plans, and
performance targets fosters a sense of ownership and alignment with organizational goals. By
demonstrating how  employee  contributions  impact  the  company's  value  creation  efforts,
management can motivate and engage employees towards achieving collective objectives.

Additionally, business valuation supports communication with other key stakeholders, such as
customers, suppliers, and regulatory authorities. Transparent disclosure of valuation information
enhances credibility and trust in business relationships, facilitating collaboration and partnership
opportunities. Valuation insights also enable management to address stakeholder concerns and
expectations proactively, thereby enhancing stakeholder satisfaction and loyalty.

In conclusion, business valuation plays a vital role in stakeholder communication by providing
transparent and credible insights into the company's value creation and financial performance. By
leveraging valuation as a communication tool, companies can foster trust, enhance transparency,
and build stronger relationships with investors, employees, customers, and other key stakeholders,
thereby driving sustainable growth and value creation in the long term.
