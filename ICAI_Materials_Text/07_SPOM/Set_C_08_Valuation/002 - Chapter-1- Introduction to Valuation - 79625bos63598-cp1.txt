     1.  GENESIS OF VALUATION

“Price is what you pay; value is what you get.”

This was one of Warren Buffet’s most famous words on value and the basis of his investment
philosophy based on which he achieved unparalleled success.

Valuation is the process of identifying the real worth of an asset, financial or non-financial. “Value
lies in the eyes of beholder”; similarly, value of any asset depends on the circumstances and the
purpose for which valuation is being undertaken.

We come across instances pertaining to value and valuation in our day-to-day  life. A person
generally wants to retain his/her ancestral property irrespective of the price offered, because he
finds the house to be more valuable when compared to the money offered.

A builder buys a plot of land near the construction site of a metro train. The builder anticipates that
the price of the land will appreciate once the metro train in that locality is operational. In essence,
he is buying the property today as he feels the value of the land is much higher than the price being
paid today.

The value of the same asset may be different for different investors based on the utilities associated
with it. As per ICAI valuation standards 2018,

Value is -

i    An economic concept.

i    An estimate of the likely price to be concluded by the buyers and sellers of a good or service
       that is available for purchase.

i    Not a fact.

i    An estimate of likely price at a given time in accordance with a particular definition of value.

The US Appraisal Foundation defines market value as:

“The most probable price which a property should bring in a competitive and open market under all
conditions requisite to a fair sale, the buyer and seller each acting prudently and knowledgeable,
and assuming the price is not affected by undue stimulus.”

Valuation can be done  for tangible assets such as land and building, plant and machinery,
marketable securities like company’s shares, intangible assets like patents, copyright, trademarks

etc.) and for liabilities (for example: bonds issued by a company). It can also be done for the business

Valuations are also sought with respect to intangibles like assembled workforce and customer lists
which add value to the worth of the business. Even though techniques used for valuation of each
asset differ, valuation is guided by a set of common principles and standards. One cannot use the
same technique to value land and a publicly traded stock, but the guiding principle for valuation is
similar.

In business parlance, valuations are mainly used in the case of mergers and acquisitions, start-up
valuations, to value investments in stocks and securities, financial reporting, and tax purposes.

Business Valuation is not just about reviewing financials, it is futuristic in nature and considers the
future cash flows, capital structure, management, and opportunities to identify the fair value of the
business.

An investor does not pay more for an asset which is more than its worth. A forecast of the future
cash flows is always considered before making an investment decision.

Valuation is a perfect combination of art and science. Valuation is considered as a science because
it is rooted in numbers and is based on the basic concept of risk and return. But it’s also an art as it
involves judgments to be made on the growth rate and future opportunities.

There is no exact number defining the worth of an asset or business. A valuer must consider both
objective and subjective information to derive the value of an asset. Valuation derived also involves
his/ her personal judgment based on information available in hand and based on his experience in
the field.

To value a business or an asset, we need firm specific information as well as market-wide
information. There are two approaches that the valuer uses:

i    Top-Down Approach

i    Bottom-Up approach.

It is unrealistic to expect valuations to be precise as the valuations are based on future cash flows
and estimated discount rates. Degree of precision depends upon the investment in hand. The
Valuation of a well-established company with stable cash flows will be more precise compared to
valuation of a start-up company with irregular cash flow and a new market.

     3.  VALUATION UNDER PRESENT SCENARIOS

With the introduction and the subsequent adoption of Ind AS by many Indian companies, the
emphasis on valuation has increased. Regulators like RBI and SEBI have also issued new pricing
guidelines with respect to FEMA, acquisitions, and takeovers respectively.

With the introduction of the Insolvency and Bankruptcy code 2016, valuation has gained further
relevance. Consequent to the introduction of IBC 2016, the Companies (Registered Valuers and
Valuation) Rules, 2017) was implemented on 15 July 2017.

Indian economy is growing rapidly, and number of start-ups have increased multifold. Due to this
start up valuations are also gaining prominence. There  is also an increase  in mergers and
amalgamation, strategic acquisitions which also require valuation of businesses.

The current scenario of competitive market also requires investors to conduct a valuation exercise
as a part of due diligence before making any investment. It also requires the business owner to have
a reality check on the worth of the business and  if the strategies adopted are giving the required
value addition.

     4.  IMPORTANT TERMINOLOGIES IN VALUATION

4.1  Valuation

Valuation is the process of identifying the fair value of an asset or liability. Since the value keeps
changing valuation is done on a specific date.

Valuation is an essential pre-requisite in making decisions on investments, choosing portfolio of
securities, negotiating price in a Merger and other buy and sell agreements.

Equity valuation means deriving value of a security.

Business Valuation is about finding the economic value of the business. Economic value is nothing
but the current worth of the business. To derive the economic value, an analysis of External and
Internal Business Environment  is required. Tools like SWOT analysis, PESTLE, Top – Down
approach, bottom- up approach etc. are used to identify favorable and unfavorable characteristics
of the macro and micro economic environment in which the business operates.

A proper analysis of the Macro and Micro Economic environment helps the investor to understand
the competitive edge of the company and provides better insight into the prospects of the company.

Based on the Analysis of the External and Internal environment, a valuer creates a forecast of future
cash flows and applies appropriate valuation model to derive the value of the business.

4.2  Valuer

Valuation must be undertaken by a valuer specified in the statute. Each statute has defined who can
act as valuer under the Act. For Instance, Companies Act, 2013 and Insolvency and Bankruptcy
Code, 2016 mandates that all valuations under the act should be performed by a Registered Valuer.

SARFEASI Act defines Valuer to be a person registered with Chief Commissioner of Income tax for
Valuation u/s 34AB Wealth Tax Act 1957.

FEMA Act 1999, Income Tax Act 1961 and SEBI regulations also have their own definition of who is
authorized to be valuer as per the Act.

4.3  Registered Valuer

Regulation 2(1)(m) of Insolvency and Bankruptcy Board of India (Insolvency Resolution Process
for Corporate Persons) Regulations, 2016 defines a “Registered Valuer” as a person registered as
such in accordance with the Companies Act, 2013 (18 of 2013) and rules made thereunder.
Accordingly, a Registered Valuer under IBC has the same meaning as provided under the
Companies Act, 2013. Consequently, in the exercise of the powers conferred by section 247 read
with sections 458, 459, and 469 of the Companies Act, 2013 (18 of 2013), the Central Government
made the Companies (Registered Valuers and Valuation) Rules, 2017 for Registered Valuers
thereunder which enumerate the qualification, registration, and disqualification criteria for such
valuers.

Fair Market Value (FMV)

Fair Market Value can be stated as the estimated price that an asset would fetch in an open market,
where both buyer and seller act at their own will, without any undue pressure and they are given
considerable amount of time to act knowledgeably and complete the transaction.


© The Institute of Chartered Accountants of India

Taxation and Real Estate are common areas where Fair Market Value is used.

Fair Market Value may be different from the Intrinsic Value of an asset.

Fair Value (FV)

Regulation 2(h)(b) of the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Board of India (Insolvency Resolution Process
for Corporate Persons) Regulations, 2016 defines  “fair value” as “ the estimated realizable value of
the assets of the corporate debtor, if they were to be exchanged on the insolvency commencement
date between a willing buyer and a willing seller in an arm’s length transaction, after proper
marketing and where the parties had acted knowledgeably, prudently and without compulsion”.

Ind AS 113 – defines Fair Value as: “The price that would be received to sell an asset or paid to
transfer a liability in an orderly transaction between market participants at the measurement date.

Hence, Fair Value is the estimated price at which an asset or liability would be transferred in the
principal market, between a willing buyer and a willing seller in an orderly transaction, at the
measurement date, given the current market conditions.

Fair value puts emphasis on the exit price, which is the price at which an asset would be sold, or
liability is assumed. Hence a valuer may compare current market value, growth prospects and
replacement cost to determine the fair value of the asset.

Fair Value also emphasizes the concept of principal market. Principal market is the market with
highest volume and level of activity for the subject asset or liability. In the absence of Principal
Market, IND AS 113 states that the most advantageous market may be considered in such cases.

Most Advantages market is the market which maximizes the amount received upon selling an asset
or minimizes the amount to be paid to transfer a liability after adjusting transportation cost that would
be incurred to transport the asset from its current location to that market.

Illustration: When a manufacturing company approaches a bank for a loan with Plant and Machinery
as collateral, the bank would lend based on the fair value of the asset rather than the historical value
stated in the Balance sheet.

Market Value

As per International Valuation Standards, Market Value is “the estimated amount for which an asset
or liability should exchange on the valuation date between a willing buyer and a willing seller in an
arm’s  length  transaction,  after  proper  marketing and where  the  parties had each  acted
knowledgeably, prudently and without compulsion.”

1.13                      INTRODUCTION TO VALUATION                              1.13


In simple terms market value can be defined as the price an asset would fetch in the open market
on a given date.

Market Value is easiest to identify for exchange traded assets like shares, stocks and future as the
traded price is easily available. Determining Market value is very difficult in the case of illiquid assets
like real estate where traded values are not readily available.

Illustration: Price at which shares are traded in stock market reflects the Market value of shares.

Intrinsic Value

Intrinsic Value is a measure of the true worth of an asset.  It does not take market price into
consideration. It is the present value of estimated future cash flows to be generated by the asset
discounted at an appropriate discount rate.

Intrinsic value measurement is used by investors to identify investment opportunities. It is used to
measure if the asset in hand is undervalued or overvalued. If the intrinsic value is higher than the
current market price of the asset/security, it is undervalued and if the intrinsic value is lower than
the market price of the asset/security, it is overvalued.

The term intrinsic value is also used to state the value of an option contract i.e. the difference
between underlying stock price and Strike price. The intrinsic value of an option contract will never
be negative.

Illustration: If you believe the Intrinsic Value of a stock calculated based on Discounted Cash Flow
model is ` 200/- per share whereas the market value is ` 180/-, you may conclude that the market
is undervaluing the stock by ` 20/-.

Investment Value

Investment value is the value specific and unique to an investor.  It is the value based on the
individual understanding and measurement of the subject asset’s value. It can be higher than Fair
Market Value based on the unique advantages the investor might have due to purchase of the
subject asset. It could be tax advantages, synergy to other assets, strategic benefits etc.

International Glossary of Business Valuation Terms defines Investment Value as “the value to a
particular investor based on individual investment requirements and expectations”.

Illustration: Let’s assume a plot of land near a mall is valued at Rs.10,00,000. The mall owner
requires a parking space, and he projects that the parking space will increase the volume of people
visiting the mall. Hence, he will be willing to pay a higher amount than the real worth of the land as

Synergistic Value

Synergistic value is the value addition of an asset when combined with other assets. Synergistic
value can be higher than market value. In synergy, the combined value of assets is higher than the
sum of separate values. Synergistic Value is also referred to as “Marriage Value”.

Illustration: Mergers and Amalgamations happen to take advantage of synergies like high scale
operations, extensive market share etc.

Special Value

Special Value is an extraordinary value to an asset when compared to other assets like the subject
asset, due to a functional, economic, or physical advantage associated with it.

Illustration: A house property near a metro station will have higher value than a house property
away from the station. The higher value is due to the functional advantage associated with the
property.

4.7  Bases and Premises of Valuation

Valuation bases means the type of value used in an engagement. Different valuation bases may
lead to different conclusions. Hence  it is very important to choose an appropriate valuation base
depending on the valuation engagement in hand.

1.161.16           VALUATION


ICAI Valuation standard 102 describes three valuation bases:

i     Fair Value: Fair value is the price that would be received to sell an asset or paid to transfer
      a liability in an orderly transaction between market participants at the valuation date.

i     Participant Specific Value: Participant specific value is the estimated value of an asset or
         liability considering specific advantages or disadvantages of either of the owner or identified
       acquirer or identified participants.

i    Liquidation Value: Liquidation value is the amount that will be realized on sale of an asset
       or a  group  of  assets when an  actual/hypothetical  termination  of  the  business  is
      contemplated/assumed.

Premise of Value

The premise of value is the conditions or circumstances under which an asset is used or deployed.
Premise is a set of assumptions to be considered while valuing an asset.

Choice of the bases of valuation for the valuation engagement determines the premise of value to
be considered. Valuers may consider adopting one or multiple premise of value based on the set of
circumstances provided.

Some commonly used premises of value are:

(a)    Highest and Best Use

(b)    Going Concern Vvalue

(c)   As is Where Value

(d)    Orderly Liquidation

(e)    Forced Sale

(a)    Highest and Best Use

Highest and best use from a market perspective is that which fetches maximum value. It must be
physically possible, financially feasible and legally allowed. Current use need not be the highest and
best use of an asset. The cost of bringing the asset from current use to highest and best use is to
be adjusted and it will have an impact on the net value.

Example: A house property in an industrial area is currently let out for residential purposes. The
highest and best use of the asset would be to convert  it into an office space and let  it out as an
office. The cost of interiors to convert the residential property into an office space will have an impact
at the net value and the decision involved.

(b)   Going Concern Value

As the term suggests, going concern value is the value of the business assuming it will continue to
operate in future.

As per ICAI valuation standard 102 “Intangible elements of going concern value result from factors
such as having a trained work force, an operational plan and necessary licences, system and
procedure in place”.

(c)   As is where basis value

It considers the existing use of the asset which may or may not be highest and best use.

Illustration: A house property in a commercial area is sold as residential property, but the highest
and best use might be to lease it as a commercial property or office space.

(d)    Orderly Liquidation

Orderly liquidation value refers to the realizable value of an asset at the event of liquidation, where
considerable efforts have been taken on marketing and reasonable period of time is given to find a
purchaser, where the seller selling the asset at “As is where basis value”.

(e)   Forced sale

It refers to a condition where the seller is compelled to sell the assets under constraints without
proper efforts to market the asset or time to market the asset.

4.8  Valuation Date

Valuation date refers to the specific date on which the valuer determines the value of the asset or
business. As value  is dynamic in nature, mentioning valuation date in the valuation report  is
extremely important. Value determined in a valuation report as on this date, may not hold good few
months later as the assumptions based on which valuation is derived might have changed.
