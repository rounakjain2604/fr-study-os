“The Indian Constitution is a federal Constitution in as much as it established what may be called a
dual polity which will consist of the Union at the Centre and the States at the periphery each endowed
with sovereign powers  to be exercised  in  the  field assigned  to them  respectively by  the

Centre-State Relations
Centre State relations are a vital aspect of India’s political landscape. They shed light on how power
is divided between the Central Government and  individual  States, with each  entity holding
supremacy within its own sphere. To govern and manage these relationships effectively, the Indian
Constitution includes comprehensive provisions, which we  will explore in this discussion. The
Sarkaria Commission was meant for setting up a permanent inter-state council for resolving any
issues among the states.
According to Sir Kenneth Clinton Wheare, a prominent figure in federal theory, defined federalism
as a way  of  dividing power  to  establish  co-existing and independent  central and  regional
governments. He described India’s Constitution as quasi-federal, a unique kind of federation.
Although the term “federalism” doesn’t explicitly appear in the Constitution, it is evident that India’s
governance structure leans largely towards federal principles.
In a federal country like India, Centre-State Relations play a crucial role in politics, illustrating the
division of power between the Central Government and individual states. Each holds authority in
their specific domains, and the Indian Constitution refers to the nation as a “Union of States.”
The relationship between the Central Government and Individual States is pivotal. Here are some
key aspects highlighting the significance of Centre-State relations:
(i)    Division of Powers: The Indian Constitution clearly demarcates the powers between the
      Union (central) government and the state governments through the Union List, State List, and
      Concurrent List. This division ensures that both entities have their respective domains to
       legislate upon.

(ii)   Autonomy of States: India is often referred to as a "Union of States" to emphasize the unity
       of the nation while acknowledging the autonomy of its constituent states. Each state has its
     own government, legislature, and judiciary to manage internal affairs within the boundaries
       of the Constitution.
(iii)   Distribution of Resources: Financial relations between the Centre and the States are
        crucial. The division of revenues and grants-in-aid from the Centre to the States is an
       essential aspect that helps ensure equitable development across the country.
(iv)   Inter-State Cooperation: The Constitution provides mechanisms for inter-state cooperation
      and conflict resolution through the establishment of bodies like the Inter-State Council. This
       allows states to collaborate on issues of common interest, fostering unity and mutual
      understanding.
(v)   Emergency Powers: During times of national emergency (such as war, external aggression,
       or armed rebellion), the Centre gains increased authority over the states, allowing for a more
       centralized decision-making process to address the crisis effectively.
(vi)   Dispute Resolution: The Constitution also provides for mechanisms to resolve disputes
      between the Centre and the States or among the states themselves, ensuring that conflicts
      over jurisdiction or other matters are addressed peacefully.
(vii)   Constitutional Amendments: Amendments to the Constitution that impact federal relations
        typically require the consent of both Parliament and a specified number of state legislatures.
       This reflects the consultative nature of federal decision-making.
The Centre-State relations in India embody the essence of federalism, aiming to strike a balance
between the overarching national interests and the autonomy of individual states. It's a dynamic
relationship that evolves over time, shaped by political, social, and economic factors, and often
involves dialogue, negotiation, and cooperation to ensure the country's smooth governance.
Dedicated sections  in the Constitution, known as ‘Union-State Relations,’ give details of the
distribution of executive, legislative, and financial powers between the Centre and the States. Given
their supremacy in their respective areas, maintaining harmony and balance on a larger scale is
vital. Consequently, the Constitution includes numerous clauses to govern Centre-State Relations
at various levels.

Legislative Relations
Articles 245 to 255 within the Indian Constitution focus on the intricate interplay of legislative
relationships between the Union (Parliament) and the individual states, which includes  their
respective legislatures. These Articles dissect the extent of legislative authority held by both the
Union and the states which contain subjects like defence, foreign affairs, currency, union duties and
communication etc. On the other hand, state enjoy the power over the 66th  items enumerated in
state list and 47th items of concurrent list.
A closer examination of these provisions underscores that Parliament possesses overriding powers
when it comes to state legislatures. These provisions also delineate the specific subjects on which
each can legislate, outline the repercussions of conflicts between state and national laws, define
Parliament’s residuary powers, and cover various other critical aspects.
Furthermore, Schedule VII within the Constitution plays a pivotal role by categorizing subjects into
the Union List, State List, and Concurrent List, providing a structured framework for legislative
responsibilities.

Administrative Relations

In the Indian Constitution, Articles 256 to 263 cover how the Central (national) Government and
various state governments cooperate in administrative matters. Even though India is a federal
country with power shared between these entities, there are rules in place to ensure that state
governments follow the laws made by the national Parliament and don’t carry out administrative
tasks that go against these laws (Article 256).
To make this cooperation work better, the Sarkaria Commission recommended a concept called
“Cooperative Federalism” in administrative matters. This is important because when many different
groups at both the national and state levels work independently,  it can lead to confusion and
inefficiency in running the country.
Financial Relations
Part XII of the Indian Constitution, encompassing Articles 264 to 293, is dedicated to managing the
financial ties between the central government and individual states. India’s federal system means
that responsibilities for taxation are divided, and  it falls upon the central government to distribute
funds to the states.
This section covers all aspects related to these financial relationships, including the taxation powers
of both the Centre and states, rules for imposing and sharing taxes, financial grants to states,
surcharges, and more. An important contemporary example of this financial interplay is the Goods
and Services Tax (GST), a dual-tiered tax structure that exemplifies the Centre -state financial
relationship in India.
Conflict between Centre State Relations in India
In India, our constitution mixes two important ideas - federalism and central control. To keep our
country united while addressing the unique needs of its diverse regions, we need to strengthen how

states and the central government work together. This brings us to the concept of Centre-State
relations, which involves agreements between the central government (the Union) and the individual

In terms of making laws, problems arise because the central government sometimes makes
decisions about matters that should be under state control. There are also delays in getting approval
for important laws passed by state governments. On the financial side of things, concerns arise
because the central government is taking more control over resources and important economic
decisions, like planning.
Right now, there’s an issue because too much power is held by the central government. States have
lots of responsibilities but not enough independence.
To address these problems, the government set up the Sarkaria Commission in 1983. However,
even though the Commission took five years to study the issue and make recommendations, most
of its suggestions didn’t fully solve these problems, except for some small improvements in the
financial sector.
For example, the Commission suggested that municipalities should be allowed to issue bonds
without taxes, and it supported decisions made by Chief Ministers on certain taxes. But sadly, even
these modest ideas have not been put into practice by the central government for nearly two
decades.
In 2007, the Indian government established a new commission under the leadership of Justice
Madan Mohan Punchhi. This new commission aimed to re-evaluate how the central government and
states work together, taking into account changes in India’s politics and economy since the Sarkaria
Commission’s last review over 20 years ago.
The new commission took a thorough look at how things currently work and considered court
decisions about who should do what in areas like making laws, running the government, handling
emergencies, managing finances, and planning for the future. After all this, in March 2010, the
commission made a total of 273 recommendations to improve how the central government and states
work together.

     2.  RELATED ARTICLES- PART XI
Part XI of the Indian Constitution deals with the distribution of legislative powers between the Union
(central government) and the states. Specifically, it contains provisions from Articles 245 to 263. It
is divided into two chapters:
•     Chapter I- Legislative Relations (Article 245 to 255)
•     Chapter II- Administrative Relations (Article 256 to 263)
CHAPTER I – LEGISLATIVE RELATIONS (ARTICLE 245 TO 255)
Articles 245 to 255 in the Indian Constitution focus on legislative relations between the Union
(Central Government) and the States:
 Article         Related provision
 Article 245     Deals with the extent of laws made by Parliament and the legislatures of
                   states. It establishes that Parliament has the power to make laws for the whole
                   or any part of India, and the legislature of a state has the authority to make
                 laws for the respective state
 Article 246     Divides  the  legislative powers between  the  Parliament and  the  state
                   legislatures through three lists: the Union List (for the Central Government),
                  the State List (for State Governments), and the Concurrent List (where both
               can legislate).
 Article 247     Allows Parliament to establish additional courts for the better administration
                    of laws made by it or by any existing legislature,
 Article 248     Confers the residuary powers of legislation on the Parliament, which means
                any matter not covered in the Union List, State List, or Concurrent List falls
                   within the jurisdiction of Parliament to legislate upon.

             made by a state legislature is repugnant to a law made by Parliament, the law

Article 245 deals with the extent of laws made by Parliament and by the Legislatures of States.
(1)    Subject to the provisions of this Constitution, Parliament may make laws for the whole or any

(2)   No law made by Parliament shall be deemed to be invalid on the ground that it would have

(3)   The parliament make a law for whole or any part of the country.
(4)    State legislature can make a law for whole or any other part of state.
(5)   The law of parliament never invalid even where the effect pertains to outside the country.
Article 246 specifies subject-matter of laws made by Parliament and by the Legislatures of
States.
(1)    Notwithstanding anything in clauses (2) and (3), Parliament has exclusive power to make
      laws with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List I in the Seventh Schedule (in this
       Constitution referred to as the “Union List”).
(2)    Notwithstanding anything in clause (3), Parliament, and, subject to clause (1), the Legislature
       of any State also, have power to make laws with respect to any of the matters enumerated in
        List III in the Seventh Schedule (in this Constitution referred to as the “Concurrent List”).
(3)    Subject to clauses (1) and (2), the Legislature of any State has exclusive power to make laws
        for such State or any part thereof with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List II in
       the Seventh Schedule (in this Constitution referred to as the “State List”).
(4)    Parliament has power to make laws with respect to any matter for any part of the territory of
       India not included in a State notwithstanding that such matter is a matter enumerated in the
       State List.

(1)    Notwithstanding anything contained in Articles 246 and 254, Parliament, and, subject to

CHAPTER II – ADMINISTRATIVE RELATIONS (ARTICLE 256 TO 263)
Articles 256 to 263 of the Indian Constitution deal with various aspects related to executive powers,
legislative relations, and the interplay between the Union (Central Government) and the states:
 Article 256   Emphasizes the obligation of states to ensure compliance with laws made by
               Parliament and to exercise executive power in a way that ensures compliance
             and Article 257 deals with the control of the Union over states in certain cases. It
             empowers the Union to give directions to a state regarding the execution of laws
            made by Parliament or for the maintenance of law and order.
 Article 257   Deals with the control of the Union over states in certain cases. It empowers the
              Union to give directions to a state regarding the execution of laws made by
               Parliament or for the maintenance of law and order.
 Article 258   Allows the Union to entrust functions to states or officers for executing laws made
              by Parliament or implementing its decisions.
 Article 259   Provides for the adjudication of disputes relating to waters between two or more
                 states, or between a state and the Union.
 Article 260    Specifies the jurisdiction of the Union in relation to treaties and agreements with
                 foreign countries
 Article 261   Deals with the  full faith and credit given to public acts, records, and judicial
               proceedings of states and Union territories
 Article 262   Provides for adjudication of disputes relating to waters between states, and the
               Parliament has the power to provide for the adjudication of such disputes
 Article 263   Provides  for the establishment of an Interstate Council, aiming to promote
                coordination between states and between the Union and states. It ensures better
                coordination in legislative and administrative matters

These Articles collectively establish guidelines for the cooperation and coordination between the
Union and the states in India, outlining the mechanisms to resolve disputes, maintain harmony, and
ensure compliance with laws and directives for effective governance.

Part XII of the Constitution of India is a compilation of laws related to finance, property, contracts,

the definition of various funds, the right to hold property, and borrowing by Union and State
governments. Articles 268-293, mentioned in Part XII of the Constitution, specify the financial
relations between the Centre and the States. The Parliament has the authority to charge the union
list taxes. Part XII of the Constitution of India is from Article 294 to 300 and deals with the property,
contracts, rights, liabilities, obligations, and suits of the Centre and the states.
Articles 268-293, mentioned in Part XII of the Constitution, specifies the financial relations between
the Centre and the States.

Division of taxation authorities between the federal government and the states:
•     The Parliament has the authority to charge the union list taxes.
•     The state legislature has sole authority to impose the taxes listed in the State List. The
      Concurrent List enumerates the taxes that can be levied by both the Parliament and the state
       legislatures.
•     The Parliament has the residuary power of taxation (i.e., the authority to impose taxes not
        listed in any of the three  lists). The parliament may levy a  gift tax, a wealth tax or an
      expenditure tax under this Article.
•     There are no tax entries available on the concurrent  list. In terms of tax legislation, the
      concurrent jurisdiction is inaccessible. However, the 101st Amendment Act of 2016 provided
      an exemption by establishing a unique provision for goods and services tax. The concurrent
      competence to make legislators/legislation controlling goods and services tax has been given
       to parliament and state legislatures by this amendment.
In addition to taxation shared between the Union and the states, the Constitution provides grants-
in-aid to the states from federal funds. Statutory grants and discretionary grants are the two types
of grants-in-aid to states:
(i)    Statutory Grants
      •      Article 275 empowers the Parliament to offer grants to states which are in need of
               financial assistance, rather than to all states. Each year, these grants are charged to
             the Consolidated Fund of India (CFI).
      •     Aside from this standard provision, the Constitution additionally provides for special
             funds to promote the welfare of scheduled tribes (STs) in a state or to improve the
               quality of administration of scheduled territories in a state, such as Assam.
      •     Under Article 275 statutory grants (both general and particular) are awarded to states
           on the Finance Commission’s recommendation.
(ii)    Discretionary Grants
      •      Article 282 empowers the Union and the states to give grants for any public purpose,
           even if it falls outside of their own legislative jurisdiction. The Centre is responsible for
             enforcing this regulation.

      4.   PART XIII – TRADE, COMMERCE AND INTERCOURSE
         WITHIN THE TERRITORY OF INDIA
Part XIII of the Indian Constitution is titled "Trade, Commerce, and Intercourse within the Territory
of India". It contains Articles 301–307, which cover trade, commerce, and intercourse within India.


© The Institute of Chartered Accountants of India


====================== PAGE 36 ======================


4.36          THE CONSTITUTION OF INDIA

Part XIII aims to ensure that trade, commerce, and intercourse between states and other parts of
the country are free from legal interference. It does not discriminate between citizens and non-
citizens.
The Articles in Part XIII were designed to foster unity and ensure that India functions as a single
economic entity. Articles 301 - 305 deals regarding Freedom of Trade and Commerce, and the
power of Parliament and States to impose restrictions on the same.
The free flow of trade, commerce and intercourse within and across Inter-state borders is an
important perquisite for ensuring economic unity, stability, and prosperity in a two-tier polity country.
The Indian constitution includes provisions that guarantee freedom of  inter -state trade and
commerce through India’s territory. In a federation, it is essential to reduce the barriers (tariffs, non-
tariffs, quotas, etc.) between the states as much as possible so that the people feel that they are
members of the same country though living in different geographical areas of the nation.
Example: If Mr. A lives in Gujarat and expands his business and sell his products in other states,
let’s say he wants to do business in Punjab so he has freedom of trade, commerce and intercourse
as per Article 301 of the Constitution of India.
Key Terminologies
•    Trade: Trade means buying and selling of goods for profit-making purposes. In the context of
      Article 301, trade is not limited to mere transactions but extends to organized activities that
    have a clear economic purpose. It is synonymous with business, emphasizing its organized
     nature.
•   Commerce: Commerce encompasses the entire spectrum of activities that facilitate trade. This
     includes transportation, communication, and any medium that aids the movement of goods and
     services. It is the backbone that supports trade, ensuring that goods reach from producers to
     consumers.
•    Intercourse: The term “intercourse” in Article 301 is used in juxtaposition with trade and
    commerce, implying “commercial-intercourse.”  It doesn’t refer to purposeless motion but to
     meaningful commercial interactions. However, for the purpose of Article 301, the focus is
      primarily on commercial intercourse, ensuring a seamless flow of trade across states.
In today’s globalized world, the provisions of Part XIII are more relevant than ever. With the rise of
e-commerce and digital trade, the definitions of trade and commerce are evolving. While the
Constitution provides a robust framework, it’s essential to adapt to the changing times, ensuring that
India remains a vibrant hub for trade and commerce.

The Indian judiciary has played a pivotal role in interpreting and upholding the freedom of trade
