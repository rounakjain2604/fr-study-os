The Constitution of a country may be regarded as the fundamental law of the land, pertaining to the
institution of the State and Government of that country. It outlines the powers and responsibilities of
the various agencies of the State, imposes limitations upon them and regulates the relations between
the State and its population.

    1   CONSTITUTIONAL HISTORY AND VARIOUS SOURCES
         IN THE MAKING OF THE CONSTITUTION
The Indian Constitution, adopted on November 26, 1949 and came into force on 26th January, 1950,
is the supreme legal document that governs the Republic of India.  It serves as the foundational
framework that outlines the country's political principles, establishes the structure of the government,
delineates the fundamental rights and duties of citizens, and defines the powers and responsibilities
of various institutions.

The Charter Act, 1853: The Charter Act, 1853 was governed by British East India Company for
retaining its territories & revenue in India for holding of crown for an uncertain period. This Act
underlined the establishment of a distinct 'Legislature' in the form of a Legislative Council comprised
of 12 members.
Following  this, the Indian Councils Act,1861, considered the primary charter  for the Indian
Legislature, marked the initiation of the "system of legislative devolution in India." Subsequent to
this, the Indian Councils Act, 1892 & Indian Councils Act 1909 were followed.
The Indian Councils Act 1909, aligned with the Morley-Minto Reforms, introduced an element of
election and representation in the Union's Legislative Council. Two new provinces of Sind &
Punjab were added in this Act. It also lead to creation of Assam and Burma (i.e. Myanmar).
However, none of these Acts granted decisive authority to native elements in matters of legislation
and administration.

The Government  of  India Act,1919:     It implemented  the Montague-Chelmsford Reforms,
introducing a Bicameral Legislature at the Union and incorporating elements of a “Responsible
form of Government” in the Provinces. Though, the leaders of the nation regarded the 1919 reforms
as insufficient, unsatisfactory, and disappointing. They called upon the British Parliament to initiate
actions to establish a fully responsible Government in India, aligning with the principle of self-
determination. In move of an early revision of the Government of India Act, 1919, the Central
Legislative Assembly adopted a resolution, known as the 'National Demand.' This marked the first
instance of the assembly supporting the growing demand that the future Constitution of India should
be  crafted by  Indians themselves. Diarchy was introduced  i.e.  there were two classes  of
administrators, Executive Counsellors and Ministers. The governor was the executive head of
provinces. The ministers were responsible to the people who elected them through the legislature.
In 1922, Mahatma Gandhi asserted the demand that India’s destiny should be determined by the Indians
themselves. He stated: “Swaraj will not be a free gift of the British Parliament.  It will be a declaration of
India’s full self-expression, expressed through an Act of Parliament.”

The Government of India Act, 1935: The Government of India Act, 1935, holds a crucial position
in the constitutional history of India, aiming to provide the country with a written Constitution. It
was the longest Act that British Parliament ever enacted. Despite its enactment, the structure of the
Central Government  in India largely retained  its essence from the 1919 Act, as the federal
component of the 1935 Act was never implemented. Only certain adjustments in practice and
procedure were made, primarily driven by the introduction of 'autonomy' in the Provinces. Later this
Act was replaced by Constitution of India.
The Quit India Movement, 1942 was launched in Bombay Session also known as August kranti,
injected new energy into the quest for freedom. Numerous initiatives were undertaken to facilitate
the transfer of power and establish a constitutional framework for an independent India. As part of
these endeavours, a British Cabinet Mission reached India on March 24, 1946. The mission's
declared objective was to aid the Viceroy in establishing the machinery in India through which
Indians could formulate their own constitution. Throughout the negotiations, there was a persistent
focus on core issues, including the attainment  of independence and the establishment of a
representative  Constituent  Assembly  for  crafting  the  future  Constitution  without  external
interference.
The Cabinet Mission: On May 16, 1946, presented its own proposal, outlining principles and
procedures for drafting the future Constitution of India. Main focus of mission is to transforming the
power that the British Government had to the Indian Government. Regarding the quick establishment
of the Constituent Assembly, the plan suggested:

The India Independence Act, 1947: It granted the Constituent Assembly unlimited authority to draft
and adopt any constitution, even enabling it to override the India Independence Act itself, without
requiring additional legislation from the  British Parliament. The Act clearly ended the  British
Parliament's power  to  legislate  for the Dominion  after August 15, 1947. Consequently, the
Constituent Assembly evolved into a fully representative and sovereign body, inclusive of the States
and Provinces  in India, free from external authority. In  its sovereign capacity, the Assembly
successfully concluded the task of formulating the Constitution for India in the best interests of the
people, devoid of any external interference.
Most of India was under direct British rule from 1857 to 1947. Upon independence it became clear
that a new constitution for the country is required to be chartered. But for that the all of India needed
to be bought into the union. This meant the Princely States needed to be convinced to become a
part of the Indian union. Either by force or diplomacy. The First Home Minister Sardar Vallabhai
Patel and V.P Menon did this unenviable task. Until this happened, India was still legally a dominion
under the British, responsible for external security.
Dr. Rajendra Prasad was elected as the Chairman of the Constituent Assembly on December 11,

under the Chairmanship of Dr. B.R Ambedkar for preparation of a draft Constitution. The Constituent
Assembly was able to complete the historical task of drafting a Constitution for independent India
within a period of less than three years i.e. two years, eleven months and seventeen days.
Constituent Assembly produced a fine document, handwritten in 90,000 words. Constitution was
written in English and Hindi language. Total expenditure on constitution was ₹ 6.40 million. On the
26th day of November 1949,  it could proudly declare on behalf of the people of India that we do
hereby Adopt, Enact and Give to ourselves this constitution.

1.6          THE CONSTITUTION OF INDIA

Thus, the constitution of India repealed the Indian Independence Act, 1947 and Government of India
Act, 1935 when it became effective on 26 January,1950. India ceased to be a dominion of the British
Crown and became a sovereign democratic republic with the constitution.
A total of 284 members affixed their signatures to the final version. The Original Constitution
comprised of a Preamble, 395 Articles, and 8 Schedules. Immediate effect was given to provisions
concerning citizenship, elections, provisional Parliament, and temporary and transitional measures.
The remainder of the Constitution of India came into force on January 26, 1950. On this historic day,
the Constituent Assembly ceased to exist, transforming itself into the Provisional Parliament of India
until a new Parliament was constituted in 1952.
The Indian Constitution is one of the most comprehensive and lengthy constitutions in the world,
reflecting a balance between various ideologies, principles, and diverse cultural backgrounds
present in the country. It has played a pivotal role in shaping India's democratic governance and
continues to be the cornerstone of the nation's legal and political system.
Constitutional Law of India
It the supreme law of  India.  It Iays the framework defining fundamental  political  principles,
establishes the structure, procedures, powers and duties of government institutions and sets out
fundamental rights, directive principles and duties of the citizens.
The Constitution is said to be the pillar to Indian democracy. It is more than a collection of Articles
and Clauses. It is an ideal document of the society that we are and even the better society we are
endeavouring to be. The Constitution embodies the collective wisdom of the Founding Father of our
Republic and, in essence, it represents the sovereign will of the people of India.

     3.  PREAMBLE
Article 1 of the Constitution of India, 1950, prescribes the preamble of the Constitution. The
Preamble to the Constitution of India embodies and reflects the fundamental values, philosophy and
objectives on which the Constitution is based. "The Preamble is the most precious part of the
Constitution. It is the soul of the Constitution. It is the key to the Constitution... It is a jewel set in the
Constitution... It is a proper yardstick with which one can measure the worth of the Constitution."
                             PREAMBLE
WE, THE PEOPLE OF INDIA, having solemnly resolved to constitute India into a Sovereign Socialist
Secular Democratic Republic and to secure to all its citizens:
JUSTICE, social, economic and political;
LIBERTY of thought, expression, belief, faith and worship;
EQUALITY of status and of opportunity; and to promote among them all
FRATERNITY assuring the dignity of the individual and the unity and integrity of the Nation;
IN OUR CONSTITUENT ASSEMBLY this twenty-sixth day of November, 1949, do HEREBY ADOPT,
ENACT AND GIVE TO OURSELVES THIS CONSTITUTION.
Each phrase in the Preamble carries significant meaning:
1.    Sovereign: India is independent and free from external control.
2.     Socialist: The commitment to social and economic equality, seeking to reduce disparities
     among citizens
3.    Secular: The state maintains an equal distance from all religions and allows individuals to
      have the freedom to follow any religion.
4.    Democratic: Governance  is based on the participation of the people through elected
       representatives.

5.    Republic: The head of the state is elected, and there is no hereditary monarchy
The Preamble reflects the core values of justice, liberty, equality, and fraternity, which form the
bedrock of the Indian Constitution and represent the aspirations of the people of India for a just and

Constitutions all over the world generally have a preamble. The essence of the Constitution lies in
its Preamble, serving as the heart that imparts purpose, direction, and outlines the objectives. This
integral component reflects the Constitution's source, emphasizing that the Constitution should be
understood and interpreted in alignment with the substantial vision articulated in the Preamble.
Accordingly, the preamble is a legitimate aid in the interpretation of the Constitution.
The Preamble of the Indian Constitution has only been amended once, in 1976, by the 42nd
Constitutional Amendment Act. This amendment added the words "Socialist, Secular, and Integrity"
to the Preamble. The amendment also changed the description of India from "sovereign, democratic
republic" to "sovereign, socialist, secular, democratic republic. The 42nd Amendment also changed
the words "unity of the nation" to "unity and integrity of the nation".
Doctrine of Basic Structure
The concept of ‘basic structure’ came into existence in the landmark judgment in Kesavananda
Bharati vs State of Kerala case (1973) 50 years ago. In this case the Constitutional Bench of Hon’ble
Supreme Court ruled by 7-6 verdict that Parliament could amend any part of the Constitution so
long as it did not alter or amend the basic structure or essential features of the Constitution.
However, the court did not define the term ‘basic structure’, and only listed a few principles —
federalism, secularism, democracy — as being its part.
The ‘basic structure’ doctrine has since been interpreted to include
♦     the supremacy of the Constitution,
♦     the rule of law,
♦    Independence of the judiciary,
♦     doctrine of separation of powers,
♦    sovereign democratic republic,
♦     the parliamentary system of government,
♦     the principle of free and fair elections,
♦     welfare state, etc

     4.  OBJECTIVES OF THE CONSTITUTION OF INDIA
The following are the ideals and objectives of our Constitution as described in the Preamble.
Preamble is a preface which highlights the entire Constitution.
1.    Sovereignty: The initial phrase in the Preamble of the Constitution states that, "We the
People of India," declares globally that the supreme authority resides within the collective populace
of India. Therefore,  it suggests that the constitutional authorities and governmental organs derive
their authority solely from the people of India.
2.    Socialism: Several provisions within the Constitution support the principles of a Welfare
State, aiming to eliminate all forms of exploitation across various aspects of existence. According to
the constitutional vision, the State is obligated to actively follow the advancement of a social order
wherein justice—social, economic, and political—influences all facets of national life.
The distribution of the community's material resources is intended to serve the common good,
ensuring that the functioning of the economic system does not lead to the undue concentration of
wealth and means of production to the detriment of the public.
The Constitution has facilitated land reforms, supported the well-being of the working class, and
advocated for the social control of crucial natural resources and means of production for the benefit
of all segments of society. The core principle guiding our public policies has been the assurance of
a basic minimum for all.
3.    Secularism: India accommodates nearly all major world religions. In the Indian context, the
concept of secularism signifies that the State does not endorse any particular religion and ensures
equal protection to all religions. Our State is not influenced by any specific religious beliefs or
considerations. Aligned with the principles of 'belief, faith, and worship' outlined in the Preamble, the
Constitution prohibits discrimination based on religion. It grants every individual the right to freedom
of religion, encompassing the freedom of conscience, the liberty to profess, practice, and propagate
religion, the freedom to manage religious affairs, the right to contribute taxes without bias toward
any particular religion, and the freedom to participate in religious instruction or worship in certain
educational institutions. Additionally, the Constitution safeguards cultural and educational rights,
including the protection of minority interests and their right to establish and administer educational
institutions.

4.    Democracy: The term ‘democratic’ is used in the Preamble in the broader sense taking up
not only political democracy but also social and economic democracy. Also adopted parliamentary
democracy to ensure a responsible and stable Government which derives its authority from the
people and remains accountable to the people at all times.
Through a system of universal adult franchise, the citizens of India elect their governments at various
levels (Union, State, and local), with periodic elections ensuring ongoing approval from the people.
All citizens, without discrimination based on caste, creed, color, sex, religion, or education, enjoy
freedom of speech, thought, expression, and association.
Democracy promotes stability in society and  it secures peaceful change of governments.  It also
allows dissent and encourages tolerance and stands for a constitutional government, Rule of Law,
absolute rights of citizens, independence of the Judiciary, free and fair elections and freedom of the
Press.
5.     Republic: The word ‘Republic’ infers that the Head of the State in India shall be an elected
person and shall hold office for a fixed term. The President of India is the chief executive head of
the country.
6.     Justice: The term ‘justice' in the Preamble covers three distinct forms - social, economic and
political, which are secured through various provisions outlined in Fundamental Rights and Directive
Principles.
The framers of our Constitution acknowledged that political freedom alone would not automatically
resolve  the  deeply  rooted  socio-economic  challenges  within  society.  Consequently,  they
emphasized that the positive and constructive aspects of political freedom must play a crucial role
in establishing a new social order based on the principles of socioeconomic justice.
Our Constitution put an end to untouchability, prohibits exploitation of women, children and other
weaker sections, protects interests and  rights of minorities and  tribal people and advocates
affirmative action to raise the standard of the people oppressed over the ages. This ideal of a just
and egalitarian society remains one of the foremost commitments under our Constitution.
7.     Liberty: The Founding Fathers realized that the ideal of democracy was impossible without
the presence of rights which are essential for a free and civilized existence. The Preamble secures
to all citizens of India liberty of thought, expression, belief, faith and worship, as Fundamental Rights.
However it is subject to reasonable restrictions as specified under Article 19 of the Constitution.
8.     Equality: As a human being, every individual has a dignified self. To fully realize and enjoy
this dignity, all forms of inequality must be prohibited. Equality, a fundamental value crucial for
democracy and justice, is therefore emphasized in the Constitution. The Constitution ensures

equality of status and opportunity for every citizen, without any discrimination based on religion,
race, caste, sex, or place of birth. This is achieved by opening public places to all citizens, abolishing

9.     Fraternity: Fraternity stands for the spirit of common brotherhood. In the absence of that, a
plural society like India would stand divided. Therefore, to give meaning to the ideals of justice,
liberty and equality, the Constitution makes fraternity as one of the foremost objectives to be
achieved in a country like ours composed of many races, religions, languages and cultures. It serves

fraternity through single citizenship. Also, Article 51A of the Constitution makes  it a Fundamental

transcending religious, linguistic, regional or sectional diversities.
10.   Dignity of the Individual: Our Constitution recognizes that every citizen, regardless of
gender, possesses an equal right to development and advancement. To realize this objective, the
Constitution ensures the provision of equal fundamental rights to all individuals. Additionally,  it
mandates that the State, in accordance with the Directive Principles, formulate policies directed
towards ensuring both men and women have access to sufficient means of livelihood, receive equal
pay for equal work, and experience just and humane working conditions.
11.   Unity and Integrity: Our Constitution mandates that every Indian citizen has a duty to
preserve and uphold the unity and integrity of the nation. It places particular emphasis on the concept
of fraternity, seeing it as a force that develops unity among citizens.
Article 1 of the Constitution explicitly defines India as a 'Union of States,' clarifying that states do not
possess the right to split from the Union. This constitutional provision is designed to address
challenges to national integration, including regionalism, communalism, linguistic differences, caste
divisions, and tendencies toward secession.

     6.  CITIZENSHIP PART-II (ARTICLES 5- 11)
Part II of the Indian Constitution indeed deals with citizenship. The Constitution of India came into
effect on January 26, 1950, and at that time, certain classes of persons were deemed to be citizens.

explicitly grants this power to Parliament. The Part II of Indian Constitution came into effect from
26th November, 1949 i.e. the date when the constitution was adopted by the Constituent Assembly.

1955, provides the framework for the acquisition and termination of Indian citizenship after the
commencement of the Constitution. It outlines the conditions under which a person can become an

One of the most contentious issues in relation to Article 13 has been whether Constitutional

Constitution cannot be subjected to review under Article 13.

of Kerala where the Supreme Court upheld the validity of the 24th Amendment Act and held that any
fundamental right can be amended by the Parliament but the basic structure of the Constitution
cannot be altered.

1.     Equality before Law (Article 14): The State shall not deny to any person equality before the
law or the equal protection of the laws within the territory of India.
Article 14 is based on the principle of equality before the law and prohibits discrimination on various
grounds  It states that the State shall not deny to any person equality before the law or equal
protection of the laws within the territory of India. This fundamental right ensures that all individuals,
irrespective of their caste, race, religion, sex, or place of birth, are treated equally and fairly under
the law. Article 14 plays a crucial role in upholding the principles of justice, non-discrimination, and
rule of law in the country, fostering an inclusive and just society for all citizens.
In the case of Ram Krishna Dalmia v. Justice Tendolkar, the jurisprudence of equality before the law
is described by the Supreme Court. In this instance, the well-known “classification test” had been
administered. Simply put,  it permits the State to classify subjects differently (which is otherwise
prohibited by Article 14) as long as the classification is based on comprehensible differences (i.e.,
objects within the class are easily distinguishable from those outside) and has a rational connection
to the goal it seeks to pursue.
In the case of Maggan Lal Chaggan Lal v. Municipal Corporation of Greater Bombay gave the
reasonable classification test the clarification  it required. In this case, the court distinguished
between statutes that classify things on their own and those that provide the executive the authority
to classify them. If the statute in the first instance does not pass the reasonable categorization test,
it is invalid. In the latter scenario, only the executive action will be deemed illegal and not the statute
itself if the statute offers directions, whether explicitly stated or implicitly suggested, to the executive
to make classification.
In Shayara Bano v. UOI, the Triple Talaq Case, the Supreme Court ruled that the practise of
instantaneous triple talaq (Talaq-ul-biddat) was unlawful. The Bench noted that the equality of status
was a manifestation of the fundamental right to equality protected by Article 14 of the Constitution.

This means that the State cannot treat individuals or groups differently or unfairly based on these

In the case of State of Rajasthan v. Pratap Singh (1969), the Supreme Court dealt with the issue of

individuals who can serve society and the nation as a whole. The court held that it is in the larger
interest of society and the nation to admit the best candidates, irrespective of their residence.

emolument or office of any kind from or under any foreign State. Clauses (3) and (4) have been
added to ensure that a non-citizen should remain loyal to the State i.e. do not commit the breach of
trust reposed in him.
A ‘title’ is something that hangs to one’s name, as an appendage (either prefix or suffix e.g. Sir,

Article 22 of the Indian Constitution pertains to the protection of a person’s right to personal liberty
and safeguards against detention or arrest without proper legal procedure. This Article is considered
to be one of the fundamental rights guaranteed to citizens of India. This Article applies to both
citizens and non-citizens and protects individuals in cases of arrest and detention in certain cases
by providing certain important rights. The core object behind this clause is to ensure the personal
liberty of individuals is maintained by preventing arbitrary arrests and detention.
In the case of Joginder Kumar v. State of U.P. it was held that a detained person should know the
cause of his detention and is entitled to let any third person know the location of his detention.
The second right enables the arrested person/detenu to get a chance to be represented by a legal
practitioner of their choice. This right is available right from the moment a person has been arrested.
This enables the arrested person to get a fair trial. The Supreme Court in Hussainara Khatoon v.
State of Bihar has held that the right to a speedy trial is a constitutional right.
In the case of State of West Bengal v. Anwar Ali Sarkar, the Supreme Court held that the right to
legal representation was a fundamental right and that the state was obligated to provide legal aid to
a person who could not afford  it. This decision was significant in ensuring that the right to legal
representation was available to all, regardless of their financial means.
In the case of D.K. Basu v. State of West Bengal, the Supreme Court issued guidelines for the arrest
and detention of a person, including the requirement that the arresting officer must inform the person
of their right to legal representation and that a relative or friend of the person must be informed of
their arrest. This decision was significant in ensuring that the arrest and detention of a person was
conducted in a humane and fair manner.
In conclusion, Article 22 of the Indian Constitution is an essential component of individual rights and
protects against arbitrary arrest and detention. The provision for legal representation and the right
to be produced before a magistrate within a reasonable time ensures that the detained person is not
left in the dark and has access to legal assistance. The provision for restriction on preventive
detention ensures that a person is not kept in custody indefinitely and that preventive detention is
not misused by the government. The various court decisions have helped to clarify and strengthen
the protection of individual rights under Article 22.

provisions that guarantee the rights of an individual citizen (not related to the community to which

Types of Writs
There are five types of Writs as provided under Article 32 of the Constitution:
♦    Habeas Corpus: It is one of the important writs for personal liberty which says “You have
       the Body”. The main purpose of this writ is to seek relief from the unlawful detention of an
       individual. It is for the protection of the individual from being harmed by the administrative
      system and it is for safeguarding the freedom of the individual against arbitrary state action
      which violates Fundamental Rights under Articles 19, 21 & 22 of the Constitution. This writ
      provides immediate relief in case of unlawful detention.
       In the case of ADM Jabalpur v. Shivakant Shukla which is also known as the Habeas
      Corpus case, it was held that the writ of Habeas Corpus cannot be suspended even during
      an emergency (Article 359).
♦   Quo Warranto: Writ of Quo Warranto implies thereby “By what means”. This writ is invoked
        in cases of public offices and it is issued to restrain persons from acting in public office to
      which he is not entitled to. Appointment to public office can be challenged by any person
       irrespective of the fact whether his fundamental or any legal right has been infringed or not.
       In the case of Bharati Reddy v. The State of Karnataka (2018), the Hon’ble Supreme Court
      held that a writ of quo warranto cannot be issued based on assumptions, inferences, or
      speculations concerning the fact of accomplishment of qualifying conditions. There must
      be an establishment of the fact that a public officer is abusing lawful powers not vested to
      him within the public authority.
♦    Mandamus: Writ of Mandamus means “We Command” in Latin. This writ is issued for the
       correct performance of mandatory and purely ministerial duties and is issued by a superior
       court to a lower court or government officer. However, this writ cannot be issued against
       the President and the Governor. Its main purpose is to ensure that the powers or duties are
       not misused by the administration or the executive and are fulfilled duly. Also, it safeguards
       the public from the misuse of authority by administrative bodies. The mandamus is “neither
      a writ of course nor a writ of right but that it will be granted if the duty is in nature of public
      duty and it especially affects the right of an individual, provided there is no more appropriate

     9.  FUNDAMENTAL DUTIES PART-IVA (ARTICLES 51A)
The fundamental duties which were added by the 42nd Amendment Act of the Constitution in 1976,
in addition to creating and promoting culture, also strengthen the hands of the legislature in enforcing
these duties vis-a-vis the fundamental rights. It is important to understand that no democratic system
could ever succeed  if the citizens are not willing to participate actively by discharging their duties
which are expected to be done by them.
In Chandra Bhavan Boarding and Lodging Bangalore v. State of Mysore and Another, the Supreme
Court held that our Constitution can't protect all the rights of citizens without assigning certain duties
to its citizens. However, this decision was taken before the insertion of fundamental duties in the
Indian Constitution. It clearly explains the need for fundamental duties for making a welfare society.
Swaran Singh’s Committee in 1976 recommended more than 10 Fundamental Duties, however, not
all were included in the Constitution.
Article 51A. Fundamental duties—
It shall be the duty of every citizen of India—
(a)    to abide by the Constitution and respect its ideals and institutions, the National Flag and
       the National Anthem;
(b)    to cherish and follow the noble ideals which inspired our national struggle for freedom;
(c)    to uphold and protect the sovereignty, unity and integrity of India;
(d)    to defend the country and render national service when called upon to do so;
(e)    to promote harmony and the spirit of common brotherhood amongst all the people of India
      transcending religious, linguistic and regional or sectional diversities; to renounce practices
      derogatory to the dignity of women;
(f)      to value and preserve the rich heritage of our composite culture;

1.    They remind Indian citizen of their duty towards their society fellow citizens and the nation.

3.    They inspire citizens and promotes a sense of discipline & commitment among them.
4.    They help the court in examining and determining the constitutional validity of law.
