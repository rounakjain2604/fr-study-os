     1.  EVOLUTION OF LEGAL SYSTEM IN INDIA
Legal System in Ancient India
India's legal history is the oldest in the world which can be traced back to the Neolithic age, consisting
of the civil and criminal adjudication process, which followed till the Indus Valley Civilization, but the
main evidence of India's historical legal system and heritage can be traced from the Vedic period,
where the main idea of justice and law is given by the idea of Dharma as illustrated by various Hindu
texts like Puranas and Smritis.
Dharma is generally mean principle of righteousness or duty, principle of holiness and also the
principle of unity. Yudhishthira says in his instructions to Bhishma that whatever creates conflict is
Adharma, and whatever puts an end to conflict and brings about unity and harmony is Dharma.

According to Brihaspati Smriti, there was a hierarchy of courts in Ancient India starting from the
family courts and ending with the King. The lowest was the family arbitrator. The next higher court
was that of the judge, the next of the Chief Justice who was called Praadivivaka, or adhyaksha; and
at the top was the Kings court.
There was also a concept of popular courts which was very similar to the present panchayat system
in India and the government and King had not to control  it. As the king was responsible for his
subjects and has to protect them. Disputes in these courts were decided on the graveness of the
situation and decisions were also overturned by the higher court of authority, as existed in the
present system of India. Therefore the present structure has some resemblance in the order of
hierarchy to the ancient one.
In the administration of justice, King was given paramount importance and he/she was expected to
follow  it with due diligence and integrity and there were learned and fearless judges to guide the
king according to the prevalent rule of law at that time. But with the progress in time, the king had to
perform several other tasks and had to bear many other responsibilities due to which special judges
were selected for the task of administration of justice, which can be illustrated from the reign of
Mauryan Empire where a proper judicial system existed.
The integrity and the transparency in the judicial system which existed at that time was unparalleled
with strict adherence to rules and regulations, which Brihaspati described in ancient Hindu texts as
a Judge should decide cases without any personal bias or without any expectation of profit in
accordance with prescribed regulations. One of the most striking feature of ancient or vedic judicial
system was the Jury System or sabhyas, where councilors or jurors act as the advisers of the king
or judge, which is similar to the Jury System in India which existed after the Independence, but was
later abolished after the famous KM Nanavati vs State of Maharashtra case.
The jury system at that time used caste as the basis for determining jurors or abayas for different
cases. For Example, any important question of law or shastra to be determined was given to a
Brahmin juror and if there was a dispute between two communities, the jurors had to be from that
communities to decide the judgment. Thus, we can see that a caste-based judicial system existed
in Ancient India.
The nature of the trial in those days was inquisitorial where the judges instead of lawyers were

Legal System in Medieval India
During the Medieval period in India, the Mitakshara school of law was the most prevalent law
compiled by a Chalukya ruler in the 11th century, which was a very definite interpretation of the law.
Today this law has become the basis of Hindu Joint Family laws. Then Islam came into India during
the 11th century when Mohammed Ghori defeated Prithviraj Chauhan at the Second Battle of Tarain
in 1192 AD, with which Qutub ud-din Aibak became the first Sultan of Delhi, belonging to the Slave
Dynasty.
During the Islamic era in India, the Hanafi school of law was predominantly administered and
practiced. This school of thought did not mandate conversion to Islam for non-Muslims. However, it
required non-Muslims to pay a tax called Jizya. For Muslims, the Quran is considered the supreme
and the only book containing the words of Allah.
The Islamic rule in India under the title Delhi Sultanate ruled from Slave Dynasty to Lodhi Dynasty
(1206 A.D. -1526 A.D.). After that Babur defeated Ibrahim Lodhi which started the rule of the Mughal
Empire effectively up to A.D. 1707 and then after the death of Bahadurshah Zafar, the Muslim rule
in India came to an end.
During the Sultanate period, there were seven courts of Justice, related to seven different branches
of law. For Example- Diwan-i-Mazlim deals  with disputes concerning  with administration  or
bureaucracy. The Qazi did not come under any judicial supervision and they deal with religious laws.
Non-Muslims had their autonomy to practice their personal laws and to have separate courts. Thus,
the judicial system during the Sultanate period was not properly organized.
Legal System in India before Independence - The Colonial Period
India has one of the oldest legal systems in the world with its laws and jurisprudence dating back
centuries and evolving like a living way of life with the people of India adapting to the changing times.
It is a misconception that the Indian legal system gained a systematic shape and development only
during British rule rather we have a traditional mindset of following laws and regulations and doing
our duties for the society which can be inferred from the verses of Manu smriti that Dharma i.e., Rule
of Law is the supreme power in the state and the King is also subject to Law to realize the goal of
Dharma The common law system, which is based on recorded judicial precedents, was introduced
in  India with the arrival of the British East India Company. Before the arrival of British East India
Company, the Mughal emperor set up a separate justice department called Mahakuma-e Adalat to
govern and then see the proper administration of justice within the empire.

Establishment of Mayor's Courts: King George-I granted a charter to the East India Company,
enabling the establishment of Mayor's Courts in Madras, Bombay, and Calcutta (now known as
Chennai, Mumbai, and Kolkata). These courts had authority over both civil and criminal cases but
derived their power from the Company. There were quite important distinguishing feature between
the Company’s Mayer’s Court and the Crown’s Mayor’s Courts established under the Charter of
1726. While the mayor's courts established under the Charter of 1726 were given jurisdiction in civil
matters including testamentary and probate of wills jurisdiction, Criminal matters were left to be
decided within the jurisdiction of, Governor-in-Council which acted as a court in such matters.
The Regulating Act, 1773: Subsequently after the British victory in the Battle of Plassey in 1757,
the British Parliament passed the Regulating Act of 1773. This act led to the establishment of a
Supreme Court in Calcutta.
Transfer of Power: After the First War of Independence, the administration and control of India was
transferred from the East India Company to the British Crown. This transition was marked by the
enactment of important acts such as the High Courts Act, 1861 and the Indian Council Act, 1861.
These Acts led to the establishment of High courts in Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras. The Supreme
Courts were abolished, and the Privy Council became the highest court of appeal for nearly two
centuries.

The Government of India Act, 1935: This particular act further transformed the legal structure in
India. It established a federal structure, with a new Federal Court in Delhi having jurisdiction over
both states and provinces. The Federal Court possessed both appellate and advisory jurisdiction
and could interpret substantive questions of law. It was to have 1 Chief Justice and not more than 6
judges.
Commencement of Legal Codification: The British Parliament's Charter Act of 1833 established
a Law Commission to codify and consolidate Indian laws. Codification is the systematic reduction of
legal principles or matter into codes of legislation. The codification of law made the law uniform
throughout the country and fostered a kind of legal unity of the country in fundamental laws The
process of codifying laws began in earnest with the formation of the first Law Commission. Thomas
Babington Macaulay, as the commission's chairman, played a pivotal role in drafting, enacting, and
implementing significant legal codes. The Indian Penal Code was one such code, which became
effective in 1862. Additionally, statutes and codes like the Code of Criminal Procedure, the Evidence
Act, 1872, and the Indian Contract Act, 1872 were introduced during this period.
This transformation of the legal system during the Colonial Period laid the groundwork for the modern
legal framework  in India.  It introduced common law principles, established important  judicial
institutions, and codified key laws that continue to shape the Indian legal system today.

Dedication to Legal Foundations: The framers of the Indian Constitution recognized the profound
connection between law and society. This understanding drove their tireless efforts to create a
Constitution of unprecedented scope and length. The Constituent Assembly, representing diverse
sections of society, undertook this monumental task, with Dr. B.R. Ambedkar heading the drafting
committee and Dr. Rajendra Prasad serving as the President of the Constituent Assembly.
The Birth of a Republic: After nearly three years of dedicated work, the Constitution of India came
into effect on January 26, 1950, marking the birth of the Republic of India.
Supreme Legal Authority: The Constitution of India serves as the supreme legal authority, guiding
the formulation of legislations and Acts throughout the country. Any law that contradicts the
provisions of the constitution or the basic structure of the constitution is considered unconstitutional.
Retained British- Era Laws: Despite formulating its own constitution, India retained numerous laws
and codes from the British colonial era, such as the Indian Contract Act, 1872, the Indian Penal
Code, 1860, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, the Sales of Goods Act, 1930, the Partnership Act,
1932, the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, Transfer of Property Act, 1882 and the Law of Evidence
Act, 1872 to name few, among others.
Comprehensive and Meticulously Designed: The Constitution of India  is all-encompassing,
touching upon matters of  all  its three branches, the executive, legislative, and judiciary.    It is
designed to be sensitive to the diverse needs and rights of its citizens.
A shift towards social welfare: The Constitution of India represents a transformative shift from the
colonial system, which primarily served imperial interests. It is firmly directed toward social welfare
and empowers the most vulnerable members of society through fundamental rights which the people
of India have given to themselves. The Constitution of India itself provide for a socialist approach of
governance in its preamble.
Common Law System and Social Justice
India's legal system, rooted in the common law tradition, has evolved to embrace a social justice
paradigm through judicial rulings and legislative actions. This shift mirrors similar changes in other
common law jurisdictions which reflects the convergence of common law principles with the pursuit
of social justice in India’s legal system.
Evolution of the Indian Legal System:
From its colonial origins, the present Indian legal system has evolved to become an integral part of
the world's largest democracy, playing a vital role in securing constitutional rights for every citizen
& ensuring social justice.

Council of States (Upper House): Rajya Sabha, also known as the Council of States, is the upper
house of India's bicameral legislature. It is the second chamber of parliament and represents the
states and union  territories of India. This chamber's primary membership  is elected by state
legislatures. It plays a significant role in representing the states' interests at the national level. The
Rajya Sabha is designed to check the power of the Lok Sabha, the legislature's lower house. It has
powers to protect the interests of the states and union territories if the Centre interferes in their work.
The Rajya Sabha is a permanent house and is not subject to dissolution. However, nearly one-third
of its members retire every second year.

House of People (Lower House): The Lok Sabha, also known as the House of the People, is the
lower house of India's Parliament. The Lok Sabha is part of a bicameral Parliament, with the upper
house being the Rajya Sabha. Members of the House of People are directly elected by the citizens
of India during general elections. This chamber ensures that the people's voices are heard and that
their chosen representatives deliberate on critical national matters. The members serve a five-year
term, after which the Assembly and the members dissolve to have a fresh election.

India's democratic framework, with its unique blend of federalism and representative governance, is
exemplified by this multifaceted political structure.

     3.  JUDICIAL HIERARCHY  IN  INDIA: UPHOLDING THE
      RULE OF LAW
The judiciary  is that branch  of the government that interprets the law, settles disputes and
administers justice to all citizens. The judiciary is considered the watchdog of democracy, and also
the guardian of the Constitution. For democracy to function effectively, it is imperative to have an
impartial and independent judiciary.
India has a single integrated judicial system. The judiciary in India has a pyramidal structure with
the Supreme Court (SC) at the top. High Courts are below the SC, and below them are the district
and subordinate courts. The lower courts function under the direct superintendence of the higher
courts.

Guardians of Rights
The Constitution bestows upon the judiciary a unique and vital role in India. The writ jurisdiction,
enshrined in Articles 32 and 226, with Supreme Court & High Court of the state respectively serves
as a powerful recourse whenever fundamental rights are under threat or infringement.
Some basic differences between Article 32 and Article 226
•      Article 32 constitutes a fundamental right, while Article 226 is a constitutional right.
•     Under Article 32, the Supreme Court of India has exclusive jurisdiction, whereas under Article
      226, the jurisdiction is vested with the High Court of the respective state.
•      Article 32 can be suspended during a declared emergency by the President, whereas Article
      226 remains unsuspended even in emergency situations.
•    The scope of Article 32 is limited to the violation of fundamental rights, while Article 226
      extends its reach to violations of both fundamental and legal rights.
•      Article 32 is a Fundamental Right, it cannot be dismissed by the Supreme Court. On the other
      hand, Article 226 grants discretionary power to the High Court, providing it with the authority
       to decide whether or not to issue a writ.
Article 141 mandates that decisions rendered by the Supreme Court carry binding authority across
all Indian courts in the like-wise matters.
Beyond overseeing the actions of the executive and legislative branches, the Supreme Court and
High Courts are entrusted with safeguarding citizens' rights through their writ jurisdiction. This
mechanism affords a swift and cost-effective means for individuals to protect their constitutionally
guaranteed rights.

     4.  ELIGIBILITY TO FILE WRIT PETITION

Any Indian citizen can initiate a writ petition when their fundamental rights are violated. This legal
document is issued by a court to instruct a person to either carry out a specific action or refrain from
a particular activity.

To file a Writ Petition in India, the aggrieved party must approach a court with writ jurisdiction and
present their case. This right is available to all citizens. While there is no strict time limit for filing
Writs in India, there should be a compelling justification for any delay.

   Mandamus

•   Administration of justice: The chief function of the judiciary is to apply the law to specific
    cases or in settling disputes. When a dispute is brought before the courts  it ‘determines the
     facts’ involved through evidence presented by the contestants. The law then proceeds to decide
    what law is applicable to the case and applies it. If someone is found guilty of violating the law
     in the course of the trial, the court will impose a penalty on the guilty person.

    unconstitutional or null and void by the judiciary. This is called ‘judicial review.’ Judicial review
    has the merit of guaranteeing the fundamental rights of individuals and ensuring a balance
    between the union and the units in a federal state.
•   Protector of Fundamental Rights: The judiciary ensures that people’s rights are not trampled
   upon by the State or any other agency. The superior courts enforce Fundamental Rights by
    issuing writs.
•   Supervisory functions: The higher courts also perform the function  of supervising the
    subordinate courts in India.
•   Advisory functions: The Supreme Court in India performs an advisory function as well. It can
    give its advisory opinions on constitutional questions. This is done in the absence of disputes
   and when the executive so desires.
•   Administrative functions: Some functions of the courts are non-judicial or administrative in
    nature. The courts may grant certain licenses, administer the estates (property) of deceased
    persons and appoint receivers. They register marriages, appoint guardians of minor children
   and lunatics.
•   Special role in a federation: In a federal system like India’s, the judiciary also performs the
    important task of settling disputes between the centre and states. It also acts as an arbiter of
    disputes between states.
•  Conducting judicial enquiries: Judges normally are called to head commissions that enquire
     into cases of errors or omissions on the part of public servants.

     5.  LEGAL PROCEDURES AND STATUTES: GOVERNING
      THE COURTS
Most proceedings  in the Supreme Court and High Courts operate under self-made Rules of
Procedure, empowered by statutory provisions. Furthermore, the Civil and Criminal Procedure
Codes and the Evidence Act apply to judicial proceedings in these courts, ensuring a structured and
just legal process. The legal procedure in India involves a systematic and structured process for the
resolution of disputes and the administration of justice.  It encompasses various stages and is
governed by laws, rules, and regulations. Here is a detailed overview of the legal procedure in India
Civil Procedure
•      Filing of Plaint/Petition: The legal process begins with the filing of a plaint (in civil cases) or
      a petition (in writ and certain other cases) outlining the facts and legal grounds for the claim.
•     Service of Summons: The court issues summonses to the defendant, informing them of the
       legal proceedings.
•      Written Statements: The defendant responds with a written statement, admitting or denying
       the allegations in the plaint.
•     Issues Framing: The court frames the issues to be decided based on the pleadings of the
       parties.
•     Evidence: Both parties present evidence through witnesses, documents, and other means.
•     Arguments: After the evidence is presented, the parties make their legal arguments.
•     Judgment: The court delivers a judgment based on the evidence, arguments, and applicable
       law.
Criminal Procedure
•     FIR (First Information Report): In criminal cases, the process often starts with the filing of an
      FIR with the police.
•      Investigation: The police investigate the case, collect evidence, and may make arrests.
•     Framing of Charges: The court frames charges against the accused based on the evidence.
•       Trial: The prosecution and defense present their cases, and witnesses are examined and
      cross-examined.
•      Verdict: The court delivers a verdict of guilt or innocence.

     6.  ALTERNATIVE DISPUTE RESOLUTION (ADR)
In addition to litigation, India encourages alternative dispute resolution methods such as mediation,
arbitration, and conciliation.
ADR offers a quicker and more informal way to resolve disputes outside of the traditional court
system.
Execution of Decrees:
Once a court issues a decree or order, the successful party may need to  initiate execution
proceedings to enforce the judgment.
Legal Aid and Services:
The legal system in India also includes provisions for legal aid and services to ensure access to
justice for all, especially those who cannot afford legal representation.
In summary, the legal procedure in India involves a structured process from the initiation of a case
through trial, appeal, and, if necessary, alternative dispute resolution methods. The system aims to
ensure justice, fairness, and the protection of rights.
India's judiciary plays a vital role in upholding the rule of law, offering accessible and impartial justice
to its diverse citizenry.

      THE INDIAN CONSTITUTION
In the history of Indian legal principles since enactment of Indian Constitution, numerous pivotal
court judgments have played a crucial role in delineating the connection between Parliament and
the Constitution. These landmark cases have established significant precedents in upholding the
sanctity of India's constitutional framework.

    Shankari Prasad vs Union of India (1951): Parliament's Amendment Authority
       In this landmark case, the court asserted that Parliament possesses the authority to amend
       the constitution. This judgment decisively dismissed the assertion that amendments couldn't
        curtail fundamental liberties, establishing a significant precedent in the early stages.
    Sajjan Singh vs State of Rajasthan (1965): Challenges to Article 368 Amendments
       This  leading  matter  dealt  with  the  Constitutional  validity  of  the  17th  Constitutional
     Amendment. The Supreme Court upholding the validity of the 17th Amendment held that the
      Parliament has the authority to amend any part of the Constitution including any Fundamental
       Rights. The ruling in this particular case clarified that objections to amendments carried out
      under  Article 368 were deemed without  merit.  It underscored the robustness  of the
     amendment process inherent in the Indian Constitution, emphasizing its enduring strength.
    The Golaknath Case (1967): Safeguarding Fundamental Safeguards
       In a significant development, the court ruled that Article 368's amendment power should not
      be used to undermine or eliminate fundamental safeguards. The court ruled that Parliament
      could not  limit any of the Constitution's Fundamental Rights. This case reinforced the
      importance of protecting the core principles of the Constitution.
    Kesavananda Bharati vs State of Kerala (1973): Limiting Parliament's Amending Authority
       This case, was a landmark decision of the Supreme Court of India that established the basic
       structure doctrine of the constitution. This case set an influential precedent, issued a ground-
      breaking judgment affirming  that Parliament cannot employ  its amending  authority  to
      undermine the fundamental framework of the Constitution.  It marked a pivotal moment in
       preserving the foundational principles of the Constitution.
    Minerva Mills Ltd. vs Union of India (1980): Upholding the Constitutional Structure
     The  court's  decision  in  this case was  instrumental  in  preserving  the  Constitution's
      fundamental  structure. By  invalidating  certain amendments, the court underscored  its
      commitment to safeguarding the constitutional integrity of India.
      These landmark  judicial decisions have collectively contributed to shaping the delicate
      balance between constitutional amendments and the enduring principles of the Indian
       Constitution. They stand as pillars of jurisprudential wisdom in India's legal landscape.
