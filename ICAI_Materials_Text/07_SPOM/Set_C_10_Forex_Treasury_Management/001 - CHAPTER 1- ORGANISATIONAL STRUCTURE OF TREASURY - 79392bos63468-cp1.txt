     1.  TREASURY – ITS INTRODUCTION, EVOLUTION AND
       OBJECTIVES
1.1  Introduction and Evolution of the Treasury Function
In the early stages, the treasury function was undertaken by banks, which managed the inflow and
outflow  of cash. As economies grew complex,  the  regulators  rolled out guidelines  for the
management of funds. The role of the treasury in banks was then enhanced but still restricted to
dealing in permissible securities and managing the funds flow.
Over a period, the role of the treasury in Indian banks has evolved from just being the cash manager
to the risk manager. The departments have now assumed a strategic role and undertake profit-
making activities within the stipulated risk framework to reduce the cost of funds.
A bank treasury can be segregated into:
♦    Money market desk
♦    Forex desk
♦     Capital market desk
♦    Asset liability management desk
♦     Risk management desk
♦     Transfer pricing desk
In large and mid-sized corporations, the corporate treasury has grown enormously due to the drive
to save costs. You could think of a corporate treasury department as an internal answer for the
business house's money management. The corporate treasury handles tasks for the company
including cash management, preparing for liquidity, obtaining funding, and risk management.
One reason for the rise in corporate treasuries in India is the erratic and tumultuous state of the
world economy. Businesses today strive to make risk-free profits while also protecting wealth.
Consider the company treasury as an internal bank that serves several organizational departments.
For banks, however, the scope of operations is far greater. Corporate treasurers face constant
challenges, including exposure recognition, cash visibility, and the lack of a long-term treasury plan.
The ultimate objective of treasury management, also known as treasury operations, is to maximize
an organization's liquidity and minimize its risk to its finances, operations, and reputation. The
actions of a company's funding, concentration, investments, and disbursements are all included in
Treasury Management. It might also involve trading in currencies, bonds, and financial derivatives,
along with the related financial risk management, in larger businesses.

Most banks have entire divisions dedicated to handling treasury and meeting the demands of their
customers. Large banks dominated the market for treasury management goods and services until
recently.
However, due to the favorable economic environment (banks of all sizes concentrating on the clients
they serve best), the availability of highly experienced treasury management professionals, access
to industry-standard third-party technology providers' products and services that are tier-arranged
to meet the needs of smaller clients, and investments in best practices and education, smaller banks
are progressively initiating and/or expanding their treasury management functions and offerings.
Although  the  terms  cash  management  and  treasury  management  are  sometimes  used
synonymously for non-banking businesses, treasury management covers a wider range of activities,
including the funding and investment activities previously described. The CFO, Vice-President  /
Director of Finance, or Treasurer typically oversees a firm's treasury operations, which are managed
daily by the treasury staff, controller, or comptroller of the corporation.
Some departments that Bank Treasuries may have are as follows:
♦   A money market or fixed income desk that is solely responsible for purchasing and disposing
       of securities bearing interest.
♦   A desk for buying and selling foreign currencies, sometimes known as a "FX" desk.
♦    An equity or capital markets desk that deals in shares that are listed on the stock exchange.
♦   A commodity Desk to deal in commodities like crude, gold, and metals.
A "Proprietary Trading" desk that handles trading for the bank's capital and account, an Asset
Liability Management (ALM) desk that controls liquidity and interest rate mismatch risk, and a
Transfer Pricing or Pooling function that sets prices for liquidity business lines (Liability & Asset
sales teams) within the bank are other possible additions to the Treasury function.
Treasury management is becoming more and more important as companies bounce back from the
global financial crisis. A significant component of the financial management of many corporations
has always been treasury management. It guarantees that the company has enough liquidity to meet
both anticipated and unforeseen financial obligations, as well as that it is effectively tracking its daily
sales and payments.
The upcoming years will see several significant regulatory changes that will fundamentally alter the
work that treasury managers do. The use and perceived importance of Treasury Management
Systems (TMS) will be impacted by these regulations, particularly Basel III of Banks.

    3.   FUNCTIONS OF TREASURY DEPARTMENT
Managing a company's cash flow is the treasury department's main responsibility. To make sure that
there is enough money to support business operations and that any excess cash is invested wisely,
it is necessary to keep a close eye on both current and projected cash inflows and outflows. While
accomplishing this mission, the treasurer must exercise considerable prudence to ensure that
existing assets are safeguarded using safe forms of investment and hedging activities.
3.1  Centralisation and Decentralisation of Treasury
Today, every cash manager must look after not only to keeping things running smoothly but also
efficiently. Companies around the globe are looking for ways to streamline their operations and pull-
out cash tied up because of business process inefficiencies. With the advent of global business,
corporates need to decide whether to position the treasury as a centralised department or a
decentralised department.

In a centralised set-up, the key decisions and functions shall be taken by a single team on behalf of
all the group companies located elsewhere. In such cases, risk management and fund management
for the entire group is handled centrally. In contrast to this set-up, the decentralised set-up vests the
decision-making power in the smaller unit, which may be located geographically apart, and cash
management  is done  locally. Only reporting could be centralised  for the purpose  of group
consolidation.
Although a  fully centralised environment may appear  to be the most suitable structure  for
multinational corporations, this may not be the case. Many companies end up with a hybrid or
decentralised arrangement, simply because it is impractical to operate in any other way. The better
idea is to have a hybrid state of management and reap the benefits of both setups.
In larger firms, the treasury is usually centralised at the head office and provides services to all parts
of the business. This allows  it to achieve economies of scale for example, by obtaining better
borrowing rates. The financial control function is often delegated to individual units, where  it can
respond more  easily  to customers and  suppliers and  relate more  specifically  to the  units’
competitors. As a result, the treasury and financial control tend to be separated by location as well
as responsibility.
3.2  Details of Treasury Functions
The Treasury Department needs to do the following to fulfill its mission:
♦    Forecasting of cash. Gather data from all over the business to produce a continuous cash
       forecast. The budget, capital budget, board minutes (about dividend payments), accounting
       records, and even the CEO (about acquisition-related expenses) may  all provide  this
       information.
♦    Monitoring working capital. Examine the company's working capital policies and simulate
     how they will affect cash flows. For instance, poor credit policy may leads to a higher cash-
      consuming investment in accounts receivable.
♦    Concentration of cash. Establish a mechanism to direct funds into a single investment
      account where they can be used most wisely. Cash sweeps and notional pooling may be used
        in this.
♦     Capital Outlays. To allocate excess cash to different investment types based on their rates
       of return and the speed at which they can be turned into cash, use the corporate investment
        policy.
♦    Grant Credit. Give consumers credit, which entails overseeing the guidelines by which credit
      terms are approved.

    4.  ROLE OF TREASURY MANAGER
(a)   Raising Capital
The treasury manager arranges funds for the unit. It is the duty of the treasury manager to ensure
required quantity of funds. The amount of money needed for the unit's daily operations is referred to
as the quantity. The company can obtain the quantity through internal generation or external loans.
The loan quantity is arranged in the form of working capital by treasury against the security of
inventory and trade receivables.
Again, the adequacy of funds must be determined carefully. For this purpose, the cash flows for the
relevant period must be accurately charted out.  In this instance, cash flows represent actual cash
flows because there may be a lag in the realization of the anticipated flows. Thus, actual cash flows
only must be considered while determining adequacy.
Further, while actual inflows should be ascertained, as regards outflows, a margin of contingency
should be maintained to take care of the uncertainties. Here, "cash" refers to both cash and bank

(b)   Managing Bank Relationships
A business only needs a select few relationship banks to buy its treasury products from. Clearly

Effective banking relationships revolve around the company's ability to take advantage of the banks'
preferred products and market positions for the benefit of both the banks and the company.
How many banking relationships?
A business that operates large operations in a territory needs at least one bank. This bank must be
able to provide effective international payments, short-term credit facilities, and current account
administration. Regarding the quantity of banking relationships, there is no set rule. A lot of treasury

significant acquisitions or sales generally comes from the company's merchant banks, whose
representatives have close ties to the organization's top executives.
Most banks that take part in significant financing argue that the returns from conventional bank

Consequently, they are always searching for ancillary business from a company to efficiently fund
the bank lending activities.
Since many companies do not have that volume of ancillary business, this is a real problem for many

relationship banks the chance to submit a bid for any pertinent treasury business.

(e)   Liquidity Management
Cash forecasts are generally made over three-time horizons: short, medium, and long term.
Short-term cash forecasts
Generally, these cover approximately 30 days. Short-term forecasts are usually used exclusively by
the treasury department to manage liquidity on a day-to-day basis. Their purpose is to aid decisions
on the management of short-term borrowings and deposits, to ensure that there are no idle balances
sitting in bank accounts and that shortages are found and financed cost effectively.
Short-term cash forecasts are generally prepared daily for the next five to ten days. Opening cleared
cash balances at banks are calculated and adjusted for anticipated daily cash receipts and cash
payments. This day-by-day forecast is ideally updated daily. The following days are often prepared

Medium-term cash forecast is generally prepared for financial management purposes by the various

forecasting of cash over the budget or current financial year and beyond.
Most companies will produce medium-term cash forecasts on a month by-month basis, which are

Medium-term cash forecasts are usually prepared on a receipts- and- payments basis, with
emphasis being placed on the key drivers in cash management.

(g)   Forex Management
Deployment of foreign currency resources needs to be managed in such a way that  it would not
create any excess position i.e., “Overbought” or “Oversold” and at the same time cash flow is not
affected. For Forex Cash Management, there are several funding options available, including
Currency Swaps, which involve lending in one currency and borrowing in another, and Foreign
Exchange, which involves converting one currency into another foreign currency or local currency
for deployment to maximize yield without taking on a position and impacting cash flow.
According to the guidelines set forth by the Foreign Exchange Dealers Association of India (FEDAI),
bank customers, including importers, exporters, and other clients, approach Authorized Dealers (AD)
to handle their foreign currency payments and receipts. They then request that AD provide these
services for immediate delivery, such as on cash or ready terms, as well as for delivery later or
during a future option period.  Risk of exposure arises for authorized dealers only on business
concluded with customers at firm rates and not on business for which indicative rates have been
given.
When an Authorized Dealer (AD) contracts with a customer to buy or sell foreign currency for Spot
or Forward delivery, the Authorized Dealer takes over the risk of change in exchange rate for the
foreign currency against the Rupee that might change after the time when the contract is made.
Next, to obtain the cover required to shield the bank against loss on such contracts, the AD looks to
interbank markets (both domestically and internationally).
When trading foreign currencies on the market, whether against the Rupee or other currencies, AD
also assumes a similar level of risk. When ADs have more spot assets than spot liabilities or vice
versa, they are  frequently subject  to  variations  in operating costs  related  to  funding. The
creditworthiness of counterparties poses a risk as well.
The risks are managed by ADs by maintaining surveillance over Net Position, maturity mismatching,
funding accounts, payments under documentary credits, export bills, overdue export bills, maturity
gaps, market conditions.

(h)   Risk Management
Every organization wants to operate without taking any risks. But the reality is that, despite their
best efforts, they run the risk of encountering unforeseen and unplanned threats. Detecting and
controlling these risks before they become expensive issues can be achieved most effectively by
implementing a risk management policy across the entire organization.
By incorporating such a policy into routine business operations, decision-makers are also better able
to comprehend and assess the broader effects of their actions, which leads to more informed
decision-making. A strong framework for (i) risk identification and assessment (ii) risk ranking (iii)
action planning (iv) assessment and review (v) compliance and (vi) feedback and improvement is
created by a well-crafted risk management policy.
Looking at risk management from the perspective of the corporate treasurer, the treasurer oversees
overseeing the  financial  risk concept  that was employed. A combination  of  regulatory and
compliance demands on businesses has led to a greater focus on risk management across the entire
organization.
The necessity of explaining a company's risk management procedures to different stakeholders is
growing, as is the understanding of the advantages that an active, corporate-wide risk management
program can have in accomplishing the organization's strategic goals and generating shareholder
value.
Given the rising risks that smaller listed companies face, an evaluation of their internal control
system is just as important for them as it is for larger ones. Reducing the likelihood that unanticipated
events will compromise company objectives requires effective risk management. It entails actively
managing those risk exposures that have the potential to impact every goal the organization is
attempting to accomplish.
Implementing and maintaining an efficient risk management system involves several steps,
including:
♦     identifying risks;
♦     ranking them;
♦    agreeing on control strategies and a risk management policy;
♦     acting;
♦     routinely monitoring;
♦    and routinely reporting and reviewing risk and control.

The procedure that the treasurer established for handling treasury-related risks is basically the same

Identifying risks
Risks are often classified into business, operational, financial and compliance risks.
♦    Business risks: These result from the company's decision to follow a certain course of action
      as well as from operating in a specific industry and region. The risks can include adopting the
       incorrect business plan, making poor or unsuccessful acquisitions, not being able to secure
       additional funding, competitive pressures on pricing and market share, political risks, or the
      downturn of a particular industry sector.
♦    Operations risks: These have to do with the different operational and administrative
      processes that the company employs to carry out  its strategy. They may include skills
       shortages, stock-out of raw materials, physical disasters, loss of physical assets, quality
      problems, loss of key contracts, or poor brand management.
♦     Financial risks: Financial risks can also comprise going concern problems, overtrading,
      misuse of financial resources and occurrence of fraud, misstatement risk relating to published
       financial information, unrecorded liabilities, and penetration of IT systems by hackers.
♦    Compliance risks: These result from the need to maintain adherence to the laws and rules
        that, if broken, could harm a business. These may include infractions of the Companies Act,
        listing  regulations, GST  issues,  tax  fines, hazards  to  one's  health and  safety, and
      environmental issues.
It's crucial to identify risks without choosing them from a generic list of possibilities. The hazards
must be unique to the company's circumstances and industry sector. It is also helpful to tie them to
the potential roadblocks that the crucial success factors—which support the accomplishment of the
organization's goals—will face.
Quantifying and ranking risks
The company's challenge lies in quantifying or measuring the identified risks, much like it does in
the measurement of financial risks related to treasury. While many treasury financial risks are related
to price movements in the market, making it possible to calculate the potential impact of unfavorable
price movements within given ranges, many of the identified enterprise risks cannot be measured in
this way.
As a result, most organizations rate these risks using:
♦    High impact, high probability of occurrence: Take prompt action.

♦    Accept them. Certain risks, like economic risks or volatility, might be part of the business's

♦    Transfer them. Usually, derivatives or insurance are used for this.
♦    Reduce or manage them by improving controls within existing processes; for instance,
      by strengthening production control strategies to lessen the possibility of raw material
       stockouts.
♦    Eliminate them, usually by implementing current tactics.
For instance, repositioning products and broadening the product line are two current strategies that
may be used to mitigate the risk of market share pressures.

    6.   INTEGRATED TREASURY - COST CENTRE AND PROFIT
      CENTRE
A comprehensive strategy for funding the balance sheet (a balance sheet loan or lending or funding
is that which is kept on the original lender’s books) and allocating capital to local, international, and
foreign exchange markets is known as integrated treasury. With this strategy, the bank can maximize
arbitrage opportunities and optimize asset-liability management.
In the past, a bank's forex dealing room handled dealings in foreign exchange that mostly resulted
from merchant transactions, or buying and selling forex to clients, and as a result, covered activities
in the interbank market. The domestic investment and treasury operations were separate from a
bank's foreign exchange activities. Treasury operations were viewed as a cost center dedicated to
funds management and reserve management (SLR and CRR).  Additionally, the Treasury invested
in both government and non-government securities.
The deregulation of interest rates, the liberalization of exchange controls, the growth of the foreign
exchange market, the introduction of derivative products, and the advancement of technology in
settlement systems and dealing environments have all contributed to the need for the integration of
foreign exchange dealings and domestic treasury operations.   In addition to carrying out the
conventional duties of treasury unit and forex dealing room, the integrated treasury also handles a
variety of other tasks.

One significant profit center is an integrated treasury. It ought to measure profit and loss differently.

6.2  Benefits of Integration

Enhancing portfolio profitability, risk mitigation, and maximizing the synergy between banking and
trading assets are the main goals of integration. Trading assets are held primarily to generate profits
on short-term differences in prices  / yields, whereas banking assets are held primarily for client
relationships, steady income, and statutory obligations and are typically held until maturity. The goal
is accomplished through effective use of funds, economical sourcing of liability, appropriate transfer
pricing, taking advantage of arbitrage opportunities, online and offline information exchange between
money and forex dealers, providing customers with a single point of contact, efficient MIS, enhanced
internal control, reduced risk, and improved regulatory compliance.

An integrated treasury serves as the hub for hedging and arbitrage operations. To continue being a
proactive profit center,  it aims to optimize its currency portfolio and allow free money transfers
between currencies.  It will be possible for banks with integrated treasury to structure multi-currency
balance sheets and benefit from strategic positioning with the gradual liberalization of capital
account convertibility.
