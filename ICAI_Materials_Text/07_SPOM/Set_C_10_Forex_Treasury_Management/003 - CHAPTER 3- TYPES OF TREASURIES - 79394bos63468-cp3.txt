     2.  BACKGROUND
A key position in the modern corporation's finance is held by the treasury department. Treasury
management is very dynamic and includes Cash, Risk, Investment and Debt management besides
day-to-day procedural parts. Further rules and regulations of various statutory bodies like SEBI, RBI,
FEMA etc. are also to be complied with by the treasury department.
It assumes accountability for the company's  liquidity and guarantees that there  is always an
adequate amount of cash on hand to cover the demands of the company's main business operations.
It seems simple. Correct? Is it, though? It's not.
To really meet the goal, a treasury department would need to do the following roles over time:
2.1.  Liquidity Management
The ability of a security or asset to be swiftly purchased or sold on the market without depressing
the asset's price is known as liquidity.
The degree to which assets can be purchased and sold at steady prices in a market—like the stock
market of a nation or the real estate market of a city—is referred to as market liquidity. The most
liquid asset is cash; collectibles, fine art, and real estate are all comparatively illiquid. Accounting
liquidity gauges how easily a person or business can use their liquid assets to satisfy their financial
obligations. Accounting liquidity is expressed by multiple ratios.
Because it can be changed into other assets the quickest and easiest, cash is regarded as the gold
standard for liquidity. Cash is the asset that can be used to buy a $1,000 refrigerator, the easiest for
an individual to desire. That person is unlikely to find someone who is willing to trade the refrigerator
for their $1,000 rare book collection if they have no cash, but the collection is valuable. Rather, they
will have to sell the collection and pay for the refrigerator with the proceeds. That might not be an
issue  if the buyer has several days to make the purchase, but  it might be  if they cannot wait for
months or years.
2.1.1 Types of Liquidity
(i)    Market Liquidity
Refers to the ease with which an asset can be bought or sold in the market without having a
significant impact on its price. In the aforementioned example, the market for refrigerators in return
for rare books is so illiquid as to be considered non-existent. Higher market liquidity, however, is
what distinguishes the stock market. The price a buyer offers per share (the bid price) and the price
a seller is willing to accept (the ask price) will be relatively close to each other if an exchange has a
high volume of trade that is not dominated by selling. Consequently, investors won't have to forfeit

(ii)   Accounting Liquidity
Accounting liquidity is a measure of an entity's capacity to make payments on debts as they become
due, or to access funds when needed. This can apply to both individuals and businesses. The rare
book collector in the aforementioned example has relatively illiquid assets, so in an emergency, they
probably wouldn't be worth their full $1,000. Comparing liquid assets to current liabilities, or debts
that are due within a year, is the practical method of evaluating accounting liquidity. Accounting
liquidity is measured by several ratios, which vary in how narrowly they define "liquid assets."
Ratios to measure Accounting Liquidity
(a)    Current Ratio
The current ratio is the simplest and least strict ratio. Current assets are those that can reasonably
be converted to cash in one year.
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
(b)    Acid-Test or Quick Ratio
The acid-test or quick ratio is slightly stricter. It excludes inventories and other current assets, which
are not as liquid as cash and cash equivalents, accounts receivable and short-term investments.
Acid-Test Ratio = (Cash and Cash Equivalents + Short-Term Investments + Accounts
Receivable) / Current Liabilities
A variation of the acid-test ratio simply subtracts inventory from current assets, making it a bit more
generous than the version listed above:
Acid-Test Ratio = (Current Assets - Inventories) / Current Liabilities
(c)   Cash Ratio
Except for inventories and other current assets, the cash ratio is the most precise of the liquidity
ratios. It evaluates an organization's capacity to maintain solvent status in an emergency more so
than the current ratio or acid-test ratio. If they lack the liquidity to respond to unanticipated events,
even extremely profitable businesses may experience difficulties.

Depending on how one defines liquidity, there are two approaches to managing it in finance. Liquidity
can be defined as the capacity to exchange an asset at its current price, like a stock or bond. Large
organizations, like financial institutions, fall under the second definition of liquidity. The ability of
banks to meet their cash and collateral obligations without suffering significant losses is a key
criterion for evaluating them. Liquidity management pertains to the measures taken by investors or
managers to minimize their exposure to liquidity risk.
2.1.2 Liquidity Management in Business
Liquidity risk is assessed by managers, lenders, and investors utilizing liquidity measurement ratios
found in a company's financial statements. Comparing liquid assets and short-term liabilities is
typically how this is accomplished. Overleveraged companies need to take action to close the
difference between their cash on hand and their debt commitments.
Liquidity risk affects all governments and businesses with debt commitments, but major banks'
liquidity is particularly closely watched. Since these companies are deemed economically vital, they
are exposed to stringent regulations and stress tests to evaluate their liquidity management. In this
case, liquidity risk management evaluates the requirement for cash or collateral to satisfy financial
commitments using accounting methods.
2.1.3. Liquidity Management in Investing
Investors are concerned with a different kind of liquidity management, but they still use liquidity ratios
to assess a company's stocks or bonds. Stock market traders are not able to purchase or sell any
asset at any time; buyers and sellers are mutually dependent.
In most cases, a buyer must increase their bid to persuade a seller to part with the asset if they are
unable to find one at the asking price. On the other hand, sellers need to lower their asking prices
to attract buyers. Illiquid assets are those that cannot be traded at their current value.
Liquidity risk is mitigated by traders and investors by limiting the percentage of their portfolios held
in illiquid markets. Furthermore, liquid markets like the forex currency market are preferred by high
volume traders.

2.2.1 Types of Foreign Exchange Risks
(i)    Transaction risk
This is the gain or loss that results from a conversion and is associated with the possibility that the
exchange rate will fluctuate between the transaction date and the subsequent settlement date.
The main factors connected to this kind of risk are imports and exports. When a business exports
goods on credit,  it records a debtor figure in its accounting. The final amount  it will receive is
determined by the movement of foreign exchange between the transaction and settlement dates.
Most businesses decide to hedge against transaction risk since  it may influence their cash flow.
Typically, one of the most crucial aspects of treasury risk management is measuring and tracking
transaction risk.
The size of the transaction and the hedge period—the amount of time before the anticipated cash
flows occur—determine the extent of exposure. The expected fluctuations in exchange rates over
the course of the hedge period.
What level of exposure is acceptable should be specified in the corporate risk management policy.
Whether the Treasury Department has been set up as a cost center or a profit center will likely
determine this.
(ii)   Economic risk
While economic exposure includes both the longer-term effects of exchange rate fluctuations on a
company's market value, transaction exposure concentrates on the relatively short-term cash flow
effects. This essentially indicates that fluctuations in exchange rates will affect the present value of
future after-tax cash flows.
An organization can be exposed to economic risk in two different ways:
(a)    Directly:  If the value of your company's domestic currency increases, foreign rivals may
acquire market share at your expense by reducing the price of their products or lowering your profit
margins in the eyes of domestic and international consumers.
(b)    Indirectly: You might become less competitive even if the value of your home currency does
not change in relation to the currency of your customers. Let's take an example where a South
African company is selling to Hong Kong and its primary rival is a company based in New Zealand.
A decline in the New Zealand dollar's value relative to the Hong Kong dollar would mean a reduction
in the South African company's competitive edge.
Economic risk is hard to measure, but diversifying globally in terms of sales, where to locate
production facilities, where to get raw materials, and how to finance  it is a preferred method of

(iii)   Translation risk
To be combined into the financial statements of the group, the financial statements of foreign

Translation exposure is typically not hedged unless managers feel that a loss on the company's
translation exposure will cause the share price to drop. In an efficient market, the share price of the
company should only respond to exposure that is likely to affect cash flows.

2.2.2 Hedging transaction risk – the internal techniques
For financial reasons, internal strategies for managing or minimizing foreign exchange risk should
always take precedence over external ones.
Among the internal methods are the following:
(i)    Invoice in local currency
One simple method is to require that your business pays for all imports in your home currency and
that all foreign customers pay in your home currency.
But the exchange-rate risk has simply been transferred to the customer rather than disappearing.
While this is feasible in a monopoly situation,  it is an impractical strategy in a market where
competition is fierce.
(ii)   Leading and lagging
An importer (payment) may try to postpone payment if it anticipates a decline in the currency it is
expected to pay. This can be done through reaching a consensus or by going over credit limits.

2.3  Credit Exposures
It is the total amount of credit that a lender has given to a borrower. The amount of risk that the
lender bears if the borrower defaults is indicated by the credit exposure magnitude. By investing in
credit default swaps or other financial instruments, credit exposure can be reduced.
A bank will typically aim to have less exposure to clients with a lower credit rating and more exposure
to those with the highest credit rating. To lessen the risk of loss from a potential default, the bank
may try to decrease its credit exposure if a customer experience unforeseen financial difficulty.
Treasury faces less credit risk than market risk because most of its counterparties are banks, which
have credit ratings that are unquestionably higher than those of individuals and corporations.
Nonetheless, the treasuries will be equipped with procedures to take on credit risks while carrying
out their operations.

2.4  Debt Restructurings
Corporate Debt Restructuring (“CDR”) mechanism is a voluntary non-statutory mechanism under
which financial institutions and banks come together to restructure the debt of companies facing
financial difficulties due to internal or external factors, to provide timely support to such companies.
The intention behind the mechanism is to revive such companies and safeguard the interests of the
lending institutions and other stakeholders. The CDR mechanism is available to companies which
enjoy credit facilities from more than one lending institution. The mechanism allows such institutions
to restructure the debt in a speedy and transparent manner for the benefit of all.

The objects of the CDR mechanism, as enunciated by the Reserve Bank of India, the central
bank of the country, are-
•     “To aim at preserving viable corporates that are affected by certain internal and external
       factors”
•     “To minimize the losses to creditors and other stakeholders through an orderly and co-

•     “To ensure timely and transparent mechanism for restructuring of corporate debts of viable
       entities facing problems, for the benefit of all concerned.”
The CDR mechanism has a three-tier structure.
CDR standing Forum: The representative general body  of banks and  financial  institutions
participating in the CDR system.  It is a self-empowered body which lays down the policies and
guidelines, such as the time frame within which a unit shall become viable and the minimum level of
promoter contribution. It also monitors the progress of corporate debt restructuring. The Forum also
provides a platform for borrowers and creditors to amicably evolve policies for working out debt
restructuring plans in the interest of everyone.
The CDR Standing Forum comprises of Chairman and Managing Director, Industrial Development
Bank of India Ltd; Chairman, State Bank of India; Managing Director and CEO, ICICI Bank Limited;
Chairman, Indian Banks’ Association as well as Chairmen and Managing Directors of all banks and
financial institutions participating as permanent members in the system. Most of the big financial
institutions in India that lend money to companies are permanent participating members of the
standing forum.
CDR Core Group: A CDR Core Group is carved out of the CDR Standing Forum to assist the
Standing Forum in convening the meetings and taking decisions relating to policy, on behalf of the
Standing Forum. The Core Group consists of Chief Executives of Industrial Development Bank of
India Ltd., State Bank of India, ICICI Bank Ltd, Bank of Baroda, Bank of India, Punjab National Bank,
Indian Banks’ Association and Deputy Chairman of Indian Banks’ Association representing foreign
banks in India. It lays down policies and guidelines to be followed by the CDR Empowered Group
and CDR Cell for debt restructuring, including policies regarding the operational difficulties faced by
the CDR Empowered Group. It also prescribes time- frame, and modalities for the enforcement of
time frame for cases that are referred for the CDR mechanism.
CDR Empowered Group: The individual cases of corporate debt restructuring are decided by the
CDR Empowered Group. This group consists of Executive director level representatives of Industrial
Development Bank of India Ltd., ICICI Bank Ltd. and State Bank of India as standing members, in

Restructuring is a procedure in which a business changes the strategy or direction of its organization.
Many cases of restructuring involve downsizing. The business may reduce the staff, eliminate
departments, or close some of its retail locations. Businesses attempting to downsize may also
outsource some of their operations to save money. In other cases, restructuring may involve the
reassignment or alteration of duties within the organization to improve performance or incorporate
new technologies.
Advantages
If a business downsizes during restructuring, its operational costs may decrease. For example,
payroll expenses will be lower if the business reduced its staff. Likewise, outsourced operations are
usually less expensive than in-house labor. When a business eliminates layers of management
during its restructuring, communication and decision making often improve. Finally, businesses
restructuring  to introduce new technologies may enjoy increased operational  efficiency. For
instance, records become more accurate and easier  to access   if a business implements a
computerized filing system.

Disadvantages
If a business downsizes during restructuring,  it may lose highly skilled workers. Reassigning the
duties of these workers to remaining employees often involves added training expenses. Workers
remaining after downsizing often feel insecure about their jobs, which may lead to low worker morale
and poor customer service.
If a company's restructuring involves new technology or changes in employee responsibilities,
productivity may suffer while employees learn their new roles.
Other Considerations
If a business restructures, investors may question the company's future. Small businesses supported
by only a few investors may lose necessary funding if they are unable to provide reassurance. Small
businesses already operating with a limited number of employees may be unable to dismiss enough
personnel to make a significant difference in payroll expenses. Furthermore, a small business that
decreases its workforce too dramatically may spend more in overtime pay for the remaining workers
than it saved through downsizing.

     3.  TREASURY - BANKING
Significant policies have been put in place by the Reserve Bank of India and other regulatory bodies
to improve the efficiency and vibrancy of different financial market segments. The creation of new

treasury managers to play a major part in enhancing the overall financial performance of their
companies.
In light of this, financial institutions and banks have realized the value of the treasury function and
have taken several steps to integrate their forex and rupee treasury operations. The money,

3.1  Background
Treasury operations have changed significantly because of increased globalization and market
integration on a worldwide scale. India has been experiencing this shift, which has increased the
significance of treasury operations integration for Indian banks. The primary goals of this integration
are to reduce risks and increase bank profitability. To optimize their profits from these activities,
banks are modifying their organizational designs and operational procedures. Nonetheless, there
are some issues that must be resolved.
Treasury, as used in the context of commercial banking, generally refers to the funds and revenue
that the bank owns and manages daily. Since idle money is typically a source of loss—whether
actual or anticipated—it must be managed, invested in, and used with the goal of increasing
profitability. Without associated risk, there can be neither profit nor reward. Treasury operations,
then, aim to invest available funds at a reasonable degree of risk to maximize profit and earnings.
Risks and returns must both be controlled.
In this context, the importance of treasury operations to banks is growing, and corporates are starting
to recognize the need for both horizontal and vertical integration. Enhancing portfolio profitability,
risk mitigation, and maximizing the synergy between banking and trading assets are the main goals
of integration. While all current and diverse trading and arbitrage activities are brought under one
control with one common pool of funding and contributions in vertical integration, dealing/trading
rooms engaged  in the same trading  activity are brought under the same  policy, hierarchy,
technological, and accounting platform in horizontal integration.
3.2  Capital Adequacy
The Basel Accords, or capital requirements rules, were published by the Basel Committee on
Banking Supervision (BCBS), a committee of banking supervisory authorities. A comprehensive set
of  reform  initiatives, BCBS aims  to improve banking  industry  oversight,  control, and  risk
management. The Basel I capital measurement system was first introduced by BCBS in 1988. The
updated, revised, and more complicated Basel II guidelines were released by BCBS in 2004. Basel
II takes operational and market risks into account in addition to credit risk, whereas Basel  I only
address credit risk.
With the release of Basel  III in December 2010, the capital base's quality, consistency, and
transparency came under increased scrutiny. Basel I guidelines were adopted by India in 1999, and
by 2009, Basel  II guidelines had been phased in. India began implementing the Basel  III capital
regulation on April 1, 2013, and by March 31, 2018, it was completely established.
The minimum capital requirement is the amount of capital that banks must maintain on their own as
mandated by financial regulators. When giving loans and advances to different sectors, banks take

3.2.1 Components of Capital
Tier I Capital: The components of Tier I capital comprise paid-up capital (ordinary shares), capital
reserves representing excess proceeds from asset  sales,  statutory reserves, disclosed  free
reserves, Perpetual Non-cumulative Preference Shares (PNCPS) subject to laws in effect from time
to time, and Innovative Perpetual Debt Instruments (IPDI). It is commonly known as the core capital
because it can absorb losses without forcing a bank to shut down operations, giving its depositors
greater security.
Tier  II Capital: This category consists of subordinated debt, hybrid capital instruments, general
provisions and loss reserves, revaluation reserves, undisclosed reserves, and investment reserve
accounts. In the event of winding up, the additional capital is what absorbs losses, giving  its
depositors less  protection. To the extent  that they absorb losses  resulting from the bank's
operations, Tier II items are considered regulatory capital.
Tier III Capital: This is set up to offset some of the market risk, such as fluctuations in interest rates,
currency rates, stock prices, commodity prices, etc. Assets must be unsecured, subordinated, and
have a minimum maturity of two years in order to qualify as Tier III capital, which is limited to 250%
of a bank's Tier I capital.
The capital adequacy ratio keeps banks out of trouble and guards against excessive leverage and
insolvency. Its definition is the proportion of a bank's capital to its risk-weighted assets and current
liabilities. Assets held by the bank that have been weighted according to risk are called risk-weighted
assets. A suitable level of capital adequacy guarantees that the bank has enough capital to grow
both its net worth and its business. is sufficient to withstand any economic downturns without going
bankrupt.
The bank's ability to meet time obligations and other risks, like credit risk, market risk, operational
risk, etc., is determined by this ratio. RBI guidelines state that Indian SCBs should have a CAR of
9%, or 1% higher than the Basel minimum requirements, while public sector banks are advised to
maintain this ratio at 12%. Capital adequacy ratio is defined as:
CAR= Tier I + Tier II + Tier III capital (capital funds) / Risk Weighted Assets (RWA)
The popular wisdom holds that the yield curve and banks' net interest margins are logically related.
Banks use short-term interest rates to pay interest on deposits while using long-term interest rates
to make loans. Therefore, the difference between interest paid and received, or net interest margin,

The historical evidence supporting the international discourse on this issue dates back to 1984 and

For a variety of reasons, banks have recently grown less susceptible to changes in the yield curve.

Two, the creation of new financial products like interest rate swaps, securitization, and adjustable-

Three, by supporting a larger portion of their assets with non-interest-bearing liabilities like equities
and demand deposits, banks have been able to counteract the negative effect of falling rates on
their net interest margins.
Another noteworthy finding is that small banks have shown indications of decoupling from the yield
curve's mandated trend, while the net interest margins of major banks have moved in tandem with
the curve's changes. The disparity in funding costs and asset composition between big and small
banks could be the cause of this.

3.7  Asset Liability Management
In India's commercial banks, one of the key instruments for risk management is asset-liability
management, or ALM. The Indian banking sector is subject to several market hazards, including
interest rate risk, financial risk, and market risk. These variables or risks have a significant impact
on the banks' net income. The Reserve Bank of India (RBI), which oversees the Indian banking
sector, developed the ALM tool with this goal in mind.
The treasury department must adhere to the ALM policy of the Bank as defined internally as well as
statutory rules regarding the same. ALM is a thorough and flexible framework for calculating,
tracking, and controlling a bank's market risk. It is the structure of the balance sheet (liabilities and
assets) managed to optimize net interest profits within the institutions' overall risk-preference (future
and present). Liquidity risk management, market risk management, trading risk management,
finance and capital planning, profit planning, and growth projection are all included in the ALM
functions.
The first stage of long-term strategic planning is asset-liability management. As a result, it qualifies
as a planning function for a term in between. The different facets of managing the balance sheet in
a sense  all have to do with planning, controlling, and directing the amounts, movements, and
combinations of capital, liabilities, and assets.
Commercial banks must allocate the inflows and withdrawals among several residual maturity
periods, or "time buckets," in accordance with RBI norms. Based on the amount of time left before

4.8 Profit Centre Vs. Non-Profit Treasury
If the treasury function is set up as a cost center, the department's costs can be attributed to the
different departments and subsidiaries in a way that is thought to fairly reflect the advantages those
departments and subsidiaries receive from the treasury department and the use of treasury
services.
•        If this isn't feasible, the costs can be treated as head office expenses.
•      Alternatively,  if income from treasury can be discovered, the treasury function could be
      created as a profit center.
Revenues could be recognized as follows:
(a)   A price for the Treasury’s services based on current market rates could be paid to all other
       departments/subsidiaries and other head office departments (the total value charged to the
      group should exceed the Treasury's costs enabling it to show a profit).
(b)    Treasury may also profit from managing the group's exposure to foreign exchange and
       interest rate risk. In some cases, treasury department specialists may choose not to hedge
      group exposure to these risks. The treasury department may benefit  if interest rates, or
      currency rates move in the company's favor later. However, the treasury would bear the
       financial burden if these choices led to losses along the road.
(c)    Deciding not to insure against all interest rate and currency risks. Treasury specialists could
      choose which risks leaving unhedged in the hopes of profiting from favorable changes in
       interest rates and currency rates.

The following advantages may result from establishing the treasury function as a profit centre:

         A BRIEF ON ROLE OF ‘TREASURY FUNCTION’ IN CORPORATES
  The position of the treasurer has come under scrutiny as corporate treasury has emerged as
   a significant growth engine in worldwide operations. Given the rapid changes in the global
   business scene, the new age treasurer, as the leader of this crucial and strategic operation
   within the organization, must exhibit more sophisticated treasury  abilities  in areas  like
   funding, strategic cash management, and risk management.
     It is important to recognize the treasury's contribution to the management of the company's
   short-term assets, including cash, to control its liabilities. The cash management function of
   the most successful companies and financial service providers in the world uses a lot of
   resources and treasury intelligence, despite the function being simple.
   For a business to be healthy, it must be able to convert longer-term assets into short-term,
   manageable, and convertible assets like cash. If the corporation transfers too much, it could
   miss out on lucrative investments made in direct commercial ventures, bonds, or savings
   returns. On the other hand, if the business lacks sufficient cash on hand, it may be penalized
   for failing to pay short-term obligations.
  The treasury function oversees the flow of funds across jurisdictions and across the system
   for multinational corporations. This calls for a high degree of treasury intelligence in order to
   allocate the appropriate amounts across the various divisions while accounting for the
   requirement to hold cash  in a centralized system  in order to account  for unforeseen
   circumstances. To ensure that the business is stable and able to maintain its network, the
   corporate treasury will collaborate closely with the finance and accounting divisions about the
   cash management function.
   Corporate treasury's function has been changing recently, moving from managing financial
   risk and  liquidity  to taking on a more strategic  role.  Historically, large and mid-sized
   corporations' corporate treasury has grown dramatically as a result of cost-cutting efforts.
  You may think of a corporate treasury department as an internal solution for handling an
   organization's finances. Traditionally, the corporate treasury handles tasks including cash
  management, planning for liquidity, obtaining funding, and risk management for the company.
   In addition to the conventional roles of funding long-term growth and financing working
   capital, which are unlikely to change anytime soon, the treasury is expanding globally into
   novel areas, such as offering advice to business units and managing credit relations.
   Investments and funding
  The corporate treasury department's capacity to effectively invest available cash while

   indicators.
