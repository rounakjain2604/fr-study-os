2.4          FOREX AND TREASURY MANAGEMENT

Evidently, the corporate treasury function has evolved a lot since then!
Historically, the key focus of treasurers was around collections, handling and usage of cash and
hence was called ‘Cash Management’. Traditionally, the corporate treasurer was viewed primarily
as the company's bill payer and cash flow manager. As cash management got increasingly complex
and involved bank relationship management, global cash currencies, liabilities, forex investments,
and hedging, it began to be increasingly called ‘Treasury Management’.
Today, the corporate treasurer’s key objectives are around efficient cash management, low-cost
funding, and effective risk management. The corporate treasurer is a key decision-maker and
strategic partner to the business, and typically handles debt management, capital management and
regulatory reporting. While the expectations, role and responsibilities are increasing by the day, is
the new age corporate treasurer equipped to handle all these functions?
Some of the challenges that corporate treasures are facing today revolve around a lack of global
cash visibility, lack of accuracy of global cash forecasts, multiple risk dimensions and direct / indirect
impact of regulatory compliance. Lack of a long term ‘treasury strategy’, limited technology adoption
and the prevalence of manual processing / use of spreadsheets / outdated technologies, multiple
banking relationships, diverse ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) systems and organization
structures / silos within an enterprise all contribute to the challenges a treasury faces.
Modern banks are huge entities employing thousands of people in their workforce. However,
amongst the various departments present at banks, the treasury department has the most important
function to perform.
1.1  Asset Liability Management
The primary function of the treasury department of any bank is to ensure that its assets match its
liabilities in every possible way. It is the job of the treasury department to prepare various financial
models which help in forecasting the amount of net interest income that the bank stands to make if
different economic scenarios play out. It is also the job of the treasury department to predict exactly
how sensitive this non-interest income is to external shocks like changes in the interest rate.
After compiling this vital data, the treasury department forwards it to decision-makers, who use it to
determine what kind of assets to include on the banks' balance sheet. Bank employees are then
required to meet loan targets that are further translated from these decisions. Additionally, the bank
avoids using specific types of deposit liabilities in accordance with the information obtained from the
Treasury. As a result, the Treasury Department has a significant impact on the bank's ability to
accept deposits and approve loans.

1.2  Capital and Reserve Requirements
Since the treasury department oversees the bank's balance sheet, it is also in charge of allocating
reserves to satisfy the Central Bank's reserve requirements. It is also necessary to meet the capital
requirements outlined  in the Basel norms. Because the Central Bank imposes penalties  for
noncompliance, there are negative effects. However, keeping excess reserves in reserve does not
help the bank since it loses out on opportunities to earn interest on it.
1.3  Liaising with Regulatory Bodies
Bank treasury departments are subject to strict regulations. They are also expected to communicate
with regulatory bodies on these matters as they are the ones responsible for maintaining the reserve
and capital adequacy ratios. When decisions pertaining to the banking industry need to be made,
the government typically extends invitations to Treasury Department executives.
1.4  Liquid Investments in Government Securities
Banks' treasury departments are also in charge of keeping a specific percentage of their holdings in
extremely liquid government securities. This is carried out in nations such as the United States,
where banks serve as intermediaries between the government and the public, holding these
securities prior to their subsequent sale. Banks are mandated by law to keep a specific portion of
their portfolio in liquid government securities in other nations, such as India. This makes the market
for government securities extremely liquid while also adding safety to the bank's portfolio.
1.5  Disaster Management
Any bank's treasury department oversees its operations in case of an emergency. The treasury
department must therefore foresee the risks that may eventually materialize to be ready for the
same. The treasury department oversees hedging the bank's exposure to various risks by utilizing
instruments like derivatives. Credit derivatives became widely available in the market in the late
2000s, enabling banks to hedge even their most fundamental risk—the risk of counterparties'
nonpayment. In addition, treasury departments are crucial for insurance coverage of all tangible
assets, the loss of which could materially affect the bank's operations.
1.6  Back Office Functions - Treasury departments also must perform a
lot of normal back-office activities.
They are expected to keep their branches informed about the amount of deposits received and the
amount of loans that are available on a regular basis. To keep track of the amount of risk that the
bank can accept in real time, they must also communicate with the Forex and proprietary trading

    3.   DELEGATION WITH ACCOUNTABILITY
Dealers do a host of transactions in different currencies in the interbank market or for customers. In
view of the large volumes, transactions offset one another leaving net open (uncovered) positions
in various currencies. The Treasury Front Office maintains and manages all the forex positions of
the bank.
When the dealing room closes “shop” every day, the open position for each currency is arrived at. It
is essential to ensure that this should be within the approved limits; otherwise, it should be justified
and ratified in accordance with the delegation of powers.
Dealers may or may not cover a customer’s deals immediately, depending upon the market situation,
movements, and dealers’ views on the markets. If not covered, they add to the bank’s open position.
Thus, the forex deal book of the bank is the portfolio of long and short forex positions in different
currencies. These positions vary in amount and maturity. Spot deals mature in two working days (as
of trade day) and forwards may mature any time up to six months or even a year.
3.1  Open Positions Limits
Daylight: Daylight open positions, as the name suggests, are exposures that are opened while
trading day and will invariably be closed (squared) before the close of the day. Daylight exposures
may last only for a few seconds, minutes or hours and arise when dealers try to take advantage of
the volatility during a trading session, either in the domestic (USD/INR) market or Far East, European
or US markets, if trading in the Crosses.
Example:
The bank may buy USD 1 Million and sell equivalent INR at 10 a.m. for Rs.48.75 and sell USD 1
Million, buying INR, for Rs.48.80 at 11 a.m. for a profit of Re.0.05 / USD. The bank has an open
position of USD 1 Million for one hour  till the transaction is squared and risks USD depreciation
during this time.
Overnight: Overnight positions are positions carried over from one trading day to the next. If the
bank elects not to close positions at the close of trading, it has an overnight exposure. Over Night
exposures are monitored on a real time basis. All Over Night open positions carry hundred percent
risk weight.

Asset Position Limit: Dealing exclusively with counterparties who are creditworthy is the most
popular strategy for risk mitigation.  It's easier said than done to do this! The lender bears the
responsibility of comprehending and assessing the risk. By first determining the risk categories and
then dealing with the problems related to each  of these categories, one can arrive  at  this
understanding. Risk exposure analysis is one of the strategies that could be used.
A reliable framework of integrated institution-wide limitations and guidelines for taking risks is a
crucial part of the risk management procedure. For every significant category of risk, global limits
for different assets ought to be established. These limits ought to be in line with the Bank's overall
strategy for measuring risk and ought to be completely integrated with institution-wide restrictions
on those risks as they emerge in all other business operations. The ability to assign limits to specific
business units should be included in the asset limits system. Traders can push the boundaries from
time to time, particularly in volatile markets.
When such an exception arises, senior management should be informed of the circumstances, and
only authorized staff should be able to approve that.
Intra-day Limit (IDL): This, so called “technical” limit is a very important parameter for real cash
pooling. It allows the client to execute payment transactions on the connected account even if there
is zero balance because of last night’s automatic transfer. The level of IDL is updated automatically
every day based on the available balance on the master account.
3.2  Deal Size
Dealing Limits: Dealing limits can be created to facilitate monitoring and management of a dealer’s
Value at Risk, Net Present Value and Asset and Liability Gap. These can be configured by the
dealer, desk or for wider positions. More granular restrictions can also be applied, i.e., maximum
deal size, by way of a configurable deal management rules engine.
Individual Dealer’s Limit: A dealer's limits in progressive Treasury operations vary according to
their hot and cold streaks. A smart Manager increases dealer’s limits when they are on a roll, and
then reduces them when his profit run turns cold. The dealer of course is affected as a result, but
this is the only way to keep the traders’ egos in cheque. It is a fluid thing and if not monitored daily
with respect to profits and losses, revaluations, and listening to tape conversations, the traders’
mental capacity and results becomes the trigger for Management to act accordingly.
3.3  Stop Loss Limits
Stop loss limits are the ones which are set to avoid losses exceeding tolerable levels. For example,
a USD long position at INR 80 can be combined with stop loss limit of INR 75/USD to ensure that
loss does not exceed INR 5/USD (long and short positions have been explained in fifth chapter). If

the market price of USD starts falling and reaches below INR 75, then stop loss order gets executed
and the position is closed by shorting USD at the prevailing market prices to avoid further decline in

A stop loss serves as a safety measure if a trade or position starts to go south. Specifically, it is the
amount that a portfolio’s single - period trading or mark-to-market loss should not exceed. These
limits state that specified action (reduction in exposure or close-out position) must take place if the

Strict "stop loss limits" lower the maximum loss that could occur, which lowers the necessary capital.

reward, and limits. Therefore, "stop loss limits" are adjusted more frequently than market risk policy
guidelines. This is primarily because market risk volatility levels change daily. When market and
price volatility is high, ‘stop loss limits’ are tightened. When market and price volatility is low, ‘stop
loss limits’ can be relaxed.
‘Stop loss orders’ are orders that come into play when a ‘stop loss limit’ is breached. They turn limit
orders linked to a market price into market orders that can then be executed at the available market
price if the stop loss threshold has been hit.

    4.  CONTROL AND REPORTING REQUIREMENTS
While the treasury policies and authority levels help to manage the treasury department in an overall
context, certain controls and division of duties are necessary to ensure effective control over the
company’s assets and the smooth running of the day-to-day operations within the department. The
following controls ensure the efficient running of the department.
4.1  Controls over dealing, deal recording and confirmations
These controls guarantee that all dealing activities are accurately recorded and accounted for, and
that the company gets the best price possible for its transactions.  It is necessary to ensure the
following:
•      Multiple quotes are obtained wherever feasible to ensure that the company gets the best
       price. A record of the quotes obtained, along with execution  of the deals should be
       maintained.
•         It is the best practice to maintain a tape recording for all the telephonic deal confirmations.
     The recordings should be preserved for a reasonable period as discussed in the manual
        earlier. Recorded tapes provide a speedy resolution in case of disputes.

•    When deals are separately recorded (for example, in a diary or on deal tickets) there should
      be a cheque in place to ensure that  all the deals have been recorded in the Treasury
     Management System (TMS).
•     Deal  confirmations should be generated on a  separate system.  This ensures  that

Where the treasury department  is too small to allow for such a division of duties, then the

as checking counterparty confirmations and resolving discrepancies. Should the organization decide
not to implement such a division of duties, it should be aware of the risks it is facing.
It's also important to think about whether the dealer(s) should operate independently or through an
organization, and whether the dealers' access to dealing and payment facilities should be limited.
This prevents staff from attempting to deal on their own behalf.

4.3  Payment controls
Most companies make treasury payments, such as interest and principal payments and settlement
of derivative contracts, via an electronic banking system. Access to these machines needs to be

•     Payment requests should be written and appropriately authorized.
•     Requests should be reviewed by the treasury team to ensure they are properly authorized.

to manage these risks. It is good practice if this report is prepared by the treasury middle office.

Ethics to be observed by dealers in respect of confidential information, gifts and pleasantries, new

5.1  Control and Orderly Conduct
The Management should put in place appropriate controls and procedures in respect of their
dealings in the markets covered by the handbook. It should be ensured that the staff members who
deal in the market and other support staff follow the controls and procedures so laid down.
The Management should periodically review the controls and procedures.
The functional segregation of the front office, back office, and settlement functions is a fundamental
principle of maintaining internal controls. Nonetheless, given the volume and complexity of the
transactions, it could be wise to divide the dealing department's responsibilities as under:
       Function                            Responsible For
   Front Office          Dealing
   Mid-Office           Risk Management, Accounting and Management information
  Back Office           Confirmations, settlement, and reconciliation
Since there may be an overlap of the mid-office and back-office functions over each other, the
managements of individual institutions may resort to any other level of segregation that it thinks fit.
Personnel in the back-office function and mid-office function should functionally be segregated from
those in the front office. Persons who deal with trades must not be involved in the confirmation or
settlement of trades.

    6.  AN INSIGHT INTO:
6.1  Float
Float is the amount of time that passes between a business preparing a payment instrument to be
sent to a supplier and the supplier having money. When it comes to cheques, float results from the
time required to prepare the cheque, mail it, have it delivered, handle it by the supplier and deposit
it into the bank, have it gone through the clearing system, and then receive payment from the bank.
Any one of these elements may result in a float related to cheque payments that range from four to
ten days or longer. Cheques offered a relatively valuable float for the payer during a time when
interest rates were high globally. These days, the value of float has all but disappeared due to the
extremely low interest rates seen worldwide.
6.2  Availability
The deposit bank, or processing time, location, and clearing agent availability schedules, all play a
role in choosing the best channel for clearing cheques.  If the cheque arrives at the endpoint (or

channel, computes costs, and works backward from those endpoints' deposit deadlines to create its
own deposit deadlines for clients to create the availability schedule.

    7.  BENCHMARK RATES FOR DISCOUNTING CASH FLOWS
When a cash flow is removed from the present by a percentage, it is valued less in a discounted
cash flow (DCF) valuation. This is known as the discount rate.
Many times, the most challenging and unpredictable aspect of a DCF is estimating an appropriate
discount rate. A minor alteration in the discount rate leads to a significant shift in the value, making
the outcome even more dependent on the selection of discount rate.
Typically, the Capital Assets Pricing Model (CAPM) is used to calculate the discount rate for listed
securities. This makes sense because services that offer beta estimates and data for calculating
betas are readily available.
Cash flows in case of unlisted securities:
It gets even more challenging for unlisted securities and other cash flow sources. If there are listed
securities with comparable un-diversifiable risk, these can be used as benchmarks to determine the
appropriate discount rate.

Finding a pure play company in a business that is as similar as possible and figuring out its WACC

8.4  Gap Management
Let us understand gap management with the help of an example. Suppose RXY Bank has granted
loans worth INR 100 crores and has deposits from public in the form of time and demand deposits
to the tune of 125 crores. The loans earn interest for the bank at an average rate of 10% which is
floating in nature while deposits require interest payment at an average rate of 5% which is fixed in
nature. Thus, Interest income stands at 10 crores and interest expense stands at 6.25 crores. So,
the bank has a net interest gap of 3.75 crores.
However, as the interest income is floating in nature the gap may widen or narrow if interest rates
go up or down respectively.  It is the responsibility of the treasury department to anticipate such
interest rate movements and take appropriate actions to avoid negative gaps.
Gap Management enables banks to monitor and manage interest rate risks from transactions so
they can make strategic decisions about gap positions for defined points in time. Liquidity analysis
and the cash flow evaluation enable banks to manage their liquidity requirements and NPV risks.
NPV analysis records risks based on NPVs and future values; in gap management, on important
dates or for periods, the position and maturity volumes, as well as cash flows and liquidities, are
shown. The risks that are disclosed in this manner are then shown, including gap positions, interest
rate risk, currency risk, and liquidity risk.

The following assessments are generally part of the gap analysis:
Assessment of position: The lending and borrowing positions from both the balance sheet and off-
balance-sheet domains are compared by the system. It is possible to perform an average position
evaluation in addition to a key date position evaluation.
Evaluation of maturity: Using fixed-rate cash flows, the system displays the net present value

Cash flow evaluation: Only the cash flows up to the point at which the interest rate was fixed are

Assessment of liquidity: The system shows the payments that come in and go out for the capital

calculate full scenarios and delta scenarios.
Net interest income evaluation: The system calculates the potential net interest income for each
maturity band. The capital tie-up is used as the basis for this. For variable items, the interest revenue
or the interest expenses that have not been determined are calculated by using the forward interest
rate.

    11.  DEALING ROOM OPERATIONS
(a)  Nostro
Nostro accounts are currency accounts that are kept with Correspondent banks to help the bank
clear its foreign exchange operations. Stated differently, a Nostro Account is characterized as a
bank account opened in another foreign country using foreign money, by any commercial bank of a
certain nation. It comes from the Italian word Nostro account, which translates to "our."
For instance, the State Bank of India maintained a dollar-denominated account with the Bank of
America. To put  it plainly, a Nostro Account is created by any Indian bank to handle transactions
with a foreign bank.
(b)  Vostro
Banks outside of India have rupee accounts known as "Vostro accounts," through which they settle
and clear their rupee transactions with Indian banks. An Italian term meaning "yours" is the source
of the name Vostro Account. Because of this, it refers to your account, which is under our ownership.
If it is receivable on demand and is represented by the nation's domestic currency, a Vostro account
is treated by the domestic bank exactly like any other regular bank account. The home nation is
where Vostro accounts are kept up to date.
Consider the following scenario: a certain bank in the United States wishes to create an account in
a specific bank in India that accepts Indian Rupees (INR). Then this type of account is called a
Vostro account.
(c)  Loro
The term "Their account with them" refers to the Loro account, which takes its name from the Italian
word "loro," which means "theirs." Typically, a bank other than the one that maintains the account
or with which  it is kept holds such an account. An account maintained by a foreign bank with a
domestic bank in foreign currency on behalf of another domestic bank is known as a Loro account.
For instance, SBI keeps an account with the UK's HSBC bank. There isn't an account between Bank
of India (BOI) and HSBC, UK. When used by BOI for transactions in the UK, the SBI account with
HSBC is referred to as a Loro account.

(d)  Mirror A/Cs
Every bank keeps a duplicate of its Nostro account at its own bank that is linked to a foreign bank.
For the bank's internal records and operational needs, an account of this kind is kept in local
currency. The purpose of a Mirror account is indicated by the name "mirror." It keeps meticulous
records of all foreign exchange inflows and outflows made through a bank account called Nostro.
Knowing the true account position is aided by mirror accounts.
The Mirror account aggregates money in local currency from various Nostro accounts held with
various foreign banks at various locations and in various currencies. Through inter-entity lending,
the pooling of money enables the client to limit the use of operating bank credit lines or to receive
greater interest returns.
(e)  Open Currency Position
The measure of foreign exchange risk is the total size of the open currency forward position and the
open currency spot position. The difference between foreign currency liabilities and assets as well
as the forward (conditional and unconditional) foreign exchange purchases and sales makes up the
open currency position. The foreign exchange risk is measured by the open currency position.
(f)  Cash Position by Dealers
The amount owed on the bank's foreign Nostro account is known as the cash position. The bank
maintains its foreign exchange reserves in the form of balances with its correspondent bank in the
relevant foreign exchange center. These nostro accounts serve as the gateway for all the bank's
international transactions. For instance, a Bank of America location in New York will have an
account with an Indian bank. If the bank is asked to issue a demand draft in US dollars, Bank of
America, New York will receive the draft. The bank's account with Bank of America will be deducted
upon presentation in New York.

In view of the voluminous and complex nature of transactions handled by a treasury, various func-
tions are segregated as under:
1.     Front- Office: Dealing–Risk Taking.
2.     Mid-Office: Risk Management and Management Information.
3.     Back-Office: Confirmations, Settlements, Accounting and Reconciliation.
The number of activities managed determines how a treasury is organized. The three tasks must be
distinct and performed in watertight compartments. Accounts and settlement are not meant to be
handled by the dealers. While accounting functions may be performed by the accounting section,
dealing should not be performed by the accounting back office.
An appropriate senior executive oversees, directs, and coordinates the treasury's operations on
behalf of the corporation. In addition, he or she oversees the coordination of work among the chief
dealer, head of research, head of back  office, and  is solely  in charge  of managing funds,
investments, and forex activity.
Additionally, he or she will serve on the Assets Liability Management Committee (ALCO) and assist
the group in making decisions regarding different treasury management policies. Dealers for forex
operations are employed by banks that have separate forex operations.
There will be a distinct research division within the Treasury. Officers will support the Head of
Research in conducting research and analysis on a variety of securities. For the money market,
debt, stocks, and  foreign exchange, research departments may be common. The research
department would also offer a market analysis.
Treasury management requires the use of appropriate information technology, including processes,
packages, and infrastructure, because the operations and transactions involved are different from
those of branch banking and are of utmost importance. Because software packages on the market
might not be sufficient, banks might need to alter the software to accommodate their requirements,
fluctuating conditions, and changing circumstances.
The fund manager monitors the level of liquidity, fund movements, and preservation of reserve
requirement(s). Treasury should post risk managers to help with scenario evaluation, independent
line/limit excess review, transaction reviews to ensure compliance with regulations, monitoring of
risk factors such as credit, liquidity, interest rate, and operational risk in transactions, and guidance
to dealers to stay abreast of product and market developments.
