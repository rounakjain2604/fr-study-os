     1.  RISK IDENTIFICATION
1.1  Risk  identification  is  the  initial  step  in  the process  of  risk
management
Risk identification is the action or process of identifying some potential internal or external event,
or threat or vulnerability or a fact that could cause damage to the entity or prevent it from achieving
its objectives. It includes documenting the potential risks in the form of a risk questionnaire or risk
register and communicating the risks to the executive management.
Identifying risks involves recording potential risks either through a risk questionnaire or a risk
register and conveying these risks to executive management. Effective risk identification relies on
the risk management team's understanding of the business, industry, or sector in which the
business operates, as well as the key management objectives or key performance indicators. A
robust risk identification exercise hinges on creative thinking and utilizing indicators of potential
problems. Approaches to risk identification can involve either a top-down exercise from senior to
junior levels or vice versa, though empirical evidence suggests that top-down exercises tend to
yield more favorable outcomes for businesses.

The process of risk identification entails determining which risks may impact the business or
project and documenting their characteristics. Typically, participants in the identification process
include business managers, project teams,  risk management teams, subject matter experts,
customers, end users, other project stakeholders, and external experts.
1.2  Risk identification sets out to identify an organisation’s exposure to
uncertainty
This exercise can be successfully executed if the risk management team has reasonable degree of
business knowledge and related variables  in which the business operates. The various  risk
variables include legal, social, community, political and other factors that impact the business
model of the entity. The risk management project team should intimately understand the business
strategy and the market place in which the entity operates. Further, the risk management team
should undertake a Strength, Weakness, Opportunity and Threat assessment exercise so as to
document the factors that could give rise to potential risks in future. The SWOT analysis exercise
will  facilitate development of sound business knowledge and communication of key business
weaknesses, threats and opportunities to seize in the risk management exercise.
Following the Risk Identification process, the organization gains awareness of different risks and
subsequently addresses them. It is imperative for the entity to establish objectives that align with
sales,  production,  marketing,  financial, and  other  operational  activities  to ensure cohesive
operations. Additionally, mechanisms need to be put in place to identify, analyze, and manage
associated risks effectively.
The entity identifies risks to the achievement of its objectives across the entity and analyses risks
as a basis for determining how the risks should be managed. It:
•     Involves appropriate levels of management;
•     Includes entity, subsidiary division, operating unit, and functional levels;
•     Analyzes internal and external factors;
•     Estimates significance of risks identified;
•     Determines how to respond to risks
All above activities should be approached in a methodical manner so that any significant business
activity or risk item is not missed out by the risk management project team. One of the best ways
to identify risks is by flow-charting the key business processes and thereafter undertaking a “what
can go wrong exercise”.

     2.  RISK EVALUATION (IMPACT/ LIKELIHOOD)
Risk is evaluated by answering the above questions for various risk factors and assessing the
probability of failure and the impact of exposure for each risk factor. Risk is the probability of
impact of the exposure.
The purpose of a risk evaluation is to:
•      Identify the probabilities of failures and threats,
•     Calculate the exposure, i.e., the damage or loss to assets, and
•    Make control recommendations keeping the cost-benefit analysis in mind.
2.1 Analysing the Level of Risk
To analyse risks, we need to work out the likelihood of its happening (frequency or probability) and
the consequences it would have (the impact) of the risks that are identified. This is referred to as
the level of risk, and can be calculated using this formula:-
Level of risk = consequence x likelihood
Level of risk is often described as low, medium, high or very high. It should be analysed in relation
to what is currently being done to control it. Control measures decrease the level of risk, but do not
always eliminate it.
2.2 Likelihood scale
A risk analysis can be presented in the form a matrix, such as this:
   Level   Likelihood    Description
     4     Very likely    Happens more than once a year in the industry
     3      Likely        Happens about once a year in the industry
     2      Unlikely      Happens every 10 years or more in the industry
     1     Very unlikely  Has only happened once in the industry

      3.   RISK QUANTIFICATION & RATINGS
Risk quantification and ratings are essential components of risk management, whether in the
context of financial, business, or other domains. They provide a systematic way to assess and
communicate the level of risk associated with specific assets, activities, or investments.
Risk Quantification is the process of evaluating and defining the cost and benefits associated with
the risk consequences. For example historical share price data of public listed entities can be
mined to make assessments of possible future price movements, in light of past fluctuations of the
share price for the purpose of making an investment decision.

Quantifying risk enables organizations to prioritize and make informed decisions regarding risk
management strategies. It also allows for better resource allocation and risk mitigation.
Risk ratings are a way to express the level of risk in a standardized format, often through a scale
or a set of categories. They help stakeholders quickly understand the relative risk associated with
different assets or activities.
Risk ratings are commonly used in credit risk assessment, investment analysis, and operational
risk management. They provide a basis for decision-making and risk communication.
In credit risk assessment, ratings are used to evaluate the likelihood of borrowers defaulting on
their debt obligations. Credit rating agencies assign ratings to bonds, loans, and other financial
instruments to inform investors and creditors.
In operational risk management, risk ratings can be used to assess the potential impact and
likelihood of events such as system failures, fraud, or supply chain disruptions.
Investors often rely on risk ratings to evaluate the risk-return trade-off of various investments.
Higher-rated assets typically offer lower returns but come with less risk, while lower-rated assets
offer the potential for higher returns but with increased risk.
Challenges
•    One of the challenges  in  risk quantification and ratings  is the subjectivity involved  in
       assigning numerical values or categories to risks. Different stakeholders may have varying
       perceptions of risk.
•     Overreliance on  historical  data and  assumptions  can  lead  to  inaccuracies  in  risk
       quantification.
•     Catastrophic events or black swan events that have not been previously observed can pose
      challenges for risk quantification and ratings.
In summary, risk quantification and ratings are crucial tools for understanding and managing risk in
various domains. They provide a structured and standardized way to assess and communicate the
level of  risk, enabling organizations and individuals to make informed decisions and allocate
resources effectively. However, they should be used in conjunction with a deep understanding of
the specific context and potential limitations of these methods.

      4. RISK QUANTIFICATION TOOLS
Following are some of the tools and techniques that are available to assess and evaluate risks:
4.1  Judgment and intuition
In numerous scenarios, both management and auditors rely on their judgment and intuition to
assess  risks.  This  heavily  relies on  the  collective  personal and  professional  expertise  of
management and auditors, as well as their comprehension of the business, its systems, and its
surroundings. Furthermore, a structured education and continuous professional development are
essential requirements in this process.
4.2  Delphi approach
Originally developed by the Rand Corporation, the Delphi Technique  is employed to reach a
consensus among experts. In this method, a panel of experts is selected, each providing their
individual opinions in a written format. They assess factors such as costs, benefits, reasons for
system selection, risks, and exposures. These assessments are collated, and those falling within a
predetermined acceptable range are considered. The process may iterate up to four times to
revise estimates outside the range. Subsequently, a curve is plotted using all estimates as points
on a graph, with the median representing the consensus opinion.
The Delphi method is an approach used in risk management, particularly in the context of risk
assessment and forecasting.  It is a structured and iterative technique that involves experts or
stakeholders providing their opinions and insights on various aspects of a specific topic, such as
identifying and assessing risks.
4.2.1 Steps Involved
Following steps are involved in the applying Delphi approach:
(a)  Expert Panel: A group of experts or knowledgeable individuals in the field of interest is
selected. These experts can come from various backgrounds, including risk management, industry
specialists, or subject matter experts.
(b)  Structured Questionnaire: The process begins with a structured questionnaire or survey
that is designed to gather opinions and insights from the experts. This questionnaire typically
contains open-ended questions or statements related to the topic under consideration. In the
context of risk management, these questions may pertain to identifying, assessing, or prioritizing
risks.

(c)  Iterative Rounds: The Delphi method involves  multiple rounds  of data  collection and
feedback. After receiving the responses to the  initial questionnaire, the facilitator compiles the
results and anonymizes the feedback. The aggregated responses are then provided to the experts
in the next round, along with any comments, arguments, or justifications provided by the experts in
the previous round.
(d)  Consensus Building: Experts are encouraged to review the collective feedback and,  if
necessary, revise their responses in subsequent rounds. The process continues iteratively until a
consensus or convergence of opinions is reached among the experts. The number of rounds can
vary depending on the topic and the level of consensus achieved.
(e)  Anonymity: Anonymity  is a key feature of the Delphi method. Experts do not know the
identities of the other participants, which can help reduce bias and social influence  in  their
responses.
(f)   Final Report: Once consensus is reached or the predefined stopping criteria are met, a final
report is generated, summarizing the findings, the degree of consensus, and any areas of ongoing
disagreement or uncertainty.
4.2.2 Benefits
The Delphi approach in risk management has several benefits:
(i)   Expert Opinion:  It leverages the knowledge and expertise of a diverse group of experts,
which can lead to a more comprehensive and well-informed risk assessment.
(ii)  Structured Process: The Delphi method provides a structured process for gathering and
refining expert opinions, reducing the influence of individual biases and preconceptions.
(iii) Anonymity: Anonymity can encourage experts to express their opinions freely, particularly
when dealing with contentious or sensitive risk issues.
(iv) Consensus Building: By seeking consensus, the Delphi method helps in identifying areas of
agreement and disagreement among experts.
The Delphi method is especially useful when dealing with complex or uncertain risk scenarios, as it
can help in systematically exploring and understanding the diverse perspectives of experts.  It is
often used in various fields of risk management, including strategic risk assessment, technology
risk analysis, and environmental risk assessment, among others.

4.3  Scoring
The Scoring approach involves identifying risks within the business and system, along with their
respective exposures. We then assign weights to these risks and exposures based on severity,
likelihood of occurrence, and associated costs. Multiplying the risk weight by the exposure weight
for each characteristic yields the weighted score. Adding these weighted scores together provides
the overall risk and exposure score for the system. Subsequently, the system's risk and exposure
are ranked based on these scores.
4.4  Quantitative techniques
These techniques  involve  the  calculation  of an annual  loss exposure value based on  the
probability of the event and the exposure in terms of estimated costs. This helps the organization
to select cost effective solutions.  It  is the assessment of potential damage  in the event of
occurrence of unfavorable events, keeping in mind how often such an event may occur.
4.5  Qualitative techniques
These techniques are most widely used approaches to  risk analysis. Probability data  is not
required and only estimated potential loss is used. Most qualitative risk analysis methodologies
use a number of interrelated elements:
•     Threats: These are things that can go wrong or that can 'attack' the system. Examples
      might include fire or fraud. Threats are ever present for every system.
•      Vulnerabilities: These make a system more prone to attack by a threat or make an attack
     more likely to have some success or impact. For example, for fire, vulnerability would be the
      presence of inflammable materials (e.g. Paper).
•      Controls: These are the countermeasures for vulnerabilities. They are of four types:
          (i)     Deterrent controls reduce the likelihood of a deliberate attack.
           (ii)    Preventative controls protect vulnerabilities and make an attack unsuccessful or
            reduce its impact.
           (iii)    Corrective controls reduce the effect of an attack.
         (iv)    Detective controls discover attacks and trigger preventative or corrective controls.

4.6  Expected Monetary Value
Expected monetary value, a technique  for quantifying  risk,  is determined by multiplying two
factors:
•    The likelihood of the risk event occurring, which is an estimation of its probability.
•    The potential impact of the risk event, which is an estimation of the financial gain or loss
      associated with its occurrence.
It is crucial that the assessment of risk event value considers both tangible and intangible aspects.
For instance,  if Project A anticipates minimal intangible effects while Project B foresees potential
consequences leading to the organization's insolvency, these risks cannot be considered equal.
Similarly, neglecting to account for intangibles can skew results significantly, such as equating a
small loss with a high probability to a substantial loss with a low probability. Expected monetary
value is commonly utilized as input for further analyses, such as decision tree modelling, as risk
events may unfold individually, concurrently, or sequentially.
4.7  Simulation
Simulation uses a representation or model of a system to analyze the behaviour or performance of
the system. The most common form of simulation on a project is schedule simulation using the
project network as the model of the project. Most schedule simulations are based on some form of
Monte Carlo analysis. This technique, adapted from general management, "performs" the project
many times to provide a statistical distribution of the calculated results.
Simulation techniques are widely used in risk management to assess and analyze the potential
outcomes of uncertain events, decisions, or scenarios. These techniques involve creating models
that replicate real-world situations and repeatedly running these models to estimate the likelihood
and impact of different risk factors. Simulation in risk management can be particularly valuable in
understanding and preparing for the potential consequences of risks.
Monte Carlo simulation is a widely used technique that generates random variables for uncertain
parameters within a model. It performs thousands or even millions of iterations to calculate a range
of possible outcomes.
It is used to assess financial risks, project risks, and any scenario with uncertain variables. For
example, it's commonly employed in portfolio risk assessment for investment decisions.

3.     Select a value at random from probability distribution of each of the exogenous variables.
4.    Determine NPV corresponding to the randomly generated value of exogenous variables and

5.    Repeat steps (3) & (4) a large number of times to get a large number of simulated NPVs.
6.     Plot probability distribution of NPVs and compute a mean and Standard Deviation of returns
       to gauge the project’s level of risk.

4.8  Decision Tree
Decision Tree is a diagram that depicts key interactions among decisions and associated chance
events as they are understood by the decision maker. The branches of the tree represent either
decisions (shown as boxes) or chance events (shown as circles).
Information relating to the probability of occurrence of the forecasted value is considered useful. A
range of values of the variable with probabilities of occurrence of each value is taken up. The
limitations are reduced through decision tree analysis and use of simulation techniques.
In decision tree analysis, the decision is taken sequentially with probabilities attached to each
sequence. To obtain the probability of final outcome, various sequential decisions given along with
probabilities, the probability of each sequence is to be multiplied and them summed up.

4.11 Scenario Analysis
Utilize scenario analysis to evaluate the risk associated with potential downturns in real estate or
other asset values, as well as fluctuations in interest rates or other market variables. Through
scenario analysis, you analyze the potential effects of different scenarios on the business. For
instance, consider a company with a variable interest rate line of credit. By employing scenario
analysis, you can assess the company's risk of default if the interest rate were to increase by three
percentage points over the course of the year.
Scenario analysis involves constructing various scenarios and assessing  their likelihood and
impact. These scenarios are often based on different assumptions and variables. It is useful in risk
management for strategic planning and decision-making. It helps identify potential risks associated
with different future scenarios.
The analysis commences by establishing the base case, which represents the most probable
values for input variables. Subsequently,  it explores the worst-case scenario, characterized by
factors such as low unit sales, sale price, and high variable costs. Conversely, it also examines the
best-case scenario, featuring high unit sales, sale price, and low variable costs. Alternatively,
scenario analysis offers the  flexibility to adjust certain factors positively while altering others
negatively to assess various potential outcomes.
So, in a nutshell Scenario analysis examine the risk of investment, to analyse the impact of
alternative combinations of variables, on the project’s NPV (or IRR).

Scenario Analysis Vs. Sensitivity Analysis
Sensitivity analysis and Scenario analysis both help to understand the impact of the change in
input variable on the outcome of the project. However, there are certain basic differences between
the two.
Sensitivity analysis calculates the impact of the change of a single input variable on the outcome
of the project viz., NPV or IRR. The sensitivity analysis thus enables to identify that single critical
variable which can impact the outcome in a huge way and the range of outcomes of the project
given the change in the input variable.
Scenario analysis, on the other hand, is based on a scenario. The scenario may be recession or a
boom wherein depending on the scenario, all input variables change. Scenario Analysis calculates
the outcome  of  the  project  considering  this  scenario where  the  variables have changed
simultaneously. Similarly, the outcome of the project would also be considered for the normal and
recessionary situation. The variability in the outcome under the three different scenarios would
help the management to assess the risk a project carries. Higher deviation in the outcome can be
assessed as higher risk and lower to medium deviation can be assessed accordingly.
Scenario analysis is far more complex than sensitivity analysis because in scenario analysis all
inputs are changed simultaneously, considering the situation in hand while in sensitivity analysis,
only one input is changed, and others are kept constant.

      5.   RISK PRIORITIZATION & RISK MATRIX
Once risks have been identified, analyzed, and assessed, the subsequent phase entails prioritizing
them based on  their  significance.  Successful  risk management  hinges on  the  meticulous
examination and ranking of risk factors utilizing probabilistic models, which directly correlate with
the potential impact of each risk. Similarly, prioritizing stakeholders according to their authority,
level of involvement, and exposure to risk threats  is imperative. This comprehensive analysis
serves as valuable input for crafting a risk mitigation strategy, ensuring that adequate resources
and attention are allocated to stakeholders facing or presenting the highest project risks.
A "Risk matrix" or "Heat map" in risk management is a visual representation of risks and their
attributes, typically using a matrix or grid format.  It's a tool that helps organizations assess,
prioritize, and communicate risks effectively.
The heat matrix is often used to evaluate and compare risks based on two main dimensions: the
impact and likelihood of each risk event.

Impact: This dimension represents the potential consequences or severity of a risk event if it were
to occur. Impact can be categorized as low, medium, or high, or  it can use a numerical scale to
quantify the extent of damage or loss. The greater the impact , the more significant the potential
harm or negative outcome.
Likelihood: This dimension represents the probability or likelihood of the risk event occurring.
Likelihood  is  often categorized as  rare, occasional, frequent, or can be quantified using a
probability scale. Higher likelihood means a higher chance of the risk event occurring.
The risk matrix is typically displayed as a grid with impact categories on one axis and likelihood
categories on the other. This creates a matrix with various  cells, each corresponding  to a
combination of impact and likelihood.
Risks are assessed and placed in the matrix based on their estimated impact and likelihood. For
example, a risk with high impact and high likelihood would be positioned differently in the matrix
compared to a risk with low impact and low likelihood.
The  matrix  is  usually color-coded  to  create a heat map  that  visually  represents  the  risk
assessment.
The heat map uses colors to indicate the level of risk. For example, risks with a high combination
of impact and likelihood might be represented in “Red”, indicating a high level of risk.

Risks with lower combinations could be shown in "cooler" colors like Green, indicating lower risk.
It's important to note that the specific categories, colors, and terminology used in a heat matrix can

The heat map helps organizations prioritize risks.
Risks in the high-impact, high-likelihood quadrant are typically considered the most critical and
require immediate attention and risk mitigation efforts. Risks in other quadrants are managed
differently, with a focus on reducing their impact or likelihood.
5.1  Benefits of Risk Heat Map/ Risk Matrix
The risk heat matrix or heat map is a valuable risk management tool because:
•         It provides a  clear, visual representation  of the  risk landscape, making   it easier  for
      stakeholders to understand and discuss risks.
•         It  assists  organizations  in  concentrating  their  risk management  efforts on  the most
        significant risks.
•         It facilitates risk communication and decision-making by presenting risks in a standardized,
       visual format.
5.2  Flaws of Risk Heat Map/ Risk Matrix
Risk matrix suffers from serious flaws some of which are as follows:
•     They are qualitative and subjective, and definition of “High”, “Medium” and “Low” may mean
        different things to different industry.
•     They are typically presented as symmetric about the diagonal, which is often invalid.
•     They do not account for risk aversion, which is manifested by almost every entity in making
       decisions.
•    The breadth of the categories may result in serious prioritization errors.
Heat maps are a way of representing the resulting qualitative evaluations of the probability of risk
occurrence and the impact on the organisation if that a particular risk is experienced.
Many organisations map risks on a heat map using a “residual risk” basis. This considers only the
remaining risk after mitigation strategies such as internal controls or other risk responses activities.
