1.1 COSO Enterprise Risk Management Framework
COSO (Committee of Sponsoring Organizations of the Treadway Commission) was founded in 1985
to fund and oversee the National Commission on Fraudulent Financial Reporting.
Following are five organizations are part of COSO
(i)    The American Accounting Association
(ii)   The American Institute of Certified Public Accountants
(iii)    Financial Executives International
(iv)   The Institute of Internal Auditors
(v)   The Institute of Management Accountants
In keeping with its overall mission, the COSO Board commissioned and published in 2004 the
Enterprise Risk Management—Integrated Framework. Over the past decade, that publication has
gained broad acceptance by organizations in their efforts to manage risk. However, also through
that period, the complexity of risk has changed, new risks have emerged, and both boards and
executives have enhanced their awareness and oversight of enterprise risk management while
asking for improved risk reporting. This update to the 2004 publication addresses the evolution of
enterprise risk management and the need for organizations to improve their approach to managing
risk to meet the demands of an evolving business environment. The updated document, titled
Enterprise Risk Management—Integrating with Strategy and Performance, highlights the importance
of considering risk in both the strategy-setting process and in driving performance.
COSO's stated mission is offering guidance on internal controls, risk management, governance and
fraud deterrence to help organizations improve their performance.
Following are components of latest Framework are as follows:
Component 1 - Risk Governance and Culture:  Effective risk oversight relies on the Board's
comprehension of the entity's strategy and industry landscape, alongside staying abreast of factors
impacting the organization. The board of directors holds a pivotal position in risk governance and
exerts considerable influence on ERM practices. Board members must possess requisite skills,
experience, and business acumen, coupled with independence, accountability, and responsibility

1.2.1 Principles
As mentioned in the standard, the Principles are the foundation for managing risk. These principles
should be considered  with the utmost importance when  establishing the organisation’s  risk
management framework and processes.
These are the guiding factors that enable the organisation to achieve  its objectives. The core
principle around which the Risk Management standard  is established  is “Value creation and
Protection”. All other principle is bounded together with this core principle.
According to the principle, Risk Management not only creates value to the internal stakeholders
namely management and shareholders who want to see their stake appreciate but also to all external
stakeholders including the customers purchasing its products and services.

1.2.2 Framework
The Framework needs to be designed to assist the organisation to integrate Risk Management with
all  the  activities and  functions  of  the  organisation. Framework development encompasses
integrating, designing, implementing, evaluating, and improving Risk Management across the
organisation. The framework should be customized as per the needs and demands  of the
organisation.

1.2.3 Process
According to this ISO, the Risk Management process involves the following activities: namely:
(i) Communicating and Consulting: Communication and Consultation should take place across
the organization which includes the internal and external stakeholders.
Further, it should also take place throughout all the stages of Risk Management. This will facilitate
the stakeholder  to understand  the  risk and make informed  decisions. Communication and
Consultation aim to bring different areas of expertise together at each stage of the Risk Management
process and provide sufficient information to facilitate risk oversight and decision-making.
(ii) Defining Scope, Context and Criteria: The organisation needs to define the scope of Risk
Management activities. Risk Management can be applied across all the levels and functions of
management namely  strategic,  operational,  project  activity among  others. To have a  clear

      3.   ENTERPRISE RISK AND LINKAGE TO BUSINESS
RM is a dynamic function and to consider any business without risk is next to impossible.
Thus, RM can be defined as a coordinated or pre decided activity meant to direct and control
challenges or threat faced by any organization to achieve its goals and objectives.
Now question arises, what is Enterprise Risk? Enterprise Risk can be defined as enterprise -wide
approach meant to address full spectrum of organization’s various risks contrary to silo approach.
Hence, not only  it provides an enterprise – wide view but considers strategically aligned portfolio
view of organizational opportunities and challenges and prioritize them to manage them in an
effective manner.
Since Enterprise Risk looks for enterprise spectrum,  it helps to determine the extent to which the
corporate strategy differs from corporate objectives and extent to which  it fails to meet these
corporate objectives. The Enterprise Risk embodies into corporate strategies the risk profile that
arise due to various factors that arises an account of various factors.
If an organization wants to minimize the effect of risk on earnings and growth it must implement a
comprehensive Enterprise Risk Management Programme. A successful ERM should not only strive
to align a firm’s organization structure, process and other factors but also develop adequate risk /
reward benchmarks that assist in risk assessments in various operational activities.
The linkage of Enterprise Risk to a business can be discussed under heads.
(1)   How ERM can help an organization.
(2)   Key components that should be considered by a firm in developing ERM that provide a
       competitive Advantage.
(3)    Integration of Business Continuity Plans and Enterprise Risk Management.

3.1 How ERM can help an organization
Specifically, ERM can help organizations:
(a)     Identify strategic risk opportunities that, if undertaken, can facilitate achieving organizational
       goals.
(b)    Introduce a common language within the organization where people recognize problems and
      adopt a problem solving approach by developing risk treatment actions.
(c)    Provide senior management with the most up-to-date information regarding risk that may be
      used in the decision-making process.
(d)    Establish linkage between the ERM initiative and adherence to capital market reporting
       disclosures and other corporate laws and regulations.
(e)    Align annual performance goals with risk identification and management.
(f)    Encourage and reward upstream reporting of business-risk opportunities and challenges.
(g)    Align other risk monitoring initiatives such as self-appraisals, internal auditing activities,
       control assessments, continuous control monitoring, to organizational objectives.
(h)    Imagining key Risk Scenarios that could potentially result in a stress on the financial position
       of the company.
(i)     Financial Risk monitoring a part of the ERM initiative can balance the financial stability
      equation of the company.

(ii) Risk Appetite: - It can be defined as the amount of risk an organization is willing to accept in
pursuit of achieving organizational goals.
A Risk Appetite statement not only reflects entity’s risk management philosophy but also impacts its
culture and operation style.
While developing Risk Appetite statement organization’s existing profile, capacity attitude, and
tolerance toward risk must be considered. This statement should be translated into a written form.
Though overall risk appetite can be communicated through a broad statement however to make it
effective,  it should be expressed individually for each category of risk to which organization is
exposed to. It should be noted that an effective risk appetite statement needs to be precise so that
it could be communicated effectively and proved helpful in decision making. It will be very effective
if it is broken into specific operating metrics which can be monitored.
For example, while analysing credit risk the operating metrics can be % of Bad Debt to Net Credit
Sales, % of outstanding payment over say 30 days. Whenever any metric deteriorates or exceeds
the required standard there is a call for required action.
Further it is important to note that risk appetite need to be continuously monitored and needs to be
revised with change in strategies and objectives.
(iii) Risk Culture: - Risk culture can be defined as a set of values, attitudes and practices followed
in an organization in day-to-day activities.
The culture arises from the repeated behavior of the people in the organization. For an effective
ERM, there should be strong Risk culture that helps in resolution of conflict and top-down / bottom
up decision making.
(iv) Risk Data: - For an effective Enterprise Risk there should be robust Risk Data system so that
required information could be assimilated, integrated, analyzed and converted into quality reports
for decision making.
(v) Internal Control: - Importance of proper Internal control cannot be ignored in an Enterprise Risk
Management. Inherent risk is the level of risk assuming no internal controls, while residual risk is
the level of risk after considering the impact of internal controls. For example, the risk of 'over/
understatement of revenue' without considering any internal controls indicates inherent risk. The
above risk when considered with internal controls in place (say, monthly reconciliation of revenue
and follow up, correction of discrepancies, etc.) indicate residual risk.

      4.  GOVERNANCE & ETHICAL DIMENSIONS
Corporate Governance is being considered the ruling mantra by stakeholders and managers since
last decade. Even in third world countries where things does not seem to move very fast, corporates
and their shareholders are doing everything in their control to implement the practices of Corporate
Governance.
But now a days managers all over the Globe have realised that mere Corporate Governance is not
sufficient to safeguard the interests of enterprise and its stakeholders. Both boards and senior
management continue to face new and increasingly complex business challenges-mergers and
acquisitions, advances in technology and the demands of conducting business in a global economy,
among many others. Particularly in India, the shift from a controlled economy to an open economy
has increased the potential for risks and, also the commensurate rewards. This paradigm shift has
put on the Board of Companies an onerous responsibility of managing with requisite skills and a
degree of caution, the risks thrown by the internal and external environment. Consequently, the once
impervious boardroom has become increasingly subject to shareholder activism and rigorous
regulatory scrutiny. Today's directors and officers face rapidly evolving standards and corresponding
new liability risks-in their pursuit of improved corporate performance. In this changing scenario,
every professional is realizing that mere 'Conformance' (read Corporate Governance) would not help
them in achieving their roles but it is 'Performance'(read Business Governance) which is also a major
contributing factor.

Chartered Accountants can play a critical role in evolving a holistic approach, which embodies not

Corporate governance can be complex.   It  is  the  relationship among  various  participants-
shareholders, management and the board of directors-who work in concert to determine the direction
and performance of corporations. The accepted governance framework is simple: management is
accountable to the board, and the board is accountable to the shareholders. For the past 10 years,
this framework, particularly the board's  role, has been evolving. The board does more than
mechanically link those who manage the corporation and those who own  it. As a surrogate for
dispersed ownership, in fact, the board is at the centre of corporate governance itself, working

Internationally  there  is now an  increasing momentum towards  constituting more laws and
government regulations that impose obligations on companies, their directors and officers, to adopt,
implement and comply with good corporate governance. Failure to do so may lead to legal and
criminal sanctions being imposed on the company, its directors and officers.
The quality of governance is of absolute importance to various stakeholders including shareholders
as it provides them with a level of assurance that the business of the company is being conducted
in a manner that adds shareholder value and safeguards its assets.  If a company adopts and
implements good governance practices, shareholders are retained and new investors attracted.
Opinion surveys have revealed that institutional investors have indicated a willingness to pay a
premium for the shares of a well-governed company.

framework is designed (explicitly or implicitly) by the executive team under the auspices of the board
of directors. Together, they strive to optimize use of resources by aligning all the activities of the
organization in order to clarify and execute strategic and operational direction as well as attitude
toward risk.

4.1  G20/  OECD  GUIDELINES  (PRINCIPLES)  FOR  CORPORATE
GOVERNANCE
Corporate governance involves a set of relationships between a company’s management, its board,
its shareholders and other stakeholders. Corporate governance also provides the structure through
which the objectives of the company are set, and the means of attaining those objectives and
monitoring performance are determined.
The G20/OECD Principles of Corporate Governance help policy makers evaluate and improve the
legal, regulatory, and institutional framework for corporate governance, with a view to supporting
economic efficiency, sustainable growth and financial stability.
The Principles are divided into six different chapters and accordingly here they have presented as
sub-headings and then sub-principles.
4.1.1 Ensuring the basis for an effective corporate governance framework
The corporate governance framework should promote transparent and fair markets, and the efficient
allocation of resources. It should be consistent with the rule of law and support effective supervision
and enforcement.
A.    The corporate governance framework should be developed with a view to its impact on overall
      economic performance, market integrity and the incentives it creates for market participants
      and the promotion of transparent and well-functioning markets.
B.    The legal and regulatory requirements that affect corporate governance practices should be
       consistent with the rule of law, transparent and enforceable.

4.2 Ethical Dimensions in Risk Management
Ethics plays a crucial role in risk management across various domains, including finance, business,
healthcare, environmental management, and more. Here are some dimensions of ethics in risk
management:
1.   Transparency and Disclosure: Ethical  risk management practices involve transparently
communicating risks to stakeholders, including investors, customers, employees, and the public.
This  includes  disclosing  potential  risks associated  with  products,  services, investments,  or
operations.

2.   Fairness and Equity: Ethical risk management requires treating all stakeholders fairly and
equitably. This means considering the potential impact of risks on different groups, ensuring that
risks are not disproportionately borne by vulnerable populations or marginalized communities.
3.   Integrity and Honesty: Ethical risk management involves maintaining integrity and honesty in
assessing and communicating risks. This includes accurately representing the probability and
potential impact of risks, avoiding misleading statements or omissions, and refraining from deceptive
practices.
4.   Responsibility and Accountability: Ethical risk management entails taking responsibility for
identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks. This includes holding individuals and organizations
accountable for their actions and decisions related to risk management, including any failures or
shortcomings.
5.   Conflicts of Interest: Ethical risk management requires identifying and managing conflicts of
interest that may arise in the decision-making process. This includes avoiding situations where
personal interests or biases could influence risk assessments or mitigation strategies.
6.   Sustainability and Long-Term Perspective: Ethical risk management involves considering
the long-term implications of risks on sustainability, both environmental and social. This includes
assessing  risks  related  to  climate change, resource  depletion,  social  inequality, and  other
sustainability challenges, and adopting strategies that promote resilience and long-term viability.
7.   Respect for Stakeholder Rights: Ethical risk management involves respecting the rights and
interests of all stakeholders, including their right to safety, privacy, and informed consent. This
includes seeking input from stakeholders in the risk management process and addressing their
concerns and preferences.
8.   Compliance with Laws and Regulations: Ethical risk management requires compliance with
applicable laws, regulations, and industry standards. This includes ensuring that risk management
practices adhere to legal requirements related to safety, environmental protection, data privacy,
financial reporting, and other areas.
9.   Continuous Improvement and Learning: Ethical risk management involves a commitment to
continuous improvement and  learning.  This  includes  regularly  reviewing and  updating  risk
assessments and mitigation strategies in light of new information, changing circumstances, and
evolving ethical standards.
10.  Crisis Management and Response: Ethical risk management includes preparedness for and
response  to crises and emergencies. This involves  prioritizing the safety and well-being  of

actions to mitigate harm and restore trust.

continuous improvement, and crisis management. By incorporating ethical considerations into risk
management  practices,  organizations  can  enhance   trust,  accountability,  and  long-term
sustainability.

misstatement. This will help the auditor to reduce the risk of material misstatement to an acceptably
low level.
