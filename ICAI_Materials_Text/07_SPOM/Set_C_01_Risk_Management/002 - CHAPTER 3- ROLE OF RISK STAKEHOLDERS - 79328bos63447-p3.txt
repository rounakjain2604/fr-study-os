      1.   INTRODUCTION
Risk stakeholders are individuals, groups, or organizations that have an interest or concern in the
potential risks associated with a project, business venture, or any other undertaking. These
stakeholders can be either internal or external to the organization and may have various levels of
influence and impact on risk management decisions.
Here are some key categories of risk stakeholders:
(i)   Internal Stakeholders: These are individuals or groups within the organization who are
directly involved in or affected by the project or business. They may include employees, managers,
executives, and shareholders. Internal stakeholders have a direct interest in the success and
potential risks of the undertaking.
(ii)  External  Stakeholders:  External  stakeholders  are  individuals  or  entities  outside  the
organization who can be affected by the risks associated with the project or business. They may
include customers, suppliers, regulatory authorities, creditors, investors, and the local community.
Their interests may be impacted by the  project's  risks, and they often have a say  in  risk
management decisions through regulatory requirements or contractual agreements.
(iii) Regulatory Authorities: Government agencies and other regulatory bodies play a crucial
role in overseeing and managing risks associated with various industries. They set standards,
laws, and regulations that organizations must follow to mitigate risks and ensure public safety.

(iv)  Investors and Shareholders: Shareholders and investors have a financial interest in the
success and risk management of a company. They closely monitor the risks associated with their
investments and may influence risk-related decisions through shareholder meetings and voting
rights.
(v)  Suppliers and Partners: Suppliers and business partners may be affected by the risks
associated with the project. For example, if a company faces financial difficulties, its suppliers may
not get paid, affecting their own operations. Collaborative ventures often involve shared risks and
responsibilities.
(vi) Customers: The customers of a business or project are key stakeholders as their satisfaction
and well-being can be affected by risks. Quality issues, supply chain disruptions, and other risks
can impact the customer experience.
(vii) Local Communities: For  projects  that have an  impact on  the  local environment  or
community, residents and community groups are important stakeholders. Their concerns regarding
environmental, social, or safety risks need to be considered and addressed.
(viii) Competitors: In some industries, competitors can indirectly influence  risk management
strategies. Understanding the strategies and risks taken by competitors can help organizations
adapt and make informed decisions.
The  effective management  of  risks  requires  identifying,  assessing,  and  involving  these
stakeholders in the risk management process. Their perspectives and interests can differ, so a
well-rounded approach to risk management should consider the needs and concerns of all relevant
stakeholders. This can help  in making informed decisions and implementing  risk  mitigation
strategies that align with the interests of these stakeholders.

operate on behalf of the owners to fulfill their responsibility of overall supervision and to look after
the interest of all the shareholders. At times they appoint committees of the Board to help them

      3.  ROLE OF CEO & MANAGEMENT
The CEO, as head  of management,  is  ultimately  the  individual who assumes  this  crucial
responsibility. More than any other individual in the company, the CEO provides the leadership
and the direction to the Risk management agenda through the following specific actions:
•    Assumes complete overall “ownership” of Risk management and the attendant policies,
      systems and process.
•     Sets the “tone at the top” for creating the overall Risk management conducive environment
      which promotes  the  discipline  of operating  with a Risk management system by  all,
       especially by the senior management team.
•     Establishes a formal Risk management function within the organisation which will assume
         full responsibility to perform all administrative risk management activities on his behalf.
•    Seek support of his functional heads to provide their expertise in the risk identification,
      assessment and mitigation efforts.
•     Assigns specific responsibilities to his team for establishing more specific risk management
       policies and procedures and to percolate these throughout the organisation.
•     Provide  full support  to and  insist on the completion  of Risk management  roles and
       responsibilities of self and all his senior management team.
•     Measure and monitor the performance of Risk management objectives and their timely
      implementation and completion.
•     Provide periodic updates to and seek inputs from the Board of Directors.
Importance of CEO Support
Most  of the above actions  of the CEO are expected  to be performed personally (and not
delegated) as  they  are aimed  at  promoting a  high-level impetus towards a  “risk-mature”
environment at the top, something which is very crucial to the success of a Risk management
system. These actions by the CEO make all the difference for Risk management to become either
a business imperative or an administrative formality. The former will be a long -term sustainable
proposition and add value to the enterprise, while the latter will be merely become a cost burden
and hence dissipate over time.

      4.  ROLE OF RISK MANAGERS, OWNERS
4.1  Risk Managers
A Risk Manager, deals  with  the  analysis, assessment, and  control  of  risks. They  identify
weaknesses that could damage a company or organization from a financial, operational or security
perspective, prevent them and resolve them. They are responsible for developing strategies,
processes, and systems for risk management. The job role of Risk Manager is quite essential for
successful running of a business.
The role of the Risk Manager are :-
•     Provide a methodology  to  identify and analyze  the  financial impact  of  loss  to  the
       organization, employees, the public, and the environment.
•     Examine the use of realistic and cost-effective opportunities to balance retention programs
       with commercial insurance.
•     Prepare risk management and insurance budgets and allocate claim costs and premiums to
      departments and divisions.
•     Provide for the establishment and maintenance of records including insurance policies,
       claim and loss experience.
•      Assist in the review of major contracts, proposed facilities, and/or new program activities for
       loss and insurance implications.
•      In cooperation with General Counsel, maintain control over the claims process to assure
       that claims are being settled fairly, consistently, and in the best interest of the entity.
4.2  Risk Owners
A risk owner can be defined as an individual generally at the senior leadership level who is
responsible and accountable  for identifying, analyzing, and mitigating various risks and aslo
ensuring  their appropriate management  within the organization. Not only  that they work  in
collaboration with various stakeholders  to coordinate  efforts  to address  specific  risk areas,
developing strategies, and implementing measures to mitigate risks. They can be considered as
the primary  point  of  contact  for overseeing  the  effectiveness  of  risk  mitigation  strategies,
collaborating with other personnel involved in risk management activities, and ensuring overall
project success.

The role of the Risk Owner are:-
(i)   Identification and Assessment of Risks: Risk Owners identifies and assess both short term
and long terms risks against business unit targets and ensure their alignment with organizational
objectives and risk tolerance.
(ii)  Emerging Risk: Not only the existing risks, Risk Owner is also expected to identify the
potential  threats and emerging  risks by continuously monitoring both  internal and  external
environment.
(iii) Assignment  to  stakeholders:   It  is  the  responsibility  of  the  Risk Owner  to  assign
responsibilities of various sub risks to various internal stakeholders within the organization and
ensuring clearing accountability.
(iv)  Allocation of Resources:  It is the duty of Risk Owner to ensure that adequate support is
available for effective risk management activities hence he is required to recommend and allocate
sufficient resources for managing risks within responsible areas.
(v)  Implementation of Plans: Risk owners implement the existing controls and mitigation plans
to the ensure the compliance as established risk management protocols.
(vi)  Fostering Risk Culture: Risk Owner should promote a culture of risk awareness within the
organization and should ensure that all subordinates should understand the importance of risk
management and integrate the same in their business unit level.
(vii) Follow-up  and  Review  of  Risk  Management:  Risk  Owner  should  oversee  the
implementation of risk management measures for Business Unit risks, ensuring that appropriate
mitigations are in place to maintain an acceptable risk level.
He should conduct quarterly reviews, or as appropriate, to assess the status of risks and mitigation
activities, identifying any gaps or areas needing improvement.
(viii) Addressing gaps: Risk Owner should address any gaps  in mitigation and monitoring
activities promptly, taking corrective actions to strengthen risk management practices.
(ix) Reporting and Communication: Risk Owners are supposed  to regularly report on the
progress  of  risk mitigation  efforts and the status  of Key Risk  Indicators (KRIs)  to relevant
stakeholders on periodic basis as necessary. Not only that he/ she should communicate the status
of mitigation and monitoring efforts to the high level to ensure transparency and accountability in
risk management practices.

for an organization's risk management framework. However, the Risk Manager focuses on the

      5.  ROLE OF AUDITORS (STATUTORY/INTERNAL)
These parties are quite interested in an independent evaluation of the organisation’s success from
their own perspective . Therefore they undertake their own risk assessment using the information
available  to them and can provide valuable feedback to the company on  their independent
perception of the risks which the company faces.
5.1  External Auditor
Their responsibility of providing an independent and objective evaluation of the financial health of
the organisation makes  it imperative on them to undertake a detailed review of all the risks that
could have a direct bearing on the financial statements. Hence they are a very authentic and rich
source of information of ERM, and the Boards tend to rely heavily on their inputs and feedback.
5.2  Internal Auditor
They evaluate the effectiveness of all governance, risk and controls measures and monitor the
company’s compliances  to ensure  that  significant exposures  are addressed  (else  reported
on).They assist both the management and the Board through their independent evaluation of the
effectiveness of the Risk management system and the reliability of the output and reports from this
system.
