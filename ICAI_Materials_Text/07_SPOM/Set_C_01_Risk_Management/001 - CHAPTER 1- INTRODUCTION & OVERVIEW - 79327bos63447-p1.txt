      1.   INTRODUCTION TO RISK & UNCERTAINTY
We often find the terms  “risk” and “uncertainty” used interchangeably. However, a distinction
needs to be drawn between the two. Risk is the chance or probability, whether high or low, that
someone will be harmed by a hazard. Risk is often thought of in terms of chance (or probability) of
loss. Uncertainty  falls into two broad categories. There are those for which the probability of
occurrence is calculable either on a priori grounds or through the statistical analysis of a series of
similar events  that have occurred  in the past. The remainder does not lend them  to such
measurement either because their occurrence follows no discernable pattern or because they are
unique events. Professor Frank Night called the first group risks, whereas the latter he described
as uncertainties, in his book Risk, Uncertainty and Profit. John Maynard Keynes a well known
economist considers  that the element  of surprise  is an important element  of a  situation  of
uncertainty. The importance of uncertainty arises from its influence on the process of decision-
making of individuals, businesses as also society.

While risk is a state of nature, uncertainty is a state of human mind.  It is therefore possible to
consider a situation risky  if a number of outcomes  is possible and the actual outcome that
materializes is not known in advance. Thus, risk is defined as the relative variation of the actual
outcome from the anticipated or expected outcome. For instance, for a manufacturing firm, the
development of a new product is risky as the profits from the sale of the product in the market are
uncertain before the actual sale. Likewise, the development of a new drug by a pharmaceutical
company is characterized by risk because of the range of possible outcomes with regard to the
market reception for the drug.

      2. CLASSIFICATION & TYPES OF RISKS
In practice, risks get classified in one of these four different ways:
(i)    Risk based on Source – Depending on the source of origination, risks can be classified as
       follows:
            Internal
            External
(ii)   Risk based on Nature – Depending on how much influence one can exert on the risk,
       risks can be classified as follows:
            Controllable
            Uncontrollable
(iii)   Risk based on Status – Depending on whether or not we have applied any risk treatment
       or mitigatory actions, risks can be classified as follows:
           Inherent
           Residual
(iv)   Risk based on Consequences – Depending on the outcome of the risk on various aspects
       or functions of a business, risks can be classified as follows:
           Strategy
           Operations
            Financial
2.1  Risk Based on source – Internal and External Risks
Internal risks pertain to events within the enterprise and usually arise during the normal course of
the business. Since they are the outcome of actions internal to the business, they are easier to
anticipate and their occurrence is more manageable in nature.
Examples of Internal Risks:

2.2  Risk Based on Nature – Controllable and Uncontrollable Risks
Risks where the outcome can be influenced by management to a great extent (either their impact
or the probability of occurrence) are Controllable Risks and conversely, those that cannot be
influenced to a reasonable extent are considered to be Uncontrollable Risks. There are various
ways to control and manage risks and there are many different ways to exert influence over their
outcome. However,  in a  nutshell,  this influence  is exerted on  either the  probability  of the
occurrence or the impact the event will have, once it occurs.
Examples of Controllable Risks:
•     Safety precautions taken on the factory floor to reduce the chance of an accident (Internal
       Risk).
•     Technology deployed to improve the product features to cater better to evolve consumer
     demand (External Risk).
•    Employee sensitization towards security threats to prevent system hacking and loss of
      company’s sensitive information (Internal and External Risk).
Examples of Uncontrollable Risks:
•    An aggrieved employee chooses to sabotage some production machinery (an Internal Risk):
•     Flooding in a certain part of the country brings to a halt all production in the region (an
       External Risk).
•      Terrorist strikes and bomb-blasts are also high up on the list of uncontrollable risks as there
        is precious little which can be done to prevent them, despite high security consciousness
      and sophisticated arrangements.
These controllable risks are easier to predict and the ability to influence their outcome is generally
much better, especially  if the sources are internal in nature. Companies are in a position to take
various preventable measures towards controllable risk, especially due to their predictability.
Uncontrollable risks are generally harder to predict since  its, difficult to anticipate their source.
Usually companies can only react to these types of risks to minimize their impact after the event
has happened. With the advancements in science and technology, man has found a way to

Relationship between Source and Nature of Risk
As can be observed from the discussion so far, there is a relationship between the source of the
risks (internal/ external) and the nature of the risk (controllable /uncontrollable). These risks are
not mutually exclusive, in that an external risk is not controllable or uncontrollable. Similarly a
controllable risk is not only internal or external. Any combination or a situation somewhere in
between  is possible. The main thing to remember  is that the internal risks are usually more
controllable, as explained earlier, while the external risks are more in the nature of uncontrollable
risks.
One way to depict this relationship is as follows:
                                     Risk Relationships
                       Controllable        Uncontrollable

         Internal         Lowest                High

        External        Low                 Highest


As can be seen, risk is highest for external uncontrollable factors and conversely, its lowest for
internal controllable factors. So, to better manage these risks, one would explore the possibility of
moving risks form the higher to the lower region, as shown by the two arrows above, and therefore
control external risks by internal measures, etc.,
Example of Controlling External Risk:
It was generally believed that external risks are impossible or very difficult to manage. A typical
example was that of laws and regulations and the belief was that one can only react to these
events. However, now with the lobbying for favourable legislation and against unfavorable laws is
an established business especially in the USA. In fact, the situation there is so bad that new laws
and regulations had to be issued recently just to curtail the “business of lobbying”.
2.3  Risk Based on Status – Inherent and Residual Risk
Earlier we mentioned how risks are  “all pervasive in nature” meaning they are inherent in  all
business activity. They are present in a latent manner in everything we do due to their inherent
uncertainty. Hence when we call a risk an “Inherent Risk”, we acknowledge the presence of a risk
in ‘raw form’ before any risk treatment / mitigation activity has been applied to it. Risk is, therefore,
implicitly assumed to exit.

2.4  Risk Based on Consequences – Outcome of Event
Risks are also classified in the manner in which these emanate and how we expect them to unfold
and have their impact on various aspects /functions of the business. Typical areas of consequence
are:
(A)    Strategic
(B)    Operations
(C)   Brand / Reputation
(D)   Customer
(E)   Finance
(F)   Law and Regulations
(G)   Knowledge
(H)   Technology
(I)    Management and Employees (People)
(J)    Location/Geography
This is by no means a complete or comprehensive  list. Many risks are quite unique to some
industries and may have their own unique impact. For example, who could have anticipated the
leakage of toxic gas from the Union Carbide plant In Bhopal in 1984 which consumed the lives of
thousands of ordinary citizens living nearby and causing the biggest industrial disaster of the time?
So, it's almost impossible to account for all possible eventualities. There will always be more than
one way of categorizing the risks and one risk could always be assigned in more than one
category.
The main aspects of each of the categories indicated above are as follows:
(A)  Strategic Risks: These are risks of a very significant nature impacting the fundamental
business of the company and impacting the long-term objectives or viability of the enterprise (as
opposed to the short-term or annual objectives). They generally concern the significant changes to
the operating business environment such as the industry or the changing political dynamics and
economic condition in the country, etc.

      3. IMPORTANCE & OBJECTIVES
3.1  Importance of Risk Management
Governance functions include planning and budgeting, performance measurement, assurance and
auditing, procurement, hiring, assessing staff as well as control over all day-to-day operations. The
management of an organization, enabled by its governance arrangements, can be described as
“coordinated activities to direct and control an organization”. Risk management  is defined as
“coordinated activities to direct and control an organization with regard to risk”. The parallels
between these two statements demonstrate how closely risk management and governance are
linked.
Risk Management is one of the important pillars of Governance and arguably the only tool to deal
with business uncertainty. Risk Management is used most successfully by Fortune 500 and other
large companies to sustain and grow their businesses. Risk management is recognised as an
integral component of good management and governance. It is an iterative process consisting of
steps, which, when undertaken in sequence, enable continual improvement in decision making.

Risk management  is the term applied to a logical and systematic method of establishing the
context, identifying, analysing, evaluating, treating, monitoring and communicating risks associated
with any activity, function or process in a way that will enable organisations to minimise losses and
maximize opportunities.
Risk management is as much about identifying opportunities as avoiding or mitigating losses.
Risk consequences can be fatal to any business. The expenditure of fixing damage and/or the loss
of valued assets or even customers to competition after a catastrophe can have a significant
impact on the bottom line of a business. By identifying and managing risks entities are able to
actively protect value from any potential catastrophes and save valuable time and money. A risk
management plan and system is there to do more than identify risk, a good system should also
quantify the risk, predict the impact, and put procedures in place to mitigate the risk, or even
eliminate it to the extent possible.
3.2  Objectives of Risk Management
The main objective of risk management is to protect the property, earnings and personnel of the
organisation against losses and legal  liabilities that may be incurred due to various  risks.  It
minimises cost of the risk and maximises the profitability. The accidental risks may not only result
in unexpected costs to a company but threaten its survival altogether. Thus, it is essential for the
management to do some exercise to know about the possibility of a ‘risk event’, sources of such
event and impact of such event beforehand and consider ways and means of preventing, reducing
or minimising the loss. A risk manager has to take necessary measures with least possible costs to
bring the functioning of the organisation back to normal when the risk event takes place. Now, the
corporate sector in India is increasingly recognising the importance of employing independent risk
managers  to manage  their  risks.  In  the changed  scenario  the main  function  of  the  risk
management  is that  it should control not only the pure risks but also extend services to the
analysis and control of all types of risks arising out of Business.

      4.  OVERVIEW OF MITIGATION & CONTROLS
4.1  Risk Mitigation
The  first step in the process of managing risk is identifying the potential risks involved in the
instant case. The risks must then be assessed as to their potential severity of likely losses that
might have to be suffered and to the probability of occurrence, time and again. Risk is a measure
of the inability to achieve overall programme objectives within the defined cost and time schedule,
and technical constraints that may be embedded therein and has two components:
(a)    the probability of failing to achieve a particular outcome and
(b)    the consequences of failing to achieve that outcome.

Possible Alternative Actions - Once risks have been identified and assessed, the next strategy is
to devise plans how to deal with the risks so identified and analysed as to their outcomes. There
cannot be any ready-made solution that will match every finding. The techniques to manage the
risk usually fall into one or more of the four major categories as may be envisaged
–     Avoidance:  It includes deliberate attempt on part of the person taking risk decision not to
      perform an activity or not to accept a proposal, which is risk prone.
–      Retention:  It involves accepting the loss when  it occurs by taking risky proposal or risky
      assignment where there are no other alternatives to avoid risk.
–      Transfer: It means causing another party to accept the risk, typically by contract. It involves
      a process of shifting risk responsibility on others.
–     Hedging: Risk hedging  is a systematic process  of reducing  risk associated  with an
      investment proposal or in some other assignments where risk is inevitable i.e. the risk is of
      such nature that it cannot be avoided altogether
Ideal use of all these strategies at any particular point of time and in respect of any specific risk
problem may not be possible in real life situations for various reasons. These strategies are not
mutually exclusive rather they are exhaustive. Some of them may involve trade-offs that are not
acceptable to the organization or person making the risk management decisions. A judicious
combination of these may at best help better risk management.
4.2  Risk Control
Controlling the risk by selecting those techniques that will reduce the frequency of loss and reduce
the severity of loss is Risk Control. Before deciding upon the specific risk control measure to be
adopted  it is necessary to conduct a cost-benefit exercise. That is an evaluation of the expected
benefits and costs involved to determine whether the decision taken is economically sound or not.
However, where there is no option in regard to the risk control activity but is part of a contract or
imposed by provision of law, cost benefit exercise has no place or validity.
Risk Control covers all those measures aimed at avoiding / eliminating / reducing the chances of
loss producing events. The purpose of risk control  is to minimize the total cost of risk to an
organization whilst at the same time ensuring the long-term economic survival of the organization.
