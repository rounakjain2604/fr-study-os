Operational risk begins with the initiation of business operations and encompasses a spectrum of
potential hazards,  including  disruptions,  fraudulent  activities, human  errors,  malicious  acts,
breaches of laws and regulations, as well as digital attacks and disasters. This risk factor has been
inherent in business operations since their inception, and the evolution of business practices has
spurred the refinement of operational risk management.
In the financial services industry, operational risk is also referred to as non-financial risk, setting it
apart from credit risk, market risk, and liquidity risk, which stem from purely financial activities such
as credit allocation and market trading. Unlike these financial risks, operational risk originates from
non-financial sources such as flawed processes, human elements, or system breakdowns.
Operational  risk  is defined as the  risk of losses resulting from inadequate or failed internal
processes,  people and  systems  or  from  external  events.  (Basel  Committee on  Banking
Supervision, 2003)

     2. OUTSOURCING RISKS & 3RD PARTY RISK
An outsourcing decision is often called a ‘make or buy’ decision.  It involves a decision about
whether to continue 'making' a product versus ‘buying’  it from an external firm. Outsourcing
enables a firm to
•     reduce costs or
•      benefit from supplier efficiencies
Outsourcing decision requires incremental analysis. The incremental amounts are based on the
difference in the cost of buying a product or service compared to the cost of producing the item or
providing the service in house.
Incremental costs are the additional costs incurred from outsourcing. The main cost  is the
purchase price of the products or the cost of the services that are being provided by external firms.

Incremental cost savings are reductions in costs that will no longer be incurred as a result of
outsourcing. They are often called avoidable costs because if a company outsources, it can 'avoid'
certain costs. Variable product cost savings are always incremental. Because they reduce total
costs, they cause profits to increase. In some circumstances, a portion of fixed costs, such as
equipment rental costs or supervisor salaries, can be avoided.
Opportunity costs are the costs forgone as a result of selecting a different alternative. They are
always incremental. For example, if a company decides to outsource, it is able to lease its factory
space that the product being outsourced will no longer occupy.
While considering the decision to outsource, the management should consider qualitative aspects
like quality of goods, reliability of suppliers, impact on customers and suppliers, etc.
2.1 When should a company outsource?
A firm generally decides to outsource:
•         If it costs less than to manufacture it internally.
•         If the return on the necessary investment to be made to manufacture  is not attractive
      enough.
•         If the company does not have the requisite skilled manpower to make.
•         If the concern feels that manufacturing internally will mean additional labour problems.
•         If adequate managerial manpower  is not available to take charge of the extra work of
      manufacturing.
•         If the component shows much seasonal demand,  resulting  in a considerable  risk  of
       maintaining inventories.
•         If transport and other infrastructure facilities are adequately available.
•         If the process of making is confidential or patented.
•         If there is a risk of technological obsolescence for the component, such that  it does not
      encourage capital investment in the component.
2.2 Risks of Outsourcing

      outsourced software development,  it is essential to acknowledge the risks associated with
       relinquishing day-to-day project management control. This inevitably results in a decreased

2.3 How to avoid outsourcing risk?
Mitigating  the  risks associated  with outsourcing may appear  daunting,  but  with meticulous
planning,   it can be achieved. BPO  firms  that  proactively address  risks gain a  significant
competitive edge. Here are some recommended steps for minimizing outsourcing risk:
(i)    Address  Critical Issues: The  initial step  involves  tackling  risks  directly. By openly
       discussing critical issues, BPO companies can proactively manage and mitigate potential
       risks before they escalate into crises.
(ii)   Shared Responsibility: Sharing  responsibility among  all  parties  involved  is another
       effective strategy. This approach prevents blame games and ensures mutual satisfaction.
(iii)   Goal Alignment: Setting clear and achievable business goals is crucial for risk mitigation.
      Thoroughly  researched  goals  help  maintain  customer   satisfaction  and  prevent
      disappointments.

     3. SUPPLY CHAIN (SALE / PURCHASE)
A complete chain of serving customers or consumers, whether linked or interdependent, is the
composition of the supply chain.  It comprises vendors that supply raw materials, producers who
convert the material into products, warehouses that store them, distribution centres that deliver to
retailers and retailers who sell the product to the ultimate user.
Supply chains encourage value chains because, without them, no producer has the ability to give
customers what they want, when and where they want, at the price they want. Deficiencies in the
supply chain reduce the ability of the producers to compete with each other.
The term supply chain can be referred to as the entire network of organisations working together to
design, produce, deliver, and service products. In other words, all activities associated with the
flow and transformation of goods from raw material to end user- is called supply chain.
The transformation of a product from node to node includes activities such as −
•     Production Planning
•     Purchasing
•      Material Management
•      Distribution

The Global Supply Chain Forum (GSCF) defines supply chain management as the “integration of
key business processes from end user through original suppliers that provides products, services,
and information that add value for customers and other stakeholders”.
The following eight supply chain management processes are included in the GSCF framework:
      Customer Relationship Management, to manage and analyse customer’s interaction and❑
      data throughout the life cycle with the main motive of improving business relations.
       Supplier  Relationship Management  provides  the  structure  for how  relationships  with❑
       suppliers are developed and maintained.
      Customer Service Management provides the key points of contact for administering product❑
      and service agreements.
     Demand Management provides the structure for optimising the customer's requirements❑
       with supply chain capabilities.
      Order Fulfilment, includes all activities necessary to define customer requirements, design❑
       the logistics network, and fill customer orders.
      Manufacturing Flow Management,  includes  all  activities necessary  to move products❑

      Product Development and Commercialization, provides the structure for developing and❑

      Returns  Management,  includes   all  activities  related  to  returns,  reverse  logistics,❑
      gatekeeping, and avoidance.
3.1 Types of Supply Chain

3.1.1 Push and Pull
During  the  traditional  chain,  suppliers were  at one  end.  Suppliers  give  their  products  to
manufacturers or distributers who further send them to retailers. Although customers are the
source of profits, they are at the end of the chain in the ‘push’ model.

Under Push Model stocks are produced on the basis of anticipated demand. Demand forecasting
can be done via a variety of sophisticated techniques, such as operations research or data mining.
Under Pull Model stocks are produced in response to actual demand. This new business model is
less product-centric and more directly focused on the individual consumer, a more marketing
oriented approach.
Electronic connections are used in the pull model to meet the needs of customers.
▪      Electronic supply chain connectivity gives end customers the opportunity to give direction to
       suppliers, for example, about the precise specifications of the products they want.
▪      Ultimately, customers have a direct voice in the functioning of the supply chain.
Supply chain created through E-Commerce brings benefits to both customers and manufacturers
thus facilitating the companies to fulfil customer needs, carry fewer inventories, and send products
to market more quickly.

3.1.2 Upstream and Downstream Flow
A supply chain begins right from the supplier and finally ends with the end customer or consumer.
In the total chain, there are flows of material, information, and capital or finance. When the flow
relates to the supplier it is termed as upstream flow. If the flow is with consumers or customers, it
is called downstream flow.
3.2 Benefits of the Supply Chain
Benefits of the supply chain are enormous for any business. A highly controlled supply chain
fetches  tangible  benefits  such  as  inventory  reduction,  personnel  reduction,  productivity
improvement, order management improvement, financial cycle improvement, etc. Further, it results
in information visibility, new or improved processes, customer responsiveness, standardization,
flexibility, and globalization of business performance.
3.3 Building a Resilient and Sustainable Supply Chain
Over the past decades, with the advent of globalization, supply chains have spread across many
countries. Businesses are now more likely to be impacted by developments from across the world.
In the recent past, some of the major disruptions that highlighted weaknesses in supply chains are:
▪      Disruptions to supply chains were caused by the Covid pandemic.
▪      Geo-political tensions conflict impacted critical supplies like food and energy.

     4. PRODUCT / SERVICE QUALITY ETC.
Operational  risk  refers  to the  potential  for loss  resulting from inadequate or  failed  internal
processes, systems, people, or external events. This type of risk can have a significant impact on
the quality of a product. Here are several ways in which operational risk can affect product quality:
1.    Process Failures:
         •      Production Processes:  If there are failures or inefficiencies in the manufacturing or
             production processes, it can lead to defects in the final product.

         •     Supply Chain Disruptions: Operational risks in the supply chain, such as delays,
              disruptions, or quality issues with raw materials, can  affect the overall product
               quality.
2.    Human Error:
         •     Employee Mistakes: Errors made by employees in various stages of the production
            process can result in defects or inconsistencies in the final product.
         •      Training and Competency: Insufficient training or competency of personnel can lead
               to mistakes that impact product quality.
3.    Technology Failures:
         •     Equipment  Malfunctions:  Technical  failures  or  malfunctions  in  machinery  or
            equipment used in the production process can result in defects or deviations from
               quality standards.
         •      Software Glitches:  If software systems are involved  in the production or quality
              control process, glitches or failures can compromise the quality of the product.
4.    Compliance and Regulatory Issues:
         •     Non-Compliance: Failure to adhere to industry regulations or quality standards can
               result in product recalls, fines, and damage to the brand's reputation.
         •      Regulatory Changes: Changes in regulations or standards may pose challenges in
             adapting processes to comply with new requirements, affecting product quality.
5.    Communication Breakdowns:
         •       Internal Communication: Poor communication within the organization can lead to
             misunderstandings, misinterpretations, and mistakes that affect product quality.
         •      External Communication: Communication breakdowns with suppliers or customers
            can lead to issues such as incorrect specifications or changes not being properly
            communicated.
6.     Security Concerns:
         •     Data Security: If there are breaches in data security, it can lead to the compromise of
              sensitive information related to product specifications, manufacturing processes, or
               quality control measures.
         •       Intellectual  Property  Protection:  Operational  risks  related  to  the  protection  of
               intellectual property can impact the uniqueness and quality of a product.
