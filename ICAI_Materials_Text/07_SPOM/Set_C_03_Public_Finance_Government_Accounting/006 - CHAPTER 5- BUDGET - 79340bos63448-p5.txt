5.2           PUBLIC FINANCE AND GOVERNMENT ACCOUNTING

     1.  BUDGET
The Budget is derived from the French word “Bougette” which means a small leather bag or pouch.
The phrase was first used in 1773 in England for the bag of proposals the chancellor carried to the
parliament for approval. Long history of struggle for parliamentary control over the purse was a
struggle for control over taxation,. The control over expenditure came much later and as by product
of the concern  for the protection of tax payers and expanding  activities or functions of the
government.
Ideally, the government design the budget in such a way that optimally allocates scarce resources,
after taking into account all the socio, economic and political considerations. The fiscal position of
the state is presented through budgets primarily to meet accountability and to increase transparency.
The government  budget  is  both a  product  of government  administration and  of  political
democratisation (Guoxian, 2001).1
In each financial year (according to Article 202 of the constitution) the finance department of the
State is mandated to prepare an Annual Financial Statement2 (what is popularly called as Budget).
This statement should contain all the receipts and expenditures which are collected, expected to be
realized and incurred during the financial year. The statement should report expenditures under
charged and voted in revenue and capital account separately.
India adopted Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management (2003) at central level and same law
is ratified and enacted at state level. The budget execution process is conditional upon the FRBM
laws and it is also a mechanism to achieve fiscal sustainability at state level.
Of late, Gender Budget, Child Budget, Performance Budget, Outcome Budget and SDG Budget have
proved important Public Financial Management (PFM) tools to ensure that appropriate allocations
are earmarked to priority social sectors. These specific budgeting aspects have been termed as
reforming measures and bringing transparency in the budget execution process in India.
Revenue Receipts Vs Capital Receipts: Capital Receipts are described as the funds generated
from the business of non-operating sources like proceeds from the sale of long-term assets, recovery
of loans and advances, and various other non-debt capital receipts. In contrast, revenue receipts
are the result of government’s routine activities during the financial year.

Revenue Expenditure Vs Capital Expenditure:
Revenue Expenditure is a routine expenditure incurred in the normal course of business and
includes cost of sales and maintenance of fixed assets.
Capital Expenditure incurred in acquiring or improving permanent assets not meant for resale.

     2.  FISCAL INDICATORS
Revenue Deficit: Revenue deficit is the gap between current revenue expenditure of the Central /
State government and current revenue receipts that include tax and non-tax revenue receipts.
Revenue deficit =Revenue Expenditure-Revenue Receipts

Effective Revenue Deficit: Effective Revenue Deficit is the difference between Revenue Deficit and
Grants for Creation of Capital Assets.
Effective Revenue Deficit=Revenue Deficit-Grants in Aid for Creation of Capital Assets
Fiscal Deficit: Fiscal Deficit is reflective of the total borrowing requirements of Government. It is
defined as a sum of Revenue Deficit, capital outlay and net lending.
Fiscal Deficit = Revenue Deficit (Revenue Expenditure - Revenue Receipts) + Capital Outlay + Net
Lending (Loans and Advances  - Recovery of Loans and Advances  - Other Non-debt Capital
Receipts)
Fiscal Deficit reflects the aggregate resource gap of the government. High fiscal deficit can have
adverse macroeconomic implications as  it results on high interest rate.  Fiscal deficit can be
inflationary.
Primary Deficit: Primary deficit is the concept to measure the aggregate resource gap of the
government in a current fiscal year. It is measured as Fiscal Deficit less interest payments.
Primary Deficit=Fiscal Deficit-Interest Payments

     5.  BUDGET EXECUTION
Budget Execution involves purchase of goods and services required to produce outputs, and
reporting, review of achievements in terms of delivery of goods and services.
Appropriation of funds5: After the passing of the demands under Article 113, Appropriation Bill is
introduced in the Lok Sabha / Assembly (Under Article 114) to provide for the appropriation out of
the Consolidated Fund of the state. The amounts are withdrawn to meet the expenditures proposed
under demand for grants and the expenditures charged on the Consolidated Fund of the State.
Importantly, the appropriation should exceed the amount approved and passed by the Assembly.
Further, subject to the provisions of articles 115 and 116, no money shall be withdrawn from the
Consolidated Fund of India except under appropriation made by law passed in accordance with the
provisions of this article.
Communication of Grants: Communication of Grants is the first step in budget execution. It is the
responsibility of the Finance Department to communicate the line ministries (Heads of Departments
and Budget Controlling officers) about their approved demand for grants. The Finance Department
will send written communication regarding the detailed allocations which are approved by the
Legislative Assembly.


5 ‘Appropriation’ - means the amount authorized by the Parliament for expenditure under different primary
unit of appropriation or part thereof placed at the disposal of a disbursing officer.
Reappropriation’ - means the transfer, by a competent authority, of savings from one unit of appropriation
to meet additional expenditure under another unit within the same grant or charged appropriation.

Budget Controlling Officers (BCOs): Once, the finance department transferred the funds to
respective departments in the IFMS, the funds will be at the disposal of the budget controlling
officers. Now, BCOs should distribute/release the funds to disbursing officers at zonal and district
level at the earliest depending on the requirement.
Supplementary or Additional or Excess Grants6:
According to Article-115, if the amount authorized through appropriations for a particular goods and
service is found to be insufficient for the purposes of that year or when a need has arisen due to
unforeseen  events a  supplementary demands  for  grants  proposal  shall be made  before
Parliament/Assembly. However, in cases where higher amounts are spent than approved under
certain heads, excess amount needs to request through a demand before Parliament/Assembly.
Intergovernmental Transfer System in India and Budget execution7
As highlighted above, smooth fund flow mechanism is very crucial for effective budget execution. To
understand the fund flow mechanism in India, we must understand the Intergovernmental Transfer
System in India. In the Indian context, there are two main channels through which the funds flow to
the states. The Finance Commission (FC) transfers and grants given by the ministries. Earlier a
significant part of intergovernmental transfers (IGT) for state plans were channelled through the
Planning Commission of India. Since 2015-16, this route has been subsumed within the FC with the
dissolution of the Planning Commission. The institutional structure of IGTs in India is sketched in

The Finance Commissions are tasked with the role of assigning the central taxes between the union
and the state governments and across the state governments, as well as suggest transfers to the
lower tier of the government, i.e., the local governments. This is sought to be done in a manner so
as to address the vertical and horizontal imbalances across different levels of governments and
states. Besides taxes, FC recommends two kinds of grants, general purpose grants and specific
purpose grants to states (Figure 2). An example of the latter are grants on education to states.
The budget execution at state level depends on the smooth fund flow mechanism. The magnitude
and timing of funds under state share in the tax devolutions and other finance commission (FC),
Central sector (CS), centrally sponsored schemes (CSS) play a huge role in efficient budget
execution at state level.
First, there is a significant increase in the devolution of central taxes to the states over the years.
The 14th FC signalled a compositional shift in transfers from grants to tax devolution so as to
increase the flow of unconditional transfers to the states. While the share of tax devolution increased
from 32 percent to 42 percent of the divisible pool, sector-specific Finance Commission grants were
dispensed with, by the 14th FC.

The second are the qualitative changes – embracing important institutional and technological
aspects – to increase the efficacy of the central schemes. From April, 2014, the GoI started routing
the fund flow through state treasury rather than directly disbursing to the implementing agencies
(society route). The basic motivation behind the society route was to disallow CSS transfers to be
utilized by the states for their ways and means purpose, to avoid delays due to legislative approval
process and delays of administrative nature. But, over time, due to improvement in the state
treasuries facilitated through technological interventions, the funds began to be released through
treasury mode, on the recommendations of the Rangarajan Committee on Public Expenditure
Management (GoI, 2011).
Revenue Mobilisation and collection:
When it come revenue mobilisation, the budget should make realistic projections so that there will
not be much deviation between budget estimates and revised estimates of the state. This will also
enable the state to plan its borrowings prudently.
The Government should keep tract of revenue mobilisation on monthly basis. All the departments
should be instructed  clearly  that  all the possible revenue mobilisation measures should be
implemented and realised regularly. It is the duty of the departmental Controlling Officers to see that
all sums due to Government are regularly and promptly assessed, realised and duly credited into
the Government account.
The Controlling Officers should also crosscheck the monthly accounts with the statements of
treasury credits furnished by the Accountant General, to ensure that the revenues reported as
collected have been duly credited to Government account.
Recoveries and Refunds: All the recoveries are treated as receipts and all the refunds are deducted
under the respective revenue heads. Hence, No appropriation is needed for such recoveries and
refunds.

     6.  BUDGET MONITORING AND CONTROL
This phase involves ensuring budget management and control of actual budget transactions.
The budget control systems should be at all levels of administration. The Finance Department and
departmental heads should ensure that departmental accounts are maintained by controlling officers
and the progressive actuals are verified every month with those entered in the books of the
Accountant General.

functions of the treasury. The treasury operations should be reviewed periodically to improve its
efficiency. The treasury should keep track of Personal Deposit (PD) accounts, other unspent
balances, and cash balances under each department.

Integrated Financial Management System (IFMS)8:
Budget Controlling Officer and Drawing and Disbursing Officer wise allocations made in the State
are recorded under the Integrated Financial Management System (IFMS). BCOs will maintain a
Register of Grants and Appropriations in a prescribed format. This register contains all the details
of the original grants, distribution of grants amongst subordinate officers, and modifications made
therein from time  to time.  All  this information  will be uploaded on the Integrated Financial
Management System. This will help government to monitor the budget execution in real time.
The IFMS system greatly improved the budget execution process, reduced the time lags, human
errors and enhanced real time monitoring.
Contingency Fund
The contingency fund is created and maintained under the “ Contingency Fund rules”. When there
is some unforeseen events or need, the governments are in urgency to do expenditures, the usual
process will take time to appropriate the funds. Hence, for any unforeseen expenditures which are
not approved in the appropriation act, an advance may be sanctioned from the Contingency Fund,
for meeting such expenditure pending its authorisation by the Legislature through an Appropriation
Act. The Finance Department share all the copies of all sanctions of advance from Contingency
Fund to Auditor General. The recoupment of the Contingency Fund should be made at the earliest
by presenting supplementary estimates to the Legislative Assembly at the appropriate time during
the year.

Public Account of the State:
As mentioned earlier, the government is a custodian of the Public Account. The Government acts
more like a banker. The components of Public Account are as under:
Table-1: Public Account components:

 Item                                          Major Heads

 Suspense and Miscellaneous                    8656 to 8680

The moneys thus received are kept in the public account and the connected disbursements are also
made therefrom. Importantly, public account funds do not belong to Government and have to be paid
back in some time to the people, organisations and institutions who deposited them. Hence, the
Legislative authorisation for payments from the public account is not required (GoI, 2010). There
may be certain exceptions. For example, Road Development Funds or Sugar Development Fund,
where legislative intervention is required through appropriations.
