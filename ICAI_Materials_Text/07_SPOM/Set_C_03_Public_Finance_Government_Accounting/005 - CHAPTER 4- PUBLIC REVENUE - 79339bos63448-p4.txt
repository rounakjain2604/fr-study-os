      1.  TAX AND NON-TAX REVENUE FOR CENTRE
According to the Bastable, “a tax is a compulsory contribution of the wealth of a person, or body of
persons for the service of public powers”. Black’s law dictionary defined tax as “in a general sense,
a tax is any contribution imposed by government upon individuals, for the use and service of the
state, whether under the name of toll, tribute, impost, duty, custom, excise, subsidy, aid, supply, or
other name”. Taxes are compulsory payments to the respective governments in order to enable them
to provide public goods, services and sometimes merit goods. But, tax does not guarantee any “quid
pro quo”.
There may be multiple aims and objectives to taxation, from revenue raising, production and
distribution to behavioural changes in consumption. According to Musgrave (1959), taxes may be
classified into different categories based on different dimensions:
1.    Taxes may be imposed in the product or in the factor markets.
2.    Taxes may be imposed on the seller’s or the buyer’s side of the market.
3.    Taxes may be imposed on households or firms.
4.    Taxes may be imposed the sources or users side of the taxpayer’s account.

In other words, taxes may be imposed on the holding of wealth or stocks, rather than on transactions
or flows generated  in current production. For example, Property tax and wealth  tax. Other
categorisation can be personal versus in rem taxes. Musgrave (1959) defines, personal taxes are
taxes, which are adjusted to the taxpayer’s personal ability to pay and in rem taxes are imposed on
activities or objects (such as sales, purchases or holding of property), independently of the
characteristics of the owner. Among all the classifications, direct and indirect taxes classification is
used in most of the official tax data maintenance.
Direct and Indirect Taxes:
There are many different definitions of direct and indirect taxation given over the centuries. But, the
fundamental idea in all the definitions is more or less same in all the definitions.
According to Attkinson “historically, the distinction primarily arose due to administrative method or
legal framework and later shifted to economic effects and incidence of tax”. (1980, 426)
According to Colwyn Committee (1927) and Fromm and Taubman (1973), “a direct tax system is
better from an economic view point”.
According to Buchanan, “Direct taxation is defined as taxation imposed upon the person who is
intended to be the final bearer of the burden of payment. Indirect taxation is defined as taxation
imposed upon others than the person who is intended to bear the final burden” (1970, 141).
According to Due and Friedlaender, “Some taxes, often called direct taxes, reduce the real incomes
of the persons who pay them to the government ... Other taxes may be shifted from some persons
to others ... Taxes believed to be shifted in this fashion are called indirect taxes” (1973, 229).
According to Shoup, “the most useful distinction to make between direct and indirect taxes is that
direct taxes can be “personalized” or tailored to the particular economic and social characteristics
of the household being taxed, whereas indirect taxes cannot be so tailored” (1964, 301).
According to Stiglitz “Taxes can be divided into two broad categories: direct taxes on individual and
corporations and indirect taxes on a variety of goods and services” (1986, 453).
In India, Income, property and professional taxes are main direct taxes and GST, customs, stamp
duty and registration fees are main indirect taxes.

Direct and Indirect Taxes-Theoretical aspects:
“A direct tax is one which is demanded from the very persons who, it is intended or desired, should
pay it. Indirect taxes are those which are demanded from one person in the expectation and intention
that he shall indemnify himself at the expense of another” by J.S. Mill.
A tax is said to be direct, when the impact and incidence falls on the same tax unit. In case of indirect
tax, the impact and incidence falls on different tax units. In other words, when tax is imposed on a
tax unit, whose income and welfare is reduced due to the tax forms the basis for distinction. The
feasibility to shift the tax burden depends on the elasticity of demand and supply of the tax unit or
commodity. But, in reality, this direct and indirect distinction become blur in many cases where it’s
difficult to ascertain the tax burden and incidence. The direct taxes mostly comprise of income and
capital taxes, which may be further divided into taxes imposed on households, firms and property.
Indirect taxes are broadly on sales and purchases.
There are certain merits and demerits in both direct and indirect taxes. Direct taxes are equitable,
economic, certain and accurate. Demerits of direct taxes are inconvenient and prone to evasion. On
the other hand, indirect taxes are good when  it comes to convenience, they are also suitable for
sumptuary purpose1, difficult to evade and they cover wide range of items. But, indirect taxes are
regressive, uncertain, they may increase inequalities, they effect consumption and production
decisions and hence not neutral. Henry Simmons (1938)2 argue that direct taxes are preferable over
indirect taxes. Indirect taxes viewed synonymous with hidden taxes and argued further that
government should minimise concealment and expose the full tax burden to the tax payer.
Taxation have far reaching effects at individual as well as macro level. For example, Income tax may
impact after tax returns of education, which in turn may determine the years schooling of an
individual. If there are tax soaps to certain kind of jobs with untaxed benefits, people’s job selection
may be influenced. The classic wage and work trade off may also kick in and people may decide
their labour participation and retirement age etc. When tax incentives in certain goods or regions are
announced, new investments may prefer to invest in those areas. On the whole, tax structure
determines tax culture of the society and also reveals the path of economic development in the
economy.
Progressive, proportional and regressive: a tax is said to be progressive, when tax rate rises as
the tax payer’s incomes rise. A tax is proportional when tax rate is fixed at some rate irrespective of
the level of tax payer’s income. A tax is regressive when tax rate does not falls down as taxpayer’s

Rates of tax: Global tax regimes have undergone major changes in the last century. In direct taxes,
taxes were considered the most important instrument to achieve redistribution in the economy. This
in turn, meant that the rates of tax on incomes were set up to be very progressive, with very high
marginal tax rate at the highest tax slab. In India the rate was 97.5 percent while in UK  it was 91
percent and in US  it was 94 percent. Overtime, there as a realisation that high rates of tax could
encourage evasion and avoidance thus defeating the purpose of high tax rates. In the second half
of the last century, there a change in thinking which brought in a shift to lower rates of tax with fewer
exemptions and concessions provided. The highest marginal tax rate reduced considerably and in
many countries, it now lies between 25 percent and 35 percent. The number of tax slabs too have
been reduced.

well as tax policy (tax rate) changes, etc. Elasticity – Growth in the tax revenue with only base growth
and no policy changes. Elasticity is more desirable than buoyancy.

Income: As mentioned above income taxes form major part of direct taxes. Let us understand the
concept of Income and the different issues in income tax. First, what is Income? How income is
conceptualised, measured in economics and for tax purposes. Hicks (1939) provided the classic
definition of income. According to Hicks “income is the maximum value which a man can consume
during a week and still be as well off at the end of the week as he was at the beginning’. Simons
(1938) proposed another definition of income called “comprehensive income” and  it is also called
Haig–Simons income. According to this definition, personal income is the sum of, one, the market
value of rights exercised in consumption and second, the change in the value of the store of property
rights between the beginning and end of the period.
Generally, income from salary, house property, business or profession, capital gains are broad
categories of income. Some of these incomes are straight forward to compute but needs to be
imputed. For example, imputed rent income from owner occupied house. Sometimes, people may
be accumulating incomes from non-market activities. For example, working in family farm or helping
family activities/ business. Another issue is realized and unrealized incomes. Generally, unrealized
incomes are excluded as long as they are realized. For example, capital gains of land or assets.
Finally, we should not forget that illegal activities also generate certain incomes. How to treat them?
The answer is straight forward, when an economic activity is illegal, then the proceeds generated
from that act will also be illegal.
Once income has been defined, now the question is, at what rate should it be taxed? Differentiation
between types  of personal income  is major issue. Should we consider  all incomes equally
irrespective of how and from which activity it is accumulated? There are no fixed rules on the rate of
income tax and there is no optimal rate. Income tax rates is always a contested issue in all most all
countries5. In India, income tax rates are high historically. For example, the marginal income tax

Rate and Revenues Trade off  - Laffer curve: The economist Arthur Laffer put forward this
argument, which says that, when government increases tax rates beyond certain point,  it will be
counterproductive and tax reaves start falling down. High tax rates, disincentive and demotivate
people to work to the fullest of their productive levels. But, unfortunately, there is no consensus on
the maximum upper bound of tax rate beyond which this negativity kicks in. The laffer curve effect
depends on the existing tax structure and tax rates. When government increase tax rates, when tax
structure is fragile, this will lead to tax evasion. At low rates people’s incentive to tax evasion become
unprofitable and hence increase tax compliance.
PAN, TAN and TIN: As part of the simplification procedure income tax department introduced PAN.
The Permanent Account Number (PAN) is a 10 digit alphanumeric number launched in 1994. This
is a unique identifying number  for each tax payer. This enables the tax department  link  all
transactions to tax payment, high value transactions and TDS/TCS. A numeric alphabetic PAN
consisting of 9 digits and 2 letters was attempted in 1970-71 but could not able to implement due to
lack of computer technology.
TAN (Tax Deduction and Collection Account Number) is also 10 digit alpha numeric number
needed by all persons who are responsible for deducting tax at source or collecting tax at source.
According to section 203A of the Income-tax Act, 1961, every person who deducts or collects tax at
source has to quote TAN in TDS/TCS return (including e-return).
Tax Information Network (TIN) is established to integrate all primary information of tax collection,
tax deduction and third party information. This system allows the income tax department to create
separate account for each PAN holder.

Corporate Income Tax:
History of Corporate Tax: Even though, corporate income tax was first enacted in 1894 in USA,
but  it was shortly held unconstitutional. Again, in 1909, US Congress enacted an excise tax on
corporations based on income. First World War gave a push to a tax in the name of super tax and
continued.
Corporate Income taxation in India: In India, corporations were taxed on their profits. Till 1959,
corporation taxation is based on the system of grossing up of dividends. Here, corporations were
taxed on their profits but they were viewed as taxes paid on behalf of shareholders with respect to
profits distributed by the corporations in the form of dividends. Then, the shareholders used to get
credit for this. But, after 1959, corporations are considered as a separate juristic entities and
considered for tax purposes. Many argue that the present system lead to double taxation of
corporate incomes, first in the form of corporate tax, then again individual income tax is paid on
dividends distributed.
Rational for Corporate Taxation
The word corporation originates from the Latin word “corporare”, which means “to form into a body”.
The corporate tax is a recent phenomenon. What justifies corporate taxation?
There are many arguments from time to time to justify corporate taxation. One prominent is the
privilege argument, which put forward by Thomas Adams. A corporation should pay the tax for the
privilege of being allowed to exists and function as a corporate firm which entitles it a set of unique
legal provisions in terms of savings, debt rising, especially ownership and liability. The social cost
view argues that the corporations also consume the public services of the state, which further justify
a tax. The ethical argument is also sometimes put forward to justify corporation tax. This is in line of
individual income taxation, where corporation taxation is used to reduce inequalities in wealth
distribution.
But, however, critics argue that, if the tax is imposed on the incorporation and privilege of existence,
then it should be applied to all firms (rate can vary depending on size) and it should not be based
on profits. The loss making corporations does not pay any tax even though they use the privileges
on par with others.

The Cash Flow Corporate Income Tax7: In view of above debates, the cash flow corporate income

Which means, a situation where demand  is neither stimulated nor diminished by government
spending and taxation.
Harberger (1962) findings: Harberger estimated the incidence of corporate tax in USA through

Taxation of Wealth and Property
Wealth taxes are one of the oldest forms of taxation. It can be imposed as an annual fee based on
property valuation or fixed amount. Wealth tax is imposed on estates and inheritance wealth. In
Greece, wealth tax was imposed not only on homes and land but also on cattle, furniture and slaves.
In India, based the recommendation of Kaldor committee (1954), wealth and  gift taxes were
introduced in 1957. Recently, wealth tax is abolished from 2016-17.
There are four main type of wealth taxes imposed historically. Property tax, estate and inheritance
tax, net worth tax and capital levies.
Property taxation: Property taxation is generally one of the main sources of revenue for local
governments. This is primary imposed on residential and non-residential land and structures. The
main criteria is ownership of the property. There is a debate on, how to tax property and separate
this out from the capita portion of the wealth.
Estate and inheritance tax: This tax  slightly  differ from property tax  in the sense that the
assessment happens only once, at death. This is applicable to most of the assets classes of estates
and  inheritance wealth (bequeathed).  Since,  imposition and  collection  of  estate  taxes  are
complicated, they are not successful in implementation as well as in generating revenues to the
treasury.

Overview of Tax system in India
India follows federal structure. Seventh schedule of the Indian constitution divided powers between
centre and states. The 73ed and 74th amendments to constitution provides tax powers and funds to
local governments. The centre and state tax powers are divided into, List-I (Union List), List –II (State
List) and List –III (Concurrent List) on which both centre and state have powers. Article 246 (1) states
that the parliament has exclusive powers to make laws with respect to any matters enumerated in
“List-I” of seventh schedule of the constitution. This is called “Union List”. Article 246 (3) states that
the state government has exclusive powers to make laws with respect to any matters enumerated
in “List-II” of seventh schedule of the constitution. This is called “State List”.

Centre and where ever Tax base is immovable, Tax power is given to states. The below table

3.   On the taxation of dividends and capital gains, there have been a number of changes: with

     economy as compared to the brick and mortar economy has resulted in new issues arising

      generated in the second country which is referred to as the source country while the investor
        is a resident of the first country which is referred to as a residence country. In the brick and

      an elaborate set of Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements were put in place to reduce the

        jurisdictions. So efforts are now on globally to address this issue to ensure taxes are paid in

       countries is one big initiative in this direction. In India, the government as initiated a few
      measures like rewriting existing Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements, introducing General
       Anti Avoidance Regulation, setting up elaborate transfer pricing regulation and introducing
      an equalisation levy to address some of these concerns.
Indirect taxes:
The major indirect taxes in India include customs duty and Central GST for the union government
and state GST, state excise duty and stamps and registration fees. The original assignment of tax
powers in the Constitution of India was one where the Union government had the right to levy excise
duty, i.e., the tax on manufacturing and customs duty, i.e., the tax on import of goods into the country.
The state government on the other hand, had the right the tax sale of goods. This was a period when
services were not yet a large part of the economy and so apart from specific identified services like
entertainment or transportation of goods and services, taxation of services was not assigned to
either level of government.
The taxes in place in these early years were on the turnover of the seller and hence were referred
to as turnover taxes. But turnover taxes result in cascading – a tax on top of tax or some parts of
value added being taxed more than once. The major reform in indirect taxes was therefore the
transformation of a turnover tax regime to one based on value added. The series of major reforms
can be summarised as follows:

      Union government introduced a tax on services. The tax was introduced initially on three
       select services and gradually the coverage was expanded to include more and more services.
           It was converted to comprehensive coverage with a few exemptions in 2012.
3.     State sales  tax were structured as cascading turnover taxes.  In  addition, there was
      considerable amount of competition between the states resulting in competition in the rates
       of tax as well as providing tax incentives to encourage new industry. In 1999, an agreement
     was arrived at between the chief ministers of different states in the NDC meeting to undertake
       the first series of reforms in state taxes: rationalisation of rates of tax across states to reduce
       tax competition and the resulting race to the bottom. In the same meeting, it was agreed that
       the states would jointly undertake a tax reform to replace the existing turnover taxes with a
        inter-state value added tax. This tax regime would apply to only sale of goods within the state,
       with tax credit available only for purchases made within the state. The state VAT regimes
      were introduced over a period of two years from 2003-2005.
4.   On inter-state trade, there was a tax called Central Sales Tax. This tax was levied by the
      Union government and collected and retained by the exporting state, on  all inter-state
       transactions of sale of goods. The rate of CST was reduced alongside the introduction of
       State VATs.
5.    The central and state domestic indirect tax regimes were together reformed into a dual VAT
      regime which was implemented from July 1, 2017. This regime known as the GST regime
       consists of a Central GST Act along with State GST Acts – all transactions of supply of goods
      and services are to be subject to both these levies. There are a few exceptions to the list of
       taxable transactions. For inter-state transactions, these taxes are replaced by an IGST – an
       inter-state GST which is also applicable on imported goods in addition to the basic customs
       duty. This regime proposes to set in place a system of largely homogenised taxes across the
       country.
