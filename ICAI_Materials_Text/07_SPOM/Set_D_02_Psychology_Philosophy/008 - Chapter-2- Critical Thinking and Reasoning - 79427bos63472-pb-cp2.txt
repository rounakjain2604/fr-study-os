     2.1 INTRODUCTION TO CRITICAL THINKING
Critical thinking is the careful application of reason in the determination of whether a claim is true. It
aims at making wise decisions and coming to correct conclusions, and not being waylaid by
temptation, emotion, greed, irrelevant considerations, stupidity, bias, or other similar things.  It
involves thinking about thinking; we engage in it when we consider whether our thinking abides by
the criteria of good sense and logic.
In general, engaging in regular thinking or performing activities doesn't qualify as critical thinking.
Critical thinking becomes relevant when we assess beliefs and actions, essentially subjecting them

thinking  is the process  of  critiquing opinions, judgments, decisions, plans, conclusions, and

Mistakes are commonplace among individuals, as we often overlook crucial factors, neglect

shape our thoughts and actions. Whether it is assessing a sales pitch, be  it from a stranger or a

aims to arrive at accurate conclusions by avoiding pitfalls that compromise logical reasoning and
objective judgement.

2.2.1 Propositions
Propositions are the building blocks of our reasoning. A proposition asserts that something is the
case, or  it asserts that something  is not. We may affirm a proposition or deny it—but every
proposition either asserts what really is the case, or it asserts something that is not. Therefore, every
proposition is either true or false.
A proposition is different from a sentence. Sentences in the form of questions or exclamations do
not qualify as propositions. A proposition is subject to belief or disbelief, affirmation or denial, while
interrogative or exclamatory sentences do not affirm or deny anything. Questions, commands, and
exclamations lack the characteristic of being either true or false because they make no claims about
the world. For instance, the sentence "Do you know how to play chess?"  is a grammatical
construction, but it does not assert any specific information about the world. Similarly, commands
such as "Come quickly!" and exclamations like "Oh my gosh!" do not fall into the categories of being
true or false as they do not make factual claims.
Key Components of a Sentence:
A sentence typically consists of several key components:
♦     Subject: The entity or topic of the sentence, usually performing the action.
♦     Predicate: The action or state of being associated with the subject.
♦     Object: The recipient or target of the action (not always present).
Logic  confines  itself  to  indicative  sentences when  dealing  with  propositions.  Additionally,
propositions are language-neutral entities, distinguishing them from sentences that are inherently
tied to specific languages like English or any other language. For instance, the sentence "This is a
tree’" can be translated and expressed in various languages such as English, Hindi, German,
Japanese, etc. But there is only one underlying proposition in all these differentially expressed
sentences. On the other hand, two different sentences, consisting of different words differently
arranged, may have the same meaning, and may be used to assert the very same proposition. For
example, “Leslie won the election” and “The election was won by Leslie” are plainly two different
sentences that make the same assertion.

2.2.2 Arguments
With propositions as building blocks, we construct arguments. In any argument we affirm one
proposition on the basis of some other propositions. In doing this, an inference is drawn. Inference
is a process that may tie together a cluster of propositions. Some inferences are warranted (or
correct); others are not. The logician analyses these clusters, examining the propositions with which
the process begins and with which it ends, as well as the relations among these propositions. Such
a cluster of propositions constitutes an argument. Arguments are the chief concern of logic.
Argument is a technical term in logic.  It need not involve disagreement, or controversy. In logic,
argument refers strictly to any group of propositions of which one is claimed to follow from the others,
which are regarded as providing support for the truth of that one. For every possible inference there
is a corresponding argument.
An argument is not merely a collection of propositions; it is a cluster with a structure that captures
or exhibits some inference. We describe this structure with the terms conclusion and premise.
The conclusion of an argument is the proposition that is affirmed based on other propositions of
the argument. Those other propositions, which are affirmed (or assumed) as providing support for

It is extremely important that we recognise which propositions are premises and which one is

There are words which help in identifying premise and conclusions. The use of words like "hence",

2.2.3 Types Of Arguments
Every argument makes the claim that its premises provide grounds for the truth of its conclusion;
that claim is the mark of an argument. However, there are two very different ways in which a
conclusion may be supported by its premises, and thus there are two great classes of arguments:
the deductive and the inductive. Understanding this distinction is essential in the study of logic.
♦    Deducitve Argument: A deductive argument makes the claim that  its conclusion  is
      supported by its premises conclusively. Deductive arguments represent a form of reasoning
       that aims to establish a conclusion with certainty, given that the premises are true. This logical
      method is fundamental in various fields, including philosophy, mathematics, and the sciences.
      Deductive arguments are the workhorses of formal logic, offering certainty and clarity in a
      world full of uncertainty.
     Key characteristics of deductive arguments:
        Validity: The structure of the argument ensures the conclusion necessarily follows
            from the premises, regardless of their actual truth value.
       Soundness: When combined with true premises, a sound deductive argument
            guarantees a true conclusion.
       Formality: Deductive arguments often follow specific logical rules or syllogisms,
             ensuring clarity and precision in their structure.
      Example:
      Premise 1:    All humans are mortal. (Universal statement)
      Premise 2:   Socrates is a human. (Specific statement)
      Conclusion:  Therefore, Socrates is mortal. (Specific statement)
      Premise 1:    All profitable companies generate positive cash flow. (Universal statement)
      Premise 2:  XYZ Corporation is a profitable company. (Specific statement)
      Conclusion:  Therefore, XYZ Corporation generates positive cash flow. (Specific statement)

♦    Inductive Argument: An inductive argument is a type of reasoning where the conclusion
        is likely but not guaranteed to be true based on the provided evidence or premises. Unlike
      deductive reasoning, where the conclusion necessarily follows from the premises, inductive
      arguments involve making generalisations or predictions based on specific observations or
       instances. It is often described as "bottom-up" logic, as  it moves from the particular to the
       general. In an inductive argument, the strength of the conclusion depends on the degree of
      support provided by the premises.
     Key characteristics of inductive arguments:
       Probable conclusions: The conclusions of inductive arguments are not guaranteed
               to be true, even if the premises are true. They offer a degree of probability or likelihood
           based on the available evidence.
       Evidence-based:  Inductive  arguments   rely  heavily  on  empirical  evidence,
             observations, and patterns to support their conclusions.
      Open  to  revision: As new  evidence emerges,  inductive  arguments can be
             strengthened, weakened, or even overturned.
      Example:
      Premise 1:   Every watermelon I have ever seen has been round.
      Premise 2:   Therefore, all watermelons are round.
      This is an inductive argument because  it draws a general conclusion (all watermelons are
      round) from specific observations (the watermelons I have seen). However, it is possible that
       there are watermelons of other shapes that I haven't encountered, so the conclusion is not
      guaranteed  to be  true.  If   I come across  the cube shaped watermelon from Japan,
       automatically my conclusion will be revised.
      Example:
      Premise 1:  Companies with strong corporate governance practices tend to experience
                    higher profitability.
      Premise 2:  Company C recently implemented robust corporate governance reforms.
      Conclusion: Company C's profitability is likely to improve in the coming years.

2.3.4 Validity And Truth
A deductive argument is valid when it succeeds in linking, with logical necessity, the conclusion to
its premises.  Its validity refers to the relation between  its propositions — between the set of
propositions that serve as the premises and the one proposition that serves as the conclusion of that
argument.  If the conclusion follows with logical necessity from the premises, we say that the
argument is valid. Therefore, validity can never apply to any single proposition by itself because the
needed relation cannot possibly be found within any one proposition.
Truth and. falsehood, on the other hand, are attributes of individual propositions. A single statement
that serves as a premise in an argument may be true; the statement that serves as its conclusion
may be false. This conclusion might have been validly inferred, but to say that any conclusion (or
any single premise) is itself valid or invalid makes no sense.
Truth is the attribute of those propositions that assert what really is the case. When I assert that
Lake Superior is the largest of the five Great Lakes, I assert what really is the case, what is true. If
I had claimed that Lake Michigan is the largest of the Great Lakes, my assertion would not be in
accord with the real world; therefore, it would be false. This contrast between validity and truth is
important. Truth and falsity are attributes of individual propositions or statements; validity and
invalidity are attributes of arguments.
Just as the concept of validity cannot apply to single propositions, the concept of truth cannot apply
to arguments. Of the several propositions in an argument, some (or all) may be true, and some (or
all) may be false. However, the argument is neither true nor false. Propositions, which are statements
about the world, may be true or false; deductive arguments, which consist of inferences from one
set of propositions to other propositions, may be valid or invalid.
2.2.5 How To Make Effective Arguments
Constructing a logical argument is a fundamental aspect of effective business writing, especially
when recommending a course of action. Whether proposing to open a new branch office, institute a
policy, or make a significant purchase, the argument needs to be well-constructed and supported.
As a decision maker,  it is crucial to evaluate the logical coherence of the presented argument.
Rational arguments are built on evidence, leading to a conclusion through an accepted pattern of
reasoning, often referred to as "warrants." The validity of an argument lies in assessing both the
data provided and the reasoning process connecting the evidence to the conclusion.

2.2.6 Evaluation
Ways to Evaluate an Argument - Evaluating an argument requires testing both the data and the
warrants. Key questions to ask include:
1.    Data Evaluation:
        (a)     Is the source reliable?
        (b)     Is the evidence accurate, current, and relevant?
        (c)   Have statistics been properly evaluated?
        (d)     Is the authority cited truly an expert?
        (e)     Is there sufficient evidence?
2.    Warrant Evaluation:
       (a)    For inductive reasoning, are enough representative examples cited?
       (b)    For deductive reasoning, are the major and minor premises true?

2.2.7 Validity In Deductive & Inductive Reasoning
Logicians exclusively apply the term "validity" to deductive reasoning. Stating that a deductive
argument is valid means asserting that if its premises are true, it is impossible for its conclusion to
be wrong. Validity is defined as follows: A deductive argument is considered valid if its premises are
true, necessitating that its conclusion is also true. In colloquial language, however, the term "valid"
is employed in a far less strict manner.
While  it is true that every deductive argument asserts that its premises ensure the truth of its
conclusion, not all deductive arguments fulfil this assertion. Invalid deductive arguments are those
that fail to accomplish so.

Ethos, Pathos and Logos
Aristotle introduced the idea that speakers can use three ways to persuade an audience in rhetoric.
Aristotle believed that a speaker's background, reputation, accomplishments, expertise, and other
qualities may persuade us. Aristotle called this persuasion ethos. Second, a speaker can persuade

A fallacy is a type of argument that may seem to be correct, but that proves on examination not to
be so. Logicians, however, commonly use the term “fallacy” more narrowly, to designate not just any
error in reasoning, but typical errors — mistakes in reasoning that exhibit a pattern that can be
identified and named. The great logician Gottlob Frege observed that it is one of the logician’s tasks
to “indicate the pitfalls laid by language in the way of the thinker.” In this narrower sense, each fallacy
is a type of incorrect argument. Of course, many different arguments may make an error of some
given type; that is,  it may exhibit the same kind of mistake in reasoning. Any argument that does
exhibit that kind of mistake is said to commit that fallacy. The particular argument that commits some
known fallacy is commonly said to be a fallacy, because it is an individual example of that typical
mistake.
A formal fallacy is a pattern of mistake that appears in deductive arguments of a certain specifiable
form. There are other formal fallacies.
Most fallacies, however, are not formal but informal: They are patterns of mistake that are made in
the everyday uses of language. Informal fallacies arise from confusions concerning the content of
the language used. There is no limit to the variety of forms in which that content may appear, and
thus informal fallacies are often more difficult to detect than formal ones. It is language that deceives
us here; we may be tricked by inferences that seem plausible on the surface but that are in reality
not warranted. Such traps, the “pitfalls” that language sets, can be avoided if the patterns of those
mistakes are well understood. The major informal fallacies are:
(1)     fallacies of relevance
(2)     fallacies of defective induction
(3)     fallacies of presumption
(4)     fallacies of ambiguity.
2.3.1 Fallacies of Relevance
Fallacies of relevance are the most numerous and the most frequently encountered. In these
fallacies, the premises of the argument are simply not relevant to the conclusion. However, because
they are made to appear to be relevant, they may deceive. We will distinguish and discuss:
R1. The Appeal to the Populace (ad Populum): An informal fallacy committed when the support
offered for some conclusion is an inappropriate appeal to the multitude. The oratory of Adolf Hitler,
whipping up the racist enthusiasms of his German listeners, is a classic example. Love of country is
an honourable emotion, but the appeal to that emotion in order to manipulate and mislead one’s
audience is intellectually disreputable.

2.3.2 Fallacies Of Defective Induction
In fallacies of defective induction, which are also common, the mistake arises from the fact that the
premises of the argument, although relevant to the conclusion, are so weak and ineffective that
relying on them is a blunder. We will distinguish and discuss:
D1. The Argument from Ignorance (ad Ignorantiam): An informal fallacy in which a conclusion is
supported by an illegitimate appeal to ignorance, when it is supposed that something is likely to be
true because we cannot prove that it is false. It is a logical fallacy that occurs when a proposition is
considered true simply because it has not been proven false or vice versa. In other words, the lack
of evidence for or against a claim is used as the basis for accepting or rejecting it. This fallacy relies
on the absence of evidence rather than presenting positive evidence to support a conclusion.
Example:
Tax Avoidance:
Tax Advisor: "There's no law explicitly prohibiting this tax loophole, so it must be legal to use it."
Fallacy: Absence of a specific law doesn't guarantee legality; ethical considerations and potential
future regulations matter.
D2. The Appeal to Inappropriate Authority (ad Verecundiam): An informal fallacy in which the
appeal to authority is illegitimate, either because the authority appealed to has no special claim to
expertise on the topic at issue, or, more generally, because no authority is assured to be reliable.
The fallacy of the appeal to inappropriate authority arises when the appeal is made to parties who
have no legitimate claim to authority in the matter at hand. Thus, in an argument about morality, an
appeal to the opinions of Darwin, a towering authority in biology, would be fallacious, as would be
an appeal to the opinions of a great artist such as Picasso to settle an economic dispute. Care must
be taken in determining whose authority it is reasonable to rely on, and whose to reject.

2.3.3 Fallacies of Presumption
In fallacies of presumption, too much is assumed in the premises. The inference to the conclusion
depends mistakenly on these unwarranted assumptions. We will distinguish and discuss:
P1. Accident: An informal fallacy in which a generalisation is applied to individual cases that it does
not govern. Experience teaches us that even generalizations that are widely applicable and very
useful are likely to have exceptions for which we must be on guard. The fallacy occurs when a
general rule is applied to a specific case to which the rule does not properly or fairly apply. Indeed,
there is hardly any rule or general principle that does not have plausible exceptions, and we are
likely to argue fallaciously if we reason on the supposition that some rule applies universally.
Example:
Financial Forecasting:
Financial Analyst: "Based on recent economic trends and industry data, we forecast a decline in
retail sales growth for the upcoming quarter."
Company CEO: "Our sales have always increased during this quarter in the past. There is no reason
to expect a change this year."
Fallacy: The CEO applies a general rule (past sales growth) to a specific situation (current economic
conditions) without considering potential external factors or changing market dynamics.
P2. Complex Question (Plurium Interrogationum): An informal fallacy in which a question is
asked in such a way as to presuppose the truth of some proposition buried in the question. It occurs
when a question is posed in a way that presupposes a particular answer or contains multiple
assumptions. This fallacy is manipulative, as  it forces the respondent into accepting an implicit
premise, often leading to a misleading or inaccurate response.
Example:
In a Budget Meeting:
Finance Director: "Have you stopped wasting company resources on unnecessary expenses?"
Fallacy: The Finance Director's question assumes the manager is guilty of wasting resources before
they even had a chance to respond, forcing a defensive stance.
P3. Begging the Question (Petitio Principii): An informal fallacy in which the conclusion of an
argument is stated or assumed in one of the premises. The fallacy, also known as Petitio Principii,
occurs when the premise of an argument presupposes the truth of the conclusion, creating a circular

takes advantage of the ambiguity of language by exploiting multiple meanings of a term to create a
false appearance of logical coherence. Clear definitions and consistent use of terms help prevent
equivocation and ensure that arguments are built on a solid and transparent foundation.

Statement: "The compliance officer highlighted the regulatory changes in a memo distributed to the
staff with concerns."
Fallacy: The placement of the phrase "with concerns" introduces ambiguity. It is unclear whether the
staff or the compliance officer is described as having concerns. The interpretation could impact the
way the regulatory changes are perceived by the staff.
A3. Accent: An informal fallacy committed when a term or phrase has a meaning in the conclusion
of an argument different from its meaning in one of the premises, the difference arising chiefly from
a change in emphasis given to the words used. This fallacy capitalises on the ambiguity that can
arise from changes in tone, emphasis, or inflection, leading to a misinterpretation of the intended
meaning.
Example:
Audit Report:
Auditor: "We found no material misstatements in the company's financial statements." (Emphasis on
"material")
Reality: The auditor's definition of "material" might differ from stakeholders' expectations. The
emphasis on "material" could minimise the importance of smaller errors or irregularities that could
still have negative implications.
A4. Composition: An informal fallacy in which an inference is mistakenly drawn from the attributes
of the parts of a whole to the attributes of the whole itself. This fallacy occurs when one incorrectly
assumes that what is true for a part of a whole must also be true for the whole. In other words, it
involves making an unwarranted inference from the characteristics of individual elements to the
characteristics  of  the  entire group  or  system.  This  fallacy  disregards  the  complexity and
interdependence within a whole, leading to erroneous conclusions. One may hear it seriously argued

Cost-Cutting vs. Overall Efficiency:

A5. Division: An informal fallacy in which a mistaken inference is drawn from the attributes of a
whole to the attributes of the parts of the whole. To argue that a student must have a large room
because the room is located in a large dormitory would be a kind of fallacy of division. It occurs when
one erroneously assumes that what is true for a whole must also be true for its individual parts. This
logical error arises from the incorrect inference that characteristics or properties of a group,
organisation, or system apply uniformly to each constituent element.
Example:
Employee Performance:
Manager: "Our sales team consistently exceeds its targets. Therefore, every salesperson on the
team must be a high performer."
Fallacy: The manager assumes that the overall team performance reflects the performance of each
individual salesperson, disregarding the possibility of varying levels of contribution and potential
outliers within the team.
Note:- The fallacies of composition and division are often confused with the fallacies of accident and
converse accident (hasty generalization). Unlike accident and converse accident, composition and
division are fallacies of ambiguity, resulting from the multiple meanings of terms. Wherever the words
or phrases used may mean one thing in one part of the argument and another thing in another part,
and those different meanings are deliberately or accidentally confounded, we can expect the
argument to be fallacious.

2.3.5 Fallacies Of Inference
In Indian logic, a fallacy of inference is called hetvābhāsa. It refers to a fallacy or defect in reasoning
that occurs when the middle term (hetu) presented to support a conclusion  is insufficient or
irrelevant. The term "hetu" is a crucial component Indian logic. Hetvabhasa undermines the validity
of an argument by introducing a faulty or deceptive middle term that does not genuinely establish
the connection between the premises and the conclusion. Thus, the fallacy concerning the middle
term is known as hetvābhāsa. It means that hetu appears to be a valid reason, but it is not a valid
reason.
There are five characteristics of a valid middle term. Whenever these are violated, there are fallacies.
Due to fallacious hetus, fallacious inference occurs.
2.3.6 Guarding  Against  Fallacies:  Essential  Strategies  for  Sound
Reasoning and Critical Thinking
Fallacies, or errors  in reasoning, can hinder  effective  critical thinking and decision making.
Recognizing and avoiding fallacies is essential for constructing sound arguments and making well-
informed judgments. Following strategies may prove to be effective in preventing fallacies and
cultivating sound reasoning:
♦    Understand Logical Fallacies: The first step in avoiding fallacies is to familiarise oneself
       with common logical fallacies. Recognizing these patterns of flawed reasoning is crucial for
      addressing them in one's own thinking and in evaluating the arguments of others.
♦    Question Assumptions: Critical thinkers actively question assumptions underlying their own
      arguments and  those  presented by  others.  Identifying and  challenging unsupported
      assumptions can help eliminate fallacious reasoning.
♦   Be Aware of Biases: Personal biases can contribute to fallacious reasoning. Being aware of
      one's own biases and striving for objectivity helps in avoiding fallacies based on subjective
       or prejudiced thinking.
♦     Validate Information: Verify the accuracy and reliability of information before incorporating
            it into an argument. Fallacies can arise when using inaccurate or biased data, leading to faulty
       conclusions.
♦    Check for Relevance: Ensure that all premises presented in an argument are relevant to the
       conclusion. Avoid introducing information that may divert attention (red herring) or relying on
       irrelevant considerations.

♦    Examine Analogies Carefully: Analogies can be powerful tools, but they may also lead to
       fallacious reasoning if the comparison is not truly analogous. Carefully assess whether the
      shared characteristics are relevant and appropriate.
♦   Demand Clarity in Language: Ambiguous or unclear language can contribute to fallacies.
       Strive   for  precision  and   clarity   in  expression  to  avoid  misunderstandings  and
       misinterpretations.
♦    Consider Counterarguments: Engage with potential counterarguments to your position.
      Acknowledging alternative viewpoints and addressing counterarguments helps strengthen
      your reasoning and avoid fallacies based on oversimplification.
♦    Consult Multiple Sources: Relying on a single source for information can lead to fallacies
      such  as  hasty  generalisation.  Consult  diverse and  reputable  sources  to  ensure a
      comprehensive understanding of the topic.
♦    Seek Feedback: Share your reasoning with others and invite constructive feedback. External
       perspectives can help identify potential fallacies that may have been overlooked.
Mastering the art of critical thinking involves a vigilant commitment to recognising and mitigating
fallacies in our reasoning. By understanding common logical pitfalls, questioning assumptions, and
cultivating clarity in our expressions, we can navigate the complexities of decision-making with
increased precision. The journey toward intellectual proficiency requires not just recognition but a
continuous dedication to cultivating a discerning and open-minded approach in navigating the
intricate landscapes of decision making.

SUMMARY
♦     Logic is the study of reasoning and argumentation. It provides a systematic way to evaluate
      and construct valid arguments.
♦    An argument is a set of statements, one of which (the conclusion) is claimed to follow from
       the others (the premises). Arguments have a structure involving premises that support a
       conclusion.
♦   A deductive argument is valid if the conclusion logically follows from the premises.
♦    An inductive argument is sound  if  it is valid, and  all  its premises are true. In inductive
      arguments, strength relates to the degree of support the premises provide for the conclusion.
