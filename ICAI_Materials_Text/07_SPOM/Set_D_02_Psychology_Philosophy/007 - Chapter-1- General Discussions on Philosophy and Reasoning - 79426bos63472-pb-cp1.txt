    1.1 WHAT IS PHILOSOPHY?
Human beings, akin to all living organisms, partake in a constant struggle for survival. While lower
life forms navigate this struggle mostly driven by instincts, humans, armed with intellect, consciously
grasp the significance of their life challenges. They leverage their advanced cognitive abilities to
formulate plans and tools for success. Unlike their counterparts in the animal kingdom, humans
aspire to lead enlightened lives, enriched by knowledge about themselves and the world. This pursuit
transcends immediate outcomes, taking into account the broader consequences of their actions. The
human thirst for knowledge is grounded in reason, and philosophy emerges as the avenue to satiate
this rational quest.
The term 'philosophy,' etymologically rooted in 'love for wisdom,' seeks to comprehend aspects
relevant to humanity, both in the immediate and distant spheres. These include the true nature of
man, the purpose of life, the nature of the world, the existence of a creator, and how one should live

While Western Philosophy primarily adheres to the etymological essence of 'philosophy' as an
intellectual pursuit of truth, Indian Philosophy takes a profoundly spiritual approach, consistently
emphasizing the imperative for the practical realization of truth. The term "dar͎sana" conveys both
'vision' and the 'instrument of vision,' symbolizing immediate and intuitive perception of reality,
encompassing the means to attain this realization. The common thread across all schools of Indian
Philosophy is encapsulated in the directive to 'See the Self' (ātmā vā are drastavyah). The ultimate
goal is the elimination of three types of suffering — sādhyatmika (resulting from natural and intra-
organic causes), ādhibhautika (arising from natural and extra-organic causes), and ādhidaivika
(stemming from supernatural and extra-organic causes). The desired outcome is the attainment of
supreme happiness, with the means involving shravana (hearing the truth), manana (intellectual
conviction through critical analysis), and nididhyāsana (practical realization).
While the fundamental problems of philosophy are similar in both Eastern and Western traditions,
differences arise in their methods of philosophical inquiry and the development of philosophical
thought. Indian philosophy, for instance, addresses metaphysical, ethical, logical, psychological, and
epistemological problems without strict compartmentalization. The comprehensive perspective of
Indian Philosophy has often been referred to as the synthetic outlook in philosophy.

1.2.1 Metaphysics
It is the ‘theory of reality’, an enquiry into the nature of reality and existence. What exists, and what
is its nature?
Metaphysics is a branch of philosophy that explores fundamental questions about the nature of
reality, existence, and the relationship between mind and matter. The term "metaphysics" itself
comes from the title given by ancient scholars to the works of Aristotle that followed his writings on
physics. Metaphysics delves into questions that often go beyond the scope of empirical science,
seeking to understand the fundamental nature of reality.
Some central questions in metaphysics include:
♦     Existence and Reality
       What does it mean for something to be real?
       Does reality exist independently of human perception?
       What is the nature of space and time? Are they fundamental aspects of reality, or mere
          human constructs?
♦     Identity and Change
       What defines the persistence and essence of objects over time?
♦    Mind and Body
        Investigates the relationship between consciousness and the physical body.
♦    Causation
       Explores whether events are necessarily connected or merely follow a sequence.
♦    Free Will and Determinism
       Examines human agency and the balance between free will and external determinants.

1.2.2 Epistemology
It is the ‘theory of knowledge’ is an inquiry into the nature of knowledge and how it is acquired. It
investigates the  distinctions between knowledge,  belief and opinion, seeks  to ascertain the
conditions under which a claim to know something is justified, and examines and offers responses
to skeptical challenges to knowledge. Continuing with the above example, epistemology refers to
inquiry in the source and validation of data.
The fundamental inquiries in epistemology revolve around the source of knowledge, the role of
experience in its formation, and the contribution of reason to this process. The connection between
knowledge and the accountability for errors, as well as the evolution of knowledge resulting from
new conceptualizations of the world, is an integral part of these pivotal questions. All these concerns
are interrelated with other core considerations within Indian philosophical systems. In the formation
of any knowledge, four factors come into play.
1.    The subject who knows is called Pramātā
2.    The object of knowledge is called Prameya
3.    The means of acquiring valid knowledge is called Pramāna
4.    The resultant of valid knowledge is called Prama
Indian epistemology accepts six types of pramāna, or valid sources of knowledge. They are:
(1)    Perception - Pratyaksha
(2)    Inference - Anumāna
(3)    Verbal testimony - Shabda
(4)   Comparison - Upamāna
(5)    Non-existence - Anupalabdhi
(6)    Postulation – Arthāpatti

1.6           PHILOSOPHY

(1)    Perception (Pratyaksha)
All Indian Philosophical schools accept perception as a valid source of knowledge though the
definition and types of perception accepted by them differ. Perception is considered to be the direct
source of knowledge obtained through the instrumentality of sense-organ contact with the object.
Pratyaksha is a Sanskrit term meaning ‘prati + aksh’ “that which is before one’s eyes," and is
interpreted as “perception” in Indian philosophy.
Pratyaksa  is  relevant  to Chartered Accountants as   it emphasizes the importance  of  direct
observation and firsthand verification. In the realm of accountancy, where accuracy, reliability, and
ethical considerations are paramount, this pursuit of clear and unbiased perception aligns with the
profession's commitment to ensuring the truthfulness and integrity of financial information.
(2)    Inference (Anumāna)
The concept of inference, known as "anumana," is widely accepted as a valid source of knowledge
across Indian philosophical schools. Nyāya, a prominent perspective, defines 'anumāna' as 'after
knowledge,' involving mediate and indirect reasoning through a medium called 'hetu' or reason. This
medium exhibits an invariable concomitance with the observed feature. The process of inference is
illustrated through an example, such as determining the presence of fire on a  hill through the
perception of smoke.
Nyāya syllogisms consist of components like pratijna (logical statement), hetu (reason), udāharana
(universal concomitance with an example), upanaya (application to the present case), and nigamana
(conclusion).
The process of inference can be elucidated using a common example: inferring the existence of fire
on a hill based on the observation of smoke on the same hill. Upon observing smoke emanating
from a faraway hill, an individual recollects their understanding of the invariable connection (Vyāpti)
between smoke and fire, and deduces that there must be a fire present on the far hill. The character
that is deduced (fire) is referred to as sādhya; the indicator by which the character is deduced is the
hetu (smoke); the object in which the character is deduced is paksa (hill).
The three terms correspond to the major, minor and the middle terms in the Aristotelian syllogism.
The following is a typical Nyāya syllogism.
This hill has fire (pratijnā),
Because it has smoke (hetu),
Whatever has smoke has fire e.g. an oven (udāharana),

This hill as smoke which is invariably associated with fire (upanaya),

The  first statement, known as the pratijna, refers to the logical proposition that needs to be
demonstrated or proven. The second proposition is hetu, often known as reason, which serves to
explain the rationale behind the proposition's establishment. The third proposition is udaharana,
which refers to the universal concomitance together with an accompanying illustration. The fourth
step is the upanaya, which refers to the application of the universal concomitance to the current
situation. The fifth one is nigamana, which refers to the conclusion derived from the preceding
propositions. The five constituents of Indian syllogism are referred to as Avayavas.
Anumana is relevant to Chartered Accountants as  it involves drawing logical inferences based on
available evidence and information. From deducing missing financial data and uncovering fraud
through pattern recognition, to assessing investment risks and making informed decisions based on
future inferences, Anumana empowers CAs to navigate incomplete information and make sound
judgments, ultimately contributing to the accuracy and reliability of financial reporting.
(3)    Verbal Testimony (Śabda)
Śabda, or verbal testimony, is considered an independent source of valid knowledge. It is defined
as the statement of a trustworthy person (äptaväkya) and consists in understanding its meaning. A
sentence is defined as a collection of words and a word is defined as that which is potent to convey
its meaning. Testimony is always personal. It is based on the words of a trustworthy person, human
or divine. Only the words of trustworthy persons who always speak the truth are valid; others are
not.
Chartered Accountants adhere to professional standards and regulations as codified knowledge,
akin to accepting authoritative pronouncements. Additionally, CAs rely on continuous learning
through expert seminars and workshops, valuing the wisdom of experienced professionals, much
like Śabda pramana emphasizes credible verbal sources. The focus is on critical evaluation of the
source and ensuring that the received knowledge aligns with established ethical and professional
standards.
By embracing the essence of Śabda pramana, Chartered Accountants can enhance their adherence
to professional norms, stay updated with evolving knowledge, and ultimately contribute to the ethical
and reliable practice of their profession.

(4)   Comparison (Upamāna)
Upamāna, the next source of valid knowledge, is rooted in understanding the relationship between
a word and its denotation through similarity. The term originates from the combination of "upa,"
indicating similarity, and "māna," denoting cognition. It serves as a means of knowledge derived
from the comparison between two objects, elucidating the connection between a word and what it
signifies.
In the context of upamāna, knowledge of an object is obtained by comparing it to similar objects,
treating it as an analogy. Thus, upamāna signifies the understanding of the relationship between a
name and the object  it denotes through similarity, offering insights into the nuanced process of
acquiring knowledge through comparison in various philosophical traditions. Upamana's influence
lies in its systematic approach to comparison and drawing analogies, providing valuable insights for
financial analysis and decision-making.
CAs employ Upamana's principles not just for mechanical comparisons, but for drawing reasoned
conclusions and enhancing  their understanding  of complex  financial  situations. Comparative
analysis provides valuable insights for performance evaluation, risk assessment, decision-making,
and  strategic  planning,  contributing  to  the  effective  financial management and success  of
organisations.
(6)   Non-Existence (Anupalabdhi)
Anupalabdhi is an immediate form of knowledge that pertains to the non-existence of an object. It is
associated with the category of abhāva, which refers to non-existence. Anupalabdhi acknowledges
that things can exist either in a positive or negative relation to the observer and their surroundings.
In the positive relation, objects are perceived by the senses, while in the negative relation (non-
existence), they cannot be sensed, requiring an alternative "mode of movement" of the mind, known
as anupalabdhi.
Anupalabdhi subtly permeates Chartered Accountancy. Its focus on identifying absences empowers
CAs  to  detect missing  data,  potential  errors, and even  fraudulent  activities  in  audits and
investigations. Anupalabdhi's awareness of negations also enhances risk assessment and ethical
decision making. In essence, recognising what is not there, alongside what is, allows CAs to paint a
more complete and trustworthy picture of financial reality.
(7)    Postulation (Arthāpatti)
Arthapatti is a cognitive method employed in Mimansa philosophy, serving as a valid source of
knowledge by postulating an unperceived fact to resolve conflicting perceived facts. The term
"arthāpatti"  is a combination of 'artha' (fact) and 'apatti' ('kalpana' or 'supposition' in English).

Etymologically, arthāpatti is knowledge that resolves conflicts between two facts, presupposing an
unperceived fact to reconcile inconsistencies. It involves assuming a third fact when two apparent
facts conflict, and the justified knowledge of this third fact is termed arthāpatti. In essence,  it
addresses situations where a known  fact necessitates the postulation  of another  fact  for a
comprehensive understanding. For instance, Devadatta is both alive and overweight despite fasting
during the day. To resolve this conflict, we posit a third fact: he must be eating at night. Similarly,
when John is alive but not at home, we resolve the conflict by postulating that he may be staying in
a rented house outside his home. Thus, postulation or presumption is a valid source of knowledge.
Arthapatti subtly sculpts the practice of Chartered Accountancy. It allows CAs to bridge data gaps
by inferring missing facts necessary to explain observed situations. This applies to detecting fraud
through presumed hidden transactions, filling accounting gaps with logical suppositions, and even
making reasoned assumptions for accurate valuations and risk assessments. Arthapatti, therefore,
empowers CAs to navigate inconsistencies and incomplete information, ultimately strengthening the
reliability and clarity of financial reporting.
The knowledge obtained in the above manner then is subjected to the relevant theories of truth in
an attempt to get the complete information related to the problem on hand. The theories of the truth
include:
♦    The Correspondence Theory of Truth
The correspondence theory of truth asserts that a proposition is true when  it aligns with reality,
emphasizing a unique conformity between judgments and states of affairs. This perspective, rooted
in common sense, sees truth as the agreement or conformity between a statement and the actual
state of affairs. Aristotle and St. Thomas Aquinas view truth as the accord between things and
intellect. Michael Devitt argues that truth is distinct from epistemic notions and suggests that a
sentence accurately represents reality if its components bear a relevant causal relation to objects in
the world. Correspondence as correlation posits a structural isomorphism between truth bearers and
facts, suggesting a fitting relationship between their components.
In financial reporting, correspondence theory can be applied to ensure that the information presented
accurately corresponds to the economic reality of the business. Auditors use this to ensure financial
statements accurately reflect the company's financial status. When choosing an accounting model,
CAs might favour one that best corresponds to the actual economic activity of the company, seeking
a close fit between model results and real-world outcomes.

♦    The Coherence Theory of Truth
In the coherence theory, a proposition's truth is determined by its alignment or "coherence" with
other propositions within a systematic framework. According to the coherence theory, truth is not
about conforming to reality or facts but is instead based on the internal consistency and alignment
within a system of propositions. It serves as a test for validating or rejecting truth-candidates, akin
to solving a puzzle by discarding pieces that don't  fit the coherent picture. Coherence theory
emphasizes the internal consistency and harmony of information.
In chartered accountancy, this can be applied by ensuring that financial statements and reports are
internally consistent and aligned with accounting principles and standards. Accounting standards
frameworks, like IFRS, strive  for  internal  coherence, ensuring  different  accounting  principles
logically connect and create a consistent overall picture.
♦    The Pragmatic Theory
Pragmatism, championed by thinkers such as Charles Peirce, William James, and John Dewey,
offers a conception of truth closely tied to human experience. This theory posits that the truth of a
statement is determined by its practical consequences and usefulness. In this view, truth is not an
abstract concept but is rooted in the tangible outcomes and effectiveness of a belief or assertion.
Pragmatism emphasizes the real-world impact and utility of information, suggesting that the validity
of a statement lies in its practical consequences and its ability to guide effective action. This theory
aligns with a pragmatic approach to decision making, where the practical benefits and consequences
play a crucial role in determining the truthfulness of a statement or belief.
In chartered accountancy,  this theory can be applied by considering the impact of financial
information on decision making and stakeholders. CAs might prioritize reporting significant items
even if they don't perfectly correspond to reality, because their impact on users is higher.
Chartered Accountants often draw on all three theories in their work. Choosing the most relevant
theory depends on the specific context and purpose of the decision or investigation. Ultimately, their
goal is to use truth concepts to provide reliable and useful information for stakeholders.

considered separately.

2.    Aesthetics :Aesthetics is broadly defined as the philosophical inquiry into the character of
      beauty in nature as well as in art. Aesthetics is the philosophical domain that investigates
      experiences of beauty and the appreciation of  art.  It can be narrowly defined as the
      examination of beauty or, more broadly, as encompassing the philosophy of art. While the
        traditional emphasis was on the concept of beauty, this scope expanded in the eighteenth
       century to include the sublime.

      Indian Aesthetics
     The development of Indian aesthetics can be traced back to ancient texts such as the Vedas,
       the Upanishads, and the Puranas. These texts contain discussions on the nature of beauty,
        art, and aesthetics, as well as on the relationship between  art and  spirituality. Indian
       aesthetics, woven from ancient philosophies, diverse art forms, and spiritual insights, offers
      a unique lens through which to experience the world. It transcends mere "beauty" to a realm
       of sensory engagement, symbolism, and the pursuit of higher realities.
     Key Pillars of Indian Aesthetics as applicable to the field of Accountancy include mainly
     Dharma and Karma. Ethical principles like dharma (righteousness) and karma (consequences
       of actions) permeate artistic expression. Art often serves as a vehicle for moral messages,
      promoting values like compassion, humility, and balance.
     Western Aesthetics
      Western aesthetics, a branch of philosophy examining beauty, art, and taste within Western
       culture, traces its origins to ancient Greece.
       Aesthetics plays a nuanced yet crucial role in chartered accountancy, contributing to the
       profession's efficacy and ethical practice. While the  field primarily centers on financial
       reporting, audits, and compliance, aesthetics come into play in various dimensions. In
      Western aesthetics, the study of beauty, art, and taste within Western culture influences the
      design of financial reports, ensuring they are not only accurate but also aesthetically pleasing
        for enhanced stakeholder understanding. Western theories, such as Kantian aesthetics,
      emphasizing universal principles of beauty, influence the design of user interfaces and
      dashboards in financial systems, contributing to a positive user experience.
       Similarly, in Indian aesthetics deeply rooted in diverse philosophical traditions, the emphasis
      on rasa and bhava (emotional essence) influences the presentation of financial information.

    1.3  CHARACTERISTICS   OF   INDIAN   PHILOSOPHY:
      DARSHAN SHASTRA
Indian philosophy, with its rich tapestry of diverse schools of thought, exhibits several common
characteristics that are integral to its understanding and practice. These characteristics collectively
shape the essence of Indian philosophical traditions.
1. Practical Necessity: Philosophy in India originated as a practical necessity, dispelling the
misconception that  it deals with impractical issues. Contrary to this notion, Indian philosophy is
deeply concerned with the fundamental questions of human  life. Recognizing the intrinsic link

between philosophy and practical existence, Indian philosophers sought paths to address life's

2. Initial Pessimism: A prevailing trait in Indian philosophy is its initial note of pessimism. The
philosophers expressed dissatisfaction with the prevailing situation, marked by human suffering and
the pervasive presence of  evil. This pessimistic undertone, however, typically transforms into
optimism as various philosophical systems present ways to overcome the sufferings of life, offering
paths to freedom from the miserable state of existence.
3. Belief in Moral Order: Indian philosophy commonly embraces a belief in an eternal moral order,
excluding the materialistic Cārvāka system. This philosophy asserts that the entire universe is
governed and sustained by a universal moral order, known as "rta" in the Vedas. This order is
considered eternal and inviolable, providing a framework for ethical conduct.
4. Ignorance as the Root Cause: Ignorance about the true nature of things is identified as the root
cause of all sufferings in Indian philosophical systems. Attachment to worldly objects, driven by
sensory gratification, creates bondage and hinders the path to acquiring true knowledge of ultimate
reality. Liberation from this state of bondage is seen as achievable only through the right knowledge
of things.
5. Liberation as the Ultimate Goal: The various philosophical systems in India, except for Cārvāka,
converge in considering liberation or freedom from bondage as the ultimate goal of human life.
Liberation entails the complete cessation of suffering, and while different systems offer varied views
on its nature, they unanimously agree that  it signifies an end to the miseries of life. This goal is
regarded as the highest pursuit, with different paths prescribed for its attainment, such as paths of
knowledge (jnāna), devotion (Bhakti), and action (karma).
6. Spiritualism: Indian philosophy is characterised as spiritual because it places the spirit or soul
above the body or matter. With the exception of the Chārvāka system, which advocates materialistic
philosophy, Indian philosophical traditions emphasize spiritual values. The importance of leading a
life dedicated to realising these spiritual values is underscored, aligning with the disciplined moral
life.
7. Epistemological Issues: All Indian philosophical systems engage in elaborate discussions on
epistemological issues, exploring sources of valid knowledge (pramāna), types of valid knowledge
(pramā), types of invalid knowledge (apramā), and related problems. From the Chārvāka system
onwards, these discussions form an integral part of each philosophical  tradition, providing a
foundation for both metaphysical doctrines and critical thinking.

8. Pessimism and Dogmatism Misconceptions: Misconceptions about Indian philosophy being
pessimistic and dogmatic are dispelled upon closer examination. While philosophy begins with a
recognition of the dark aspects of life and explores the root causes of suffering, it does not conclude
with hopelessness. Instead, it offers remedies and paths to overcome suffering.
In essence, Indian philosophy embodies a holistic approach to life, integrating practicality, ethical
considerations, and spiritual values. The convergence of these characteristics across diverse
schools of thought highlights the unity in diversity within the rich tapestry of Indian philosophical
traditions. Each characteristic contributes  to the comprehensive understanding of the human
condition and provides practical guidance for leading a purposeful and virtuous life.

SUMMARY
Philosophy: Philosophy  is a systematic and  critical inquiry into fundamental questions about
existence, knowledge, values, reason, mind, and language.
It involves careful reasoning and reflection to address issues that go beyond the scope of empirical
sciences, seeking to understand the nature of reality, foundations of knowledge, principles governing
morality, and the essence of human experience.
♦    The Core Branches of Philosophy viz. Metaphysics (Nature of Reality): Investigates the

       the same.
