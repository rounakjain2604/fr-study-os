    4.2  BUSINESS ETHICS
The domain of "business ethics" involves a thorough examination of ethical considerations pertinent
to entities engaged in the production and sale of goods and services. This scrutiny extends to ethical
dimensions related to commercial exchanges and activities associated with such transactions,

exhibited sustained interest in these themes, delving into the impact of commerce on character and
delving into issues like profit and pricing.
Business ethics encompasses the principles, values, and standards that dictate responsible and
ethical behaviour in the corporate sphere. It serves as the moral compass that not only delineates
the boundaries of acceptable conduct but also charts the course towards a more sustainable and
socially responsible business environment. It is the study of moral issues arising in the exchange of
goods and services, which are fundamental to daily existence. It involves examining the practices
within organisations that can impact the core values and stakeholders of a business.
Consider a small local restaurant. If the owner pays some employees less than others for the same
work or treats certain employees unfairly based on personal preferences,  it violates principles of
fairness and equality. Business ethics in this context would involve treating all employees fairly,
providing equal opportunities and fair compensation for their work.
Central themes  within  business  ethics encompass  the purpose  of  corporations,  corporate
governance, corporate moral agency, workplace rights and duties, equity in compensation and
pricing, the boundaries of markets, marketing ethics, supply chain ethics, and corporate political
involvement. While philosophers initially steered the trajectory of this field, social scientists later
contributed, incorporating business ethics into academic curricula within business schools.
The philosophy underpinning business, as mirrored in business ethics, endeavours to ascertain the
fundamental objectives of a company as expressed through its business mission.
Contrary to being synonymous with law, ethics is commonly regarded as a set of principles or
standards that govern daily decision-making. Corporations and professional organisations typically
articulate a written code of ethics outlining anticipated standards of professional conduct. The
Friedman doctrine, articulated by Milton Friedman, posits that the primary responsibility of corporate
executives is to maximize profits within legal and ethical boundaries, prompting both proponents and
critics.
Fundamentally, business ethics is founded on fundamental principles such as honesty, integrity,
transparency, accountability, and fairness. These principles provide a framework for organisations
to navigate complex moral dilemmas and make decisions that align with societal expectations. The
ethical foundations of a business are crucial in establishing trust among stakeholders, including
customers, employees, investors, and the community at large.
One of the key tenets of business ethics  is the recognition of multiple stakeholders and the
acknowledgment of their interests. Beyond a narrow focus on profit maximisation, ethical businesses

4.2.1. Contemporary Issues in Business Ethics
The field of business ethics is dynamic, reflecting the evolving nature of commerce, technology, and
societal expectations. As businesses operate in an increasingly interconnected and complex global
environment, they face a myriad of ethical challenges. Examining contemporary issues in business
ethics provides insights into the multifaceted nature of ethical dilemmas that organisations grapple
with today.
Corporate Governance and Accountability:
Effective corporate governance is essential for maintaining transparency, accountability, and ethical
conduct within organisations. Recent years have witnessed increased scrutiny of corporate boards
and executives, fueled by high-profile cases of corporate misconduct and financial scandals. Issues
such as executive compensation, board diversity, and the separation of CEO and chairman roles
have become focal points for discussions on governance. Establishing robust governance structures
that prioritize ethical decision making and stakeholder interests is crucial for building trust and
ensuring the long-term success of businesses.
Environmental Sustainability:
The impact of business activities on the environment has emerged as a pressing ethical concern.
Climate change, resource depletion, and environmental degradation pose significant threats to the
planet. Businesses are increasingly expected to adopt environmentally sustainable practices, reduce
carbon footprints, and contribute to global efforts for conservation. Balancing economic objectives
with environmental responsibility requires innovative strategies, and businesses that  prioritize

Social Justice and Diversity:
The call for social justice and equity has become a central theme in contemporary business ethics.
Stakeholders, including customers, employees, and investors, are demanding that businesses
address issues of diversity, equity, and inclusion. Ethical considerations now extend beyond financial
performance  to encompass  social  responsibility. Companies are expected  to take proactive
measures to eliminate discrimination, promote diversity in the workplace, and contribute to social
justice initiatives. Fostering an inclusive corporate culture is not only ethically sound but also
enhances organisational resilience and adaptability.
Ethics in Technology and Artificial Intelligence (AI):
The  rapid  advancement  of  technology  introduces  ethical  challenges  that  require  careful
consideration. Issues such as data privacy, algorithmic bias, and the responsible use of artificial
intelligence (AI) are at the forefront of ethical debates. Businesses must grapple with questions
surrounding the ethical development and deployment of technology, ensuring that innovation aligns
with human values and societal norms. Ethical considerations in technology are not only relevant to
tech companies but extend to all industries incorporating digital solutions into their operations.
Global Supply Chain Ethics:
As businesses operate in a globalised economy, they are intricately connected to complex supply
chains that often span multiple countries and regions. Ensuring ethical conduct throughout the
supply chain presents challenges related to labor practices, environmental impact, and adherence
to human rights standards. Issues such as child labour, exploitation of workers, and environmental
degradation  in the supply chain demand heightened  scrutiny.  Ethical  sourcing, responsible
procurement practices, and supplier accountability are crucial components of modern business
ethics.
Consumer Privacy and Data Security:
In an era dominated by digital transactions and online interactions, the protection of consumer
privacy and data security has become a critical ethical consideration. Businesses are entrusted with
vast amounts of personal data, and the responsible handling of this information is paramount. High-
profile data breaches and privacy scandals have underscored the need for robust cybersecurity
measures and transparent data practices. Striking a balance between utilising consumer data for
business insights and safeguarding individual privacy is an ongoing challenge in contemporary
business ethics.

Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) and Stakeholder Capitalism:
The concept of CSR has evolved beyond philanthropy to encompass a broader commitment to

relationships with stakeholders and enhance their overall reputation.
Ethical Leadership and Organisational Culture:
The role of leadership in shaping the ethical culture of an organisation is a persistent and evolving
issue in business ethics. Ethical leadership involves setting a tone of integrity, promoting ethical
behaviour, and fostering a culture of transparency and accountability. Organisations are recognizing
the importance of nurturing ethical leaders who prioritize values and principles over short-term gains.
Building an ethical organisational culture involves aligning policies, practices, and incentives with
ethical values, creating an environment where employees are empowered to make ethical decisions.
Whistleblowing and Ethical Reporting Mechanisms:
Encouraging a culture of transparency and accountability within organisations involves providing
mechanisms for employees to report unethical behaviour without fear of retaliation. Whistleblowing
plays a crucial role in uncovering misconduct and fostering a culture of integrity. Businesses are
increasingly implementing robust ethical reporting mechanisms to address issues internally before
they escalate. Ethical reporting not only serves as a deterrent to misconduct but also demonstrates
a commitment to ethical values.
Post-Pandemic Ethical Considerations:
The global COVID-19 pandemic has introduced new ethical challenges for businesses. Issues such
as employee safety, remote work ethics, supply chain disruptions, and fair access to resources have
come to the forefront. Businesses are reevaluating their crisis response strategies and considering
the ethical implications of decisions made during times of uncertainty. Adapting to the post-pandemic
landscape requires a renewed focus on resilience, empathy, and responsible decision making.

4.2.2.Important Framework For Business Ethics
Business ethics provides a structured framework for organisations to navigate moral complexities,
make ethical decisions, and establish a culture of integrity. Several prominent frameworks guide
businesses in their ethical considerations, offering comprehensive approaches to ethical decision
making and responsible business practices.
Choosing one "most important" framework for business ethics is difficult, as each offers valuable
perspectives. However, some frameworks have wider application and can serve as foundational for
understanding ethical decision making in business. The Bhagavad Gita serves as a profound
philosophical guide that transcends its religious context, offering timeless wisdom applicable to
various aspects of life, including the realm of business ethics. Composed as a conversation between
Prince Arjuna and Lord Krishna on the battlefield of Kurukshetra, the Gita addresses profound moral
dilemmas and ethical principles, providing a holistic framework for ethical conduct in both personal
and professional spheres.
4.2.3. Business Ethics from The Bhagavad Gita
Bhagavad Gita is a philosophical masterpiece that constitutes an essential component of the ancient
Indian epic, the Mahabharata. Translated as the "Song of God," the Gita unfolds a conversation
between Prince Arjuna and Lord Krishna, wherein the latter imparts profound wisdom to the
distressed warrior prince. Covering a broad spectrum of themes such as duty, morality, self-
realization, and the pursuit of excellence, the Gita stands as a reservoir of timeless teachings.
4.2.4. Karma Yoga for Superior Business Performance
In the fast-paced landscape of the contemporary business environment, where ambition intertwines
with competition and metrics take precedence, a fundamental question emerges: Is  it possible to
separate success from purpose? Can the pursuit of excellence occur independently of ethical
principles? Karma Yoga, a profound concept originating from ancient Indian philosophy and
particularly expounded in the Bhagavad Gita, offers a unique perspective on action, selflessness,
and ethical conduct. In the context of business, the principles of Karma Yoga can be applied to
achieve superior performance while maintaining a focus on moral and ethical behaviour.
Karma Yoga is one of the four paths to spiritual realisation outlined in the Bhagavad Gita, a sacred
Hindu scripture. Karma Yoga is the path of selfless action, emphasizing performing one's duties

without attachment to the results. The term "karma" refers to action, and "yoga" means union or
connection. Karma Yoga, therefore, is the yoga of selfless action that fosters a connection between
the individual and the divine, as well as between the individual and society. Karma Yoga (the "yoga

The Five Pillars of Karma Yoga for Business Excellence
♦    Nishkama Karma: Action without Attachment: At the heart of Karma Yoga lies the principle
       of Nishkama Karma, or selfless action. This does not imply apathy or lack of ambition, but
       rather a focus on the process of work itself, on performing every task with dedication and
       mindfulness, regardless of the outcome. In business, this manifests in a commitment to
       excellence, innovation, and ethical practices, even when immediate rewards are uncertain.
♦    Samata: Equanimity and Acceptance: Life  is a tapestry of triumphs and challenges,
      successes and failures. Karma Yoga teaches us to approach both with equanimity, neither
       rejoicing excessively in victories nor lamenting defeats. This emotional stability translates to
       resilience in the face of adversity, enabling business leaders to make sound decisions amidst
      market fluctuations and unexpected challenges.
♦    Kartavya: Duty and Responsibility: Each  role we play  in  life comes  with inherent
        responsibilities. Karma Yoga emphasizes fulfilling these duties with diligence and a sense of
       service. For business leaders, this translates to taking responsibility for their employees,
      communities, and the environment.  It means upholding ethical standards, ensuring  fair
      compensation and working conditions, and contributing to social and environmental well-
       being.
♦    Dharma: Righteousness and Ethical Conduct: Dharma serves as the compass  for
       navigating life's choices. It guides us towards actions that are aligned with rightfulness and
       social good. In business, this translates to conducting operations with integrity, avoiding
       shortcuts and unethical practices, and prioritizing transparency and honesty in all dealings.
♦    Swadharma:  Finding  Your  Purpose: Karma  Yoga  emphasizes  discovering  one's
     Swadharma, or innate purpose. This is the unique role each individual plays in the grand

    4.3  JUSTICE: FREEDOM, RESPONSIBILITY AND ACTION
Justice is a complex and multifaceted concept that encompasses fairness, equity, and the upholding
of moral and legal principles in the treatment of individuals and groups. It involves the fair distribution
of rights, resources, opportunities, and consequences within a society or a system. The concept of
justice seeks to ensure that all individuals are treated impartially, that their rights are respected, and
that they have access to a fair and unbiased legal system.
However, in the context of today’s world, the same source that discusses karma and equanimity i.e.
the Bhagavad Gita’s definition of justice is more relevant particularly to accountancy profession. As
per 18.48 verse in Bhagavad Gita, every endeavour has some or the other flaw. We will always be
tested and while there are various stakeholders involved, and every business will have assets and
liabilities. While justice is to take care of all stakeholders, in the current situation, in most instances
the decision maker is forced to take sides and cause imbalance.
For example, let us take an example of a large Titanic like ship is about to sink. It has thousand
people on board and has lifeboats just sufficient to save about a hundred people. How can one
decide who deserves to be on the lifeboat?
As in the example stated above, justice cannot be served to all stakeholders every time. However,
by keeping one’s emotions in control and having a clear understanding of various duties, their
similarities and dissimilarities with other duties and the hierarchy of duties, the person will be able

4.4.1. Ecofeminism
Ecofeminism, a segment of feminist ideology that explores the interconnections between women
and nature, was coined by French feminist Françoise d’Eaubonne in 1974. Rooted in fundamental
feminist principles such as gender equality, the reevaluation of non-patriarchal structures, and an
appreciation for perspectives outside traditional linear frameworks, ecofeminism extends its focus
to include a reverence for organic processes, holistic connections, and the merits of intuition and
collaboration.  In  addition  to  these  foundational  principles,  ecofeminism  introduces a  dual
commitment: one to environmental preservation and another to an understanding of the associations
established between women and the natural world. Specifically,  this philosophical framework
underscores the parallel ways in which both nature and women are treated within patriarchal or
male-centric societies.
Ecofeminists scrutinise the impact of gender classifications to illuminate how societal norms unjustly
exert dominance over women and nature. This philosophy contends that these norms contribute to
an incomplete worldview, and proponents of ecofeminism advocate for an alternative perspective
that venerates the earth as sacred, acknowledges humanity’s dependence on the natural world, and
recognises the intrinsic value of all life.

Ecofeminism  is a theoretical framework aiming to explore the interconnectedness of gender,
ecology, and social justice. This movement highlights the parallel oppressions faced by women and
the environment, arguing that both are intricately linked through systems of power, domination, and
exploitation.
Historical Roots of Ecofeminism:
Ecofeminism has deep roots in various social movements, including the feminist movement and the
environmental movement. In the 1970s and 1980s, as women began to participate more actively in
environmental activism, scholars and activists started to recognise the commonalities between the
exploitation of women and the exploitation of nature. The term "ecofeminism" gained prominence as
a way to articulate the interconnected struggles for gender equality and environmental sustainability.
Basic Tenets of Ecofeminism:
1.     Intersectionality: Ecofeminism emphasizes the interconnectedness of various forms of
       oppression, such as those related to gender, race, class, and the environment. It recognises
       that different systems of power intersect, creating unique experiences of oppression for
       individuals who may be subject to multiple forms of discrimination.
2.     Critique of Dualism: Ecofeminists critique the dualistic thinking that often separates nature
      from culture and women from men. This binary perspective, they argue, contributes to the
       exploitation of both women and the environment. Ecofeminism seeks to break down these
         artificial divisions, promoting a more holistic understanding of the interdependence of all living
       beings.
3.    Valuing Feminine Perspectives: Ecofeminism challenges traditional patriarchal views that
      devalue attributes traditionally associated with femininity, such as nurturing, cooperation, and
      interconnectedness. By valuing feminine perspectives, ecofeminism seeks to create a more
      balanced and sustainable approach to environmental stewardship.
4.    Activism and Advocacy: Ecofeminism encourages activism and advocacy for social and
      environmental justice. Ecofeminists are often involved in campaigns against environmental
       degradation, exploitation of natural resources, and the disproportionate impacts of climate
      change on vulnerable communities, particularly women in developing countries.
Ecofeminism has inspired and intersected with various global women's movements. Women around
the world are at the forefront of environmental conservation efforts, sustainable agriculture practices,
and community resilience building. Examples include the Chipko movement in India, where women
hugged trees to prevent deforestation, and the Green Belt Movement in Kenya, led by Nobel laureate
Wangari Maathai. The movement actively contributes to the discourse on environmental justice by
