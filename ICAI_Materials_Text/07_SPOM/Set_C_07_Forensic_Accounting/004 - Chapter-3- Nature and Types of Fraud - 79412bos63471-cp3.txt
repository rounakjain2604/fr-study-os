      3.1  CATEGORIES OF FRAUDS
3.1.1  Background
Fraud can be broadly categorized into three main categories viz. fraud against individuals, fraud
against companies, and fraud by companies.


(1)   Fraud against individuals: This includes various types of scams and fraudulent activities
       targeted at individuals. Some common examples include identity theft, phishing scams,
      investment fraud, credit card fraud, and lottery or inheritance scams. These frauds are
      aimed at deceiving individuals and unlawfully obtaining their personal information, money,
       or assets.
(2)   Fraud against corporates:  This  category  involves  fraudulent  activities  specifically
       targeting businesses or organizations. Examples include embezzlement, false  billing,
       payroll fraud, asset misappropriation, and intellectual property theft. These frauds are
        typically carried out by employees, vendors, or business partners and can result  in
       significant financial losses for the company.
(3)   Fraud by corporates: This  refers  to  fraudulent  activities committed by companies
      themselves. It can involve various illegal practices such as financial statement fraud, insider
       trading,  bribery,  tax  evasion, and  price-fixing. These  frauds  are  usually aimed  at
      manipulating financial records, deceiving investors, avoiding legal obligations, or gaining
      an unfair advantage in the market.
It is important for individuals and companies to be aware of  different types of fraud and take
appropriate measures to protect themselves. This includes implementing strong security measures,
conducting regular audits, educating employees, and reporting any suspicious activities to the
relevant authorities.

3.2.2  Banking Fraud:
Banking fraud refers to any deceitful or illegal activities conducted by individuals or groups with the
intention of acquiring money or assets from a bank or its customers through deceptive means. These
fraudulent activities can take various forms, such as identity  theft, credit card fraud, account
takeover, phishing scams, or forging documents.
Fraudsters often employ sophisticated techniques to manipulate or deceive individuals or systems
within the banking sector. They might use stolen personal information to access accounts, create
fake identities, or trick people into providing sensitive information like passwords or account details
through deceptive emails or websites.
Banking  fraud can manifest  in various  forms, each  targeting  different aspects  of  financial
transactions and customer accounts. Below are some common types:
(a)    Identity theft: Fraudsters steal personal information (such as social security numbers,
      addresses,  or  passwords)  to  impersonate  individuals, open  accounts,  or  conduct
       transactions under false identities.
(b)   Phishing scams: This involves fraudulent attempts to obtain sensitive information (like
       login credentials, credit card details) by pretending to be a trustworthy entity via emails,
      websites, or messages.
(c)   Account takeover: Hackers gain unauthorized access to a person's bank account by
       obtaining their login credentials through various means and then manipulate the account
        for their benefit.
(d)    Credit/ Debit card fraud: Criminals use stolen or cloned cards to make unauthorized
       transactions, either online or at physical stores.
(e)   ATM skimming: Criminals install devices on ATMs to steal card information, which they
      use to create counterfeit cards or make unauthorized transactions.
(f)   Cheque fraud: This involves creating counterfeit or altering genuine cheques to withdraw
      funds that do not belong to the fraudster.
(g)   Loan fraud: Individuals provide  false information  or documents  to secure loans  or
      mortgages, often resulting in non-repayment or default.
(h)    Insider fraud: Employees within financial institutions misuse their access to perform
       fraudulent activities like unauthorized transactions, data theft, or manipulation of records.

3.2.3  Corporate Fraud:
Corporate fraud involves deceptive or  illegal activities conducted within a company or by  its
employees, officers, or executives, often for personal gain or to benefit the organization in an
unlawful manner.
This type of fraud can take numerous forms and impact different facets of a company's operations.
(a)    Financial statement fraud: This is the most common type of corporate fraud where the
     company  misrepresents  its  financial  performance  to  investors,  creditors,  or  other
       stakeholders. Examples include overstating revenues or assets, understating liabilities, or
      manipulating financial records.
(b)   Asset misappropriation:  Illicit use  or  theft  of company resources,  including cash,
       inventory, intellectual property, or other assets, through schemes like embezzlement or
        theft.
(c)    Bribery and corruption: This type of fraud involves offering or receiving something of
      value in exchange for a business advantage.  It can include bribing government officials,
      customers, or suppliers to secure contracts or favourable treatment.
(d)    Insider trading: This type of fraud involves using confidential and non-public information
      about a company to make stock trades for personal gain, often by company insiders or
       individuals with privileged information. It is illegal and can result in severe penalties.
(e)   Kickbacks and procurement Fraud: Offering or receiving improper payments or favours
        in exchange for business opportunities or contracts, leading to inflated prices or biased
      decision-making.

(f)    Embezzlement: This is the theft of company funds or assets by an employee or executive
        for personal use. It can involve siphoning off cash, manipulating expense reports, or using
     company assets for personal gain.
(g)    Intellectual property theft: This type of fraud involves stealing a company's trade secrets,
       patents, or other intellectual property for personal or competitive advantage.
(h)   Money laundering: This is the process of concealing source of illegally obtained money

3.2.4  Insurance Fraud
Insurance fraud refers to any deceitful or illegal act committed with the intent to obtain a fraudulent
outcome from an insurance process.  It can involve various  parties, including policyholders,
insurance agents or brokers, and even service providers.
Insurance  fraud  represents  a  pervasive  and  costly  issue  within  the  insurance  industry,
encompassing a range of deceptive practices that exploit insurance policies for unlawful gain. This
multifaceted problem spans various sectors, from healthcare and property insurance to life and
automobile coverage.
Below are a few common forms of insurance frauds:
(a)    False claims: Individuals may submit false or exaggerated claims to an insurance company
        for the events that never occurred or for losses that are unrelated to the insurance policy.
(b)   Staged accidents: This type  of fraud involves  intentionally causing an accident or
      exaggerating the severity of an accident in order to file false insurance claims.
(c)   Premium fraud: This occurs when an individual or business provides false information to
       insurers, to obtain a lower insurance premium, such as misrepresenting the number of
      employees, the nature of their business, location of vehicle or the purpose for which  it is
      used.

(e)   Arson: This is the deliberate act of setting fire to a property in order to collect the insurance

3.2.5  Securities Fraud
Securities fraud, also known as investment fraud, is the illegal practice of deceiving investors or
manipulating financial markets for personal gain. It typically involves providing false or misleading
information about a company or investment opportunity in order to induce investors to buy or sell
securities.
This can include misrepresenting financial statements, insider trading, ponzi schemes, and other
fraudulent activities. Securities fraud not only harms individual investors, but  it can also have a
negative impact on the overall economy and financial markets. It is a serious crime that is prosecuted

by government agencies and can result in significant penalties and imprisonment.
Some common forms of securities fraud include:
(a)    Insider trading: Occurs when individuals with access to non-public, material information
      about a company trade its securities, exploiting their privileged information for profit. This
        activity is illegal because  it unfairly advantages those with insider knowledge over the
      general investing public.
(b)   Misrepresentation or false statements: Companies or individuals might provide false or
      misleading  information about a company's  financial  status,  performance,  or  future
      prospects to artificially inflate stock prices or deceive investors.
(c)   Ponzi schemes: Fraudsters promise high returns to investors, using funds from new
       investors to pay returns to earlier investors rather than generating legitimate profits from
      investments. As the scheme requires a continuous flow of new investors,  it eventually
       collapses.
(d)   Market manipulation: This is when individuals or entities artificially inflate or deflate the

(e)   Accounting fraud: Companies manipulate financial statements or accounting records to
      present a false or misleading picture of their financial health, such as inflating revenues or
       understating liabilities.
(f)   Pump and Dump schemes: This occurs when individuals or entities artificially inflate the
       price of a security by spreading false or misleading information, and then sell their shares
       at the inflated price.
(g)   Churning: This is a practice where a broker excessively trades in a client's account in
      order to generate commissions for themselves, rather than acting in the client's best
        interest.
(h)   Front running: This is when a broker or trader uses information about a client's pending
       trade to make their own trade before the client's, in order to take advantage of the market
      movement.
(i)    High-yield investment scams: This involves promising investors high returns on their
      investment with little or no risk, enticing investors to invest in fraudulent schemes, but using
       the money for personal gain instead of legitimate investments.

3.2.6 Consumer Fraud:
Consumer fraud refers to deceptive or dishonest practices that target individuals or consumers,
aiming to gain an unfair advantage or financial benefit at the consumers' expense. It involves false
or misleading representations, omissions of important information, or other deceptive tactics in the
sale of goods, services, or information.
Consumer  fraud can  occur  in  various  industries, such as  retail,  banking,  insurance, and
telecommunications, and can have serious consequences for victims, including financial loss and
damage to their credit and personal reputation.
Common forms of consumer fraud include:
(a)    False advertising: Misleading or false claims about a product or service’s features,
       benefits, or performance to attract customers.
(b)   Phishing scams: Phishing scams involve a fraudster posing as a legitimate company or
       organization and sending emails or messages to individuals to obtain  their personal
       information, such as login credentials or credit card numbers.
(c)    Identity theft: Deceptive attempts to obtain personal information such as name, address,
      passwords, credit card numbers, or social security numbers through fake emails, websites,
       or phone calls to commit fraud or other crimes.
(d)   Telemarketing fraud: Deceptive telemarketing practices that lure individuals into making
      purchases or providing financial information under false pretences.

3.2.7  Intellectual Property Fraud:
Intellectual property fraud refers to the unauthorized use or theft of someone else's ideas, inventions,
or creative works without their permission. This can include copyright infringement, trademark
infringement, patent infringement, and trade secret theft.
Intellectual property fraud not only harms the rightful owner of the property, but also violates laws
and regulations, undermines competition and innovation and can result in financial losses for both
individuals and businesses. It is a growing problem in today's digital age, as the ease of sharing and
distributing information online has made  it easier for fraudsters to exploit and profit from others'
intellectual property.
There are several forms of intellectual property frauds which are explained below:
1.    Patent infringement: This occurs when someone falsely claims to have invented or
      patented a product or technology, or when they illegally use someone else's patented
       invention for their own gain.
2.    Trademark infringement: This involves unauthorised use of logos, brand names, or marks
       that are identical or similar to registered trademarks, leading to consumer confusion about
       the source of goods or services.
3.    Copyright  infringement: Unauthorized  use,  reproduction,  distribution  or  selling  of
      copyrighted material, such as music, movies, literature, software, or artwork, without the
      permission of the copyright holder.
4.    Trade Secret theft: This involves unauthorized disclosure or use of confidential and
       proprietary  information, such as  formulas,  designs,  or processes,  for a company's
       competitive advantage.
5.    Domain name fraud: This involves registering domain names that are similar to popular
      brands or companies in order to deceive customers and profit from their confusion.
6.     Counterfeiting: Producing or selling fake or unauthorized replicas of branded goods,
       including clothing, electronics, pharmaceuticals, and more without permission from the
       original trademark owner that infringe on the intellectual property rights of the original
      brand.
7.    Plagiarism: This occurs when someone presents someone else's work as their own without
      proper attribution or permission.

Occupational fraud is typically committed by employees or managers who have access to company
resources and use their position to deceive or defraud the company for personal gain. It can result
in financial losses for the company and damage to its reputation.

3.3.2  Asset misappropriation:
Asset misappropriation refers to  fraudulent and unauthorized use or theft of an organization's
resources or assets by individuals within the organization. It's one of the primary categories of
occupational fraud and  typically involves employees or insiders manipulating or stealing the
company's resources for personal gain. Examples include theft of cash, inventory, or intellectual
property, as well as false invoicing or expense reimbursement schemes. It often occurs under the
radar, exploiting gaps in internal controls and trust within the workplace.
This deceitful conduct not only impacts a company's financial health but also erodes trust, damaging
the organizational culture and reputation.
The schemes employed in asset misappropriation are diverse and adaptable, making detection
challenging. Employees might exploit weaknesses in accounting systems, manipulate records or
collude with external parties to divert funds or resources. For instance, individuals might create
fictitious vendors, submit inflated invoices, or fabricate expense reports thereby siphoning off funds
for personal use.

necessitating robust internal controls, regular audits, and a vigilant eye for irregularities.
Preventing and addressing asset misappropriation requires a multi-layered strategy that combines
preventive measures with a culture of transparency and accountability. Instituting strict controls over
access  to  assets, segregating  duties, and implementing whistleblower  hotlines  or  reporting
mechanisms all play vital roles. Equally important is fostering a work environment where ethical
behaviour is encouraged and employees are educated about fraud risks. By establishing a strong
ethical foundation and implementing stringent controls, organizations can significantly reduce the
likelihood of falling victim to asset misappropriation methods.

3.3.3  Financial Statement Fraud:
Financial statement fraud involves intentionally manipulating financial statements to misrepresent a
company's financial performance, position, or cash flows. It involves manipulating financial reports
to either overstate or understate net worth or net income.
Overstating net worth or income entails inflating revenues, understating expenses, or improperly
valuing assets to portray a healthier financial picture than reality. This misleading representation can
attract investors, inflate stock prices, and provide a false sense of the company's financial strength.
Conversely, understating net worth or income involves hiding revenues, overstating expenses, or
undervaluing assets to present a weaker financial position. This manipulation can result in reduced
tax  liabilities, mislead stakeholders, or conceal  financial  difficulties, impacting investor  trust,
regulatory compliance, and overall business stability.

             undervaluing inventory or property, results in a decrease in  reported net worth or
            income, misleading stakeholders about the company's financial health.
       (e)   Improper disclosures: Not  fully disclosing certain  liabilities, risks, or material
             information can lead to an understatement of net worth or income. This can mislead
             stakeholders about true financial position or performance of the company.
      Impact:      It  can  misguide  investors,  lenders,  and  stakeholders,  leading  to  an
      understatement of company’s financial position.  It might result  in lower stock prices,
      decreased investor confidence, or inadequate decisions based on incomplete or inaccurate
       financial information.
      Preventive measures: Preventing understatement of net worth or net income involves
      implementing robust internal controls, conducting regular audits, and ensuring accurate
     and transparent financial reporting.
      This includes thorough verification of expenses and revenues, independent valuations of
       assets, comprehensive disclosure of  liabilities and risks, and fostering an ethical and
      accountable organizational culture.
      Educating stakeholders about financial statements and promoting transparency in reporting
      are vital for preventing and detecting instances of financial statement fraud.

Bribery:
Bribery, within the context of corruption, involves   offering, giving, receiving, or  soliciting of
something of value to influence actions of an individual in a position of power or responsibility, often
for personal gain or to gain an unfair advantage. Two common forms of bribery are invoice kickbacks
and bid rigging.
(a)    Invoice kickbacks
Invoice kickbacks occur when an employee or contractor receives a payment or kickback from a
vendor or supplier after inflating the invoices or prices for goods or services purchased by their
organization. This artificially increases the cost to the organization, and the difference is funnelled
back to the employee or contractor as a kickback or bribe.
Example: A company's employee colludes with a vendor and inflates the invoice for supplies. The
vendor charges more than the market rate, and the excess amount is returned to the employee as
a kickback.
(b)   Bid rigging
Bid rigging involves conspiring among competitors in bidding process to manipulate the outcome of
a competitive bidding process. Rather than competing fairly, individuals or companies collude to
predetermine the winner, artificially inflate prices, or decide who will submit the winning bid, allowing
one party to win the contract without legitimate competition.
Example: Several companies agree in advance on who will submit the lowest bid among them,
ensuring one designated company wins the contract. They might also agree to submit inflated bids
to give the appearance of competition while ensuring a specific company secures the contract.
Impact: Bribery through invoice kickbacks and bid rigging distorts fair competition, inflates costs,
and undermines the integrity of procurement processes. It leads to financial losses for organizations,
compromises transparency, and damages trust in the fairness of business practices.
Preventive measures: Preventing bribery involves implementing robust  anti-bribery  policies,
conducting  thorough  due  diligence  on  vendors  and  contractors,  establishing  transparent
procurement procedures, and fostering a culture of ethics and integrity. Encouraging whistleblowing,
conducting audits, and enforcing strict penalties for bribery offenses are crucial in detecting and
deterring invoice kickbacks and bid rigging, ensuring unfair and transparent business dealings.

Ponzi scheme:
It is a type of investment fraud that operates on a fraudulent promise of high returns to investors. It
is named after Charles Ponzi, who became infamous for using this method in the early 20th century.
Ponzi schemes often seem legitimate at the beginning, with early investors sometimes receiving the
promised returns. This illusion of success can attract more investors, perpetuating the cycle.
Below are the characteristics of Ponzi scheme:
(a)    False Promises: The scheme promises high returns to investors on their investments,
       generally much higher than what traditional investments offer. These returns often appear
       consistent, regardless of market conditions.
(b)   No Legitimate Business: Unlike legitimate investments, Ponzi schemes do not generate
       actual profits through viable business activities or investments. Instead, they use funds
      from new investors to pay returns to earlier investors.
(c)   Unsustainable Model: As the scheme relies on a continuous influx of new investors to pay
       returns to earlier ones, it can only sustain itself as long as there is a constant flow of new
      money. When new investors stop joining or existing investors demand their money back,
       the scheme collapses.
Impact: When the scheme can no longer recruit enough new investors to pay returns or meet
withdrawal demands, it collapses, leaving most investors with significant losses.
Preventive measures: It is advisable that individuals to be cautious of investment opportunities that
promise unusually high returns with little or no risk. Conducting thorough research, understanding
the investment, and being wary of anything that seems too good to be true are crucial steps in
avoiding falling victim to a Ponzi scheme.

Money Laundering:
Money laundering is the process of concealing the origins of illegally obtained money by transferring
it through legitimate businesses or bank accounts. This is done in order to make the money appear
to have been obtained through legal means.
Money laundering  is often associated with various criminal activities, such as drug trafficking,
bribery, fraud, and organized crime. The purpose of money laundering is to avoid detection and
prosecution by law enforcement agencies and to ultimately integrate the illegally obtained funds into
the legitimate financial system. This allows criminals to enjoy the proceeds of their illegal activities
without raising suspicion. Money laundering is a serious crime that can have significant impacts on
the economy and society as a whole.
Money laundering often involves collaboration among multiple parties, such as criminals, financial
institutions, legal professionals, and other intermediaries, making it a complex transnational issue
that requires international cooperation for effective prevention and prosecution.
Some key characteristics and elements often associated with money laundering are listed below:
(a)   LAYERING:
       Placement: Illegally obtained funds are introduced into the financial system through
             various methods, such as cash deposits, investments, or purchases, to distance the
          money from its criminal source.
       Layering: The money is then moved through complex financial transactions across
               different accounts, jurisdictions, or financial institutions to hide its origin and make
              tracing more difficult.
        Integration: Once sufficiently layered, the funds are reintroduced into the economy in
            seemingly legitimate transactions, making it challenging to distinguish between lawful
           and illicit funds.
(b)   RED FLAGS
       Large cash transactions: Sudden large cash deposits or withdrawals without a
            reasonable explanation may signal attempts to launder money.
       Frequent international transfers: Multiple transactions involving different countries
              or  jurisdictions without a clear business purpose could be indicative  of money
              laundering.

      3.6  FRAUD MECHANISM
Background:
Fraud mechanism refers to the methods and techniques used by individuals or organizations to
deceive and cheat others for financial gain or other personal benefits. These fraudulent activities
can cause significant financial losses and damage to victims, and it is important for individuals and
businesses to be aware of these mechanisms and take measures to protect themselves against
such frauds. Fraud mechanisms can vary widely depending on the context and the type of fraud
being perpetrated.
(1)    Subsidiaries or Shell companies: Fraudulent transactions within companies often involve
      complex methods aimed at deceiving stakeholders and manipulating financial records for
      personal gain. One common method is channelling funds to their own subsidiaries or shell
        entities. These transfers might seem  legitimate but often serve  to  divert money or
      manipulate financial statements. For example, companies might inflate payments to a
       subsidiary for services never rendered, thereby increasing expenses while directing funds
       to an entity controlled by insiders or stakeholders.
(2)   Related party transactions: Fraudulent  activities also include  fictitious invoicing or
       overbilling between related entities. Companies intentionally exaggerate the cost of goods
       or services obtained from a related party, enabling them to transfer excess funds to the
       related  entity. This practice creates a facade  of increased expenses while  actually
       redirecting funds to a controlled entity under the mask of legitimate transactions.
     Example 1: By engaging  in transactions with  entities sharing ownership or  control,
      companies can misrepresent revenues, understate expenses, or manipulate financial
       indicators. For instance, a company might underreport sales to a related entity, artificially
      reducing revenues to avoid taxes or mislead investors.

        (c)   Abuse  of  related  party  transactions:  Related  party  transactions,  when
             manipulated, become a breeding ground for fraudulent activities. By conducting
             transactions with  entities having shared ownership or  control, companies can
            misrepresent revenues, understate expenses, or manipulate financial metrics. For
             instance, a company might underreport sales to a related entity, artificially deflating
            revenues to evade taxes or deceive investors.
       (d)   Round tripping and circulation transactions: Another fraudulent practice involves
              round-tripping or circular transactions. This scheme creates an appearance of
              legitimate sales or purchases between related entities, but in reality, no actual
           economic activity occurs. Funds are transferred back and forth between entities to
                 artificially inflate revenues or create a false impression of market activity, deceiving
             investors and regulators.
       (e)   Complex financial structures: Fraudulent transactions  often  exploit complex
               financial structures. Companies establish intricate webs of subsidiaries, offshore
               entities, and shell companies to obscure transactions, hide ownership, or manipulate
               financial records. These convoluted structures facilitate fraudulent activities, making
                       it challenging for regulators and auditors to trace the flow of funds or discern the true
             nature of transactions.
      Fraudulent transactions through fund transfers to own entities and related party dealings
       exploit loopholes in regulatory oversight, aiming to deceive stakeholders and artificially
      enhance financial performance. Such practices erode trust, mislead investors, and can
      severely impact the financial health and reputation of companies involved. Regulatory
       scrutiny, transparency in financial reporting, and robust internal controls are essential to
       detect and prevent such fraudulent activities, safeguarding the interests of investors and
       the integrity of financial markets.

      Preventive measures:
       (a)   Implement strict controls and procedures: Companies should have strict controls
           and procedures in place for related party transactions. This includes establishing
              clear approval processes and requiring documentation and disclosure of all related
              party transactions.
       (b)   Conduct  independent  valuations:  Companies  should  conduct  independent
             valuations of assets and liabilities involved in related party transactions to ensure
             they are being valued accurately.
        (c)    Monitor conflicts of interest: Companies should monitor for potential conflicts of
              interest between related parties and take appropriate action to mitigate the risk of
              fraud.
       (d)   Require disclosure of related party transactions: Companies should require
             disclosure of all related party transactions in financial statements, including the
             nature of the relationship and the terms of the transaction.
       (e)   Conduct regular audits: Regular audits can help identify any irregularities or
             suspicious transactions, providing an opportunity to investigate and prevent fraud.
         (f)    Educate employees: Employees should be educated on the risks of related party
             transaction fraud and the importance of following proper procedures and controls.
       (g)   Enforce consequences: Companies should have consequences  in place  for
           employees who  engage  in  fraudulent  related  party  transactions,  including
              disciplinary action and legal consequences.
       In conclusion, related party transaction fraud can have serious consequences  for a
      company, including financial losses and damage to its reputation. By implementing strict
       controls and procedures, conducting regular audits, and educating employees, companies
      can reduce the risk of related party transaction fraud and protect their assets.
