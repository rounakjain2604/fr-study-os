      9.1  SUMMARY OF FINDINGS (CHECKING ALL EVIDENCE)
The Professional shall issue a written report which conveys the results of the assignment clearly and
accurately. It is a logical and clear representation of the facts collected and found necessary by the
professional to reach the result of the assignment. The findings reported shall be based on evidence
gathered which are reliable and relevant and in line with FAIS 320 on “Evidence and Documentation”.
Following are the broad points that can be considered while drafting a summary of findings:
1.     Clarity  and  Conciseness:  Ensure  the  summary   is  clear,  concise,  and  easily
      understandable by stakeholders who may not have a deep understanding of financial or
       forensic matters.
2.    Chronological Order: Present findings in a logical and chronological order. This helps in
      understanding the sequence of events leading to the issues uncovered.
3.    Scope and Objectives: Briefly outline the scope and objectives of the investigation to
      provide context for the findings.
4.    Quantify Findings: Where possible, quantify the impact of the findings. For example,  if
       financial losses occurred, specify the amount.
5.    Legal Implications: Clearly highlight any findings with legal implications. This may include
       fraud, embezzlement, or other criminal activities.
6.    Root Causes: Identify and articulate the root causes of the issues. This helps in addressing
       the problems at their source rather than just treating the symptoms.
7.    Recommendations: Provide actionable recommendations for addressing the identified
       issues. These should be practical and feasible.
8.    Supporting Evidence: Include references to supporting evidence. This could be in the
      form of documents, transaction records, or other relevant materials.
9.     Potential Future Risks: Discuss any potential future risks  if the identified issues are not
      addressed promptly.
10.   Management Response: If available, include responses or comments from management
      regarding the findings. This adds another layer of transparency.
11.   Language: Use non-technical language where possible, or provide explanations  for
       technical terms. The goal is to make the report accessible to a broad audience.

      When reporting is to be done for the interviews taken by the professional during the
              forensic accounting and investigation engagement, following must be noted and taken

♦    Objective and Neutral Tone: Maintain an objective and neutral tone throughout the report.

♦    Detailed Documentation: Document interviews thoroughly, including the date, time,

♦    Interviewee Information: Clearly state the identity and role of each interviewee. Specify

       objectively. Avoid making premature conclusions.
♦    Limitations of Interviews: Acknowledge the limitations of interviews as a source of
       information. Interviews may be subjective, and memories may be fallible.
♦    Legal and Ethical Compliance: Ensure that the interview process complies with legal and
       ethical standards. Be aware of any laws or regulations governing interviews.

      9.2  PRESENTING & DISCUSSING DRAFT REPORT (MAIN
        FACTORS - AS PER FAIS)
The principles of natural justice require a discussion of the observations with the subject party. In
some cases, this is done by the Primary Stakeholders through their own internal processes (e.g.,
disciplinary committee, show-cause  notice,  etc.). At times, the Professional  is requested  to
incorporate the discussion of draft findings as part of the interview process with the subject. Where
the engagement mandate requires a discussion of the draft findings with the subject party, any
response received from them shall also be included in the written report issued by the Professional.

The report shall be issued within reasonable time frame as per the engagement terms. The
Professional may be required to provide interim reports as per the engagement terms which can be
given to the extent practicable without compromising the progress of the Investigation. Such interim
reports are also subject to this Standard.
Where the mandate of the engagement requires a discussion of the findings with the subject party
prior to finalisation, a summary of the responses received from them shall be included in the report.
The Professional may consider the following at the time of issuance of interim reports:
♦    The report may contain ‘Interim Report’ in the title.
♦   A statement to the addressee of the Interim Report that the observations contained in the
       report are based on the work procedures performed till date, which may change on account
       of performance of complete work procedures or information gathered post the date of the
       Interim Report.
♦   A statement that the Interim Report may not be in accordance with  all the Forensic
      Accounting and Investigation Standards.
♦   A statement that due care should be taken by the addressee before taking any actions
      based on the observations in the Interim Report on account of incomplete work procedures.
♦        If, for any reason, the Professional is unable to complete a FAI engagement, the incomplete
      work procedures and the reasons thereof may be adequately captured in the Final Report
      subsequently to be issued by the Professional.

      9.3  KEY  ELEMENTS  AS  PER  FAIS  (TABLE  OF
        CONTENTS, NO OPINION)
Certain drafting qualities of the report are important to ensure its credibility. Examples of some of
these are as follows:
Free from bias: The report is prepared on the basis of facts determined during the course of the
engagement without any personal bias. The report should not include opinions or conclusions on
whether or not a person is guilty or innocent.
Factual: Facts stated in the report are generally supported with relevant and reliable evidence. Such
evidence may be annexed to the main report.
Clear and unambiguous: The language used in the report should be easy to understand.
Transparent: The report may contain complete details of all the findings. In case of any limiting

Distribution of the report to others shall be with the prior approval of the Primary Stakeholders. A
disclaimer may be added to restrict the distribution of the report beyond the primary stakeholders.
Scope and objectives of the engagement: The report may begin with the scope and objective of
the engagement defined at the time of agreeing the terms. However, any additional scope items may
be included for an assignment, if appropriate.
Approach and broad work procedures undertaken: This section documents the work procedures
performed by the Professional to gather evidence and information to determine the facts reported.
This confirms that the Professional conducted procedures that were sufficient, appropriate and
commensurate to the scope of the engagement. The work procedures may include sampling
methodologies, digital tools and techniques, people interviewed, information gathered from public
domain and site visits conducted, amongst others.
Reference to use of expert: The work of an Expert may be included in the report along with the
credentials and the scope of work.
Reference to FAIS or any other standards adhered to: The report would include a statement
confirming that the engagement has been conducted  in accordance with the FAIS and also
highlighting deviations, if any.
Detailed  findings:   The  report  includes  details  of  findings based on  the  evidence and
documentation. As a good practice, the findings are referenced to the relevant evidence and
information gathered, which may be annexed to the report. The Professional may list the findings on
the basis of evidence relied upon, the modus operandi identified and the quantum (to the extent
possible) of the deviations noted. The Professional would choose a structure most conducive to
convey the findings as per stakeholder format requirements. For example, tables or matrix with
diagrams or graphical flow charts may be used to depict fund flows.
Assumptions, limitations and disclaimers: Where the Professional makes any assumptions, such
assumptions and the reliance placed on them may be adequately disclosed in the report. When the
Professional has not been able to review relevant information or perform relevant tests, a limitation
on the scope of the work, the reasons for the limitations, the potential impact of the limitations, and
any other disclaimers, may be disclosed in the report. The Professional may exercise discretion at
the time of drafting limitations and disclaimers.
Conclusions: Based on the findings noted, the Professional may arrive at certain conclusions
regarding the objectives of the engagement. For example, the conclusions may indicate whether
there has been any deviation to procedures, legal violations etc. However, the Professional shall
refrain from expressing any opinion, such as those relating to the guilt or innocence of any person

The  report  shall not express an opinion or pass any judgment on the  guilt or innocence.
Determination of culpability is either a disciplinary process internal to the organization under review,
or a judicial process depending on the specific situation under review. The report can, at best,
highlight the circumstances and facts that may aid a Stakeholder decision or further a civil or criminal
investigation. While no fixed form or content of the report is mandated by this Standard, the report
shall include certain key elements to enable the recipient to understand the purpose of the
assignment, the  extent and scope  of work performed by  the  Professional, any  limitations,
assumptions or disclaimers, the facts and evidence gathered during the course of the engagement
and the conclusions drawn. Where the form and content of the report is mandated by the Primary
Stakeholders, or specified by the statutory or regulatory requirements, the Professional shall report
in line with those requirements, while keeping in mind the key elements.

Following are the sample best practices and standardisation that can be adopted and incorporated
as a checkpoint to have better sanity in reporting and overall presentation:
♦    Use of Capital letters. The following are the scenarios where capital letters should be used:
        In the names of people, places, or related words.
        At the beginning of a sentence.
        In abbreviations.
♦    Numbers should either have no decimals or two decimals.
♦    Use of Indent function. (Increase or Decrease of space between the left and right margin
       of a paragraph)
♦    Use of Slide Master/Heading styles. (While preparing Presentation)
♦    Alignment and formatting
♦    Table should be properly formatted. The following points should be done while formatting
      a table:
        Text: Top left aligned.
       Number: Top right aligned.
