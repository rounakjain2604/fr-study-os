      5.1  INITIALIZATION & DEFINING MANDATE
5.1.1  Initialization and defining mandate are the first steps in a project management process, where
the project manager identifies the need, opportunity, or problem that the project aims to address,
and defines the objectives, scope, and deliverables of the project. The project mandate  is a
document that summarizes the purpose, justification, and expected outcomes of the project, as well
as the roles and responsibilities of the project team and stakeholders. The project mandate also
provides the authority for the project manager to initiate the project and allocate resources.
5.1.2  Following are further steps involved in the Initialization and Defining mandate step of Process
of Forensic Accounting Investigation:
1.    Establishing Contract: The client or the legal authority will contact a forensic professional
       or a forensic audit firm to request their services. The professional will assess the feasibility
      and scope of the assignment and provide a proposal or an engagement letter that outlines
       the terms and conditions of the service.
2.    Defining Objectives: The client or the legal authority will define the objectives of the
       forensic audit, such as what type of fraud is suspected, who are the possible suspects,
      what is the estimated amount of loss, what is the desired outcome of the investigation, etc.
     The professional will also define their own objectives based on their professional standards
      and ethical principles.
3.    Signing an Agreement: The client or the legal authority will review and sign the proposal
       or the engagement letter that confirms their acceptance of the service and authorizes the
       professional to access the relevant information and records.

5.1.3 Engagement/ Appointment Letter:
Engagement  letter  is a sent by an  forensic  professional  to  its  client,  after the  receipt  of
communication  regarding  his  appointment,  but  preferably  before  the  commencement  of
engagement, seeking out the extent of his responsibilities in order to avoid any misunderstanding
with respect to its engagement and documents and confirming the acceptance of appointment, the
objectives and scope of audit, the extent of responsibilities and the form of reports to be made to
the client.

types of mandate they can issue would include the following Illustrations:

       organization. Forensic of employee fraud can help prevent, deter, and prosecute such
       actions, as well as recover losses or damages caused by them. There are many types of
      employee fraud, such as time theft, stealing office supplies, merchandise theft, cash theft,
       financial fraud, embezzlement, billing fraud, payroll fraud, and more. Employee fraud can
       affect any industry or size of organization and can result in significant losses or even
      bankruptcy.

      statement fraud, asset misappropriation, corruption, tax evasion, money laundering, and

5.3.1  Types of Evidence which a forensic professional can come through includes – Examples:
(a).   Financial records: Financial records are the most common type of evidence that a forensic
       professional will collect. Financial records can provide a wealth of information about the
      company’s financial transactions and activities. Forensic professionals will typically review
       the company’s accounting records, bank statements, credit card statements, and other
       financial documents to identify any suspicious activity.
(b).   Computer data: Computer data can also be a valuable source of evidence for forensic
       professionals. Computer data can include emails, text messages, chat logs, and other data
      stored on computers and  other  electronic  devices. Forensic professionals can use
       specialized software to analyze large volumes of computer data to identify patterns and
      anomalies that may indicate fraud or other financial irregularities.
(c).   Physical evidence: Physical evidence can also be important for forensic professionals.
      Physical evidence may include documents, checks, invoices, and other physical objects.
      For example, a forensic professional investigating suspected embezzlement may collect
      checks that were forged or that were written to unauthorized individuals.
(d).   Witness testimony: Witness testimony can also be helpful for forensic professionals.
      Forensic  professionals may  interview  employees,  customers,  vendors,  and  other
       individuals who may have  information  about  the  alleged  fraud  or  other  financial
        irregularities. Witness testimony can help to corroborate other evidence and to provide
       insights into the motivations of the perpetrators.

       potential motivations of the perpetrators.

      5.4  PERFORM THE ANALYSIS.
5.4.1  Module 6 of ICAI, analysis in a forensic accounting investigation is performed in the following
steps:
(a).   Understanding the case: The forensic accountant must first understand the nature of the
      case, the allegations being made, and the parties involved. This includes reviewing the
      case file, interviewing witnesses, and visiting the scene of the crime, if applicable.
(b).   Identifying the relevant financial information: The  forensic accountant must then
        identify the relevant financial information that will be needed to investigate the case. This
     may include accounting records, bank statements, credit card statements, and other
       financial documents.
(c).   Gathering the data: Once the relevant financial information has been identified, the
       forensic accountant must gather  it from the various sources. This may involve obtaining
      copies of documents from clients, banks, or other third parties.
(d).   Analyzing the data: The forensic accountant must then analyze the data to identify any
      anomalies or inconsistencies. This may involve using a variety of analytical techniques,
      such as ratio analysis, trend analysis, and benchmarking.
(e).   Drawing conclusions: Once the data has been analyzed, the forensic accountant must
      draw  conclusions about  the  case.  This may  involve  identifying  fraudulent  activity,
       calculating damages, or providing expert testimony in court.
5.4.2  Here are some specific examples of analytical techniques that may be used in a forensic
accounting investigation:
1.    Ratio analysis: Ratio analysis can be used to compare a company's financial performance
       to its industry peers or to its own historical performance. This can help to identify any
      unusual trends or fluctuations. For example, a forensic accountant could have calculated
      Adelphia Communications' debt-to-equity ratio and compared  it to the ratios of other
      cable companies.  If Adelphia Communications' debt-to-equity ratio was significantly
      higher than the industry average, this could have been a sign of financial distress.
2.    Trend analysis: Trend analysis can be used to track changes in a company's financial
      performance over time. This can help to identify any long-term trends that may be indicative
       of fraud or other financial wrongdoing. For example, a forensic accountant could have
      analyzed the company's revenue and earnings trends.  If Adelphia Communications'
      revenue and earnings were growing rapidly while its debt was increasing, this could have

     Money Laundering (Maintenance of Records) Rules, 2005 (“Rules”) The PMLA is an act of
       the Parliament of India enacted to prevent money laundering and to provide for confiscation
       of property derived from or involved in money laundering. The Rules are issued by the
       Central Government in consultation with the Reserve Bank of India to prescribe the manner
     and procedure of maintaining and furnishing information and records of transactions and
        identity    of    clients   by   banking   companies,    financial    institutions,   and
       intermediaries.According to Section 3 of the PMLA, money laundering is defined as any
        activity  or  process  connected  with  proceeds  of  crime,  including  its  concealment,
      possession, acquisition or use and projecting or claiming it as untainted property. Proceeds
       of crime means any property derived or obtained directly or indirectly by any person as a
       result of criminal activity relating to a scheduled offence or the value of such property. A
      scheduled offence is any offence specified under Part A or Part B or Part C of the Schedule
       to the PMLA.
      Fraud is a scheduled offence under Part A of the Schedule to the PMLA if it is punishable
      under Section 420 (cheating and dishonestly inducing delivery of property) or Section 467
       (forgery of valuable security) or Section 468 (forgery for purpose of cheating) or Section
     471 (using as genuine a forged document) of the Indian Penal Code, 1860, and if the total
      value involved in such offence or offences is thirty lakh rupees or more.
7.    Companies Act, 2013: The Companies Act 2013 is an Act of the Parliament of India that
       regulates the formation, management, and dissolution of companies in India. It replaces
       the Companies  Act 1956 and  introduces new concepts such as  corporate  social
       responsibility, class action suits, one-person company, and more. The Act has 29 chapters,
     470 sections, and 7 schedules. It was enacted on August 29, 2013, and came into force in
       stages. The Act aims to enhance disclosure, accountability, and governance of companies
        in India.
8.    Information Technology Act, 2000: The Information Technology Act, 2000 is a law that
      deals with various aspects of electronic transactions, data protection, cyber security, and
      cybercrimes in India. It also provides legal recognition to electronic records and signatures
     and establishes the regulatory framework for certifying authorities and electronic signature
        certificates.

9.    Serious Fraud Investigation Office (SFIO): SFIO stands for Serious Fraud Investigation
       Office, which is a statutory corporate fraud investigating agency in India. It has the power
       to investigate into the affairs of a company that are suspected of fraud, as defined under
      Section 447 of the Companies Act, 2013.
10.   The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC) : legal implications of fraud cases under the
      Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC) in India. The IBC is a law that aims to consolidate
     and amend the laws relating to reorganization and insolvency resolution of corporate
      persons, partnership firms and individuals in a time bound manner. The IBC has a provision
      under Section 66 that deals with fraudulent trading or wrongful trading. According to Section
       66, if during the corporate insolvency resolution process (CIRP) or a liquidation process, it
        is found that any business of the corporate debtor has been carried on with intent to defraud
       creditors or  for any fraudulent purpose, the Adjudicating Authority (AA) may on the
       application of the resolution professional (RP) pass an order that any persons who were
      knowingly parties to the fraud shall be liable to make such contributions to the assets of
       the corporate debtor as it may deem fit. The AA may also pass an order that any director
       or partner of the corporate debtor who did not exercise due diligence in minimizing the
       potential loss to the creditors shall be liable to make such contribution as it may deem fit.
     The Supreme Court of India has interpreted and applied Section 66 in several cases
       involving fraud and insolvency.
11.   Reserve Bank of India (RBI) Regulations: The RBI is the central bank of India and has
       the authority to issue directions and guidelines to banks and financial institutions on various
      aspects of banking operations, including fraud prevention and management. The RBI has
      issued a Master Direction on Frauds dated July 1, 2016, which consolidates and updates
         all the instructions issued on the subject up to June 30, 2016. The Master Direction covers
       the following topics:
       (a)    Classification of frauds based on the nature and amount involved.
       (b)    Reporting of frauds to RBI, police, CBI, and other authorities
        (c)    Quarterly and annual reviews of frauds by banks and submission of reports to RBI
       (d)    Guidelines  for  reporting  frauds  to  police/CBI and  filing  complaints  with law
            enforcement agencies.
       (e)    Reporting cases of theft, burglary, dacoity and bank robberies
         (f)    Guidelines for dealing with loan frauds, including early warning signals, detection,
              reporting, staff accountability and penal measures for fraudulent borrowers.

     Banks are required to report all cases of frauds involving Rs. 1 lakh and above to RBI within

      5.6  COURT PROCEEDINGS
5.6.1 When a fraud case of a company is found, the court procedure may vary depending on the
jurisdiction in which the case is filed. However, there are some general steps that are typically
followed.
1.    Gather Evidence and Investigation: The first step is to gather evidence and investigate
       the fraud case. This could include documents such as contracts, invoices, and emails that
     show that the company committed fraud. This may be done by the company's internal audit
      department, law enforcement agency, or a combination of the two.
2.    Framing of Charges: Once the evidence gathering and investigation is completed, the
       appropriate entity (SFIO/ Police Authority) may decide to frame charges against the
       individuals or entities responsible for the fraud.
3.    Issuing a Warning: Based on the charges framed a preliminary warning is issued to the
       entity who is accused of committing fraud to take steps to resolve to rectify such allegations.
4.     Filing of Suit: If the entity is not responsive to the allegation framed, the appropriate
       authority will file the suit under relevant law at the court of first instance having appropriate
        jurisdiction.
5.    Issuing of Summons: Going through the petition of fraud, the judge will issue summons
       to the defendant.  If the defendant is nonresponsive to the summons, then the court can
      issue ex-parte order (deciding the case without hearing the opposite party).
6.    Preparing Defence: The defendant will either plead guilty or not guilty, and the defence
      statement will accordingly be prepared.
7.     Trial: Based on the defence statement, the case will go to the Trial. This will include the
      stage of witness gathering, examination and cross examination of evidence.
8.    Issuing Judgement: The judge will consider the evidence and the arguments provided by
      both the parties and deliver a judgement which fair and equitably just. The judgement will
