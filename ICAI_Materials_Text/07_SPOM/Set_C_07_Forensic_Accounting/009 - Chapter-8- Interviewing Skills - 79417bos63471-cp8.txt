 CHAPTER OVERVIEW


INTRODUCTION
Interviews are a way of gathering evidence, information and act as effective means to corroborate
understanding of documentary evidences. Written interview statements are used to record the
information gathered making it an integral element of the work procedures.
Forensic Accounting and Investigation Standards (FAIS) 340 defines “interview” as a structured
meeting with individuals for eliciting information. While interviews are expected to be inter-personal
interactions with multiple facets, they are generally well planned and conducted within the framework
of existing laws, rules, norms and procedures.

Professionals primarily depend on documentary evidences, but may need to obtain substantial oral
statements to corroborate the understanding of documentary evidences. In addition, they may need
to take written statements from individuals to record information discovered. This makes interviewing
a necessary part of the overall FAI process.

      8.1  ART AND SCIENCE OF INTERVIEWS-NATURE & TYPE
Conducting interviews assists professionals to communicate or engage with individuals with a view
to elicit any information directly or indirectly pertaining to an assignment.
The overall objective of conducting interviews is to seek information for corroborating evidences
and, where appropriate, to record statements of admission by the concerned individuals.
Besides, there are following supplementary objectives supporting above stated overall
objective: -
(a)    to emphasise the importance of effective planning and preparation, prior to conducting
       interviews
(b)    to undertake a methodical approach and include certain necessary steps in conducting the
       interviews and
(c)    to ensure that statements obtained can be presented as suitable evidence in a Court of
       law.
Therefore, importance  of interviewing  skills needs no exaggeration. Such  skills are  vital  for
professionals in performing FAI engagements.
It is in this context that significance of conducting successful interviews has to be understood. Having
excellent interviewing skills can help professionals in reaching desired outcomes. With proper
practice, such interviewing skills can be honed up.  Science of conducting interview consists of
effective planning, preparation and developing a methodology for an effective interview. Refining
such skills can be made an art with proper practice and experience. Experience of a professional
accountant leads to enrichment of art of interviewing.
The interview to be conducted may relate to a whistle-blower, a witness, information-provider or
subject.  It is to be clearly understood that purpose of an interview is neither to confirm what the
interviewer thinks might have happened, nor to coerce the interviewee into providing information or
confessing. Its objective is to validate existing facts and seek additional information wherever

8.2.1  Planning an interview
Based on evidence gathered (factual or personal proof), an interview strategy must be drawn up and
the conversation must be structured. The strategy involves planning the order of topics or submitting
the evidence.
There are multiple factors that go  into planning and preparation  for an interview which the
professional may consider. A few important points to consider are as follows: -
♦   When planning an  interview, the interviewer may wish  to be aware  of interviewee
       classification, such as a whistleblower, witness, information provider, or subject.
♦    Understand the purpose of the interview and ensure  it is aligned with the aim of the
       investigation and not different from it.
♦    While planning an interview, explore all the hypotheses, including the possibility that the
       subject is innocent.
♦     Objective of an interview normally is to validate existing facts and also to seek additional
       information, where possible. Interviews also help in understanding the prevailing systems
      and processes adopted by client.
♦    An interview is NOT to confirm what the interviewer thinks might have happened, nor to
      coerce the interviewee into providing information or confessing.
♦    An interview is a safeguard against false confession and errors of justice. Interview assists
      communication and the flow of information and, consequently, the detection of crime,
        violation, or breach.
♦    The interview objective, hypothesis and role of the interviewee in the matter at hand, are
       essential to understand before developing an interview plan.
♦    An interview plan provides a framework on which to base the questioning, including the
      sequence and outline for questions on the activities or events.
♦    The information obtained prior to the interview may be collated and analysed.  If the
       information gathering process is incomplete,  it may lead to an interviewee giving false or
      evasive response.
♦     Linking the interview plan with certain specific hypotheses helps to make  it an effective
       interview.

♦    Prepare a set of possible questions to be asked. These questions may undergo change
      based on answers provided.
♦    Take into account considerations regarding interview participants.
♦     Interviews are normally conducted with the help of an interview team. The ideal team should
       not have the too many team members in order to avoid the impression that the interviewee
        is being interrogated. This, in turn, could negatively affect the willingness of the interviewee
       to testify. One-on-one interviews are generally discouraged. The professional conducting
       the interview (“Lead Interviewer”) is responsible for the interview and provides direction to
       the interview.
♦    Consider the need  for an interpreter,  if any. Clear and unambiguous communication
      between the interviewer and the person being interviewed  is a prerequisite  for the
      successful completion of an interview.  It may be necessary to consider the need for an
       interpreter in some interviews. Such interpreter is aware of his obligation to translate
       accurately in a confidential manner.
♦     Consider choosing to have a senior representative of the primary stakeholder present in
       the interview as an observer. Where the interviewee is a woman, there needs to be another
     woman present in the interview, either as a senior representative, or otherwise.
♦    Take into account that the interviewee may choose a representative (usually nominated
      by him) to accompany him.
♦     Location  of  interview  is an important  consideration. Depending on  the number  of
       participants, a meeting room of the appropriate size must be arranged. The choice of the
     room must consider the protection of confidentiality as well as privacy. An interview in the
       interviewee’s office is discouraged.
Once the persons for the interview are determined, it is good practice to send a formal written invite
to the interviewee, stating the time, place and purpose of the interview. When selecting a location,
it is important to select an interview location conducive to conducting business and interview timings
convenient to all parties, preferably during business hours. This also applies to interviews conducted
in virtual mode such as through Zoom or Team meeting Apps. For example, if an interview is being
conducted virtually, the Lead Interviewer may ensure the availability of the appropriate tools and
connectivity.

8.2.2  Conducting an interview
Interviews are dynamic and there cannot be a standard process that fits all situations. To increase
the credibility and admissibility of the interview evidence, the same should be gathered by the
professional without any inducement, threat or promise.
The following points may be considered when conducting an interview: -
♦     Invitation  for interview: When summoning  or  inviting personnel  to  interviews, the
       interviewee shall receive information regarding the background for the summons and why
      he/she has been asked to provide information, the scope of the investigation, and the
      procedure of verification on the information provided during the interview and how the
      minutes from the interview will be used. The interviewee has the right to solicit advice.
♦     Starting an interview: The  interview may begin  with an  introduction by the Lead
       Interviewer and other persons present. The Lead Interviewer should be courteous,
       impartial, patient, polite but firm, and converse in the language which the interviewee
      understands. This may help develop a rapport  with the interviewee and make the
       interviewee  feel comfortable. The Lead Interviewer may explain the purpose  of the
       interview, lay the ground rules and explain the rights of the interviewee before the interview
       begins. At this stage,  it is good practice to ask the interviewee  if they understand the
      procedure intended to be followed.
♦    Obligation of Interviewee: In most cases, the interviewee has a responsibility to co-
      operate and contribute to the clarification of factual circumstances being sought in an
       interview. Interviewees are usually obliged to respond to questions from the interviewer
      regarding the performance of their work tasks and other issues related to employment in
      an organisational setup.  It is in their best interest to use the opportunity to submit their
      perspective on the subject matter.
♦    Note taking: It may be decided at the beginning of the interview whether to take notes or
       not. As a good practice, professional may take continuous notes during the interview
      process. Comprehensive, objective, accurate and relevant interview notes that capture the
      key discussions during the interview may be used as a work paper.
♦    Consent: The interviewer can seek informed consent  to use and share information
      provided during the interview by the interviewee.

8.2.2.1 Recording the statements
During an interview, the interview may proceed in the following phases: -
(a)    Introduction and building rapport - Interviewer shall create an empathetic and respectful
       relationship from the outset. Explain the reasons, background, rights, formalities, and
      procedures for the interview, including audio/video recording information. Establish the
       relevant ground rules and ensure the interviewee understands all requirements. This stage
       lays down the foundations for the interview, enabling the interviewer and interviewee to
      develop a shared understanding of the interview and its dynamics.
(b)    First Free Account - The interviewer may seek a complete account from the interview in
        this phase and actively listen. This phase may begin with broad, open-ended questions,
      and then proceed to more detailed questions to ensure all aspects of the situation are
      understood. Open-ended questions are usually formulated using- Who, why, Where, What,
     When, -e.g.:
       “Can you tell me…”,
       “Describe for me…”,
       “What did you do next” or
       “What happened next?
      This phase requires the interviewer to actively listen to the free account without intervening
     when the interviewee is talking.
(c)   Review and Repeat - The Lead Interviewer may review the interviewee’s answers with
     them to clarify on their responses. Repeating a summarized version of the account provided

8.2.3  Closing an Interview:
At the end of the interview, it is recommended that the Lead Interviewer conclude the interview by
keeping the following in mind: -
♦    Provide an opportunity to the interviewee to document any additional information and
      concerns, which may be relevant to the investigation.
♦    Provide a preferred mode of contact to the interviewee to remain in touch, in case the
       interviewee wishes to share any additional information later.
♦    Give assurance to the interviewee regarding any privacy concerns and preserving the
       confidentiality of the interview, especially those who have requested anonymity (e.g.,
       whistleblower, witness, etc.).
♦    Review all written statements with the interviewee being invited to sign their name at the
      bottom of each page and initial any mistakes that have been corrected. All others present
     may also sign each page. In cases where this may not be feasible (for example in an
       interview conducted in virtual mode),  it is good practice to obtain a signed copy of the
      statements at the earliest possible.
♦    Note that such signed minutes and statements can be used as evidence. The interview
      notes need not be verbatim or in question answer format, but must capture the relevant
       information in a precise and clear manner.
After completion of the interview, the interviewer may wish to evaluate the whole interview with a
view to:
(a)    Determine whether any further action is necessary
(b)    Determine how the interviewee’s account fits in with the rest of the investigation.
(c)    Reflecting on the interviewer’s coverage.
(d)   Changes in the criticality and priorities of the assignment

Simplification of Interview Process
The interview process and extraction of information can be simplified by keeping in mind a certain
socio-psychological parameters which facilitate the conversation.
(i)    Making expectation clear –  It  is normally  easier  to  extract information when the
      expectations from him/her are communicated before the interview starts. A sense of feeling
        is created where the other person has to conform to the expectation of the interviewer.
      Expectation of co-operation from the respondent is achieved mostly through the non-verbal
      behaviour
(ii)    Recognition – Many people have a tendency to think about the recognition they will get at
       the end of the Act and then perform it. If the interviewer is skilful enough, the information
      can be extracted in exchange of sincere recognition.
(iii)   Philanthropic Appeals – Humans generally identify with some larger cause.  If their
       contribution to the investigation process can be linked to some higher good that will be
      achieved at the end of the investigation, getting sincere and truthful responses becomes
       easier for the interviewer. Such Philanthropic appeals to the respondent also help in
       increasing his or her self esteem by being involved in the process.
(iv)   Sympathetic behaviour – As mentioned above there is a need for recognition in human
       beings, but there is a different and higher need of being understood than being recognised.
           If the interviewer maintains a sympathetic behaviour towards the respondent, they tend to
      shar their fears and joys, failures and success. Such interviews are more successful.
(v)   Purging – The need for sympathetic behaviour and understanding the respondent is related
       to Purging, but they are not the same. While we dealt about understanding the interviewee
       with sympathy above, here we mean listening to the unimportant stuff.  If the interviewer
     shows unavailability and less receptivity to the egocentric or unimportant talk by the
      respondent, they might not also be willing to share the other important stuff.
(vi)   Tangible or Measurable Rewards – Although such rewards are not directly related to the
       interview, but it helps the interviewee to put value to the whole interview experience. Such
      rewards may Include some monetary benefit, increment or promotion in the job, in some
      cases eve retention of the job. Although the interviewer must be careful while promising
      these rewards so an to remain within the limits of his authority or grant.

8.3   Importance of Non-verbal Cues
Communication is based both upon spoken words and non-verbal cues. Research studies have
demonstrated that non-verbal cues like body language can be quite significant. During conduct of
an interview, understanding non-verbal cues  like  facial expressions, gestures, emotions, eye
contacts can prove to useful in interviewing. Deepening one’s understanding of non-verbal cues can
lead to conducting more effective interviews. There are some universal facial expressions and one’s
inceptive responses are through these facial expressions/body language like failure to make eye
contact, looking other way round while answering, rubbing hands etc.  Further, understanding non-
verbal cues may lead an interviewer to look for deception.
8.4   Type of Questions and its Sequence:

      regarding the person being interviewed. These questions are generally asked to achieve
       the following objectives –
       (a)   To provide introduction
       (b)   To establish a rapport
        (c)   To establish the theme and expectation of the interview and verbally make the

       (d)   To observe the body language, and non-verbal reactions of the interviewee.

       his/her own body language and the words he or she uses. If words which are accusatory in
      nature are used in the beginning it might put the respondent in a defensive frame of mind

CIN Insurance Limited, in accordance with statutory requirements, appoints an insurance surveyor
and loss assessor, who is an experienced and qualified engineer. Besides, he is also holding a law
degree. The appointment of insurance surveyor and loss assessor is made on evening of the same
day and first visit is made by him to affected site on 3rd August, 2023. He notices that affected
premises of company consisting of building are divided into sections and two sections of building
have collapsed due to heat and combustion generated during fire. These sections have fallen on
machinery and inventories lying therein. All he can see and observe is smouldering fire and debris
of building.
The company management has given him idea of loss of inventories to tune of Rs.7 crores and
provided him copies of purchases orders of overseas suppliers which were under manufacturing or
ready for shipment in those affected sections. Major parts of inventories damaged and burnt are
finished goods inventories. The management, however, fails to provide stock records for different
products. Records showing inward receipt of materials are also not provided.  Though CCTV
cameras are installed in affected premises, management is not providing recording details. After
taking an overall impression that management is probably trying to mislead him, he provides
feedback to insurance company. The insurance company, in turn, also appoints CA F (forensic
accountant) to report on cause/circumstances of loss as well as on matters affecting its liability.
CA F visits affected site on 6th August, 2023. After gathering preliminary information, he decides to
interview certain key persons/ employees of the company beginning from the one who first noticed
fire. He also plans to interview employees who are responsible for custody and maintenance of
inventories in affected sections to dig out facts. He has also, in his arsenal, plan to interview CEO
of the company. The purpose of such interviews is to establish facts as they occurred. The purpose
of a forensic engagement is to find out reality, which in turn shall be based on facts, figures and
reliable  evidential  matter.   Conducting  successful and  effective  interviews helps  forensic

INTERVIEWING SKILLS              8.17

He starts interview with employee Mr. A, who first noticed fire, in a conversational style and asks
him to a give a free account of what happened on the fateful day. He asks him to provide introduction.
Next, he asks questions on his role and duties in the company. He further asks him tenure of his
association with the company. Next, he requires Mr. A to state what he was doing when the reported
incident occurred and what he saw at first instance. He further asks him how he reacted to the
situation. During such free account provided by Mr. A, CA F keenly listens to him and observes how
Mr. A is responding to his questions. He watches his body language, facial expressions and tries to
gain a first impression regarding reliability of statements being put up by him. However, at this stage,
he does not interrupt him. He just actively listens and observes.
After Mr. A has provided initial free account, CA F reviews and repeats responses of Mr. A to him to
ensure that nothing is misunderstood. He, now, tries to gain clarity of responses and poses further
questions on basis of initial responses. He also tries to get factual accuracy of answers reconfirmed
from Mr. A. He is further nudged by CA F to provide any further information regarding questions
being asked. After gathering all factual information on the reported event, he reviews all of his
statements and requests Mr. A to sign the written statement and also  initial any mistakes in
statement that have been corrected.
CA F, then, records statements of Mr. B and C who are responsible for custody and maintenance of
inventories in affected sections in a similar manner. While recording statements of Mr. B and Mr. C,
he takes care to ask questions regarding system of stock maintenance, prevailing system of
reporting position of inventories in the company, nature of inventories lying in affected sections,
normal period for holding finished goods inventories.
Mr. O, CEO of company is also interviewed by CA F. He is posed questions on stock record
maintenance and part of similar questions posed to Mr. B and Mr C. CA F also asks Mr. O about
average time taken by company to manufacture its different products, names and details of overseas
buyers whose orders were under execution, detail of ready stocks of finished goods inventories lying
in affected sections, position of saved stocks, availability of CCTV recording, ownership documents
of company’s building, its age and nature/age of damaged machinery.
All necessary similar steps are taken while recording interviews of Mr. B, C and O. The above
interviews are a part of evidence in forensic engagement. CA F needs to determine how the
description provided by persons interviewed fits with rest of investigation.
