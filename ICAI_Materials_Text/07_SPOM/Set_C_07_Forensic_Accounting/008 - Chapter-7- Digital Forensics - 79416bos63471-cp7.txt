INTRODUCTION
In the digital era, as most business activities and transactions shift to the digital domain, evidential
matters required to complete Forensic Accounting and Investigations (FAI) assignments must be
capable of satisfying the requirements of judicial scrutiny.  It also requires a new way to gather
evidence.
Digital forensic is a term used to refer to the whole process of identification, collection, acquisition,
and preservation of digital evidence from digital media, which contains all data from the information
systems and applications of an organization. Therefore, digital forensics is a broad term covering a
gamut of activities consisting of identifying, collecting, acquiring and preserving digital evidence from
digital media by a professional in FAI assignment.
Digital forensics is a complex and time-consuming process that requires specialized skills and
expertise. It is often used in criminal investigations, civil litigation, and other legal proceedings. A
professional in an FAI assignment may be tasked with conducting digital forensics to gather evidence
and information for various purposes.

      7.1  MEANING OF DIGITAL EVIDENCE
Forensic Accounting and Investigation Standard (FAIS) 420 defines “Digital evidence” as data or
information acquired, stored, accessed, examined, transmitted and used in electronic form. Digital
evidence needs to comply with applicable laws and a proper chain of custody in order to be
admissible as evidence before a Competent Authority. The  first step in digital forensics is the

Digital evidence is present in the digital domain, i.e. the electronic environment. This may be in the

preserve digital evidence.
Digital evidence, for example, may be found in the form of: -
(i)    Emails, instant messages in an organisation’s email system.
(ii)    Electronic records in ERP, CRM, MIS, financial accounting systems, etc.
(iii)   Computer files such as documents, spreadsheets, presentations, and databases, including
      metadata such as date and time stamps, author information, and file properties.
(iv)  Web browsing history, search queries, and cookies collected from a computer or mobile
       device.
(v)   Images or videos captured by surveillance cameras or other monitoring devices.
(vi)    Social media posts, comments, or private messages.
(vii)  System logs and audit trails generated by computer systems or networks.

7.4          FORENSIC ACCOUNTING

The process used for gathering and collecting evidence in the digital domain, which is to be used by
a Professional conducting an FAI assignment or by parties other than the Professional or party
subject to legal proceedings, is known as electronic gathering or e-discovery.
The collection and acquisition of digital evidence must be done in a manner that preserves the
integrity of the data. This involves using specialized tools and techniques to extract and copy the
data while ensuring the original data is not modified or deleted. The digital evidence  is then
preserved through various methods, including digital imaging, data encryption, and secure storage.
Procedures applied to track the sequential movement of evidence through the gathering process
from collection, storage, securing, safeguarding and analysis lifecycle are referred to as the Digital
Chain of Custody. Each step is recorded, and each person who handled the evidence is documented,
noting the date and time it was collected, stored, and transferred, as well as the purpose of such
transfers.

7.2   Use of technology tools
Where  necessary,  the  Professional  deploys  appropriate  forensic  tools and  techniques  to
authenticate the evidence, analyse the data and maintain a reliable chain of custody over the
evidence. In the digital domain, gathering digital evidence requires using specific digital (software)
tools and unique forensic techniques. The Professional (or the Expert) is expected to recognise and
deploy the appropriate tools and methods for this purpose.
As discussed earlier, the identification, collection, acquisition, and preservation of digital evidence
from digital media are encompassed in digital forensics and are the set skills for digital forensic
acquisition.
(a)    Digital forensic acquisition is the forensic collection of data from all digital assets. It uses
        digital forensic acquisition software.
(b)      It requires multiple sets of digital forensic acquisition tools in which the digital forensic
      experts are trained and well-equipped.
(c)   Upon collection, acquisition, and preservation of  digital evidence media, the forensic
      process of acquired digital media starts.

skills of the Digital forensic expert, in which case the Digital forensic expert would be charged with

It may be somewhat risky for a Professional to undertake the technical work steps due to the legal
ramifications of any missteps in this regard since it’s possible that in a court of law, the cross-
examinations could get highly technical, exposing the Professional with limited expertise in this area.
With regard to the requirements of the expertise of a Digital forensic expert, the Professional is
required to look at the requirements of FAIS 230 "Using the work of an Expert" for completing the
tasks in a systematic manner.

7.3   Laboratory analysis of physical and electronic evidence
Forensic examinations may be carried out in the fields like Chemistry, Questioned documents, Digital
forensics and Physics. Physical evidence includes materials which a suspect may leave at the scene
of the incident. The purpose of the collection and examination of physical evidence is to link the
crime with the criminal. To achieve this purpose, all basic sciences and their methodologies are
being utilised for the analysis and comparison of physical evidence materials. For example, in a
forensic assignment to investigate the circumstances of a fire loss, the Professional may send
material from the affected site to know its chemical composition. The findings of the laboratory may
help him in obtaining evidence regarding the nature of the damaged material and the presence of

damaged media examination, and SIM card analysis of mobile phones.
7.4   Important legal sections of the Information Technology Act, 2000
Despite all laws being an integral part of the engagement, certain laws are specifically applicable to
FAI engagements. These laws form an essential basis  for the Professional  to conduct the
engagement and are known as direct impact laws in accordance with FAIS 130. The Professional is
expected to have a basic understanding of these direct impact laws to plan and conduct the
engagements
Direct Impact Laws are an integral part of most engagements since they are broad in nature with an

influence over most facets and types of engagements. Information Technology Act of 2000 is an
example of Direct Impact Law as an electronic gathering of evidence is governed by a special

The main objective of the Information Technology Act 2000 is to provide legal recognition for
transactions carried out by means of electronic data interchange and other means of electronic
communication, commonly  referred  to as  “electronic commerce”, which  involve  the use  of
alternatives to paper-based methods of communication and storage of information and to facilitate
electronic filing of documents with the Government agencies.
Gathering evidence in the digital domain is conducted by those with the requisite skills, expertise
and experience of working in such a domain to preserve the reliability and admissibility of digital
evidence before a Competent Authority. There are legal provisions in the Information Technology
Act of 2000 for penalties and compensation for damage to computers and computer systems,
tampering with computer source documents, violation of privacy and similar/related matters. Such
important legal provisions have to be taken into consideration for preserving the reliability and
admissibility of digital evidence during the course of legal proceedings.

Section 66E Punishment for violation of privacy–Whoever, intentionally or knowingly captures,
publishes or transmits the image of a private area of any person without his or her consent, under
circumstances violating the privacy of that person, shall be punished with imprisonment which may
extend to three years or with fine not exceeding two lakh rupees, or with both.
Explanation. –For the purposes of this section–
(a)    “transmit” means to electronically send a visual image with the intent that it be viewed by a
      person or persons;
(b)    “capture”, with respect to an image, means to videotape, photograph, film or record by any
      means;
(c)    “private area” means the naked or undergarment clad genitals, public area, buttocks or
      female breast:
(d)    “publishes” means reproduction in the printed or electronic form and making it available for
       public;
(e)    “under circumstances violating privacy” means circumstances in which a person can have
      a reasonable expectation that–

Section 79A Central Government to notify Examiner of Electronic Evidence–The Central
