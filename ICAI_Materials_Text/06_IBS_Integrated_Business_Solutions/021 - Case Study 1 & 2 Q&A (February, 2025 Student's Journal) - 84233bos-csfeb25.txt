     CA FINAL - PAPER 6: INTEGRATED BUSINESS SOLUTIONS (IBS)

 “The blind pursuit of profit at all costs is untenable. It is essential that we make money the right way. After
           all, if communities suffer as a result of a company’s actions, those returns are not sustainable.”
                                                                                - Indra Nooyi, the former CEO of PepsiCo.

  Effective decision-making requires a holistic view of the business, where decisions are not made in isolation but with an
  understanding of how different areas are interconnected. Business operates as an integrated system, where decisions
  in one area can significantly impact others. Reflecting these principles, two comprehensive agrochemical industry
  case studies were presented in the previous issue. This issue offers the Questions and Answers for those case studies,
  providing students with an opportunity to practice a wide range of questions on corporate governance, taxation, and
  strategic decision-making, all grounded in real-world scenarios.


           Synopsis of Dakshin Fertilizers and Petrochem Ltd. (DFPL)

   Headquartered in Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala, DFPL’s main manufacturing facility is in Eloor, Ernakulam, along with additional
   plants in Dahej (Gujarat), Hisar (Haryana) and Brahmapur (Odisha). The company is a leading producer of industrial chemicals,
   crop nutrition and mining chemicals.
   Backward integration: Despite strong financial results for FY 2023-24 (revenue of R12,000 crores and 60% increase in expected
    profits compared to the previous year), the company continues to face supply chain disruptions and huge volatility in import prices
   of ammonia. In order to build an agile and resilient supply chain that can also aid operational efficiencies, the management has
   taken a strategic decision to set up an ammonia manufacturing plant within DFPL’s main manufacturing complex at Eloor. With a
   capacity of 1,500 MTPD, the piped ammonia will be supplied directly to the finished goods manufacturing unit within the same
   complex. The advantages of this project are summarized include:
     (i) Backward integration allows for better realignment of supply chain, developing operational efficiencies that ensure seamless
       production of its core products
     (ii) Forex savings due to import substitution and
     (iii) Cost efficiencies due to reduced storage, transportation and logistics costs.
   The initial capital expenditure is estimated to be around R2,000 crores, for which purpose DFPL decides to borrow an additional
  sum of R1,500 crores from the company’s bankers by following the due procedure as per Companies Act, 2013. In order to establish
   better credibility with the bankers, CA. Fiona, the proprietor of FRP & Co., the statutory auditors of DFPL, has been approached
   by DFPL to have the prospective financial information examined. She must now decide whether she can get involved in such an
   examination. In order to complete the legal formalities relating to the project, DFPL hired Y & E law firm for a consolidated fee of
   R10,00,000. The work was completed by Y&E within 30 days. Ms. Aditi Singh who has been appointed as an Independent Director
   in Aug’24 at DFPL is also a partner at Y&E law firm. The law firm has an annual turnover of less than R1 crore. Post sanctioning of
   additional loan of R1,500 crores, the case study provides the timeline for utilization of these funds towards the project. This would
   determine the capitalization of borrowing costs (Ind AS 23).
   Environment sustainability and CSR initiatives: From a strategic perspective, the selling price of the product should support long-
   term profitability and competitiveness. To do this, the management explores incorporating environment costs into pricing. For two
   of the main types of fertilizers, Grade A and Grade B that the crop nutrition business unit of DFPL produces, information is provided
   cost centre wise about the cost incurred to produce them. Further information regarding the disposal of solid waste (generated
   during production) using incinerator is also provided in the case study. It explores how the unit head of the crop nutrition business
   would like to incorporate this information, to rework the pricing of each grade of fertilizer depending on their environmental
   impact. DFPL has undertaken several CSR initiatives. These include vendor management adhering to ethical labour practices,
   using technology to mitigate risks posed to the environment from ammonia emissions, increasing share of environmentally friendly
    fertilizers. To get information on these initiatives and their impact, the management uses Triple Bottom Line Reporting Framework.
  Many of the Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) included in this report are non-financial metrics.

Strategic expansion: Gaspro India Limited (GIL) is the main supplier of natural gas to DFPL’s unit at Brahmapur, Odisha.
Incidentally, GIL is setting up a Liquified Natural Gas (LPG) receipt facility at Gopalpur. DFPL finds this an opportunity to increase
its market presence in Odisha and northern belt of Andra Pradesh by setting up a fertilizer manufacturing unit at Gopalpur. This
strategic decision is taken due to the locational advantages that CYPL can have by being in close vicinity to Gaspro India Limited
(GIL) at Gopalpur. This increases the ability to scale up operations and the venture can benefit from economies of scale. The new
unit, Crop Yield Private Limited (CYPL) will be a joint venture with Greenspan Gmbh. DFPL’s unit at Brahmapur will be shut down
following the establishment of CYPL at Gopalpur. Following this the machineries will then be transferred to CYPL. In addition,
machineries with better technology and larger capacities will also be imported from one of Greenspan Gmbh’s subsidiaries in
Germany. As part of project planning, the case study highlights the need to consider tax planning that can be done by claiming
certain investment linked tax incentives that are available to specified businesses as contained in the Income Tax Act, 1961.
Corporate governance and accountability: The case study highlights the number of strategic initiatives that the management of
DFPL has to take to navigate this challenging industry landscape. It is important to have professionals with expertise in diverse fields
as part of the company’s Board of Directors in order to have a strong corporate governance. Appointments and reappointments
to various positions of key management personnel should be based on proper performance evaluation and in conformance with
the regulations. Many of the key management personnel render their expertise during the course of their tenure, for which they
may be adequately compensated in the form of professional fees. The case study highlights the importance of adhering to proper
procedures to ensure compliance with laws and regulations, particularly under the Companies Act, 2013 and SEBI regulations. All
of this has an immense bearing on the ability, quality and effectiveness of strategic decisions made by a company.
Corporate governance involves overseeing business strategies while ensuring fiscal accountability, ethical corporate behaviour,
fairness to both internal and external stakeholders. Accountability extends to statutory auditors as well, who as a part of their
role are privy to sensitive and confidential information about the company. Accountability for decisions and ethical corporate
responsibility can boost stakeholder confidence.

        Synopsis of Crop Yield Private Limited (CYPL)

In continuation to case study above, CYPL is established as a Joint Venture between Dakshin Fertilizers and Petrochem Ltd. (DFPL)
and Greenspan Gmbh at Gopalpur, Odisha. Greenspan Gmbh, widely recognized for its advanced fertilizer technologies and energy
efficient manufacturing processes, would provide the technical assistance to CYPL. This is extremely beneficial for DFPL’s strategic
expansion plans to expand its reach in the eastern cost. Meanwhile, by partnering with a domestically reputed company like DFPL,
Greenspan would be able to establish its presence in India.
As outlined above, the locational advantage of being in close vicinity to Gaspro India Limited (GIL), Gopalpur, its main supplier
of natural gas along with developing cost efficiencies by operating on a larger scale with a planned capacity of 10,00,000 MT per
year are the key benefits of this strategic decision. Both parties have hired Pro International (PI), to guide with legal and regulatory
compliance, facilitating establishment of the manufacturing facility and providing management consulting service to ensure smooth
operations and strategic alignment.
Establishing of CYPL: Since natural gas is a key raw material in the production of fertilizers, the company entered into a 5-year
long-term contract with GIL, Gopalpur. GIL will be the sole supplier of this critical raw material. Land worth R100 crores has been
procured in Gopalpur to set up CYPL. Machineries from DFPL’s erstwhile Brahmapur facility, along with imported machineries
from subsidiary of Greenspan Gmbh will form part of the total plant and machinery of CYPL. Since these require constant
maintenance, CYPL has entered into short term contracts for factory consumables, primary tools and maintenance equipment to
be supplied by small enterprises located in Gopalpur.
Resistance to CYPL’s establishment and labour unrest at Brahmapur: During project implementation, CYPL faces resistance
from the local public who feel that the company’s location within 1 kilometre of the town’s public settlements in a densely populated
area could result in concerns about public health safety. This stems from a recent gas leak incident from a similar fertilizer plant in
Paradip, Odisha. More than 1,000 residents from Gopalpur and Brahmapur gathered to protest against the establishment of CYPL
in the location, albeit it being approved by the government after complying with necessary regulations. Relocation would entail
establishing CYPL 600 kms. away, effectively making the project unviable on both operational and cost considerations. Similarly,
there is resistance from the workers at DFPL’s Brahmapur facility, spearheaded by their workers’ union, who are unhappy with the
compensation package being offered by the company to relocate to CYPL’s facility at Gopalpur. These issues can also come under
the radar of government regulatory authorities and company shareholders both in India and Germany.
There is a very real risk of these developments snowballing into a huge public outcry, that can be disastrous for both operations
and reputation of DFPL and Greenspan Gmbh. Hence, Mr. Venkata Ravi (Chairman of DFPL) and Mr. Gerhard Braun (Managing
Director of Greenspan Gmbh) approach Mr. Jeevan Sampath (partner at Pro International) to effectively handle the situation.
The case study discusses how PI makes use of Mendelow’s Matrix to guide CYPL to effectively handle each of the stakeholders
influencing this issue. Eventually an amicable settlement is reached with the disgruntled workers while all issues relating to public
resistance are addressed and resolved. Production at CYPL’s Gopalpur facility commences on January 1, 2025.

   Supply Disruption and subsequent scaling down of operations at CYPL: On September 30,2025 following a massive fire at
   GIL’s underground natural gas pipeline at Arjyapalli, Odisha, 15 people tragically lost their lives and there was significant damage
   to property in that area. Thereby for safety reasons GIL was forced to halt supply of natural gas through its pipelines in Odisha.
   Extensive repair of its pipelines and safety tests were conducted across the entire pipeline network originating from its LNG facility.
   While the technical team at GIL worked on this, supply was completely suspended due to which many of its customers including
  CYPL suffered significant losses due to unexpected stoppage of a critical raw material. To maintain operations at a minimum scale,
   in October 2025 Mr. Ramakant Bhushan, Managing Director of CYPL entered into another 5-year fixed price contract with Natural
  Gas Suppliers Limited (NGSL), a competitor of GIL. However, NGSL’s limited capability of meeting CYPL’s entire requirement
   of natural gas, inability to find other alternate suppliers forced the company to scale down its operations. In place of GIL, NGSL
  became CYPL’s supplier of natural gas. This highlights the critical need for managing the upstream supplier chain to address
   unforeseen exigencies.
  Going concern issues at CYPL: Since the cost structure consisted majorly of fixed costs that need to be incurred regardless of scale
   of operations, CYPL lost its cost efficiencies. Financial instability impacted CYPL’s ability to enter and terminate contracts with
   suppliers. Due to regulatory constraints governed by the government’s subsidy mechanism and market competition, the company
   could not pass on the increased cost of production to customers. Fertilizers were sold at a loss thereby straining its financial
   position. CYPL established a recovery committee comprising of key management personnel as well as invited Mr. Jeevan Sampath
   to provide consulting services in this regard. Strategic tools such as the Altman’s Z-score were used to assess CYPL’s financial
    stability and take appropriate measures to address them.
  CA. Bharat Chowdhary, the statutory auditor had to carefully assess the issue of going concern at CYPL, at the time of issuing the
   audit report for the financial year 2025-26.
   Additional capital infusion, optimally utilizing the subsidy support from the government and market repositioning were all part of
   the multi-pronged strategy that eventually were undertaken to stabilize operations at CYPL. Eventually in March 2028, almost 30
  months after the tragic accident, GIL resumed supply of natural gas to CYPL. Due to GIL, Gopalpur’s ability to meet the entirety of
    its natural gas requirements, CYPL renewed its business with the supplier by entering into a fresh contract. This will enable CYPL
   to scale back its operations to originally planned levels as decided at the time of its inception. This also led to CYPL prematurely
   cancelling its contract with NGSL. GIL once again became CYPL’s sole supplier of natural gas. The case study highlights that the
   impact that supply chain management decisions can have on the long-term sustainability and profitability of business.
   Performance assessment of Mr. Ramakant Bhushan, Managing Director at CYPL: Due to the extra-ordinary nature of business
   environment at CYPL, rather than focusing on financial metrics alone, the performance assessment of Mr. Ramakant Bhushan for
   the financial years 2025-26 to 2027-28 had to also be based on many non-financial metrics like building supply chain resilience,
   improving market share, improving sales, reducing costs.
   Indirect tax a significant component of CYPL’s cost structure: The profitability of the company is significantly influenced by the
   tax structure. Regarding the Goods and Services Tax (GST), the case study includes a discussion on the inverted duty structure,
   the determination of the value of supply for GST on fertilizers that receive government subsidies, and GST on the procurement
   of natural gas. Additionally, CYPL relies heavily on imports for its raw material requirements. The case study also explores the
   company’s liability and rights concerning customs duty payments in cases where raw materials are pilfered while in the custody of
   Port Trusts, which act as custodians.


                            KEY TAKEWAYS

    Based on real life scenarios within this highly regulated agrochemical industry, the above case studies bring out a series of
     interconnected issues that need to be tackled often time simultaneously in order to build a profitable and sustainable business.
     Businesses do not work in silos, the decision taken in one area can impact another. Often business leaders need to consider not
      just the financial aspects but also the non-financial aspects as well. For example, the establishment of CYPL in Gopalpur, Odisha
     not only made financial sense due to the cost advantages it offered, but also leveraged the capability of scaling up operations (a
     non-financial consideration) due to proximity to GIL, the supplier of its key raw material. This decision simultaneously resulted
     in other issues that the management had to tackle namely labour unrest at Brahmapur facility of DFPL as well as public resistance
     to establishment of CYPL at Gopalpur. Therefore, it requires the management, who is the decision maker to have a complete,
      holistic view of business rather than having a restricted view of only profitability of the venture. Therefore, business decisions
     should be based on strategic objectives. Decisions that can build dexterity and agility in business operations can be effective
     in having a positive impact on productivity, profitability and long-term growth. In today’s scenario, sustainability of business
     also depends on having a healthy business eco-system which requires business decisions to be compliant with various laws and
     regulations, be agile to supply chain disruptions, have sustainable business models that take care of environmental, societal
    and corporate governance issues. Only then will the company be able to garner stakeholder confidence that can significantly
     influence its brand image.
