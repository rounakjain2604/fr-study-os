CASE STUDY 1

Introduction
MBlend Pvt. Ltd., based in South India, is a mid-sized packaged food manufacturer with
a focus on ready-to-eat products such as instant mixes, snack items, and spice blends.
Established over two decades ago, the company initially built a strong regional presence
through distributor-led retail sales and an extensive dealer network. However, in recent
years, the business has struggled due to increasing competition, margin pressures, and
operational inefficiencies.
This financial due diligence (FDD) was initiated by a potential investor group interested in
a controlling stake as part of a strategic acquisition and turnaround proposal. The
investor’s interest was driven by the underlying brand value, market potential of MBlend’s
core SKUs, and the company's extensive physical distribution network in Tier 2 and Tier
3 cities. However, before committing capital, the investor commissioned a full-scale
diagnostic  review  to assess  financial  sustainability,  operational  bottlenecks, and
compliance health.
The review was structured into three phases: desk-based financial diagnostics, on-site
plant and warehouse  visits, and key personnel  interviews. The objective was  to
understand  historical  financial  performance,  identify  red  flags,  assess  statutory
compliance, and propose a realistic turnaround plan with phased capital infusion.
MBlend’s current situation is a classic case of a legacy brand with declining profitability
but strong recall. The due diligence highlights not only issues in pricing strategy and
receivables management but also governance gaps and working capital stress. This case
study presents a  holistic view of MBlend’s business challenges and the roadmap
proposed for revival.
Business Model and Operations
MBlend Pvt. Ltd. operates a traditional B2B2C model, manufacturing and distributing a
range of processed and packaged food products, including spice mixes, instant dosa/idli
batters, fryums, pickles, and ready-to-cook blends. Its manufacturing facility is located in
Tamil Nadu and is equipped with semi-automated lines for blending, roasting, and
packing. The plant runs single-shift operations, with peak capacity utilization touching
65% during the festive season.
The company primarily sells its products under its own brand through a widespread
distributor network spanning Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, Karnataka, and Kerala.
Products are stocked in general trade outlets, Kirana stores, and select regional modern
trade chains. Secondary sales are driven by a van-sales model and ground sales
executives (GSEs) assigned cluster-wise.

MBlend was supposed to maintain a short value chain — sourcing raw materials such as
pulses, spices, and edible oils from local mandis and converting them into packaged
SKUs at its facility. Packaging material is procured from approved vendors on a credit
cycle.
Despite a loyal customer base for select products like its sambar powder and instant rava
dosa mix, the portfolio lacks premium variants and has limited e-commerce presence.
Distribution dependency on credit-driven dealers and absence of a central warehouse
have affected working capital turnover. A digitally integrated supply chain is also missing,
with manual dispatch logs and unoptimized routes contributing to inefficiencies.
Due to its scale of operations and limited reach over the years, the company has been
increasing its B2B sales (institutional sale) in bulk of its products wherein the white
labelled products were being on-sold by the customers to large institutional opportunities
such as flight kitchens, catering contractors, and regional wholesalers etc.,
Ownership and Governance
MBlend Pvt. Ltd. is a closely held private limited company, originally founded in the early
2000s by Mr. Karthik R., a first-generation entrepreneur with a background in food
distribution and supply chain. Over time, the company was joined by two extended family
members, who together now constitute the full promoter group. The shareholding is split
approximately as follows: Mr. Karthik (51%), his cousin Mr. Rajesh (29%), and Mr.
Karthik’s sister (20%). All shares are held privately, with no institutional or external
investor involvement to date.
The company does not have a professionalized board and the two male promoters act as
directors. Strategic and financial decisions are largely centralized in the hands of Mr.
Karthik, who is also the authorized signatory for all bank and vendor dealings. Mr. Rajesh
oversees procurement and factory management but is not actively involved in financial
oversight or long-term planning.
No external advisors, independent directors, or governance structures are currently in
place. There is also no formal succession plan, and company records show limited
documentation around board resolutions or meeting minutes, which raises concerns on
compliance, transparency, and institutional readiness — especially in the context of
potential investor onboarding or exit planning.

Financial Red Flags & Risk Areas
The financial analysis of MBlend reveals several persistent red flags that contributed to
its weakened operating position and the need for a turnaround. Over the past three years,
the company has reported consistent operating losses, with negative EBITDA margins
averaging between -5% to -8%. The primary cause is the inability to pass on rising input
costs to institutional buyers, resulting in gross margin compression and below-cost pricing
for key SKUs.
Receivables are significantly overdue, with over ₹1.4 crore outstanding beyond 90 days
— primarily from modern trade and B2B clients. This has impacted cash flows, resulting
in delayed vendor payments. As of the last reported period, over ₹2 crore in payables
were pending, including several local vendors who have now paused further supply due
to lack of clarity on settlement timelines.
The company’s pricing strategy has remained stagnant despite increasing raw material
costs, and discounts offered to retain customers have further eroded margins. In addition,
lack of proper SKU-level costing and weak controls on scheme payouts have contributed
to profitability erosion.
Overall, working capital stress, high credit risk, poor margin management, and lack of
timely course correction make these financial issues central to the risk assessment and
turnaround planning.


Operational Bottlenecks
The on-ground due diligence of MBlend’s manufacturing unit revealed several critical
operational challenges that have significantly impacted efficiency, cost control, and
scalability. The company’s current plant in the Madras food park is operating at only 40–
45% capacity utilization, despite infrastructure capable of handling 12+ MT of daily output.
This underutilization is largely due to demand volatility, lack of consistent orders, and
supply-side disruptions.
A major operational concern is the age and condition of machinery. Many of the
packaging and processing units are 6–8 years old and lack modern automation. Frequent
downtimes, manual interventions, and inconsistent batch output have affected product
quality, particularly for SKUs with high precision requirements such as baked snacks and
premixes. Equipment breakdowns have also led to production backlogs and delayed
deliveries.

Supply chain gaps further compound the problem. The company lacks a robust vendor
scheduling system, often leading to raw material stockouts or overstocking. Procurement
remains ad hoc, with minimal price benchmarking or vendor consolidation, leading to high
input cost variability.
Storage and final-mile logistics are partially outsourced, with inadequate control on
dispatch tracking. Without plant modernization and supply chain integration, MBlend risks
continued inefficiencies and reputational impact in B2B segments.

Sales & Distribution Challenges
MBlend faces critical challenges in its sales and distribution ecosystem, stemming from
an unbalanced channel strategy and weak institutional retention. While the company
operates in both retail and institutional (B2B) markets, over 65% of its revenue is
skewed towards  institutional sales, including supply  to  flight kitchens, catering
contractors, and regional wholesalers. This leaves the business vulnerable to demand
fluctuations and contract renewal risks.
In the retail space, MBlend has limited penetration beyond Tier 2 cities. The brand lacks
a strong shelf presence, with most SKUs concentrated in fewer than 1,000 general trade
outlets. Modern trade placements are minimal, and the absence of a distributor-driven
expansion plan has hindered scale.
Receivables management poses another serious concern. A large share of institutional
debtors are on 90+ day credit cycles, with several accounts flagged as overdue.
Follow-ups remain irregular, and no dedicated credit control team is in place to monitor
payment timelines.
Additionally, logistics and secondary distribution remain fragmented, with limited
last-mile visibility. The lack of performance tracking by region or distributor leads to
revenue leakage and missed growth opportunities.
To unlock sustainable scale, MBlend must rebalance its channel mix, invest in retail
expansion, and deploy tighter credit control and route-to-market systems.

Cost Structure & Pricing Issues
MBlend’s cost structure reveals critical inefficiencies driven by input price volatility and
ad-hoc pricing strategies. The company sources key raw materials and packaging
materials, all of which are prone to severe price fluctuations. However, MBlend lacks a
structured procurement planning model or forward purchase contracts to hedge against
input cost inflation, resulting in high cost variability quarter-on-quarter.
On the sales side, MBlend follows a discount-heavy pricing strategy, especially in the
institutional channel, to retain bulk buyers and foodservice clients. Margin dilution has
been evident, with contribution margins falling below 15% for several SKUs, significantly
lower than the industry benchmark of 25–30%. Retail prices have remained stagnant
despite rising costs, leading to under-recovery.
Moreover, pricing decisions are not centralized — with sales teams often offering
discretionary discounts without finance team oversight. This lack of alignment has
contributed to margin leakage.
To restore financial health, MBlend must adopt a centralized pricing framework, enforce
invoice discipline, and implement structured procurement to manage cost  volatility
effectively.

Compliance & Legal Observations
The due diligence exercise uncovered multiple areas where MBlend has not maintained
robust compliance with statutory and regulatory obligations. While the company has
generally  filed GST  returns  and  complied  with FSSAI  standards,  delays and
inconsistencies were noted in income tax filings and employee-related statutory dues
such as PF and ESI contributions in certain months. In some cases, TDS deductions were
made but not remitted within the due dates, exposing the company to interest and penalty
risks.
On the legal front, MBlend has four ongoing cases—two civil suits related to packaging
disputes with a third-party vendor, and two employment-related matters filed by former
senior executives alleging wrongful termination and pending dues. While the monetary
exposure from these cases is not significant (estimated below ₹25 lakhs), the reputational
risk and potential settlement liabilities cannot be ruled out.
Additionally, during the factory walkthrough, labelling discrepancies were found on
Institutional cartons that may breach FSSAI labeling norms, which if flagged, could result
in non-compliance penalties or product recalls in certain jurisdictions.
There is also a lack of documented SOPs for statutory compliance monitoring, and no in-
house legal team to track or review exposure. Going forward, MBlend will require a
compliance calendar, internal legal tracker, and periodic audits to mitigate further risks.
Working Capital & Cash Flow Constraints
MBlend is currently facing acute working capital stress, primarily driven by elongated
receivable cycles, excessive inventory holding, and mounting trade payables. The
average receivable days stand at 92 days, significantly higher than the industry norm
of 45–60 days. A closer analysis shows that key institutional clients delay payments
beyond agreed credit terms, impacting the company’s liquidity position. Some distributors
are also holding back payments due to unresolved claims or expired schemes.
On the inventory side, the company holds an average of 78 days of raw materials and
finished goods, reflecting inefficient demand planning and procurement mismatches.
This  is especially evident in the slow-moving SKUs across certain rural and B2B
segments. Overstocking of packaging materials and low shelf-life products adds further
pressure on cash flows.
Creditor buildup is equally concerning. The company owes substantial dues to raw
material suppliers and packaging vendors, with creditor days rising to over 100 days.
Several vendors have already shifted to advance payment terms or partial deliveries,
choking production cycles.
MBlend has been bridging cash gaps through ad hoc working capital loans and
short-term borrowings,  increasing  its  interest burden. Without  better  receivable
collection, inventory optimization, and vendor negotiation, the cash flow crunch may
escalate into a solvency concern.
Turnaround Strategy Proposed
The proposed turnaround strategy for MBlend focuses on restoring profitability and
liquidity  through  cost  optimization,  pricing  corrections,  and  operational
restructuring. The first priority is to implement strict cost control across procurement,
packaging, and logistics. Vendor renegotiation, bulk procurement of core ingredients, and
rationalization of underperforming SKUs are expected to deliver 8–10% in material cost

savings.  Overheads—especially  related  to  distribution schemes and  promotional
spends—will be brought under a centralized budget with real-time monitoring.
On the pricing front, the company plans to move away from deep discounting, especially
in the institutional and B2B channels. Instead, value-based pricing will be adopted,
leveraging strong brand recognition in certain regional markets. Low-margin contracts will
be discontinued to ensure consistency between price realization and actual cost incurred.
Operationally, a phased plant modernization plan  is recommended  to address
bottlenecks and improve output consistency. Unproductive assets will be monetized –
either sold or leased to third-party processors. Additionally, the company is exploring
channel restructuring—focusing more on high-yield retail and e-commerce distribution
while pruning low-volume rural distributors.
These initiatives,  if executed diligently, are projected to improve MBlend’s EBITDA
margins by 6–8% over the next 12–18 months and restore positive cash flows.

Strategic Lessons
The MBlend due diligence presents key takeaways for investors, consultants, and
business decision-makers. One of the foremost lessons is the importance of early red
flag tracking—especially around receivable cycles, vendor dues, and pricing dilution.
Many of MBlend's current issues, such as strained cash flows and eroded margins, could
have been pre-empted  if quarterly MIS reporting, debtor aging analysis, and sales
incentive tracking had been more stringent from the start.
A critical insight is the risk of over-dependence on promoters for strategic and
financial decisions. MBlend’s decision-making was concentrated with the founding
team, with limited professional governance and weak board oversight. This led to
unchecked product launches, non-standard pricing practices, and ad-hoc distributor
selection—all of which contributed to business instability.
The due diligence also highlights the need for continuous compliance vigilance,
particularly  in high-volume FMCG setups where multiple operational  sites, vendor
contracts, and GST/tax exposures exist. Delayed statutory payments and informal pricing
arrangements increased regulatory risks.
Lastly, the importance of aligning business scalability with process maturity stands
out. As MBlend grew in topline, it failed to evolve its controls, costing models, and working
capital processes proportionately—leading to unsustainable operations. A data-driven,
professionalized approach is vital for long-term resilience.
