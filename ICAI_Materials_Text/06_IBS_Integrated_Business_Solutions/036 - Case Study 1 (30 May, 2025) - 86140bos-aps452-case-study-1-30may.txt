Case Study-1

The Indian sugar  industry has long been a  critical
component of the country’s agricultural and industrial
economy,  driven by  domestic  consumption,  export
demand, and government policies promoting ethanol
blending.  In  this competitive landscape, Greenfield
Agro Ltd., a leading listed sugar manufacturer with a
strong presence in western India, sought to expand its
market dominance by acquiring Sunrise Sugars Pvt.
Ltd., a struggling but asset-rich competitor based in
South India. The ₹1,200 crore all-cash deal was not just
a consolidation play but a strategic move to capitalize on
synergies,   regulatory   incentives,  and  operational
efficiencies.

Greenfield Agro was a well-established player in the sugar sector, with integrated operations
spanning sugarcane farming, sugar refining, and ethanol production. The company had a robust
balance sheet, with annual revenues exceeding ₹5,000 crores and a debt-to-equity ratio of 0.8,
reflecting prudent financial management. Greenfield had invested heavily in distillery operations,
aligning with the Indian government’s push for 20% ethanol blending in petrol by 2025.
However, its geographical reach was limited, with most facilities concentrated in Maharashtra and
Gujarat. To diversify its footprint and reduce regional risks, Greenfield eyed expansion into South
India, where sugarcane yields were higher due to favorable climatic conditions.

Sunrise Sugars, on the other hand, was a debt-laden but operationally sound company with
three sugar mills and an advanced ethanol distillery in Karnataka. Despite its strong infrastructure,
Sunrise had been struggling with liquidity issues, owing to ₹800 crores in debt and inefficient
working capital management. The company had accumulated tax losses of ₹450 crores, making
it an attractive target for a profitable acquirer like Greenfield. However, Sunrise’s financial distress
had led it into insolvency proceedings under the NCLT, and its lenders were keen on a resolution
that maximized recovery.

The acquisition made strategic sense for Greenfield due to market expansion and regional
diversification, as  it lacked a strong presence in South India, where Sunrise had established
supply chains and farmer relationships. The deal gave Greenfield access to Karnataka’s high-
yielding sugarcane belt, reducing dependency on existing regions. Sunrise’s modern ethanol
distillery was a key  attraction, complementing  Greenfield’s  biofuel  strategy  aligned  with
government incentives. Tax benefits through unabsorbed tax losses could shield future profits,
subject to Section 79 restrictions on ownership changes. Lenders agreed to a haircut on debt,
making the acquisition financially viable. Operational synergies such as logistics optimization
(saving ₹120 crores annually) and technology transfer opportunities further enhanced the
rationale.

Deal Structure and Funding

The acquisition was structured as an all-cash transaction, funded through a mix of 70% debt
(bank loans and External Commercial Borrowings or ECBs) and 30% equity (internal accruals
and preferential promoter allotments). Regulatory approvals from SEBI, NCLT, and RBI were
critical due to the open offer trigger and foreign debt component.

Greenfield’s valuation approach included a Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) valuation projecting
an equity value of ₹2,238 crore, using a WACC of 12% and a terminal growth rate of 4%.
However, due to Sunrise’s insolvency and liquidity risks, the final pricing was based on an
EV/EBITDA multiple of 8x, valuing Sunrise at ₹1,200 crore. Greenfield negotiated a debt haircut
with lenders and planned to monetize surplus land to partly fund the acquisition.

The ECB portion exposed Greenfield to forex risk, which was hedged through forward contracts
pegged at ₹82/USD against the spot rate of ₹83/USD. This reduced currency risk on repayments.
The company carefully monitored  its debt-to-equity ratio, which rose post-acquisition, and
managed interest cost impacts through long-term planning.

Post-Acquisition Challenges and Integration

Following  the  acquisition,  Greenfield conducted a  detailed purchase  price  allocation,
discovering that Sunrise’s inventory was overstated by ₹50 crore. This was corrected through a
prior-period adjustment in consolidated financial statements and an impairment loss was
recognized in accordance with accounting standards. Goodwill arising from the deal, amounting
to ₹320 crore, was subjected to mandatory annual impairment testing.

Operationally, Greenfield implemented Activity-Based Costing (ABC) to identify inefficiencies
in  logistics, cane procurement, and ethanol production. Centralized procurement improved
supplier bargaining power and logistics were optimized using fleet analytics. Transfer pricing
policies were introduced to ensure arm’s length transactions within the group, particularly for
ethanol sales. Legacy GST disputes amounting to ₹80 crore were addressed to regularize input
tax credits, and labour contracts were aligned with compliance requirements such as ESIC and
Provident Fund.

Despite initial earnings per share (EPS) dilution caused by increased interest expense, synergies
in logistics and ethanol production drove an ROI of 15% by the third year, validating the
acquisition rationale.

Regulatory, Tax, and Compliance Issues

The acquisition process involved strict adherence to regulations. SEBI’s disclosure requirements
were complied with, including pre-deal public announcements, board approvals, and post-
deal financial impact reporting. Trading restrictions and insider trading controls ensured no
information leaks  prior  to deal announcements. The board was  reconstituted  to include
independent directors, and internal audit scope was extended.

Tax authorities closely scrutinized the utilization of Sunrise’s unabsorbed tax losses, governed by
Section 79 of the Income Tax Act. Due to ownership changes exceeding 49%, restrictions

Financial Analysis and Valuation

The DCF model forecasted Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE) growth ranging from 11% in Year
1 to 15% in Year 5, with a terminal growth rate of 4%. Present value calculations yielded an
enterprise value of ₹3,037 crore, less ₹800 crore debt, resulting in equity value of approximately
₹2,238 crore.

However, the final acquisition price of ₹1,200 crore reflected market realities, including Sunrise’s
insolvency risks and lenders’ willingness to accept haircuts. The multiple-based valuation
approach (8x EV/EBITDA) was more conservative and aligned with distressed asset pricing.

The Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) was computed at 9.225%, balancing cost of debt
(9%) and equity (15%) weighted by the capital structure and tax shield effects.

Conclusion

1.  The acquisition allowed Greenfield Agro to expand regionally and diversify product offerings
     strategically.

2.  Valuation methods combined theory and market  reality, balancing  intrinsic value with
    insolvency risk.

3.  Complex tax and regulatory compliance issues, including FEMA, GST, and Section 79
     restrictions, were managed effectively.

4.  Operational integration focused on cost control, logistics efficiencies, and transfer pricing
    adherence.

5.  The acquisition ultimately delivered a 15% ROI by Year 3, supported by disciplined financial
   and governance frameworks.
