            Strategic Insights into the Chemical and Petrochemical (CPC) Industry

   Industry Landscape
  The Chemical and Petrochemical (CPC) industry holds a pivotal position in an economy as it provides the basic materials required
   for the functioning of other industries. It serves as a reliable indicator of manufacturing trends and supply chain challenges within an
   economy. The CPC industry is vast, encompassing various manufacturing sectors that heavily rely on chemicals. It provides critical
   feedstock to numerous industries, making it indispensable. In India, the chemical industry is highly diversified, covering more than
   80,000 commercial products1.
  The applications of the CPC industry span multiple sectors. In Agriculture and Nutrition, it plays a crucial role by contributing to
    fertilizers and crop protection. In the Pharma sector, it provides essential raw materials for the production of drugs and healthcare
   products. The Building and Construction sector benefits from key components such as adhesives, sealants, and polymers supplied
   by the CPC industry. Furthermore, it supports Mining and Infrastructure through specialized chemicals essential for processing and
   construction activities. In Personal Care and Hygiene, it enables the production of soaps, cosmetics, and sanitizers. The Automotive
  and Transportation sector depends on CPC for high-performance oils and greases, while the Paint and Coatings industry relies on
   raw materials for protective and decorative finishes. Additionally, the Consumer Goods and Electronics sector benefits from CPC
   products like plastics, adhesives, and packaging materials, emphasizing the industry’s indispensability across diverse domains.
   This diversity can be broadly classified2 into −
   •   Bulk Chemicals (Commodity Chemicals): Chemicals produced in large quantities but with lower value are considered bulk
       chemicals. They significantly cater to the downstream industries. They are further divided into 3 categories alkali, organic and
       inorganic.
   •   Petro-Chemicals: Also known as petroleum distillates, these chemicals are derived from petroleum and natural gas by refining.
         It includes polymers, and synthetic fibres, performance plastics and others.
   •   Speciality Chemicals: Chemicals with high value but low production volume are considered specialty chemicals like surfactants,
        textile chemicals, paints and coating, dyes, agrochemicals and others. This is highly researched and development-intensive
       category of chemicals. Speciality Chemicals account for more than 50% of the total chemical exports from India.
   India’s Chemical and Petrochemical (CPC) industry is worth 220 billion USD and it is expected to grow to about 300 billion USD
   by 20303. India offers a lucrative business ecosystem with favourable investment policies that have attracted global CPC industry,
   converting it into a potential investment region to that plays a pivotal role within the global supply chain.
   Various international and national megatrends, such as shifts in consumer preferences and income demographics, increasing
   digitalization, and innovation, are expected to drive the growth of India’s CPC industry in the coming years.

         Agrochemical Industry (Fertilizers/ Crop Nutrition / Speciality Chemicals)
           (a core product in our case studies)
Agrochemical industry is an important segment of the giant chemical and petrochemical (CPC) industry. Like medicines is to human
beings, agrochemicals protect plants from insects, pests, diseases, weeds among others, all of which lead to destruction of agricultural
crops. Given the rising population, agrochemicals play an important role in ensuring food security. It is a highly regulated industry
with lot of emphasis being placed on product safety.
India is an agrarian economy which needs a significant quantity of crop protection and nutrition products. Catering to food
requirements of a growing population along with a constraint for availability of arable land has created an insatiable demand for
high quality crop nutrition chemicals that can improve productivity from agricultural activities. India is emerging as a potential
hub for production of agrochemicals, it is already a significant exporter of these products. The government places special emphasis
on ensuring that farmers can get supplies in the form of fertilizers, pesticides, insecticides and other crop nutrition solutions at
reasonable rates. For this it has subsidy schemes like the Nutrient Based Subsidy (for non-urea products) and separate subsidies
for urea-based products. It also provides grants to companies/ firms to promote research and development in order to develop less
hazardous and eco-friendly products.
Snapshot of Value Chain of a Firm in the Agrochemical Industry
The value chain refers to the series of activities a business performs to create and deliver a product or service to the market. It
includes everything from product conception to final delivery and after-sales service. From forecast and planning to customer
support, each stage of the value chain contributes to creating innovative, sustainable, and safe agrochemical solutions. By leveraging
advanced technologies, maintaining rigorous quality standards, and aligning with market trends, agrochemical companies can meet
the evolving demands of farmers while addressing environmental and regulatory challenges.
Forecasting and Planning −
The industry is characterized by high production and sales volumes. Hence planning and forecasting, in particular demand forecasting
is the bedrock in ensuring seamless operations. Since these are complex activities, forecasting and planning may take the following
into account:
Grouping Diverse Products: Grouping products based on types, such as fertilizers, pesticides, and specialty chemicals, allows for more
accurate demand forecasting and better inventory management. This will help the company identify high performing, fast moving
products as against slow moving low performing products.
Seasonal Forecasting: The agrochemical industry is heavily influenced by seasons, as different crops require different treatments at
various growth stages. Seasonal forecasting helps companies prepare for demand spikes, aligning production and distribution with
planting and harvesting cycles.

  Order Book Analysis: Analysing past order data helps predict future demand patterns, identifying trends that can inform production
  planning and inventory management. It also helps improve client management systems.
  Trend Analysis: Keeping track of market trends, like increased demand for organic or eco-friendly agrochemicals, allows companies
  to adjust their strategies and invest in relevant R&D. Trend analysis is crucial in a dynamic industry where regulatory changes or
  environmental concerns can shift demand patterns.
 R&D Laboratory −
  Developing Formulations: Companies make crucial investments in R&D to discover effective, environmentally sustainable and safe
  products for crop nutrition and protection. Along with scientific experiments on chemical compounds, advanced technologies such
  as data science, artificial intelligence, predictive analysis among others are used to design compounds, predict efficacy and toxicity of
  different innovations. Such technologies can aid in company’s efforts to reduce development timelines and costs.
  Approval and Testing: Field trials, toxicity assessments and environmental impact studies are undertaken as part of rigorous testing
  procedures to ensure safety, effectiveness and environmental impact of formulations before they are released. Regulatory approvals
  from authorities (such as the Central Insecticides Board in India) are required to ensure the product meets both national and
  international safety standards.
  Patenting: Post approval of a formulation, a company patents it to protect its intellectual property rights. Patents prevent competitors
  from replicating the product for a specified period, which incentivizes innovation and allows for a competitive edge in the market.
  Material Procurement −
  Approved Supplier and Vendor Selection: Quality, reliability, compliance with health and safety standards and cost-effectiveness are
   critical criteria while choosing suppliers and vendors. Reliable suppliers are required to ensure a steady flow of quality raw materials.
  Long-Term Purchase Agreements: To secure a steady supply and potentially negotiate better rates, long-term agreements with
  suppliers are common. This also helps suppliers plan their own production cycles, which can be beneficial for both parties.
  Just-in-Time (JIT) Arrangements: JIT procurement enables companies to receive materials as needed. This helps reduce waste, limit
  exposure of raw materials to potential degradation and minimize inventory costs.
  Conformance and Quality Control Inspections: Raw materials should meet required specifications. For this, inspections and audit are
  conducted regularly to reduce the risk of non-compliant or defective material being used in production.
  Material Traceability: It is important to be able to track the origin and movement of raw materials through the supply chain. This
   is crucial for crucial for quality control, regulatory compliance, and in case of recalls or audits. It ensures that any issue at any stage
  within the supply chain can be traced back to its source.
  Product Manufacture −
  Process Types (Continuous, Batch, or JIT): The type of production will depend on demand, production capacity, storage facility and
  cost considerations. Agrochemical companies may choose continuous (constant production) or batch production (smaller, specific
  quantities). Some may employ JIT production to align manufacturing with demand, reducing storage needs and minimizing waste.
  Scalability and Capacity Utilization: In case of fluctuating market demands, the company should be able to scale up or scale down its
  production capacity to ensure that production resources are maximized leading to efficient output and cost management.
  Process Automation: Technology enabled automation of business processes is called process automation. Especially useful while
  performing repetitive tasks, automation improves precision in mixing, processing, and packaging, reducing the risk of human error
  and enhancing efficiency. It also supports scalability, allowing companies to quickly ramp up production when necessary and can
  help transform business processes.
  Maintenance: Maintaining peak performance of assets and equipment requires scheduled maintenance at regular intervals. This
   is essential for efficient operations, upkeep of equipment, prevent breakdowns and ensure consistent production quality. They
  minimize downtime and improve operational longevity.
  Quality Control and Inspection: Due to the hazardous nature of many of its products, agrochemical companies are carrying out
  stringent quality checks, ensuring adherence to regulatory and company standards. Analytical testing, such as chemical composition
  analysis and stability testing, helps ensure product efficacy and safety.
  Inventory Management and Distribution −
  Warehouse Management: Receiving, storing, packing and shipping products are the day-to-day processes in warehouse management.
  These vital behind the scene activities ensure on-time delivery to customers. Warehouse management systems should optimize use
  of warehouse space to store maximum inventory, it should be easy for staff to find the required inventory, accurate and efficient order
  fulfilment, co-ordinating with suppliers and transportation companies so that materials arrive and orders ship on time. Efficient
  management of these activities improves productivity while keeping the costs low.
  Storing and Handling Hazardous Goods: Since agrochemicals can be hazardous, specialized storage facilities with proper ventilation,
  temperature control, and spill containment are necessary. Minimizing accumulation of expiring products through better inventory
  management. Staff must also be trained in the safe handling of chemicals to ensure safety and regulatory compliance.
  Inventory Traceability and Expiry: Inventory management systems help track expiration dates and ensure proper rotation of stock.
  Traceability ensures any issues with a batch can be quickly isolated, which is especially crucial in the case of hazardous or sensitive
  chemicals. Use barcode, QR code, or RFID technology for accurate and efficient track and trace across the supply chain. Traceability
   is also important to combat the grey market where spurious products are sold.
  Packing and Dispatch: Proper packaging is essential to maintain product integrity during transportation. Packing protocols are
  followed to protect against damage, leaks, and contamination. Efficient dispatch processes help reduce lead times, ensuring timely
  delivery to customers.

Sales Management −
Managing distribution channels: Distribution channels play an important role in maximising profits for the company. Strong
relationships with distributors, wholesalers and retailers is needed to motivate them to promote the company’s products. Identifying
high performing distributors and high performing products is essential to understand which products to focus on and which
distributor network to utilize to maximise sales. Engaging distributors by communicating about product launches and promotional
activities is important. In order to have a wider reach, a company can have multiple channels for distribution such as direct sales or
retail partnerships. It is vital to integrate the different distribution channels to ensure coordination and collaboration between them.
Sales order management: Optimizing warehouse location, warehouse management systems, robust inventory management systems,
minimising lead times and stock outs, real time tracking and efficient transportation networks, error free and timely delivery of
orders to customers, efficient complaint management processes are essential to ensure customer satisfaction, strengthens client
relationships and drives business growth. Companies enter into long term supply contracts with their customers hence it is important
to adhere to the terms and conditions in the contract. Companies, particularly those manufacturing fertilizers are covered under
government subsidy schemes. The government subsidy is based on the sales made by the company and hence accountability and
traceability of orders is very important.
Product Pricing: Pricing strategies are influenced by production costs, market demand, competitor pricing, and regulated pricing,
where applicable. In some cases, the price is regulated by government policies or industry norms. Companies also consider external
factors such as raw material costs and exchange rates, which are particularly significant in the agrochemical industry due to its
reliance on imported raw materials.
Customer Support and After-Sales Service −
Technical Support: As a unique selling proposition, agrochemical companies often provide on-ground technical support to help
farmers with product application and answer questions regarding usage, safety, and compatibility with crops. Better understanding
about the products would improve crop yield. This would be a key driver for ensuring food security to a growing population.
Feedback Mechanisms: Feedback from farmers and distributors helps companies improve products and identify potential issues. This
information is valuable for refining R&D and manufacturing processes.
Product Recall and Traceability: In case of defective or harmful products, companies must have efficient recall systems to withdraw
products from the market and communicate with users promptly. Traceability systems ensure companies can quickly identify
affected batches.
Sustainability and Environmental Impact Initiatives: Many agrochemical companies now offer post-sales services focused on
sustainability, such as recycling programs for used containers and programs to help farmers reduce chemical usage.

 Summary
 Each of these steps collectively supports an efficient, resilient, and compliant supply chain in the agrochemical industry. By
 integrating strong quality controls, safety protocols, and responsive customer service practices, agrochemical companies can not
 only meet regulatory requirements but also drive growth in a competitive, regulation-heavy market.

                                              Strategic Role of Ammonia and Natural Gas
  Ammonia plays an essential role within industrial chemicals, fertilizers, and mining chemicals. Its applications
   range from being a foundational ingredient in nitrogen-based fertilizers that drive agricultural productivity to
   serving as a critical component in the production of various industrial and mining chemicals. Ammonia’s
   importance in these sectors stems not only from its chemical properties but also from the essential products
   derived from it, which support the broader economy by ensuring food security, advancing mining efficiency, and driving
   industrial manufacturing. The production of ammonia itself heavily relies on natural gas, which serves as both a feedstock and
   a fuel source in the synthesis process.
   Natural gas is primarily converted into hydrogen, which is then combined with nitrogen to produce ammonia. The dependency
  on natural gas in ammonia production is a key strategic factor, as fluctuations in natural gas prices directly affect the cost of
  ammonia production. Reliable access to natural gas can therefore stabilize ammonia production costs, ensuring operational
   continuity and allowing for predictable pricing of its ammonia-based products.

                                           About Case Studies
Two case studies connected with the Agrochemical Industry highlight the need for effective supply chain
management to tackle disruptions and price volatility. In Case Study 1, Dakshin Fertilizer and Petrochem Ltd.
(DFPL) made the strategic decision to produce ammonia domestically within its manufacturing facility at Eloor.
This decision was aimed at mitigating the impact of supply chain disruptions and volatility in procurement
prices. This backward integration allowed for better realignment of the supply chain, ensuring seamless production of its core
products. Similarly, in Case Study 2, Crop Yield Private Limited (CYPL) established a joint venture in Gopalpur, Odisha. The
management’s strategy in this case was to gain cost efficiencies through economies of scale by manufacturing fertilizers on a
large scale. As part of supply chain management, it was decided that setting up a plant in the same region as its supplier, GIL,
Gopalpur, would ensure an adequate supply of natural gas, a critical raw material.
The profitability of any business is significantly influenced by the tax structure. Case Study 1 discusses how setting up fertilizer
manufacturing facilities, can help companies avail themselves of income tax benefit under Section 35AD of the Income Tax Act.
Similarly, Case Study 2 addresses indirect tax issues relating to Goods and Services Tax (GST) and customs duty. It touches upon
the impact of:
(i) government subsidy on while determining value of supply of fertilizers for GST,
(ii) how continuous procurement of natural gas requires proper internal checks to ensure that invoices under GST regulations
    are received on time,
(iii) not-so-common scenarios of dealing with unutilized Input Tax Credit (ITC) with respect to GST,
(iv) liability to pay custom duty and seek compensation in case of pilfered raw material while in the custody of custodians like
    the Port Trusts.
Both case studies demonstrate how strategic decisions that drive cost leadership can help maintain a company’s competitive
position. DFPL, in Case Study 1, plans to achieve this through backward integration by setting up its in-house ammonia plant in
Eloor, which can reduce storage, transportation, and logistics costs while mitigating price volatility. In Case Study 2, CYPL aims
to achieve economies of scale to gain cost efficiencies. Additionally, Case Study 2 illustrates how disruptions at key supplier GIL’s
facilities in Gopalpur can have a detrimental impact on CYPL’s operations. Limited quantities of natural gas had to be procured
from alternative suppliers, and the inability to operate at planned capacity for a prolonged period affected CYPL’s business
viability. This highlights the critical need for managing the upstream supplier chain to address unforeseen exigencies. Financial
stability also impacts CYPL’s negotiating power when entering and terminating contracts with suppliers.
Strategic tools, such as the Altman Z-score, helped CYPL’s management assess its financial stability and take appropriate
measures. The issue of going concern and the possibility of onerous contracts should also be carefully evaluated by the statutory
auditor during the audit process. These considerations are discussed in Case Study 2.
With increasing awareness of the health hazards and environmental damage caused by chemicals, there has been heightened
regulation and scrutiny of companies in this industry. Case Study 2 demonstrates how Mendelow’s Matrix can be employed
by CYPL to address stakeholder concerns effectively, ensuring minimal disruptions to business activities. Case Study 1
illustrates how environmental cost accounting can be incorporated into product pricing to support long-term profitability and
competitiveness, helping the company build a sustainable business model. In Case Study 2, government subsidy support is
shown to partly alleviate financial distress, while the need for CYPL to remain agile and continuously reposition itself for long-
term sustainability is emphasized.
To navigate this challenging industrial landscape, strong corporate governance is essential. The Board of Directors should
include professionals with expertise in diverse fields to provide balanced and informed decision-making. Case Study 1 highlights
the importance of adhering to proper procedures to ensure compliance with laws and regulations, particularly under the
Companies Act, 2013, and SEBI regulations. Effective corporate governance emphasizes accountability for decisions taken and
reinforces ethical corporate responsibility, which can significantly boost stakeholder confidence. Accountability extends not
only to the Board of Directors and internal officers but also to the statutory auditor, who, as part of their role, is privy to sensitive
and confidential information about the company and its prospects. Case Study 2 addresses the evaluation of top management’s
performance during critical situations, such as those faced by CYPL, and underscores the value of integrating non-financial
performance measures with financial metrics to ensure a comprehensive assessment of management effectiveness and overall
organizational resilience.

                             Dakshin Fertilizers and Petrochem Ltd.

   Dakshin Fertilizers and Petrochem Ltd. (DFPL), a leading producer of industrial chemicals, crop nutrition, and mining chemicals,
   stands as a testament to the vision and entrepreneurial spirit of its founder, Mr. N. Venkata Ravi. Born in October 1948 in the modest
    village of Nungambakkam, Tirupati, Venkata Ravi displayed a natural acumen for business from an early age. At just 18, he began his
   career in trade, demonstrating an insatiable curiosity and a drive to build something extraordinary. In 1962-64, he established Dakshin
   Trading Co., a venture through which he quickly gained prominence, earning the nickname “The Chemical Architect” for his expertise
   and influence in the chemical market.
   Sustained by the success of Dakshin Trading Co., Mr. Ravi expanded into manufacturing, promoting Dakshin Chemicals Private
   Limited in 1975. Under his leadership, the company achieved profitability within three years, reinforcing his reputation as a visionary
   in the chemicals industry. Building on this success, he founded Dakshin Fertilizers and Petrochem Ltd. (DFPL) in 1980, with an aim
   to meet the increasing demand for industrial and agricultural chemicals across India. Headquartered in Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala,
   DFPL’s main manufacturing facility is in Eloor, Ernakulam, along with additional plants in Dahej (Gujarat), Hisar (Haryana), and
   Brahmapur (Odisha). These facilities produce specialty products that serve critical sectors of the Indian economy, including agriculture,
   pharmaceuticals, mining, and infrastructure. The units in Hisar and Brahmapur are currently small-scale units. The Board of DFPL
   decided to set up these units in order to have a pan India presence. In the coming years, DFPL plans to deepen its market presence
   in northern and eastern India. As and when this is achieved, the scale of production at these units will be ramped up to cater for the
   increased demand. The Board felt that this ramping up of capacity will be easier if there is already an existing manufacturing set up in
   these areas.
   Venkata Ravi’s long-term aspirations continue to guide DFPL’s business strategy and operations. The company is committed to
   leadership in chemicals and agri-nutrients, underpinned by a dedication to excellence and sustainability. DFPL’s vision to become
  a leader in chemicals and agri-nutrients, reflects its emphasis on sustainable growth, aligning closely with India’s rapid development
   in key sectors like agriculture and infrastructure. With a mission to manufacture and deliver sustainable chemicals and
   agri-nutrients, DFPL aims to be the trusted partner for industries that drive India’s growth. Through these foundational values, the
  company continually strives for profitable growth while remaining committed to a sustainable future, honoring Venkata Ravi’s legacy
   of foresight, determination, and excellence.
   At a recent board meeting convened to discuss the company’s strategic direction, Mr. Venkata Ravi, the Chairman of DFPL, addressed
   the board members, sharing his insights on the challenges and opportunities faced by the company.

    “The financial years 2023-24 and 2024-25 have been among the most volatile on record. All our raw material inputs saw an unprecedented
     hike due to global uncertainties, forcing us to raise the price of all our finished products. Fortunately, despite these price hikes to offset the increase
     in raw material costs, there has been no impact on the demand for our products from any of our core sectors and markets. The robust domestic

Dakshin Fertilizers and Petrochem Ltd. (DFPL) is a reputed company manufacturing industrial chemicals, crop nutrition and
mining chemicals. Headquartered in Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala, DFPL’s main manufacturing facility is in Eloor, Ernakulam,
along with additional plants in Dahej (Gujarat), Hisar (Haryana), and Brahmapur (Odisha). These facilities produce specialty
products that serve critical sectors of the Indian economy, including agriculture, pharmaceuticals, mining, and infrastructure. The
units in Hisar and Brahmapur are currently small-scale units. The Board of DFPL decided to set up these units in order to have a
pan India presence. In the coming years, DFPL plans to deepen its market presence in northern and eastern India. As and when
this is achieved, the scale of production at these units will be ramped up to cater for the increased demand. The Board felt that this
ramping up of capacity will be easier if there is already an existing manufacturing set up in these areas.
Setting up new manufacturing facility at Gopalpur and closure of Brahmapur facility
DFPL has established a strong foothold in the crop nutrition market, including fertilizers, with significant penetration in Odisha
and the northern belt of Andhra Pradesh. To capitalize on rising demand in these markets, the company is poised to expand its
production capacity, paving the way for increased market presence.
The fertilizer industry operates under stringent regulations and is supported by government subsidies aimed at ensuring affordability
for farmers. In such a regulated environment, achieving cost efficiencies is crucial for enhancing profitability. As a capital-intensive
sector, the fertilizer industry depends heavily on economies of scale, which play a vital role in driving these efficiencies.
To meet its expansion goals, DFPL plans to set up a new manufacturing unit exclusively for fertilizer production, with a planned
capacity of 10,00,000 MT per year. The facility will produce both urea-based and non-urea-based fertilizers, requiring a substantial
and consistent supply of natural gas—a key raw material in fertilizer manufacturing.
Currently, Gaspro India Limited (GIL), through its Paradip unit, is the primary supplier of natural gas to DFPL’s Brahmapur facility.
However, the Brahmapur unit operates on a modest scale, making it a smaller customer for GIL. Recently, GIL has commissioned a
larger Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) facility at Gopalpur, Odisha, which has the capacity to meet the significant natural gas demands
of DFPL’s proposed fertilizer unit.
Leveraging its strong business relationship with GIL and the cost benefits achievable through economies of scale, DFPL’s Board of

                                    Gas Leak in pipeline of Gaspro India Limited
                                        Gaspro India Limited (GIL) is a well-established supplier of natural gas to various
                                                  industrial and commercial companies. It recently set up its Liquefied Natural Gas
                                     (LNG) facility in Gopalpur, Odisha, from which it supplies natural gas to customers
                                                  via a pipeline network spanning nearly 1,000 kilometers across Odisha and Andhra
                                             Pradesh. In January 2025, CYPL and GIL, Gopalpur, entered into a 5-year long-
                                        term contract for the supply of natural gas, covering the period from January 2025
                                               to January 2030. Given the large scale of CYPL’s operations, it became one of GIL’s
                                                   largest customers.
                              On September 30, 2025, a massive fire broke out following a gas leak that led to an
explosion in GIL’s underground natural gas pipeline at Arjyapalli, Odisha. The incident tragically claimed the lives of 15 people and
caused significant property damage. As a result, GIL was forced to halt the supply of natural gas through its pipelines in Odisha to
all customers for an extended period until the safety of operations could be fully ensured. This time was necessary for GIL’s technical
team to repair the pipelines and conduct safety tests across the entire pipeline network originating from its LNG facility in the region.
The suspension of natural gas supply caused major disruptions for all of GIL’s customers, who relied heavily on gas for their production
needs. CYPL also suffered significant losses due to the sudden stoppage of the gas supply from GIL. Despite the critical business need
for natural gas, the extraordinary nature of the accident made it impossible to predict when GIL would be able to resume its supply.
Consequently, CYPL canceled its long-term contract with GIL.
To maintain minimal operations, in October 2025, Mr. Ramakant Bhushan, Managing Director of CYPL, approached Natural Gas
Suppliers Limited (NGSL)—a competitor of GIL—for the procurement of natural gas. NGSL, with facilities in Paradip, Odisha,
agreed to supply natural gas to CYPL.
To secure a stable procurement price, CYPL entered into a 5-year fixed-price contract with NGSL, spanning from October 2025 to
September 2030. Compared to GIL, NGSL’s production capability was significantly smaller, and the volume of gas NGSL could supply
to meet CYPL’s demands was much lower. Additionally, since the Gopalpur region is still developing as an industrial hub, there were
no other alternative suppliers willing to deliver natural gas to CYPL’s Gopalpur facility. Therefore, NGSL became CYPL’s sole supplier
from October 2025 onward.
Since CYPL is currently operating at a minimal scale of production, it has lost the cost efficiencies it could generate from economies
of scale. Most of its cost structure consists of fixed costs that must be incurred regardless of the actual scale of production. CYPL had
to bear fixed expenses such as loan repayments, rent, and regular minimum maintenance of production facilities, despite operating
at minimal capacity. This increased the per-unit cost of production for CYPL. However, due to regulatory constraints governed by
the government’s subsidy mechanism and market competition, the company could not pass on the increased cost of production to
customers. As a result, CYPL sold its fertilizers at a loss, further straining its financial position.
Uncertainty regarding “Going Concern” of CYPL
In April 2026, CYPL’s management is in the process of finalizing the financial statements for the fiscal year 2025-26. The prolonged
aftermath of the GIL pipeline explosion has left the company in a precarious position, with no clear timeline for GIL’s resumption
of natural gas supplies to Gopalpur. The company has been compelled to scale down operations to the bare minimum, which has
resulted in the loss of a substantial number of customers and severely impacted its revenue generation capabilities. Dis-economies of
scale took away cost efficiencies that were originally expected to be garnered from such a large venture.
Cash flow has turned negative due to limited inflows, while outflows continue for fixed costs such as interest payments, staff
salaries, rentals, plant maintenance and upkeep among other costs. This financial strain has raised serious concerns about the joint
venture between DFPL and Greenspan GmbH, which is becoming increasingly economically unviable. CYPL’s management, led by
Mr. Ramakant Bhushan, is uncertain about the company’s ability to continue operations in the near future. The management has
decided to disclose the uncertainty regarding the company’s “Going Concern” status in the notes to the financial statements, signaling
a potential risk to the company’s continued operations as well as the management’s plans to deal with these conditions.
In light of this situation, CA. Bharat Chowdhary was appointed as the statutory auditor for the financial years 2024-25 and 2025-26.
The management informed him of the adverse business conditions and potential “going concern” issues. Recognizing the gravity of
the situation, the auditor noted the company’s financial instability. On June 15, 2026, CA. Bharat Chowdhary issued his audit report
